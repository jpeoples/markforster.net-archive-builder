Bev is correct. IMHO, there is no one size fits all system. In fact, even for the same person one system works well in certain circumstance and another works better in other times. It also depends on the mental makeup of the person.

For example, in my case, I use both AF and DIT. I really like AF and use it most of the time to move forward on multiple fronts. I have found that "Little & often" gives me small victories that add up to big victories over time. However, there are times when I use DIT exclusively. I use AF list as a sort of master list and DIT items come from this list. When I have a largish project with tight deadlines, I tend to use DIT. On all the other situations, I am very happy with AF.

Interestingly, (for me) I have found that AF works best when I am more or less in control. And, the higher the influence of external circumstances, the less effective AF becomes - unless I can seize control at some point. So, as long as I have a certain degree of freedom to choose what I need to do, AF works great. If this is universally true (and that's a big IF), then each person will probably fall somewhere along this spectrum.

As a side note, this awareness has helped me significantly to influence some of my own circumstances. While I don't think I can eliminate all the external pressures, I do think I can minimize them if I am vigilant enough.

Have been daydreaming lately about integrating Autofocus into my GTD/DIT hybrid system because of the abundance of creativity and thoughts AF has brought into my work.

The system would consist of three parts.

First there could be a time-driven part consisting of a calendar, a task diary and some kind of hold-file system (e.g. tickler file or writing on-hold-items in the diary). The diary would only contain several weeks to keep it managable.

Second there would be a list of projects, responsibilities, duties, roles, etc (the work program) with additional working plans for each item on this work program list.

Third there would be an Autofocus list for capturing all kinds of input from papers or thoughts, working on that input, making decisions and distributing the work either to the calendar-diary-thing or to the projects-planning-thing or letting it stay on the AF list to be further worked on there.

Items that are too time-sensitive would go into the calendar-diary direction.
Items that were dismissed from the AF list but still have to be done would become items on the work-program-list with the need for planning.
The planning-task itself would either go into the diary or the AF list.

The weekly review would keep all this tied together and updated.
The weekly review itself would be a task either in the diary or the calendar.

Right now it's just a daydream. What would you say? Too chaotic?

Hi everyone,
I'm a fan of AF - and of DIT and of GTD. They all have gems of wisdom and powerful techniques, most of which are complementary, but it seems to me that we need to blend the best bits (for us) together in a way which fits for our particular style, and the nature of our work.

I use AF both at home and work, and I find it works differently in each context. At home AF suits well - when I have some free time and want to get on with things. But at work the AF list needs to sit alongside calendar commitments, daily essentials and imminent deadlines when I am choosing what to spend time on.

For what it's worth, my work system is to have a must-do list for the day, which has the essentials and regular stuff, plus any same-day urgent tasks. And I keep a list of major tasks with due dates this week or next week alongside the AF list (a bit like the index card some people use) to make sure I don't lose track and get caught out on deadlines. In the background I have a master project list, and project notes, and I also keep agenda lists of things to discuss with different people/ groups. In reality I often have to revert to the 'common sense' mode to do things when they need to be done or can most efficiently be done.

The real benefit of AF for me even if used in this way is that it encourages just getting started and working on things little and often - by helping us phrase things in do-able chunks.

On the other hand, the downside is that this forum is very addictive! I'm not sure yet if it feeds our procrastination, or is motivational and educational in our quests to be more productive!

Looking forward to hearing more views on the hybrid approach!
