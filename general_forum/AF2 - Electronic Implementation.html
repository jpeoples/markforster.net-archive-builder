Rather than clutter up Mark's AF2 blog posting with an electronic implementation discussion, I've taken the liberty to start this thread...

Firstly, thanks very much for AF2, Mark - I can feel right away this approach removes the major worry of having must-do-now tasks hidden away on other task pages.

It seems much less fuss to implement electronically than AF1...if your ToDo List software allows you a "natural" sort order where it defaults to putting the last task entered at the top of your electronic list, but then allows you to drag tasks around to customize the order.

I have created a marker task to use to drag down to where the "unactioned task block" begins. i.e. a task named ">>>>".

Of course, you will need to leave completed items on show to see where the first contiguous "on notice for dismissal" tasks block begins. My software dims the completed items so it is fairly clear what is active and what is not.

On completing a task, mark it as complete but then make an immediate copy and paste it back into the ToDo list. My software automatically then creates a clean new task with the same name at the top of the list.

You will need another task marker (say, a task named "*******" ) to drag to mark where the dismissed tasks begin. Anything below that marker is considered dismissed.

I already have an electronic implementation working on my Blackberry...seems pretty slick so far...though obviously haven't dismissed anything yet. :)

2009-06-28 13:34
Been thinking it over and there seems to be a way to improve on the paper version of AF2 in an electronic approach.

At the start of the day after you've identified the "On Notice" tasks for the day, add a property to them such as a different colour, a text prefix (e.g. "***"), a different status or category or whatever is necessary to make them stand out from other tasks on your main task list.

Then simply re-create these tasks at the top of the task list. Takes just a keystroke to do on my Blackberry PDA.

Now you have the advantage of the "On Notice" tasks being towards the top of your list throughout the day together with your urgent/must-do tasks...seems like the best of both worlds.

Once any of these "On Notice" tasks are done, just remove the property you added earlier and re-add to the start of the list so they look like ordinary tasks again...since the task has now escaped being "On Notice".

Tomorrow morning, any "On Notice" tasks that are not touched are moved into a "Dismissed tasks" folder or filtered out of the main task list, or whatever your preferred method of dealing with them is.

2009-06-29 10:12
I'm using Things for iPhone to implement AF2:

Pros:

- new tasks are automatically added to the top of the list
- you can drag and drop tasks which makes positioning an 'on notice' line simple
- tasks that cannot be started today can be scheduled for a future date and remain hidden from view until they pop up at the top of your list on their start date, reducing list clutter. 
- tasks with due dates are highlighted in red up to two weeks in advance of their deadline, which provides a little help for your intuition.
- dismissed tasks can be moved to a separate list, making it easy to isolate and identify them for review.
- completed tasks are automatically moved to a logbook on the following day where they can be reviewed for possible follow-up. A pop-up notification tells you how many tasks we're moved when the app is first launched each day.
- a notification badge on the iPhone's home screen tells you how many overdue/urgent items are on your list.
- task entry is relatively quick and painless
- you can add tags to tasks and then filter your tasks by tag. I find this particularly useful for errands, as it makes them easy to isolate when I am out and about.           

Cons: 

Repeating tasks aren't created automatically, you have to reschedule them manually.

Tips: 

The AF2 instructions say: "At the BEGINNING of each day, go to your oldest active page, and draw a line after the first block of unactioned tasks..."

Because of the way Things logs completed items, it is best to "draw" this line at the END of each day and to dismiss the previous days on notice tasks at the same time.

EXPERIMENT: AF1 ELECTRONIC VERSION

I used my vacation as an opportunity to try an electronic implementation of AF1. It was a working vacation as I was doing voluntary work for a charity - so I still had a healthy work-load to 'stress' the AF system. But I had been too afraid to damage my productivity at work with this new approach - the paper based system was working so well.

I wont go into all with the implementation details - I used my blackberry mostly and synchronised its task-list with Outlook on my laptop. I had spent some time figuring out a good 'notation' to use to preserve the key properties of my AF1 pages on both the blackberry and on outlook. Then on my first day of vacation I typed in all the items on my AF list and left my notebook behind as I headed off. The nature of our journey made the use of the blackberry much better than a big paper notebook - and each night I synched with my laptop and could review the lists in a larger format.

I developed a lot of really neat tricks for looking at my list in various ways - I could query a persons name and see all the stuff pertaining to that person (or company, or location, or context etc). I could put reminders on things and colour-code the display based on those. I was able to sort, slice and dice the list any way I liked - and I frequently did. In fact I spent more time looking at my tasks then I did getting them done.

So in the end the experiment was a failure. My productivity declined to what it used to be before AF! Procrastination of resisted tasks became a huge problem. Not because I was on vacation. Not because of any limitation of the blackberry or the list software. When I switched back to a paper system a few days ago, I experienced a productivity boost straight away. Sigh.

I am a geek and a techie - I just LOVE gadgets and electronic tools. I can do things with spread-sheets you wouldn't believe. But when it comes to getting things done - the electronic version seems to get in the way instead of helping!

So I look closely at what happens when I use a paper system. Something about the ritual and the physicality of the pages allows my subconscious and intuitive mind to filter out and focus on the right things, overcome my tendency to procrastinate and not get lost in endless analysis and prioritising and re-organising - I just get on with the tasks!

I envy you who can make this work using the latest eletronic devices and software - I love that stuff - but for me its back to the notebook and pen.

I am implementing AF2 today - starting over with a blank book - so I will only use my blackberry for emails, calendar and as a short-term note-taker - the real AF2 list will be in my trusty hard-backed A4 notebook.

At least I tried and now I need wonder no more. Thanks to everyone here for their advice on implementing an electronic AF system. And a BIG thank you to Mark for his wisdom and generosity in making AF1 and AF2 available to us.

Paul

Trevor:

The tip you mention at the end of your post – putting tasks on notice at the end of each day, rather than the beginning – had occurred to me, but note that it's only a partial solution to the problem I described. It will avoid the worst case result of having the earliest block of unactioned tasks contain *all* your unactioned tasks; but the earliest block is still likely be a different block, and a bigger one, than it would be if no logging had taken place on previous days. (On paper, as Mark envisages AF2 in the first instance, there is no equivalent of logging, of course.) And that probably means either that more tasks are going to get dismissed than would be if you were working the system on paper, or that there'll be more pressure to keep moving to the early part of the list as quickly as possible to prevent dismissal, thus leaving less room for dealing with recently arrived but urgent tasks. Of course, it's possible that the difference won't make a difference to the effectiveness of the system, or even that it will improve it (for all I know); but it's definitely not going to be working in the way Mark intends. And my own preference is to try the system the way Mark's designed it first (just because I tend to trust him on this stuff, given my past experiences with his ideas, and with tweaking them).

Not an exact reply to Martin, but I hope this tip helps solve the problem.

Let me share my set up that has worked really well for AF1 and is working even better for AF2. Here it goes:
1. I chose a simple outline program with check box function rather than a “to do” or GTD program. (Since I use iPhone, mine is CarbonFin Outliner, but any other outline program with check box should work.)
2. I created just two parent headings: Action and Archive.
3. Under Action, I simply enter the list of “actionable” items, just like in a notebook, although I do limit the number of items to 32 (an arbitrary number simulating the lines of a typical notebook). After Item #32, I simply create another Action (1. Action, 2. Action, etc.) heading. -- Under AF1, this created the closed-list effect. Under AF2, I kept this practice as it makes archiving easier.
4. After this setup, all I have to do is to do AF2: read through the list backwards, read again to do what stands out, dismiss, etc. I check off the completed items. For dismissing, I change the unchecked check box into a plain bullet. In this way, the “Hide completed items” function only shows either the non-completed or the dismissed items.
5. In the morning, I undo the “Hide completed items” to identify the dismissal candidate block and mark the last item of the block with something very visible. (I use the Emoji up arrow).
6. After the 32 items in one heading are either completed or dismissed, I change the heading from “Action” to a completed date (e.g., 6/29/09) and move it under “Archive.”
The beauty of this is that it is basically an exact duplicate of the notebook method with several advantages:
- It keeps the list cleaner: I can use the “Hide completed items” function to see just the “actionable” (and dismissed) items.
- Reentry is a breeze: just duplicate the item, check off the original, and move the duplicated one to the bottom.
- Reviewing of the dismissed items is also a breeze. Under the Archive heading, the “Hide completed items” function allows me to see just the dismissed items. My software also automatically records the entry and the completion date of an item for future reference.

Now I can check off "Post AF forum." ;)
