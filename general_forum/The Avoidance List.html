Hi all

I may be a bit unique (but I don't think so) but I know that much of my resistance is genetically based. Boredom is actually painful. It's like the mental equivalent of sticking my hand in fire. What affects it most is my prediction based on experience. If I remember something to be boring, my initial response is to recoil. My powers of prediction are flawed because of this. If I feel resistance to what I think is boredom, then I'll just start. I plan the size of the start (first task of job or first step of the first task of the job) according to the anxiety level I experience when I think about it. Then I'll alternate with a lower anxiety task. This acts as a respite. The beauty part is that my initial evaluation isn't always accurate about the entire job. I just keep starting until one or two things happen: Either I get engaged in the work or the starts add up to completion for the day. For jobs that I'm afraid to start because I don't know what I'm going to do (creativity) or what the job entails (a learning curve or problem solving), I feel the same resistance. For this type of challenge, I resolve in my mind that I can't predict how long it will take and just see where it takes me.


Reading this may seem like it's not very difficult. It's been like this since grade school. The best that I can do is make sure that I've chosen my MITs and worthy alternate tasks well.




If the job requires physical or cognitive stamina that I don't have, I'll only start it early despite the pain if I really need the extra time (just in case the pain gets worse later. I can't count on it abating. I just have to do the best I can with generous breaks with easier work. This requires even better judgement choosing my work. I just can't count on my body or brain getting better in enough time to meet deadline or routine rotations. If the routine rotations can wait without much fallout, I'll stop after my MITs are done.


I envy people who can manipulate their resistance. The best that I can do is cope with it as effectively as possible. That's why I always choose MITs. In case I peter out for whatever reason, the most important work is current no matter what. (excepting things truly outside my control. I had surgery on Friday so I did as much as possible beforehand so I don't get too far behind.) We can't control everything but with effort we can do better than we give ourselves credit for.


Bottom line: My resistance is genetic and based on actual damage. I've gotten good at getting my work done despite, but it's never gotten any easier. The bright side is the gratitude I feel when I have a job that has little or no resistance. It feels like an oasis in the desert.


I hope for your sake that your resistance isn't permanent. Chris's wasn't permanent. I wish the same for all of you. I hope you are the lucky people who fine a magic bullet or a process that ease the burden. OTOH, if you can't help it, don't be so hard on yourself. I've been pushing through resistance for many, many decades. It doesn't have to interfere with your success.

My advice: If you have to labor through resistance, make sure you're doing the right work.

The longer the list the more probable it’s that there are hanging some high resistance tasks that should have been done a long time ago. In my case the long list is the reason for using a specific list to highlight those tasks.

When I plan the next week I make a sublist ”Don’t want to see next week” and pick those items from the long list which I don’t want for various reasons to see in the list next week. Usually they are tasks I resist. I write the list to the top left corner of a my weekly plan.

When I take the plan on Monday morning something curious happens. My mind seems to think that if I dont’ want to see those tasks next week so I don’t want to see them tomorrow either. So I begin to do those tasks with some kind of acceptance that it’s time to do them now. And there is a pattern how they get done.

There are no bigger problems with the easiest ones. The dentist is called.

The more difficult ones may require working with somebody else or bribing myself with a reward ’Do first Unpleasant and then Pleasant’

The tasks requiring more time to do benefit from SMEMA approach ’Unpleasant ’ / ’Else 1’ / ’Unpleasant (2)’ // ’Else 2’ / Unpleasant (3)’ // ’Else 3’ / ’Unpleasant (4’) // etc. In SMEMA approach I especially like that after the first 2 tasks are done, it’s time to choose what ’Else’ to do between the next Unpleasants. What I choose to do depends on my mood and energy level.

Else 1,2,3,… may be another task, a cup of tea, a meeting, walking around 5 minutes, speaking with a colleague, playing a computer game or whatever keeps me going so that Unpleasant task gets done.

(By the way, the mystery number in the parenthesis in the task chain is the number how many times I have touched the task. I use it in all tasks which I’m going to continue so that they don’t get lost in a long list.)

When I tested this approach the most difficult thing was to find a right name for the list. "Dont't want to see next week" is pretty stupid but it triggers an automatic reaction in my head: Just get rid of those. So if you test this approach try it with different list names.

Mark: "Having written the above, I must see if I can write a system which will achieve it in the best possible way. I need a method which will isolate the high-resistance tasks, hit them hard but also allow easier tasks to get done."

How about something like this? A new modification called Intervention.

1. Capture all your tasks in the usual way and work them as per your favourite system (AF, FV, AF4R, etc)

2. Notice when certain tasks are hanging around longer than might feel comfortable. These would be tasks that are likely to be untouched and hence dismissed if left for much longer. Once such tasks are identified, mark them with an "H" for High resistance.

3. Intervention! This takes over from the system's normal workflow temporarily. Take one of the H tasks and a number of tasks which stand out and are straightforward and circle them all.

4. You now start working on the H task and are allowed to break it up with the smaller tasks. But you cannot continue with the list until you have done the H task. Use as many of the smaller tasks as needed but treat the H tasks and the smaller tasks as a temporary closed list.

5. Once the H task is done, the remaining small tasks return to the system and normal rules processing resumes for the system.

This means you get to use your favourite system in normal use, but high-resistance tasks are periodically isolated and hammered away at, with other tasks being used to break them up so they get done also.

michael:

"...it's not the fear of the first step of the resisting list that holds me back, it's my fear of what the 10th step MIGHT be."


Very important distinction.

For a lot of people, many of these thoughts lie at the root of their resistance:

1. If I start this, it's going to take forever to finish.

2. Once I start this and get rolling, I may feel a compulsion to finish it. This will ensure everything else falls through the cracks.

3. If I start this, new things will continue rolling in.

4. I'll be constantly interrupted and I don't want to appear rude or lose potential opportunities by walling myself off. Also, I'm easily distracted.

5. If I do this I'll have even more tasks coming in because I've moved the project forward. I want a closed project, not a continually expanding one! I don't want more tasks and responsibilities!

6. If I do this, I know I'll have to feel super uncomfortable and may be rejected in a really unpleasant way which I'll obsess about for days, weeks, or years! I'll replay the conversation over and over again on my internal record player until it melts down into a pool of black magma.

7. This task is UTTERLY BORING and holds ZERO interest for me. I REFUSE to let me feel anything but mild anxiety about it. FEAR this task! Or ignore it! Barely touch it! It will make me feel uncomfortable anyhow. Do something less important. Something easy. That you're passionate about! Spin your wheels! (Not your conscious mind doing the talking, by the way).

8. What if, in the end, this task or project was a complete waste of time? How will I know NOW? BEFORE I begin? I won't start it until I know FOR SURE that the upside is huge and—GUARANTEED—and the downside is mild to non-existent and doing the task produces no feelings of being really uncomfortable.

I don't think anyone has ever claimed that it's the fear of the first step that holds one back. In fact doing the first step is the way to get moving on the project because the first step is easy if taken in isolation. Beginning is half the secret to time management. One could sum up the way to get any project done as "Keep beginning". Once you've begun inertia turns into momentum.

This is why one of the most useful phrases is:

"I'm not really going to work on Project X today, but I'll just get the file out..."

Very useful list. I re-organized a live website for my needlecraft guild yesterday, after putting it off for ages.

Number 2 (eveything else will fall through the cracks) happens a lot for me. Maybe "Why am I resisting?" is one of those spiraling questions. Once you clear up one answer (If I don't do it now when I'm on schedule with most things, I'll have to do it at the last minute, which will likely be when I'm behind on many things, and things will fall through the cracks.) another answer jumps up to be dealt with.

Like Learning, I love those flow times when I'm in them. I definitely go too deep for too long with reading and some creative projects. Less often with actual work.

This one surprised me because I thought I was good at dividing deep-focus projects into chunks with defined break points, but the fear still exists.

This one also works in reverse. If I'm afraid of something slipping through the cracks, I might work on it.

Number 4 (fear of locking self off). Oh, yes, but it's silly. I'm alone in the house most of the day! The same chunking that helps with number 2 helps here -- at least in theory.

Number 5 (moving the project forward creates more tasks) is also familiar. When I get into a project, the list of tasks gets longer -- not because the project is any bigger, but because I've identified individual tasks at increased resolution. That expansion makes it feel like an open list, but it's still a single project. I rarely feel Number 1, probably because I internalized the concept of closed and open-ended goals very early. (Closed = Done. Yay! E.g., wrote exam. Open = Always a work in progres, often maintenance, often with a hard bit when you get going, e.g., exercise program.)

Number 7 (boring) relates to an insight I saw an hour ago. More on that in another thread. So does Number 8 (what if I find it was a waste of time), and Number ?? (it's the 10th step that I fear).

Number 9 (not knowing the investment required) is also a big one. I fool myself into thinking I know, or will know before making the next decision, but when looking at my own things I'm as susceptible to the "sunk cost" fallacy as anyone.

Number 10 (unknown investment required) should stop me sooner than it does sometimes, and really holds me back for others. (That 10,000 hours to mastery theory. Eeps!)

This list is a lot longer and more insightful than I would have come up with. I generally stop with "Surely there's something more important / rewarding" and "all those reasons are silly or easy enough to counter".
