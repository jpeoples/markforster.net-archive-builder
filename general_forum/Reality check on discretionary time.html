There's a post on stats started by Lynn, which gave rise to a few questions for me.

If someone has a home notebook with 25 active pages and 30 tasks per page, and 50% of these tasks are complete, there's a potential 375 tasks remaining to do. If these tasks take an average of 20 minutes to do - which is probably a fairly conservative estimate, there are 7500 minutes or 125 hours of potential tasks in the book at this time. That's about 4 hours a day for a month - assuming nothing else was added and no time off of the book was taken.

Where am I going with this? If you didn't want the tasks to linger in the book for over a month, you've got to have either less tasks/things you've committed to or more discretionary time. If you 've got a regular 50+ hour/week job including commute time - it just isn't possible to commit to a whole lot.

I was just doing the math to figure out how to reinforce to myself that dumping tasks into the book is very bad. Does the logic seem sound to you all?

I agree with Ludlow. I like being able to dump everything in my notebook and letting AF handle them. There used to be a lot of things I knew I wanted to do or felt I needed to do. And I was always beating myself for not getting around to most of them. Putting them in my AF notebook has put them in a new perspective. I've realized I don't want or need to do some of them as much as I thought. I've realized that, realistically, I won't be able to get all of those things done, and that's okay because I'm not Superwoman, and there's only so much I can do. So there's no more beating myself up over every little thing I'm not doing.

That's the thing. Even if you don't write something down, it'll probably be on your mind somewhere, nagging at you. By writing it down, you get it out of your head and into a place where you can evaluate it, and possibly dismiss it. But it'll still be there for you to reconsider later. (Which is why I like the idea of using physical notebooks, and storing them to look over later.)

Also, I think if you use AF with the attitude that you are not going to dismiss anything, or that dismissing is somehow bad, you're in trouble. I look at dismissed items as my Someday/Maybe list, only one that was created organically rather than me trying to evaluate things before. Dismissed items are things I might get to at some point (because dismissed doesn't mean deleted), but my mind is telling me it's okay to let them go for now.

Jacqueline,

You really should have been crossing off the garage door thing several times, going by what you said. As soon as you make a call you have "worked on it" and it NEEDS to get crossed off, and rewritten at the end ... possibly reworded.

By "organically" I think Mark just means that you should not "plan" what you put onto the list, but rather just add things as they come up. Look, I made the same mistake you did, I did a dump onto the list and that has resulted in some very tough things to handle. Some of them I just took off again so that I could get the list working as it should. Now I realize that if I just do "something" on the tough things I can cross them off and put them back on at the bottom. If nothing else, that mixes them up ... IOW, when I was doing my "dump", things tended to cluster. I need to work on the attic and there are about a dozen things to do there (add ceiling lights for the top floor, ceiling fans, insulation, wire mesh to keep the critters out, etc.). So when that occured to me, I about filled a page with todos for that one thing alone. Bad idea. Very depressing. I reworked the list to just have:

"Work on attic (April)"

I put the approximate date I want to start work based on the weather. So now I ignore it because of the date but I'm doing some planning every time I see the item. Is there something on sale I need at the hardware store? Are there any good ideas in any of the home magazines? When will my wife not be out of town on April weekends? (I'm not dumb enough to go up there and mess with electricity with no backup in case I fry myself ;-) The real upside is that I don't have to look at line after line of depressingly hard work ahead of me.

Now, the actual WORK is not lessened, just the psychological impact of the list. The work on the attic is going to be hard long back breaking hours in a cramped space. I expect that it will take several partial weekends ... and that is if I plan it right and get my supplies ahead of time. So I get the disappointment you experience with the garage door. Nothing fixes that except being able to handle it as best you can and not letting the list itself get in the way.
