I'd think the first question would be: What system are you using? Paper? Digital? SF? Another?

This might be too simplistic for your purpose, but it's what came to mind. I use SF on paper. I recently had to complete 4 hours worth of work (log 4 hours worth of progress) on a particular project over two days time. When I realized this, I put it into C2. I worked on it for 40 min and when I crossed it out and wrote it on the next page in C2 I wrote '40 min'. When I arrived at the item in C2 of the following page I worked on it for 20 min and did the same thing (cross out, write time, re-enter on next page C2). At the end of the first day, I counted up my time and I had completed 3 hours. I didn't have an entry in SF to add the time up but in your case, maybe you could/would. The next day, I completed the final hours work in two 30 min sessions in the same way. It was GREAT. And I picked off some easy things from C1 on each page to complete along the way to boot.

I have just the solution! I've been using this for years: http://davidseah.com/node/the-emergent-task-timer/

I use the wide version which holds up to 12 hours in 15 minute segments. Download the PDF and print out like 50 copies. Put them in a file folder which is within easy reach. First thing in the morning grab a fresh copy, put the day and date on it, and number the hours starting with the current hour.

On the left side I put the relevant budget lines as they come up (Overhead, Customer A, Customer B, etc.). Then fill in the bubbles as you go. I also tend to draw brackets around scheduled meetings.

On the bottom I write the detail (sideways) of precisely what I was doing i.e. what goes on my status report at the end of the week.

At the end of the day I count up the little bubbles on each row and that's what goes in my timesheet (2.25 for cust A, 2.0 overhead, etc.).

I also just mark down an X instead of a solid bubble for unproductive time just so every time is accounted for somehow.
