As promised in the recent thread "What system do you use right now?", here is my current system and favourite AF tweak. With apologies to Mark and the Nintendo company, I named it Autofocus Advance.

Rules

Each AF page is divided into two areas (such as by a dividing line about half way down the page, using two columns, or using facing pages.) The first is the new task area, the second is the reentered task area.

Rules for cycling through the pages, task selection, and dismissal are as in Autofocus 1. The two areas are treated as if they formed a single, contiguous page. Free space in the reentered task area doesn't keep from finishing or dismissing pages.

New tasks are entered in the new task area on the last page of the list. A new page is started when the last page's new task area is full.

Reentered tasks are entered in the reentered task area of the NEXT page, i.e., the page that you'll work on right after you leave the current one. If that area is full, put the task wherever you like, except that you never write on the current page unless it is also the last page. If you can't find a space, add it as a new task instead.

Experience

The flow of Autofocus Advance is clearly different from Autofocus 1. It is easier to cycle through the list. Your ongoing projects can advance through the list along with you. They don't cluster at the end. Instead of keeping you attached to the end of the list, they help you advance to the next page.

At the same time key properties of Autofocus 1 are preserved. There still is the intuitive selection process focusing on a single page at a time. There is now an open area on most pages, yet pages (other than the last one) still behave as closed lists while being actively worked on.

Future Work

The relative sizes of the two areas may be able to fine-tune properties of the system. So far I've always used an equal 50/50 split.

Anticipated FAQ

Q: Isn't this more like SuperFocus than Autofocus?

A: It takes a bit of geekery to see why, but it is actually important in Autofocus Advance that you don't reenter a task back on the current page. The defining twist of SuperFocus is that you can do just that! So the two systems are mutually exclusive twists on the common Autfocus theme.

Q: May I enter a really urgent task on the current page?

A: A rule tweak that would occasionally allow urgent new tasks (not reentered tasks) onto the current page is possible--in theory--without breaking the system. So far I haven't used such a tweak.

I eliminated the two areas. The resulting ruleset is even simpler and items are always contiguous on a page. The balance between new tasks and reentered tasks on a page is slightly shifted. I compensated by including the urgent task tweak into the ruleset. If you feel that the balance is off (whether using the original ruleset or this one), you can experiment by slightly shifting the barrier position on new pages.

The Rules

Arrange for each page to have a visible barrier about halfway into the page. You could draw a line, or use the natural jump between two columns or facing pages.

Rules for cycling through the pages, task selection, and dismissal are as in AF1. Free space behind the barrier doesn't keep from finishing or dismissing pages.

New tasks are normally entered on the youngest page of the list. If the new entry would go behind the barrier, a new page is started instead. Exceptionally urgent tasks may be entered on the current page.

Reentered tasks are entered on the NEXT page, i.e., the page that you'll work on right after you leave the current one. If that page is full, put the task on any other page you like, except for the current page. If you can't find such a place, add it as a non-urgent new task.

2011-02-7 12:34
I don't use my AF system as either a clock, timer or calendar. I'd find it unreliable as a timer or clock and clumsy as a calendar.

I feed the system with new tasks sourcing from the calendar and from weekly/monthly checklists. That covers recurring tasks that run weekly or more infrequently. What few daily recurring tasks I have, I run off a small morning checklist. They only go into the system if I don't do them right away, which isn't often.

Over time I've found that wherever I keep track of the current day's scheduled timeline (maybe a calendar page, maybe a scrap of paper) is a good place to keep track of urgent same-day tasks. That's because urgency usually implies deadlines and time commitments.

Items on my AF list therefore tend have two properties related to time: They are ready to be worked on now but don't have a deadline today. It's not an iron rule, but is usually true.

Again, all of this is because --personally-- I don't think of the Autofocus systems as timing devices.

That leaves some kinds recurring tasks that may alternatively be viewed as neverending tasks/projects, such as answering your e-mail. Yes, I treat these like other re-entered tasks, i.e., put them on the next page.

2011-02-8 9:12
Mark, very interesting new system!

I spent some time yesterday digesting it. I now regret not having used the alternative formulation ("barrier" instead of "areas") already when I first posted Autofocus Advance.

Apart from the fact that I just like the second way better, I suspect the first wording may have also made you think of Autofocus Advance as more similar to SFv3 than it may turn out to be.

AF Advance is original Autofocus with a ruleset tweak designed to reduce resistance within the system. It is not a move away or beyond autofocus.

SFv3 is more than a tweak of AF1; it is essentially a sandwich of two parallel systems. You run a (surprisingly un-tweaked) AF 1 in the first column of each page. You use space in the second columns to run a second and much simpler system --let's call it Column 2 System, C2S. You run the systems in lockstep. AF1 feeds tasks into C2S. C2S gates progress in AF1. You could actually pull C2S out of your notebook, put it on a whiteboard, and get a mostly equivalent composite system.

The important difference regarding those similar looking rules is that in AF Advance, they tweak AF1. In SFv3, these rules do not live in the AF1 part of the sandwich, but are part of the way the C2S operates in the second columns. This makes me suspect the similarity will turn out to be somewhat smaller than the similarity in wording lead us to expect at first.
