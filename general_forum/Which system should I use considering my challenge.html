I'm looking for a system to assist me with my challenge. So far I have not been able to stick to either DIT or AF for any length of time. Perhaps there isn't a "system" answer to my challenge, but at least I'll post a question to this forum. This is my challenge:

I have a great ability to focus. I will feel compelled to get ONE thing done until I'm finished. This is usually some type of "Current Initiative." The problem is everything else on my list will get held up. Sometimes for more than a year. Usually I'll use the AF list for a while until some "Current Initiative" pops up. So I tend to do work on each specific task in bursts and spurts. I can keep honing my "current big project" until I feel it's just right. The AF list fades into the background, and the other things are left sort of half done, as they've lost their "charm," if they had any in the first place. Here's one example:

I wrote a masters thesis this spring. Just about everything else got put on the back burner. My family for one. The house. The garden. The car. All the things I had listed in my Autofocus list are still on there. I simply parked everything until I got the thesis done. It got done, and it got done really well, btw.

The challenge is, life doesn't stop. Other family members get cross, or at least frustrated. My way of prioritizing is to do what compels, and most of the mundande tasks are "parked." Of course I still pay the bills, because I have to, but most stuff gets parked. I tell myself I'll get to them "later." However when "later" arrives I'll stick with the AF list for a while, but usually some new fresh and exciting project is usually already in the pipeline.

In a sense it's a way of procrastrinating.

So, with this profile in mind, would there be any thoughts on what kind of system could assist me in getting progress on the unmotivating tasks as well?

Occasionally I do one thing to the detriment of much else, but not for long weeks. I think your focus ability is a great thing, provided you balance it a little. Assuming your ONE thing has real value and is worth lots of time daily. Ideas:
1) Set a time limit per day.

2) Set a time limit within your day. Pomodoro technique for up to 2 hours, Then 1/2 hour must do AF list.
3) For most people, the advice is to first of each day work on the big project. Maybe you need to first work on something else. Your reward for staying current is you get to do the fun project.

It sounds like perfectionism and obsession should also be considered.

Try Covey's books. One of them has you identify your roles (usually 5 to 12) and identify one goal for each role each week. Consciously thinking about each role each week can make a big difference.

Sometimes doing something that's not part of the current obsession is uncomfortable. The more you do it, the easier it gets. Baby steps.

Unless your goals cover all aspects of your life, that can't be a complete basis for time management. Goals do help you set aside the irrelevant, but it sounds like they go to far in your case.

I went through an exercise recently where I tagged every activity, every item in AF, with a category. I grouped and generalized and analyzed and thought deeply about all these. @ Gadgety

You seem to be very much like me. This was one of the things that made me think that I didn't need a Time managing System since I don't procrastinate on the big things I have to or want to do. Here are a few ideas and concepts that helped me.

Time Management is the art of Efficiency. It is how to be able to be productive with every ounce of time that you have. It is looking at things at a Micro level. It is therefore good for those that lack or those that work best with that kind of Focus. It's very close to tactics which is defined as a level of "Means" to get the job done.

Setting Goals is the art of Effectiveness. Goals are not "Means" but "Ends". They cover what is most important to you and they should be considered with a different kind of mindset and tools. It is looking at things on a Macro level.

Both of these are not necessarily self exclusive but it is difficult to reconcile both ways of thinking in one system or even one taught because of their different nature.

You are an Effective person and that is what drives you. You are inspired by creating change more then in maintaining what is already there. There is not shame in that.

From your question, I gather that you most need Balance and Flow...
Balance in your maintenance of your life (mostly efficiency things) and your Projects (mostly effective alterations to your world). And Flow on when to do one and when the other. So here comes a new concept:
Life Management or what some call Life Style Design.

In a nutshel, Life Management is the Art of Balance and Flow. It recognizes the power of Effectiveness and the necessity of Efficiency. It emphasizes doing what is the most important in the most efficient way. It is looking at things on a Macro level to then dive in the Micro level. Life Management is an "End" and a "Means". It is a kind of "It's not the destination that's important, it's the journey". It's NOT "The End justifies the Means" but rather "The Means are the End".

In my experience, Balance is best handled through budgeting (any kind will do but I budget time through Pomodoros). Flow is best handled with actually visible objects that you can throw in the stream of time.

I always was a Single Tasker. I cannot leave a thing unfinished and I will often not start a project if I don't have time to finish it in the same go or else it will stay on my mind. Using time management systems has wreck havoc on my nerves because they encourage the mindset that you can eave something open because it's on a list and you won't forget. It just doesn't work for me. So limiting my work in progress at any given time has always been at the core of my thinking even though I couldn't define it.

It was when I discovered about the Personal Kanban (and Kanban) in general that the concept of WIP (work in progress) was made clear to me. And now I apply it everywhere and it works like a charm!

You should definitely look at your projects and your maintenance as 2 different aspects of your life needing different "levels of attention" and "kinds of engagement". Mind map a tree of those with the Maintenance as your Roots and your Branches as Projects and your Goals as your Leaves or Fruits. You'll see why and where maintenance is vital to great Projects :)
