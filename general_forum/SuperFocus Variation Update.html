I'm still working on finding rules for SuperFocus which will get the balance between C1 and C2 exactly right.

My latest attempt is so far working extremely well:

----------------------------------------------

Current rules for SF (see SuperFocus Rules link in top menu), except:

Both urgent and unfinished tasks are entered on the page *after* the one you are currently working on.

When no further tasks stand out on a page, any remaining tasks in C2 are transferred to the next page.

There is only one occasion on which tasks are dismissed, which is when no tasks at all are done on visiting a page - in which case C1 tasks are dismissed and C2 tasks are transferred to the next page as above.

----------------------------------

Reply by Mark Forster

2011-05-2 0:20
My main reasons for adopting these rules are:

1) Entering all C2 tasks on the next page avoids getting stuck on one page while a succession of urgent tasks are added.

2) The compulsory finishing of C2 tasks on a page tended to cause resistance, so C2 tasks may now be transferred to the next page.

3) The two above changes reduce the need for two different methods of dismissal, so this has been simplified so it is similar to the AF1 rule for dismissal.

Reply by Alan Baljeu

2011-05-2 14:25
To make this point explicit:
When a C2 is full, we no longer dismiss that page just because it's full. Wrapping around the list again, we attempt to enter things in the next C2, but because it's full we write on the following page instead. Shortly after we turn to the full page. C2 is both full and crossed out. Therefore we must work one or more C1 tasks or dismiss the page. It's likely we'll dismiss the page soon because (a) its tasks we've ignored for a while, and (b) we really want to get to the next page to get back on those C2's. This is a good thing.

As a matter of habit, we don't want to be forwarding C2 tasks forever. Forwarding a C2 task wouldn't count for the "when no tasks at all are done on visiting a page" rule, so you must do at least one C1 or C2 task each turn of the page.

Be flexible, and be focused on your goal: finishing the important tasks.

2011-05-4 6:28
For me, the best solution (or at least the one that has stuck) is:

- Standard SF rules, except the following.
- Enter urgent items in C1 on last page unless that makes me anxious that I won't get to it fast enough, in which case enter them on C2 of next page unless THAT makes me anxious, in which case enter them on C2 of current page.
- Clear C2 before starting C1. This keeps a strong focus on completing the C2 items.
- I always retain the right to demote any C2 item back to C1 - re-enter in C1 of last page - when it just doesn't make sense to keep an item in C2 any more. Maybe it will get re-promoted sometime.
- I also always retain the right to copy any C2 item forward to the next page if I want to keep giving it high attention but just can't take action on it right now for some reason.
- I try to apply the same rigor to the last two items that I would to re-instating a dismissed item -- i.e., there must be a very good reason why I am exercising my right to demote or to copy forward.

This gives me total control over C2 and allows me to use it just like I use the gear shift in my truck. I can shift into high gear or shift back down to low gear. Or I can park in the garage for a while. :-)

This seems to work as long as I always make sure I "have a good reason" for failing to take the required action on a C2 item.

This flexible approach has worked great for me for over a month now. But if Mark hits on a rule-based approach that he really feels satisfied with, I'd be happy to give it a try.

2011-05-4 20:00
Jeff N - The current rules already have built-in dynamics that are very much like slight rule modifications that automatically adjust to your situation.

For example, simply cycling through the list produces this effect.

The first time I start acting on a page, there are usually several items that are quick and easy to finish off. Others are easy to start but need more work before completion. And then there are others that just don't want to get started yet.

On the second pass, the dynamic has changed: one or two of those items has now "ripened" and can be easily plucked. Maybe one or two others have lost their relevance altogether and can be deleted. The rest remain for the future.

With each pass, the dynamics change again, as the number of items on the page shrinks. It is faster to process the page, and it becomes more clear which items really matter, which ones don't, and which ones do matter but are creating resistance for some reason.

Finally the page is deleted or dismissed. Dismissal introduces a whole new dynamic that triggers deeper reflection about one's commitments and priorities.

All these different dynamics come into play as you work the list, changing from page to page, from cycle to cycle.

I do admit, every now and then I just want to throw the whole list out the window, but that's generally because I'm getting stir-crazy and just need to get away from my computer or my desk. Or maybe because some days I don't have any discretionary time and I feel frustrated about my slow speed of cycling. I don't think it's the list or the method itself. Once I take a break, go outside and water the trees, or play tag with my toddler, or walk down for a coffee break with my colleagues, I'm always happy to get back to my list. It provides variety as well as stability. And dismissal helps ensure the overall workload is reasonable and can really fit within the discretionary time available -- which varies from week to week.

Long way of saying: all Mark's systems from AF onward already provide dynamic adjustment of how one's work actually get done, depending on your situation. I think Mark is just trying to find a better way to make this happen so that C2 adjusts itself to your situation better than it does with the current SF rules.

I've been thinking of C2 as "shifting to high gear". The analogy works pretty well.

When I'm driving my truck, it's usually very clear what gear I should be driving in. If I'm on the highway, I need to be in high gear, and I can get somewhere quickly (= drive a task to completion, or take quick action on an urgent task).

But if there's an accident on the road, or some other obstacle, I may need to switch to low gear for awhile (= demote the task to C1 because something else is taking priority right now).

I can get off the highway, go down a side road, do some errands, etc. Lower gear. (= ordinary C1 tasks).

I do follow rules when I'm driving, but circumstances sometimes demand that I apply the rules differently or set them aside temporarily. When there's an emergency situation that demands special attention and unusual action, following the ordinary rules can be deadly.

In the same way, I generally follow the C2 rules pretty strictly as written in the original rules, but if I really need to demote a task back to C2, then I do it. And if I want to move a bit faster, then I clear C2 before looking at C1.

neumatist, I agree with all that, and I think you might find Seraphim's "anxiety" criterion has a lot of adaptive power to address it: When reentering a task, first consider C1. If you feel too anxious about reaching it again promptly in C1, then consider C2 of next page. If that too makes you worry, then write it in C2 of this page.

When I get confused about the variation(s) I am trying, I often revert to Seraphim's method, often unintentionally and only noticing it after the pattern has built up for a while. In fact, this morning I found myself doing it, as I had stayed up way too late, slept in, and then rushed through the morning. Being in a hurry kills the satisfaction of "all C2's on next page," so while I mostly tried to keep to this next-page-C2 variation, I wrote my especially-impending items on the current page. Why? Because they made me nervous.

One could extend this to cover the rule(s) about working C2 vs. C1. In other words, if you are worried about the list facing you in C2, then by all means, follow the path of least anxiety, and knock out those C2's before turning to C1. But if C2 is nice and relaxed, then take a stroll through C1 for a bit. Either way, I think one needs to clear C2, even if it means carrying forward tasks that are un-actioned or out of context. But even then, who's to say you can't "park" a C2 task and move on if you can feel it losing its relevance but are not ready to think about it right now?

Seems to me, these "anxiety" criteria provide much situational flexibility and are very much in keeping with Mark Forster's intuitive design principles. They also provide a superset encompassing most (if not all) of the SF variations we've been trying here.

Perhaps we merely need modify canonical SFv3's "common sense rule" to read "anxiety rule." How far does that take us toward neumatist's Holy Grail?
