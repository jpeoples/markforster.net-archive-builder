I just joined the AutoFocus beta a couple of days ago, and it makes a good amount of sense to me overall. One point that seemed problematic (for me, at least) was the requirement for "separate notebooks for separate places, like Home and Workplace", however...

My job is such that I'm traveling from half to two-thirds of the time, and when I'm not, I can--more often than not--do most of my work from home, convenient, since it's a 37-mile drive to my actual office.

I also don't tend to make a very strong distinction between "work stuff" and "home stuff". I'll work for a few hours during the day, go out and run some personal errands, come back,, watch a movie, do a few more hours work in the evening, etc. I tend, overall, to intersperse "Workplace" activities from "Home" activities; moreover, what puts me in the "Workplace" as opposed to "Home" is simply what I'm working _on_ at the moment.

Given all of that, it seems more sensible for me to maintain a single Autofocus notebook. What do you think?

Lefty,

I'm in much the same situation, but I try (not always successfully!) to keep work and personal items separate in order to focus on the one or the other at any given time. i started using AF in manual mode, and used the front of the notebook for Office/Work, turned the book upside down and started Personal from the other end. It separates the tasks physically, but enables one notebook.

I'm now using ListPro for AF and it's working well for me with separate files. However, I transfer ERRANDS and CHORES entered in either the Office/Work or the Personal list and group them together so I can batch them and do a mixture when I go out.

The best advice I can give to a newcomer to AF is to try it as per Mark F recommends, see how it works, then tweak it until you have a Lefty-friendly model. With AF I have found my productivity and energy levels rising and my procrastination propensity falling, but not eliminated. What is important is what works for YOU, not for Roger J, Christine B or even Mark F. But reread Mark's instructions every now and then, and the comments from him and the other testers, some real gems to be found.

Echoing what Roger says, I'd recommend trying it both ways for a week and see what works best. I found the mixed list works better when you can use your discretion to choose when to work and when to do personal stuff. The separated lists work better when there are distinct blocks of time dedicated to work, and distinct blocks for personal.

To try it both ways, here is what I did. I was already using a mixed list for a few weeks. It was probably 60% personal, 40% work. So, I kept it as my personal book, and started a separate book for work. I immediately jotted down the first work items that popped into my head. I also added "transfer work items from personal AF book". And then away I went. I keep both books handy at all times, so I can write down whatever pops into my head, into the appropriate book. But I actually work through only one book at a time.

I have experienced what Seraphim describes.

I have enjoyed much success using a separate AF notebook for a specific writing project that gets worked on in 4 hour blocks of time each day. Entries started off slowly and I did not think it was going to help me write; I feared it would become just another thing to fiddle with instead of actually writing. Contrary to my initial fears, I have found my separate AF project notebook to be a trusted writing companion. It willingly receives and collects random tasks and new ideas and "think abouts". I don't use it to collect creative ideas as these go into another AF type open list that may be a paragraph long or just a sentence about a character note I don't want to forget. Even on this longer list, I borrow the AF technique of reviewing these creative notes and trolling for a stand out item for the particular bit I am working on in the manuscript. I just love the freedom I have gained knowing that everything is down on paper. I don't have to worry about where the juicy ideas and bits have been stored. What's best, I trust that the reviewing and stand out process is keeping creative idea I could possibly need floating on the surface available to be picked up and used by both my subconscious and conscious mind.

With the separate AF project notebook I get the benefits of AF's simple collection process and I use the "stand out" to jump start my long writing sessions. If I am feeling a bit rebellious, I get to pick whatever item on the list I want to work on. Being able to choose is like candy for my inner rebel.

I have one notebook but 3 sections.
At the front I enter my home items. From the back and in reverse order I enter my work related items. In the center I enter the errands.
Having 3 sections is very helpful when I need to review quickly. For example say I go back home after work, and I remember I had something to do maybe so I open the notebook in the middle and scan rapidly my errands list. There is no point in having my errands on my home or work list because they always be out of context and will be dismissed in the end.

O just pleat the corner of the start page of my errands list so I can access it easily.

My notebook is 8.5x13cm and fits in every pocket. And there is a slot for slim mechanical pencil (I could use a spiral notebook either).

Since I can't write 25 items on a single page, I consider a double page as a single AF page. I don't know why mark insists on 25 lines per page, since a smaller notebook is easier to carry, so 13x2 lines feels like the same, and more compact.

ISD,

Mark does not "insist" on anything. He finds that for him, 30 lines or so works best. We have, from time to time, speculated on the effect of the number of lines per page on how well the system works. I have a pocket 3x5 inch book which has 15 lines. I've tried writing two items on each line (near the top and near the bottom of the line) and it is a bit hard to read, but workable. I'm not sure which Iike best yet.

One effect of the number of lines is that it controls how many times you see a task that you are resistant to doing. You see it at most 15 times (in a 15 line page) before you MUST do it or dismiss it ... or 30 times if your book has 30 lines per page. Experiment and see what works best for you.
