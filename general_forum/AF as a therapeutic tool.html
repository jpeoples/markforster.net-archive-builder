Two questions one must remember before responding to a critic of psychoanalytic/psychodynamic therapy:

1) Are you a therapist?
2) If yes, have you attended a psychoanalytic training program and/or have experienced psychodynamic/analytic therapy yourself?

Here's an interesting tidbit... as a 5th year psychiatry resident, my wife was presented with a paper (authored by a psychologist) debunking much of the foundational research supporting CBT. The article was set to publish in 2010. Moreover, for the past 2-3 years, the American Journal of Psychiatry has printed study after study demonstrating the efficacy of psychodynamic psychotherapies, particularly for people struggling with personality and relational issues. Er, that's most of us.

Having spent too many years working as a therapist in addiction/mental health institutions, I can tell you that many of our "quick-fix" approaches to suffering have little to do with quality care, and everything to do with appeasing accountants.

I'll leave you with a quote from Dr. Irving Yalom - http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irvin_D._Yalom - taken from his book "The Gift of Therapy", chapter 76 "CBT Is Not What It's Cracked up to Be"

"I can't resist raising one more mischievous point. I have a strong hunch (substantiated only anecdotally) that EVT (empirically-validated therapy) practitioners requiring personal psychotherapeutic help do not seek brief therapy, but instead turn to highly trained, experienced dynamic, manual-less therapists."

Side note: Most of my academic/professional training is in CBT, DBT and other forms of brief-oriented psychotherapy.
