He is basically just restating the common observation of shame versus guilt cultures.

Guilt is internalized sense of failure and disappointment. Shame failure and disappointment as judged by those around you. To make a gross observation.

Just in the NT you can see the beginning of the move from shame to guilt. Jesus often speaking to developing a sense of guilt rather than shame. Sinning in our heart the same as committing the sin. What comes out of the mouth greater than what goes into it, those who have sinned without the community knowing about it being as wrong as the sinner who sins shameless in the community (first stone and all).

Kant wrote some interesting stuff on the gradually shift in the Western World from shame to guilt and this shift's relationship to Europeanizing of Christianity. Specifically a guilt without knowing exactly what transgression one particularly committed. Original guilt displacing original sin.

Most Mediterranean cultures and members of their diasporas still operate primarily in a shame based manner. Hence the stereotype of the typical Jewish mother.

In American Irish Catholicism you get worst of both worlds.

This is another instance where breaking out Homer, Aeschylus, Augustine, Kant, Nietzsche, Freud, Philip Roth, and Larry David, etc. is greater than reading another blog.

In short, we Westerns for the most part are riddled with guilt and shame. I am not sure developing any more of it or "other" kinds would be very helpful, even if Spartans are really cool at the moment.

Mark Forster:

"Shame in this context presumably equates to "It's fine as long as you don't get found out"?"

That is what shame is. Especially when seen in the ancient Mediterranean. If you read literature from the period, it is pretty clear that the virtue, better translated as "excellence", (arete) of one's behavior didn't make much sense outside one's relations within the City State nor the possibility of "happiness" (eudaimonia).

When Aristotle writes: "Man is (by nature) a (the) political animal." He means that outside the relationship to the polis, man is not. One's "private" actions or thoughts do not come in question much in antiquity.

Now what actions were considered shameful were at times highly contested. Take a look at the greatest misanthrope of all time Diogenes the Cynic. "Dog"-like and an advocate of an arete rooted in "nature" as the only possible path to eudamonia, he still made his home in the agora.

As the rise of private life increased throughout the Western world and growing importance of the home (oikos), the emphasis on guilt grew as well.

As mentioned above, the teachings of Jesus cannot be underestimated in this turn of events.

IMHO of course. This was pretty much Nietzsche's analysis of the moral development of the West taken up by Freud to understand the development of a person.
