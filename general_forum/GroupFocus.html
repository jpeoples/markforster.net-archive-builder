Alan, not sure if it's over-kill for your situation, but in most places where I've worked that were efficiently run, we did up a list of deliverables and deadlines for projects and whose responsibility it was to get them to us (for external people). They weren't necessarily the ones actually doing it.

We also kept internal calendars of deadlines, deliverables and who was assigned to what deliverable.

We did it all in excel and just emailed it out.

I've also done the same thing managing it in Outlook (the delegate function). I preferred excel though for some reason, probably because I'm lazy and could get the junior intern type people to just change everything for me. If it was in my outlook, I'd have to either grant them access or do it myself.

Where I am now, they have an internal calendar set up in Outlook for the whole finance team with all the stuff due on it.

A Google Docs spreadsheet is available to your whole team. Everyone can update it. At the same time.

You need to force people to use it for a month or so, until it is baked into the normal way of doing things.

Wave is a kind of mashup of eMail, instant messaging and Wiki. It is pretty free form and can be quite clunky in its present beta form. And can't do tables.

I think a simple Google spreadsheet is the way to go. Google's account setup process can be a bit tricky: someone almost certainly has gmail and can help talk you through.

Of course, the very best way is to get everyone in one room and cover one large wall with writing.

Hey Will, I'm not sure that I understand the question correctly. We used to do that as well for Sarbanes Oxley tracking (have to show what had changed). When reviewing, if there was conflicts, I would set things up so that I manually overruled other users changes. (Hmm. Can I do that here too? LOL) What version are you running? I'm on 2003 at this client, missing the new one badly. Did you do this from the help?:
_______________________________________________________________________
Turn on change tracking for a workbook
On the Tools menu, click Share Workbook, and then click the Editing tab.
Select the Allow changes by more than one user at the same time check box.
Click the Advanced tab.
Under Track changes, click Keep change history for, and in the Days box, type the number of days of change history that you want to keep.
Be sure to enter a large-enough number of days because Microsoft Excel permanently erases any change history older than this number of days.

Click OK, and if prompted to save the file, click OK.
Note Turning on change tracking also shares the workbook.
________________________________________________________________________
