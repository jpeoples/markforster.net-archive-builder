I have a simple technique for "must do now" items : every morning I quickly read through my AF list, and I highlight in yellow all items that really must be done today.
Then I go through the pages again, actioning only these highlighted items before going through the rest of the list.
It's easy enough because I usually have 4-5 active pages only.
The trick is to strictly discriminate what HAS TO be done today, and to not include what I WOULD REALLY LIKE to do today.

The good thing is that if ever one urgent action is not done (eg you can't reach someone on the phone), it still there, highlighted. So during the day, while you go through the pages, it jumps to your eyes that an urgent item is still undone.

Actually, I do that any time of the day, when I get the impression I have stuff to do that just became urgent.

The good thing about that is that if I have 3 urgent items, I have no fear of forgetting to carry on to the rest of them when I start acting the first one. A quick glance at all the pages and they are there.

If one stays undone, and there is no consequence, I knox I have to tune up my estimation of priorities and urgency...

Hi Derek

I've actually given this topic a lot of thought as I am struggling with the "must do now issue". The conclusion I have come to for what it's worth is as follows:

I think there are a number of categories of must do now items, and the control of those needs to be looked at differently

Scheduled Tasks
Meetings, appointments etc should be handled through a calendar or schedule, and not through the AF list.

Genuine Emergency Tasks
House on fire, crying child, emergency call from someone in need etc. - such tasks are unforeseen and clearly just need to be actioned as they arise

Necessary Urgent Tasks
Important interruptions, external deadlines etc., While such tasks need to be actioned as they arise, they are more able to be controlled. Systems should be in place to reduce interruptions and using AF fewer items should become "last minute" and end up in this category.

All of the above are genuine "must do now" items although the last group can be controlled to some extent.

The main areas of difficulty seem to be on perceived "must do now" items or "unstable backlogs". These may or may not be "genuine" must do now items but do have probably more impact than the above groups.

Perceived "Must Do Now" Items
Habit and custom tasks, preference tasks, and some "want to do now" tasks fall into this area. This can include things like email, post, household chores etc. It may be custom to check email 6 times a day but is it habit or genuine necessity? We may be required to have post opened and identified and perhaps on someone else's desk by 9:30. That is different to being able to address paper when "it stands out". If there is a specific time requirement then it should be scheduled in some way. If not AF can handle this type of task perfectly well.

Unstable Backlogs
This is the area I struggle with the most. These are the backlogs of tasks that, using the quadrant system, would have fallen into the "important and non-urgent" quadrant, but by virtue of not having been addressed are becoming sufficiently urgent to warrant action. They may not have a specific timed deadline but the non-doing of them has or will have a massive impact.

Stable backlogs, ie those that really can be cleared using the "little and often" approach without a major effect on anything else (eg sorting a pile of old photos, cataloguing a DVD collection, sorting an old archive) can be cleared amazingly well within AF, and without effort.

Unstable backlogs however are a major energy drain, because regardless of the actual impact, perhaps affecting us financially, impacting our efficiency or even our reputation, they constantly remain in our minds, and as Mel and learning as I go posted, destroying our peace. In my opinion, if something is genuinely destroying our peace then that is a perfectly acceptable reason for treating it as a genuine "must do". That is in effect our intuition informing us of it's impact but we need to be honest with ourselves about whether that is a genuine issue or just an annoyance. However, in my opinion, any of the above effects qualify that area for perhaps emergency measures, such as specific timeblocks or an intensive push to clear it at all costs. The solution may well vary from person to person. Having cleared two unstable backlogs and now working on my third and final one (my other backlogs are happily quite stable) I know for me that I have to approach it on the all or nothing basis. I can manage that within AF using my Tandem tweak but am fully aware that that is not pure AF but rather a means to an end.

Being able to look at the types of "urgent" items has helped me to look at what works and what doesn't but this is all from a purely personal perspective. Hope it may help in some way .....

Jacqueline,

ROTFLMAO :-) Jacob has to be a 5w6, no doubt about it! Hell, he even included an inequality in his formula! ;-) Just about all writers on change have come up with the same basic idea ... mostly because it is kind of simple and obvious (conceptually speaking, not in implementation ;-)

Tony Robbins screamed it in his horse voice ;-) for years and combined it with some of the simpler NLP techniques. I like the way Robbins put it because he made it less abstract. (and therefore less easy to conceptualize and get lost in procrastination ;-)

Basically his steps are:

- Carefully and clearly describe the desired end state. Use emotional words and do so in all sensory modalities. (Other writers advise building a multi-sensory image with music, pictures cut from magazines, longish paragraphs describing how you will love having achieved that goal, etc.) This is: V (vision of the future)

- Do the same for your current state. (For your current state build an emotional picture and exaggerate the negative feelings you have about it.) This is: D (dissatisfaction with current state)

- List all of the reasons why you MUST change. This is again: V (vision of the future), but more concretely identifying the particular reasons.

- List all of the reasons why you really do NOT want to change ... why it will be hard. This is: C (cost of the change) looked at from one perspective.

- List all of the reasons why you really HATE being where you are. This is again: D (dissatisfaction with current state) but more concretely identifying the particular reasons.

- List all of the reasons why you really LIKE your current situation. This is: C (cost of the change) looked at from the opposite perspective.

- Amplify those reasons which will lead you to change and minimize those which will not, or which hold you back. This is the NLP aspect of it.

- List the actions you must take to move you toward the desired state and away from the one you no longer want. This is P (the plan). Most writers seem to concentrate on this part and the next.

- Start taking regular actions as dictated by the above.

- Take feedback from the result of the actions and use that feedback to change your actions to account for problems. This last step is often taken for granted by most writers but should really be explicitly included. Where we often go wrong is by assuming that things will just work out on their own if we put our plan on autopilot. But even an autopilot takes feedback from the environment, compares it to the planned course, and compensates to change the actual course so as to keep on track with the plan!

That is it broken down as minutely as I can imagine. It is the same formula as given by the writer you cite, and many many others ... just defined so minutely that there can be no mistake. Oh, and don't forget the shouting. You gotta love that shouting! ;-)

In writing this and recalling the various takes on it by others, I'm struck by the range of detail which is included or excluded. The basic navigation metaphor is:

- Identify where you are
- Identify where you want to go
- Draw a line between the two
- Follow it.

It can't get much simpler. But it can get a whole lot more complex (i.e. detailed ;-)
