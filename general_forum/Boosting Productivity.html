Hi Bob
Timeboxing is perfect for this! Be sure to log your time working as well. Sometimes I'll top-load my work at the beginning of the day and also at the beginning of the week to assure myself that the most important stuff doesn't drag into the last hours or days. Hi Bob, nice timing with your question:

I got a tip from another reader - check out my post on AF (any version) and setting limits. what is working well for me at the moment is writing 'start af' and then 'stop af' directly underneath it. I write in the start time and then cross the task out. When 'stop af' stands out I log the time in and then cross 'stop af' out.

Although this does not log the time spent on individual tasks, it does provide feedback about the duration of time spent doing quality work (similar in some ways to the Now Habit). In any case I'd prefer not to time individual tasks because I think this works against the main benefit of AF, that is working for as long as you feel like.

I like this concept because it helps me switch on and off mentally also - so I can enjoy time when I'm not working from AF.

I'll probably tally up the amount of quality hours I put in during a week and set some sort of reward system up and an upper weekly time target (again using ideas from the Now Habit).

Joseph: I write just about everything I'm about to do down at the end of the list. eg: (a) PC is booted up, there are a few sites I want to go to check out news, I write it down and cross it off once done. (b) i'm in a middle of a task, and a call comes in requiring me to do something right away, I cross off what I was doing, re-enter it at the end, and also write down what I am about to do regarding the call.

Just gives me a sense of control. And when I am procrastinating, writing down those mindless tasks I am doing raises my awareness that they are not productive, and helps me ease myself into the more important tasks, once that bout is out of my system.
