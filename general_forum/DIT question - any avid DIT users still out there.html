2010-02-25 18:24


Mark suggests that a project should be handled according to whether it is a ‘continuous’ (such as learning a language) or an ‘organisational’ one (e.g. planning a marketing campaign).

Continuous projects consist of essentially repetitious tasks and handled by scheduling specific times on one’s calendar/schedule. Organisational projects (which lead to a specific goal) consist of tasks which are not repetitive. Mark’s advice is to break the project down into its subtasks, and then bring them forward for action by putting them into the task diary. He says “A small number of tasks can be put into the task diary for tomorrow’s date. Larger or more complicated projects can be spread a few tasks at a time over a week or more”.

Mark also identifies a third kind of project – too short to be called continuous but can’t really be broken down into sub-tasks as they are essentially repetitious. They should be re-entered in the task diary each day until complete. His example is ‘sort out bookshelves’ which, coincidently, has been on my list for a few days!

So perhaps we should decide which kind of project it is, and then handle it in the most appropriate way. The type of project I struggle with the most is the organisational one – as it depends on how well I have broken down the project and identified the tasks and I always forget to include tasks like ‘Think about …’ or ‘Review…’ on the project task list!

Hi Leon

One piece of advice I read from ‘The Power of Less’ – have a list of projects that you want to tackle, but choose only the three most important ones to work on at any one time. That’s three altogether, not three work and three personal projects. Ideally, one of them should be related to a long-term goal. Only when all three are completed should you select three more to work on. The idea is so that you don’t spread yourself too thinly and lose focus. The author says this works best when projects are about two weeks to one month long. Longer projects can be broken down into one-month mini-projects. I have tried this and found it a good discipline but hard to keep it up as new projects start creeping in when I'm not looking!

2010-02-28 8:10
David

Regarding a 2-week backlog, I don’t tend to let the backlog get beyond a week if I can help it, but it depends on the circumstances. If the backlog is a clear, temporary state of affairs caused by a specific situation – say an illness, or a big project which has taken priority over everything else – and will end within a couple of weeks or so - then I’ll accept the fact that other things will start to back up.

There are periods of intense work in my job, interspersed with quieter periods and in the intense parts I will be working evenings/weekends to keep everything going. Other parts of my life will slip particularly home and personal tasks. I have a minimum “will do” list for home and personal to keep those ticking over as best I can.

What I find most helpful in a backlog situation, is to sit down with my task diary and calendar and review ALL my tasks and the commitments both delayed and upcoming. Delayed tasks fall into three categories – 1) no point in doing it now as the next recurrence of the task is coming up in to next few days or the commitment/meeting/appointment it was in aid of has passed. 2) I still need/want to do the task soon, in which case I will re-enter it in the task diary for the next day when it is possible to do the task, and fix a time slot on the calendar as well to highlight it 3) I would really like to do the task but can’t really commit time for it at the moment, so I enter it on a Someday/Maybe list which I review weekly. When things get really hairy, I do this review on a daily basis.

That only works for short, temporary backlog situations caused by specific circumstances. When backlogs happen a lot, or becomes the normal state of affairs then I agree with other posters that no time management system can solve the problem of too much work.
