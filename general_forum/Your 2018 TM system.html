Hello and happy new year everyone!

As we wait with bated breath for mark to release the rules on his new way to do HITM, I thought it may be interesting to see how others are managing their time and tasks as we begin 2018.

Some may recall I had been doing what I called "Random RAF," which was just using the randomizer per Mark's rules and doing a DDD for each task that came up. i did that until Mark talked more about simple scanning...

Now I do a combo of "Simple Scanning" and AF2. In the AM, when I feel pressure and groggyiness, I work my list AF2 style, scanning from the last item until I find something to do and then returning to the bottom of the list. I do this until I feel less pressure and/or less grogginess, whereupon I move to the front of the list and and follow the current "simple scanning" rules.

I use paper and pencil. My paper is a bBackwing slate notebook (looks like a moleskine) but I am very fond these days of Baron Fig's Confidant notebook and cannot wait to finish this book (I have a long way to go) and go back to that one.

I also use Outlook and will often move emails to tasks, and give it the same name as what I write in my book. This way it is an easy search to bring up more info and the related email associated with the task in the paper list.

Currently I am experimenting with categorizing tasks in Outlook to view tasks by project. I add the project name to my paper list and when i select it review all tasks under that project.

As I have for years now, track all of my "waiting fors" in Outlook with the @ and their initials, so when I sort in outlook alphabetically they are all together at the top of the list. I then have a recurrent task called 'waiting" that prompts me to do this.

I also use One Note for taking notes and importing things from the web and from the real world (using Microsoft Lens to capture an image). tasks in One Note get flagged which makes them appear in my task list and then in my paper list.

Finally, I keep a notebook in my pocket at all times and transfer any ideas that come up into the paper list.

So far, this is my 2018 setup. What's yours?

Anchored AF2 in a spiral bound A4 notebook.

My only tweak from Mark's instructions is what I call the 5 minute morning review. I set my timer for 5 minutes and review each task in order from the beginning of the notebook: a task can either be deleted, deleted and re-entered with rephrasing or deleted and entered in my diary. The review process stops when I hit 5 minutes and resumes the next working day.

Tasks that are not actioned promptly are effectively moving back in the list until they are caught by the morning review.

I am attempting in this new year an analog long list with simple scanning. I have long done my lists digitally but I was intrigued by the post by Mark regarding attenuation, clumping and maturity.

http://markforster.squarespace.com/blog/2017/12/13/simple-scanning-clumping-attenuation-and-maturity.html

This seemed easier to accomplish with an analog list. I have already seen some extra insight gained by scanning a page of half done tasks or mostly done tasks and seeing what remains. I use a highlighter to cross out done items so the undone items really stand out.

You can bet I'm motivated to just do it or delete it when it is the only thing remaining on the notebook page. I have been using a smaller notebook to make seeing that one last undone task on a page a quicker experience.

The notebook I'm using is a 4.5 in X 3.25 composition notebook that fits nicely in an old index card wallet . Easily pocket-able, so it is always with me.

I also decided to keep personal tasks on the left hand pages and work items on right hand pages. This allows me to skip scanning pages based on my work or personal context. Having them in the same book is good though because I can still easily scan both when needed.

I still use digital (dynalist.io) for tracking meeting notes and project notes as the search and outline organization features are invaluable for me. But i'm not keeping my running task list there.

I also on occasion use a "panic list" on an index card in the same wallet. As Mark said in another post, sometimes you just need to focus on a small and pointed list for short period of time to get out of panic or emergency mode. I transfer the panic list back to the main list if anything turned out to be less of an emergency. In other words, I don't keep the panic list around for long.

All in all, the discussions over the last few months have really led me to organize and group things less and try to let intuition lead out. I spend less time organizing lists in to groups now. I feel slightly less on top of "everything" but I feel like I'm actually getting more done faster. The trade-off is so far good as the "keep scanning to see what I'm doing next is actually faster than re-organizing as long as I keep the focus on crossing things off. I'm hoping the velocity of doing keeps the list shorter. The shorter the list, the less I will feel I have to categorize and organize.

Brent

Still doing that Daily FAF thing, but with a minor change in that I go back to the start of the "day" I am "trapped" in instead of going back to where my just-done task was. I found that this improves flow.

1. Close the list every start or end of the day with a drawn line.
2. Scan down until a task "stands out". Dot and do this. After doing it rewrite it if needed at the end of the list, then cross it out.
3. Go up the list to the nearest drawn line above your just crossed-out task, then scan down until a task "stands out". Repeat ad infinitum.

My system (inspired by Matt Perman What`s best next):

1) The core is weekly schedule, not tasks. Weekly schedule is not cast in stone and is not overly detailed. Each day I have 4-7 "blocks" of activities (various lengths, from 20 minutes to 2-3 hours). I try to make the schedule as simple as possible so as it repeats most days (Tue,Wed, Thu are almost identical, Mon and Fri are quite similar). As Matt Perman said: the key is having so simple schedule that you do not have to look at it. You know it by memory.

Of course, I also use regular calendar for appointments, but I only schedule e.g. all my client appointments in "clients" blocks (Mon, Fri - 15-18:30, Tue-Thu 10-12:30) etc.

That said, I try to by quite flexible. When I started to experiment with weekly schedule, I felt stress and felt still under pressure. Than I relaxed a bit, loose the times and chose only general blocks inside which I define more concrete activities not before these blocks starts. (And of course if unpredictable or inspiration strikes, I change the schedule as needed)

MY CURRENT BLOCKS = "THEMES" FOR ACTIVITIES:

Every working day I have (in different orders):
- Morning routines (yoga, meditation, cup of tea etc - in strict order so that I do not have to think what to do next)
- Client meetings
- Admin work (from emails to banking, invoicing, tidying, communication....)
- Creative work (articles, preparing marketing, preparing workshops, development...)
- Learning Programming
- Running

On some days:
- Relax/Friends/Out
- Personal/Home Maintenance

Sunday is free / Saturday is loosely structured

2) As I said, tasklists are not the core of the systems as used to be for me before. They just complement (specify) what I want do in my blocks - not in all of them, as morning routines are clear and client meetings are based on calendar and notes.

So I have 4 "projects" for my blocks in Things (app for mac and iPhone):
- Admin
- Creative
- Personal/Things/Home
- Programming

What I try is to put only active (current) tasks/projects in these lists and never to have more tasks in every list than is visible plus minus without scrolling the screen. I cluster tasks by subthemes (eg.banking, communication, client payments, tidy-up in Admin project). I do it on the go, as new subprojects or areas within my main blocks arise - so the structure inside tasklists is still flexible.

That is more or less the system. I use other Things functions like Due dates or Schedule but I use it within these "projects" = tasklists, so it does not change the main idea much.

What was very interesting for me was how this system (based on regular weekly schedule) started to simplify itself. I started with regular weekly reviews and daily reviews but as I go now, I use weekly review very sporadically and I do not make my "daily tasklist" every day as I used to do, only sometimes. Because I just know what to do based on my lists/schedule and I know what is important (I am constantly looking inside lists when I am inside any block, re-categorizing, re-prioritizing tasks etc). What I do in these blocks? What I know is important from the subprojects/subareas in tasklists. No standing out, no rules, just common sense, going for balance and importance.

Every morning I briefly look at "today" tasks (scheduled or due for today), which are already positioned in individual tasklists. I try not to schedule many tasks, so they are usually just 1-5 of them, including "deferred". Even if I would not check it in the morning, I would see them as the block starts as I look into its tasklist.

The rest of the day, I switch between blocks (and look into their tasklists) based on my schedule . That is all.

(I also use "someday-maybe" lists, which are more specific by themes/areas, but it is not the core of the system, just addition.)

For me it is best system I ever tried and the paradox is that I refused the idea of such strict "weekly schedule" quite long, trying to experiment still with long-lists, daily tasklists etc. I was worried about my freedom, spontaneity etc. At the end when nothing worked much, I get it a try, experimented with it a bit (had to loose it, not overplan, overcomplicate etc) and it is not before now when I really find my freedom and spontaneity in my tasks...within the border of my blocks, beyond which my head stays clear.

Daneb:

There is a sense in which scheduling things is necessarily the primary mode of operating, and making things routine in that schedule is the only way to be fully productive. A long list can't quite get you there because the fact is there are fixed moments in your week, and activities that must happen every time, and the way to do those fastest is to make them automatic. No scheduling, no reading, no preparing because you are already prepared. It's hard to beat the efficiency of going to bed at a regular hour, doing the exact same thing before bed each time, and then getting up at a regular hour and doing the exact same thing in the morning to prepare for work. If anything therein is not fully efficient you can change it to optimize it, and if you have time to spare you can then your tools to aid you in discretionary activities.

After the routine blocks are established, the remaining blocks of time you fill however you choose. For that, I still prefer lists.

I reckon you have to adapt a system depending on your own needs.
For instance (in my view), a long list and simple scanning works if you have a quick turnover of tasks.
When the list gets too long to manage (in terms of you loose track of what needs doing next and potentially miss deadlines), then something more sophisticated is needed.
Perhaps to include some priority measures. e.g. have some urgency zones - maybe 3 lists to work from (do now, do within a week, do next month etc). That way you can work through a shorter list and not get overwhelmed and use stand out on that list only.
I have been doing that for a while and it helps.

One point is that I do the standout bit when I first see the task and then drop it in the right list. Cut out the wheat from the chaff! The other tasks all need to be done at some point, but can wait.
There are so many ways of doing tasks, I guess we just need to find one that keeps us all happy and worry free!
Or maybe we can pick out bits of time management systems that seem to work for us and combine them into one.
PS urgency zone is from Michael Linenberger, but I did not like all the other ideas he has.
