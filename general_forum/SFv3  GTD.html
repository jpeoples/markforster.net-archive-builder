I agree, but I think some of the teaching of GTD could help make SFv3 more fluid. I think that some GTD vertrans will be successful sooner in SFv3 is they have internalized some parts of GTD.

Amongst those would be:

1) Recognizing multiple step project in the GTD flavor. So that you can put the project on the list; start a phase of it; check it off; move it to column 2 as unfinished; finish that phase of the project check it off in column two and then give yourself permission to put it back in column 1 because the next discrete step is a different action of a larger project that might take weeks. That approach saves you having to remember to put each discrete system into the system.

2) Learning to write tasks in an clean actionable manner that make them more appealing to do.

3) Keeping everything in your trusted system when possible; don't demand it; but strive for it when it fits the moment.

4) Reviewing the entire system as often as necessary. And reviewing trigger lists when you have that nagging feeling that you forgetting something.

5) Learning to get back on the wagon really fast when you fall off. seems a common gtd problem

6) Recognizing when a days "context" has nothing to do with anything else on your list; putting the list away and getting through the day without guilt knowing that tomorrow you can get back on list and start cleaning up the mess...

7) Having checklists for recurrent procedures and just putting "do checklist XXX" into the system and not trying to manage the details.



There are others; and I don't claim they are unique to GTD; but learning GTD teaches them and they make you more efficient in any system if you can taylor them to your unique reality; which is key.

Perspective is king: In my world I can't make the "timed" start on the coffee pot save enough effort to justify using it; and yet the delayed start on the oven; I use that all the time to ensure dinner is started on time.
