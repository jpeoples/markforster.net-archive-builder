I have a tweak. Actually two. Hopefully they’ll be of help to someone.

I’ve always had a problem with dismissing items. I hate the finality of saying “I am not going to do that”, and sometimes I’m sure a dismissed task is actually one I both want to do and need to do, but for whatever reason (usually time, or expense, or both) has not come “ripe”.

I also struggled with highlighting dismissed tasks, as to me (am very visual), a highlighted task suggests an urgent task, not one I can afford to ignore for the time being.

I have therefore taken to crossing out dismissed items, (as I would a completed task), and re-writing them on the back page of my Autofocus notebook. Every Friday, I review the dismissed items, so I always know what’s on my peripheral radar, but now that they’re no longer visible on my active pages, they aren’t the distraction they used to be.

NB I don’t dismiss items that are out of context, just those that are not practical, possible, or sufficiently urgent for me to do in the immediate future. (I’m guessing everyone’s natural timeline is different, but my “immediate future” is about eight weeks. YMMV.)

My second tweak is one to aid resistance. It works on the principle that a hardened procrastinator (moi!) will always choose the least challenging task on their active page.

Opposite my most recent active page, I will write a whole page (approx 25 lines in my notebook) listing the task I am resisting over and over (like writing lines at school, not that I used to do that much, ahem). I find it works best when it incorporates a timeframe eg “work on Semiotics paper for twenty minutes”.

I treat the double page spread of tasks as one closed list.

So, to give you an idea, on my current active page I have items like “scrub bathroom floor”, “iron 7 shirts for week ahead” “collect parcels at post-office”, and “find new cleaner”. Faced with tasks like that, who wouldn’t prefer to spend fifteen minutes working off the opposite page, tackling that Semiotics paper bit by bit?

Hi little b

The AF rules specify dismissing "all" remaining items on a page if nothing stands out. If you dismiss items on an active page before that final closure then it may well have an impact on the flow of reading the page and make it distracting. You are better to wait until you dismiss the entire page (or perhaps you will find that you end up actioning the tasks instead).

Mark created some other term--which escapes me for the moment--for items that we decide not to do. For this post, I will call them "cancelled." It is not very unusual for me to cancel an item even when there are still open items on a page. I look at an item and decide that I no longer wish to do the item or I am no longer able to do the item. As far as I am concerned, canceling and dismissing are functionally identical. One has a single item on a page with other open items as its object. The other has all the items on a page as its object.

little b,

like you I find dismissal very difficult and have right from the start developed something similar to you. I don't actually highlight dismissed items, since I can find that distracting (either if I am working on the page opposite, or if the highlighter shows through), but rather write all dismissed items into a list at the back of the notebook, from where they may be returned one day. I call this my "Someday" list (something I added to DIT when I was using that). I don't look at it very frequently, maybe once a month, and funnily enough, so far have never returned anything back to the main list. So in the end its just a security blanket for me.

Its quite a few, which I find interesting.

I do something similar with dismissed items (or actually canceled items as well):
I write a "x" through the checkbox (instead of a check mark) and if I think this is something I might do later I write the item on a A4 size piece of paper and put it into my tickler file system, e.g. for next month or beginning of next quarter or ...
When my tickler file than "throws it out" again I consider whether and when I might do it. I might put the piece of paper into the bin, or defer it again or write the task into my AF book again (and throw the paper into the bin as well).
