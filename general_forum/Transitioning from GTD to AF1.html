Erik,

In a previous manual/paper version, I used the back of the notebook, but you can even try using the back upside-down for home stuff so that both work and home look "normal".

However in my new manual DWM approach, I am trying the following in an A5 notebook:

For work I write down the tasks on the right-hand page only.
For home/other, I use the back of that same sheet, ie a left-hand page, starting at the bottom of that page.
This keeps the two contexts (I have several more!) separate, so when I want to scan work items, I just look at the right-hand pages; for home the left-hand pages.

Before someone asks why i don't write home stuff opposite the right-hand page for that date, it's because I rip out the first sheet when that date has gone by and everything on it left undone is dismissed.

Erik, I tried maintaining two running books for a while before I abandoned AF. While it does keep you FOCUSED, it also had a haphazard effect for me in that I would be at home and remember a few things that needed to go to the work list and vice versa. I found that to be annoying. I do always carry around a post-it for such events, but then I ended up transcribing the tasks for each notebook, etc. So I think if you're working in AF and you have a lot of free flow between your home and work space, or just don't like being annoyed in having to remember specific tasks to add to different workstations, a one-list system is optimal.

Erik:

"One thing that I had missed up until this point is the suggestion to have separate notebooks for Home and Office. I like this concept because it keeps you FOCUSED where you need to be"

If you know what or where you need to be FOCUSED on, why use AF at all? In fact, I would say that AF ain't so much as focusing me, but bringing the world into focus around me and highlighting where I can be most useful in it.

Some people find two separate notebooks useful, others not.

To think that your situation is similar to others' is a leap of faith.

The simple solution is to try both. Give each an honest try (few months). Some of the tips and pointers above might help you decide which might be best to start with or maintain, but ultimately experience will bear this out for you.

My suggestion overall is to give whatever choice you choose some time. Try AF as it is, before bending it to focus on what you think needs focusing on. That already exists; it is called a to-do list.

I use one notebook FWIW. And I do take back and forth from work. And I use a "satellite" notebook (folded up piece of paper) when carrying the larger notebook doesn't make sense.

This is what I have come to with experience.

When I was doing AF, I used the "stalactite / stalagmite" method: write work tasks from the bottom of the page going down, and write home tasks from the bottom of the page going up, and draw a horizontal line across the page where they meet.

This way, the two areas are separated so you can focus, but you only need one notebook and can still see both areas any time you need.
