Now here is an idea: in DWM2, make the end of the list mobile.

1) Before starting the day, close your DWM2's previous day as originally instructed.
2) Get a clean lined piece of paper and label it "TODAY" at the top.
3) Treat this piece of paper as both the end of your DWM2 list and as a bookmarker.
3.1) Write all the re-entered and new items on this piece of paper as you would at the end of a usual DWM2 list.
3.2) Insert this paper between the pages where you are in working the DWM2 list.
4.) At the end of the day, write the remaining tasks at the end of the list then close it as per usual DWM2 instructions.

I think this will also work with DWM1...probably much better if one's using a binder notebook.

So what happens is you practically have the DWM's version of Column 2 in SAF, but instead of a different Column 2 per page of SAF, you have a "traveling Column 2" wherever you are in your DWM list.

Of course the downside is the re-writing at the end...unless, as I had mentioned, one's using DWM1 and a binder notebook wherein he/she can remove/insert pages at will.

I think I've done it! I have been trialing a new way of processing DWM2 that does minimal changes to it that promises to be closely resembling SAF both in appearance AND effect. And for the past three days I have using it, it's been very effective.

Now what I really like about it are the very unexpected results:

1) It stabilizes the use of DWM2. The problem with "Ping-Ponging", which is how I tend to use DWM2 before this, is that it disturbs the decision making when processing the list. This eliminates it.
2) As you will see, it can be ALSO be used in plain vanilla paper based DWM, and probably other TMS's as well.
3) The flexibility of use that I experienced with DWM2 is retained, and even enhanced.
4) Most surprisingly and delightfully, it can be used to manage projects. Yes, PROJECTS!

There are, however, some minor disadvantages.

1) You will have to follow my own way of DWM2; that is, only unfinished tasks are to be treated as "re-entered" tasks (to be dismissed on the 7th day after re-entering) or the system will be too awkward to use.
2) You will have to re-write a half page to a whole page of tasks every few days.
3) You may easily loose the separate piece of paper that you are going to use, so BE CAREFUL!
4) I cannot think of any way to duplicate this electronically.

So here we go: the instructions!

Part I: main DWM list
1) You need your DWM or DWM2 list and a separate piece of paper.
2) Use the separate piece of paper as a bookmark for your current location in the DWM list.
3) You will never write your "re-entered" tasks into your main DWM list ever again, but ALWAYS into the separate piece of paper. As a review, "re-entered" tasks are unfinished tasks which, if unactioned, will be deleted after 7 days. All new and finished recurrent tasks are entered in the main DWM list at the 30-days-away mark.
4) You CAN (not always) also write your urgent or important tasks on the separate piece of paper; however, when they are added there, they MUST be treated always as "re-entered" tasks.
5) You can work on any task either on the separate piece of paper or on your current location on the main DWM list anytime.
5.a) If a task on the main DWM list OR on the separate piece of paper was completed but is recurrent, re-write it into your main DWM list.
5.b) If a task on the main DWM list OR on the separate piece of paper was not completed but have to be stopped for now, then cross it out and re-enter into the end of the list at the separate piece of paper.
6) At the end of the day or on the morning of the next day, if there are remaining tasks that were re-entered on the spent day, draw a line across the separate piece of paper at the end of the list with some space for tally marks (http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tally_marks ), and write a tally mark for "1" into the space. The piece of paper is then ready for the next day.
6.a) For each day onward, as long as there are remaining tasks before each, add a tally mark into the space of each line until it reaches "7", upon which you dismiss everything before the line.
7) Once the separate piece of paper is about to be filled out, re-write all the remaining tasks into another piece of paper, with their appropriate positions and separating lines with tally marks preserved, and use this for the succeeding days. You may throw away the old one.

* I will, in the very near future, put a scan here of my piece of paper for clarification.

Now, here is how you use it for projects, if you have separate project files like Alan or me.

Part II: Projects
1) You may anytime add "Work on Project X" to your main DWM list ONLY, UNLESS the project is urgent, upon which you may add it to the separate paper list, upon which it is now subject to the Part I rules above.
2) When you are working at the main DWM list and you think of something to add to one of your projects, you have the option of writing the task into the piece of paper list BUT (a) with a different mark before it to indicate that it is not subject to the "re-entered tasks" rules of Part I above (unless it is a very urgent task, upon which it is become subject to Part I rules), and (b) with an indication that it is to be added to the project, eg. (using Alan's example) "Task --> X"
3) When you decide to work on your project, add another bookmark to your main DWM list to indicate where you are last, bring the separate paper into your project file, and work on the project
3.a) Transfer all "Task --> Project" tasks from the separate paper into the appropriate projects and cross them out from the separate paper.
3.b) You may anytime work on the project tasks OR on the tasks in the separate paper, using ALL the rules of Part I, this time with the project tasks taking the place of main DWM list tasks, with the minor revision of indicating the "re-entered tasks" as part of a project, eg. "X: Task"
3.c) If you think of something to add to the main DWM list, you have the option of writing the task into the piece of paper list BUT (a) with a different mark before it to indicate that it is not subject to the "re-entered tasks" rules of Part I above (unless it is a very urgent task, upon which it is become subject to Part I rules), and (b) with an indication that it is to be added to the main DWM list, eg."Task --> DWM"
3.d) Upon leaving the project, you may add the project into the separate piece of paper under the rules of Part I ONLY if it is urgent.
4) Upon leaving the project, bring your separate paper into the main DWM list where you left it, transfer all "Task --> DWM" items into your main DWM, and work on the list as before.

So that's it! If there are any questions (and I know there will be, as I know myself to be notoriously overly-verbose and vague in my instructions) please post them here.

From my observations, the current paradigms we have right now in the forums (and I think everywhere else) in trying to unify our different management systems are two. One is to bring the projects, calendars, to-dos, etc. into one over-arching system that emphasizes one of these. Or, if you will pardon the analogy, it's like a company dominating other companies to make one megacorporation. This is the paradigm of Covey's Seven Habits (goal setting and accomplishment dominating others) and GTD (contexts dominating others). The other paradigm is that one system gathers information from all the other systems to make decisions, and sometimes sending back information to the other systems. Continuing the previous analogy, it is like one company receiving parts from other companies to make the finished products. This is the paradigm of Mark's AF and DWM systems.

This Super DWM is different. From my use, I find that it is trying to unite the other systems by informing ALL the systems of PERTINENT information while letting the different systems DO what they do best. And again using the previous analogy, it is like a courier company sending the needed parts and products from one company to another to make the whole economy work. And the system is able to do this because of the separate piece of paper: it holds the most important tasks you have --- the urgent, important, and unfinished tasks --- and keeps them always before you even when you work the other systems like checklists, projects, the main DWM list, calendars etc., while at the same time, it facilitates the transfer of information from one system to another in an unobtrusive manner. And the thing is, I think it can be used for any of Mark's systems. It is nothing like I have ever used before.

Which one, Alan? The separate piece of paper (from henceforth known as SPP) as a uniting system, the different paradigms, or Super DWM using Post-Its?

Well the Post-Its thing is just an idea, haven't implemented it yet. The different paradigms...well, lemme think about them more. But SDWM as a uniting system, I think I can give it a go.

What makes the DWM version I'm using right now unique from other TMS's is the SPP. The SPP, as I defined it from the rules above, contains all the unfinished tasks as well as the very urgent and important tasks too pressing to be placed in the 30-days-from-now point in the main DWM list or on any other system, and these tasks are to be dismissed 7 days from now if not actioned by then. The SPP also contains items you want to transfer from one system to another, for example from the DWM list to a project file, or vice versa. These "to-be-transferred" items are not under the 7-day-dismissal rule and are marked differently. And since the SPP is, well, a separate piece of paper, you can bring it to any system you are concentrating on, either on the main DWM list, or your calendar, or your project files, or beside your computer.

Now if you can picture what I had just said, then you might just realize the power of the SPP. It carries the most important tasks you need to do -- the very urgent, very important, and the unfinished tasks --- and you bring it to every system you have to work on; you can also add the unfinished and important/urgent tasks from those other systems to the SPP. So what you have is a very powerful reminder of how much is on your plate right now. And yet in spite of that you can still concentrate on whatever system you are working on, be it your calendar, your contacts, your projects, your checklists, etc, because of its handling of "to be transferred" items.

For example, while you are working on your DWM list, you remember you need to add a task to Project X, so instead of either the hassle of getting that "Project x" folder from the cabinet and writing the task there or losing that reminder in the quagmire of your DWM list you just add "Task--> Project X" to your SPP. You then have to work now on Project X so you get your SPP from the DWM list, transfer the tasks you wrote on the SPP to the project file, and put the SPP beside you as you work on the project's files, instead of putting that bulky DWM notebook alongside the project folder. You then remember you need to add something to the DWM list, so you add "Task-->DWM" on your SPP without getting the DWM notebook and looking for that last page. Somebody calls you to tell you of the new deadline of that project, so you write "Deadline 1/04/11 Project X --> Calendar" on your SPP. However, you have to stop working on your project, leaving the task of that project you were doing unfinished, so you write "X: Finish task" and mark it as a "re-entered task." You then return to your main DWM list with your SPP on the last page you were previously and continue processing it. Later, you decide to take your SPP to your calendar and transfer the deadline date there, then transfer the pertinent tasks from your calendar to your SPP for transfer to your main DWM list and project files.

I hope you can now see the promised power of the SPP. I just need to know how to overcome the re-writing dilemma of the SPP, and then it will be perfect ^____^
