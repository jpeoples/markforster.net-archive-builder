Happy New Year!

A thought crossed my mind today, that I think makes following AF easier.

Put big tasks on your list, rather than small ones. Because AF only requires a little bit of work on a task to get credit, this takes a lot less planning. If a task is too big and you are stuck, you may break it down. But I think Clean House may work just fine instead of Clean Bathroom, Clean Kitchen, Clean Living Room, etc. It keeps the list small. Of course if you are neglecting the bathroom every time you Clean House (little and often), then it's necessary to specialize Clean House - Bathroom.

If I took this notion to the extreme, I might have a list that went:

Family Stuff
House Stuff
Work Stuff
Friends Stuff
Community Stuff
God Stuff
Health Stuff

And that would work just fine until specific things come up that are getting neglected. So I put smaller things into the list. Yet I think it's useful to keep the big ones around.

I can set goals and put those on the list. If I know how to pursue the goal, it will get worked on. Otherwise, it becomes backlogged and I know I need to plan the goal better, and put subgoals or tasks into the list.

Bigger is better because:
SHORT) Your list is shorter
COMPLETE) All your duties are covered.
PROJECTS) Document your project plans elsewhere, and put the Project on your list. Then to work on your project, just open up the plans and do something. This is much more manageable than dumping your entire project onto the AF4 list.
OVERVIEW) It's easier to get an overview, easier to make the "feels right" decision.

Bigger is worse when:
VAGUE) The task is too vague and you never act on it.
SCARY) The task is too intimidating and you never act on it.
NO DETAIL) There is a more specific task that you MUST act on, and the bigger task fails to remind you. (This may come out of working on a project.)


In cases VAGUE and SCARY, this will become obvious when these tasks get stuck on your Closed list, and at that time you should flesh out the detail (and simultaneously realize that you are completely ignoring a big aspect of your life/work.) So if big tasks don't get you working, THEN break out a smaller task.

Case NO DETAIL shouldn't be an issue, because whenever a specific duty comes up, you SHOULD put it on your list. Whenever a Project has a MUST DO task, put it on the list. These are exceptions to the Big Task philosophy.

One thing I do with my Task/Page implementation is put details within each Task/Page. Only when details are needed though. So the title is a Big task, and the rest of the page highlights details I need to follow.

I think one of the reasons AF works is that you don't need all the organization that Mike uses for just stuff that needs to be done. Projects are different and often need to be planned and organized.

But something like House Stuff is too big. In reality, it could have so much stuff that the lack of detail leads it to be almost a useless item. You would need a separate AF list for House Stuff that might include:

Change flood light bulb
Clean (to whatever level of detail you wanted)
Fix fence
Schedule furnace maintenance
Fix squeeky drawer
laundry
cook supper
shop
etc.

Some of those might only come to mind when you stumble on the problem again unless it is visible on a list.

I do agree that you shouldn't add detail that isn't needed, but you do need to recognize what level of detail you need. Some of my tasks are very detailed just so I don't forget those important details. But they really are single tasks (no multiple steps like a project) so it is fine by me. Like Little B, I use the list to record almost everything.
