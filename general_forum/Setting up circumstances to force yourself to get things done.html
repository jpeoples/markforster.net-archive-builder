I have been standing back watching this thread evolve and am struck by how it has worked itself right back to the earlier discussions about efficiency vs. effectiveness, planning vs. not planning, goals vs. no goals and now, even happiness. Fascinating ;-)

Jaroslov questions: "... I agree that the range of what we can do has immensely expanded with technological development and we now have far more freedom and choice. But how many of us ... know how to use this freedom wisely and to our benefit. Or to put it bluntly, does it make us happier?"

I think that before one can consider how to achieve happiness one has to define it … for oneself. (Note that I am not saying that one has to define it before it can be achieved, clearly many people are happy and have no clue why. I'm saying that if one wants to deliberately choose to be happy, or to change unhappiness to happiness, one must first define what it is one is after.)

Does productivity ensure happiness? I think not. One can be the award winning widget producer at one's factory and blow one's weekly pay at the local bar (pub?) getting drunk to forget one's misery. That state is almost our icon of the human toll of the early industrial era. (And note that is where productivity measurement, time and motion studies, and all of that had its heyday.)

Does choice ensure happiness? Similarly, I think not. Choice without some personal standard by which to choose almost guarantees frustration. OTOH, lack of choice can also frustrate us.

My conclusion here is that neither productivity nor choice has meaningful impact on our happiness unless we define goals for ourselves and have a methodology to achieve our goals. IOW, the perfect time management system will only get us SOMEWHERE faster … but if we don't want to get there in the first place, what's the point? Having infinite choice likewise is not helpful if we don't have some means of choosing a path that will get us to our own personal nirvana.

Once again, I am not saying that no one who does not consciously choose goals and personal standards is doomed to misery. I am saying that if one is not already sure of ones path, then the only way to consciously find that path is through conscious deliberation. Clearly there are some who just always knew what they wanted. Star athletes often report that they only ever wanted to be athletes. Great musicians were born humming a tune. Great artists were never without a Crayola in their hands to decorate the walls. But some of us are interested in many things and so have the dilemma of choice … and so must have some way to choose … and focusing on the best time management system to get things done more efficiently does not help with that need. Indeed, it can make the situation worse.

That person would then chew on the ideas for some weeks more, then write a thoughtful reply … which in turn would go by horse and arrive weeks later. One can see that process as intolerably and wastefully slow ... or part of the process that led to thoughtful development of profound ideas.

I see the short attention span of today's culture to be problematic. I don't think it is conducive to thinking or a high quality of work. Mike wrote:

In my view of things one FIRST chooses goals, then SECONDLY aligns actions with goals through a system of time management. If we all agree that goals are necessary, why does the debate about it continue?


My response:

I don't think there is any disagreement about the necessity of goals. But perhaps there are some different ways that goals can be formed.

What if I have five goals, and all of them are good, but it's not really practical to go after all of them? I have to decide to focus on less than 5, or else risk never accomplishing any of them.

This can be further complicated by the degree to which the goals have been clearly articulated. Some goals might be very clear and follow the "SMART" rules (specific, measurable, attainable, relevant, time-bound). Other goals might resist such clear formulation; we might have a vague wish, an unanswered question, and we're not sure how to proceed, or even exactly how to articulate what the goal is. We just have a general idea. So, how do I choose which of the five goals to pursue, if some are well-articulated and others aren't?

And then again, goals can change over time, as life experience, values, priorities, and circumstances are all changing. At what point does an idea become an aspiration, and an aspiration become a goal?

You can't "choose" a goal until you can articulate what that goal is, and how it balances in priority with all your other goals.

How do you deal with that?


I suppose one way is to make it a habit of calling it a "goal" only when it's clearly articulated and fits all the SMART criteria. So, you maintain your list of goals (as so defined), and then you have a separate list of "someday/maybe" ideas. Perhaps those someday/maybe ideas will eventually become goals, once you've more clearly defined them, and then they go on your list of SMART goals. Perhaps this is what you mean by "FIRST choosing your goals". I think this approach works well if you have successfully provided a SMART articulation for your goals and feel pretty happy that the things on your goal list are really the things you want to do with your life.


But there's another way you can do it, which works wonderfully with Autofocus. You can decide not to be so focused in your definition of what a "goal" is, and just write ALL of them down on your list, whether they are clear and SMART, or not.
