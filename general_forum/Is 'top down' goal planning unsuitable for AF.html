Leon:

My own feeling is that there's nothing incompatible between AF and top-down planning. Indeed where you've been given the goals by others then you have little choice in the matter. However I think that AF will tend to favour the goals which you really identify with. The trouble with top-down planning - in my experience at least - is that you often tend to give yourself a lot of goals which you think you "ought" to have, rather than the ones you really want.

Each year I make an annual plan that runs from June to May. It is based on Ditzler's book Your Best Year Yet! I can't see any reason why I would not do the same thing this year, even though I am doing AF.

I will spend the last week of May creating the plan. Then, once a week, there will be a reminder in my calendar to review the plan. I delete the item from my calendar and put it in my AF list.

My personal experience has been that any effective bottoms-up system is much more important than a top-down system. In other words, I reaped many more gains by getting organized at the level of tasks and actions than I did by developing 1-year or 5-year or longer plans.

Covey has you start at the top--missions, values, etc.--and work your way down to tasks. It makes sense, but it didn't work for me any many others.

But . . . after I was able to get myself well-organized at the task level for a year or two, I started to crave higher-level planning. Then I welcomed the ideas of Covey, Ditzler, Lakein, et. al.

Some time in May, I will enter onto my AF list "Annual Plan."

I found Jacqueline's original post on Goal Orientated Autofocus very thought provoking and think that it is natural to start AF (or any TM system for that matter) from where we are, i.e. with the goals, projects, targets etc that we have in place. However, in using AF, it then seems that AF sifts those goals/projects/targets to identify whether they genuinely are the "right" goals/projects/targets for us. I think we need to be aware that AF will identify any inconsistencies between any goals we enter into the system and our genuine values and beliefs, and also cause us to recognise and perhaps act on them differently, according to whether they are "ours", imposed by others, or "oughts".

I am finding that AF is causing me to look at the way in which I define my goals etc. and that will in the long term impact upon what those goals are. It seems to me that we start with "Goal Orientated Autofocus" and graduate in a way to "AutoFocus Orientated Goals". It would make sense to me that, once Autofocus enables us to identify what Mark refers to as the goals we really identify with (from a bottom up approach), then the planning of them can then be actioned in a top down planning session in the way moises describes.

I'd like to add that there are situations where you absolutely need to plan your larger goals at least a year in advance. One such situation is when you have to plan your holidays for the whole year at the beginning of the year as mandated by your employer and you have to coordinate them with your colleagues on one side and your family on the other.
If you want to do some advanced professional training (sorry, don't know the correct English expression for what I mean), or are planning to do the final exams of your part-time studies this year, it's probably not the right year for that trip to Nepal.
Sometimes you might even need to plan several years in advance. Say, for example, you want to build a house in the next two years.

Of course, this doesn't explain how you identify which long-range goals to follow in the first place.

In other words, planning the next year to some detail is an absolute necessity, IMO, while your long-range plans might come from anywhere, be it Lakein or Covey or intuition, as long as you really want it. Drawing lessons from the past, like Mark describes, will be helpful, but not always sufficient. You might not have enough basis for conclusions, especially if you want to open up new horizons.

Personally, I have always struggled with planning horizons between pipe dreams and planning for the next six months. Every planning perspective longer than half a year has always overwhelmed me, and not doing said 12-months-or-longer planning has done severe damage to my life in the past.

Oh, and I agree that 10 major goals for a year is way too many.

For me top-down goals are best articulated as what I call "process" goals.

I put in a full year with Best Year Yet in 2007 and though it was very beneficial, I found that a number of goals became stale towards the end of the year. I do not feel inclined to follow this course again.

However, for many years now, my wife and I have sat down at the end of the year and reviewed the past year and set down "dreams" for the following year. We call them dreams in order to avoid any feeling of compulsion about them. But I have found that it is better to set in place "processes" that will assist a proper balance in my life.

eg FITNESS: Join the gym and visit maybe 100 times for the year. (I began doing weight-lifting but finished up doing aerobics, yoga and swimming -- You see, the process led to a evolution in the activity)

eg TECHNOLOGY: Attend the local community centre weekly for the year (I started doing Photoshop but then followed on with numerous other skills that the chosen teacher was adept at - doing many wonderful things I had not specifically planned.)

eg. SPIRITUALITY: Choose a "spiritual companion" and visit her for an hour a month. (A wonderful way to encourage me to be "on a path" without knowing where specifically it may lead.

eg LIFE-PLANNING: Experiment with Autofocus for 2009.

What all these "goals" have in common is that they involve a commitment to a process and the outcomes are left open.

This approach gives me an ideal basic structure for the time span of a year. The goals are very easy to keep at the forefront of my mind. They stimulate growth but they avoid most of the dangers of being "over-prescriptive".
