Jacqueline:

With things like writing books my own method has always been the fixed time method. However only the starting time is fixed. My aim is to write "something" each day. Some days that something will be 5 minutes (or less) but most days it will be a lot more. But as long as I've done something, I've succeeded in my daily goal.

This works for other things like exercise, where the thought of doing a long session may be too much for one's willpower, but once one's got started often one doesn't want to stop.

For example, when I ask myself WHY I want to accomplish a certain goal (and I actually write this underneath my electronic goal on my AF list), it helps enormously. Maybe instead of or in addition to adding your writing or exercise task, write numerous reasons why you want to achieve the goal on your list or add the task, "Review reasons to achieve" or something of that nature. Mel:

<< I also agree with Mark about having a daily writing goal based on the deadline. >>

That wasn't quite what I meant (though you can certainly do it that way). If you keep track of the number of words per day needed in order to meet the deadline, it helps to keep you motivated because it decreases if you do more than that number and increases if you do less. So your goal becomes to decrease the average number.

Example:

10 days to go and 10,000 words to write.
Average number of words needed: 1,000 per day

Today I write 2,000 words
9 days to go and 8,000 words to write
Average number of words needed pd: 889

I have decreased the number of words needed per day from 1,000 to 889. This is motivating because I can see the task get easier and easier the more I write.

The next day I don't write anything
8 days to go and 8,000 words to write
Average number of words needed pd: 1,000

Again this is motivating because I can immediately see the effect of my neglect on the target. I've lost my gains from the previous day. It's not a disaster but I can see that I need to pick my ideas up.

2009-07-14 17:47
Learning, the CARVER kind of assessment is something I picked up from "Unleash the Warrior Within", but I like it's applicability to projects in general. I used to use something like it at work to evaluate what kind of improvements to make in our systems that would give the biggest bang for the buck. Here's a description.

http://www.solien.com/content/articles/Project_Management_Warfare_v1.0.htm

I did it like this (1-5 grading) with my goals/projects on the rows and factors on the columns:

Critical - Does the goal move me towards my general life goals (or values) or away?
Accessible - is the goal even possible to achieve (given time / resource / $ constraints)?
Return - How great is the return on your investment of time?
Vulnerability - projects that can be totally finished quickly or with less $ get a high score
Effect - what kind of effect will it have in my life / others' lives?
Recognizable - is the goal and the steps to get there clear? (high marks if so)

And then, after all that analysis, flip a coin and go with your gut! :-)

The tension in limited overs cricket comes from tracking the average number of runs the second team to bat needs to score each over. The batting side needs to force the average down initially, as it will rise when their weaker players come in later. If they can get it down to zero, they win. Otherwise, they lose.

Without this measure, cricket would be a tedious and incomprehensible background to a rambling discussion of cake.
