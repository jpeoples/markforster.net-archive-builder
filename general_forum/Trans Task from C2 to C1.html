I think you are breaking the rules if you work on a Column 2 task but don't finish it, and re-enter it into Column 1 on the last page. But there are other ways of handling the situation that don't break the rules, and keep fully within the spirit of the rules as well.

Let's say I have a Column 2 task called "Project X". Maybe since the time I started working on that project, my priorities have changed (new project, new disruption of some kind, etc.) and I just don't have time for Project X right now.

Following the rules, I still MUST take SOME action on Project X before leaving the page (since it's in Column 2), and I MUST copy it forward to Column 2 of the next page if it's not finished. However, the rules allow me to re-enter the task however I want, whenever I take action and move the task forward.

So, here is how I handle it. My "action" for "Project X" might be to take a quick look at the Project X files, or something like that. After this quick action, I might re-enter it in Column 2 of the next page as "Wrap up Project X for now".

When I come to THAT task, I wrap it up for now, which is COMPLETED, thus allowing me to escape from Column 2. Then I can re-enter the task as "Project X" in Column 1 of the last page.

Reply by Don R

jens,

Pending is another matter. I think when you have done eveything for now that you can, and are now waiting for something, you cross it off and it goes in column 1. Let's say your project is to upgrade your computer. After you research your options and place your order, you're waiting for the shipment to arrive. So now you have a new column 1 action of waiting for computer to arrive.

If you're talking about a supervisor telling you to drop a project then I guess it is done. No point in working on it anymore. Cross it off because it's done. You could put a column 1 item to ask about the project later. Or just trust that the subject will come up again if it's important.

If it's just low priority, I think you could still keep working on it little and often. Since you write it on the next page, it shouldn't cause a huge issue. I find it's nice to make progress on low priority tasks this way.

Remember what it says at the beginning of the SF rules: it balances urgent and non-urgent, and it makes you finish what you start.
