Janis - I'll give this a shot.

Yesterday, I had the following tasks on "notice for review":

* read "x" book
* contact "x" client
* remix song
* laundry
and about 5-6 more items as well.

As the day (yesterday) progressed, I made some movement on some of the tasks, but still, by this AM, the following tasks remained unactioned, and hence, placed on review:

* read "x"
* remix song

And though frustrated that I didn't take action on these items, and even experienced a tinge of motivation to work on them, I followed the rules and put 'em up for review.

At some point (today, tomorrow, next week), I'm going to come across "review tasks" on my autofocus list. If the mood strikes (read: guilt, fear, etc), I'll look at my "tasks for review". For this example, let's assume that:

* read "x"
* remix song

are the only two tasks being reviewed. I look at the first task "read x" and ask: Do I want to read x. I don't. Not now anyway, so it remains as a review item. However I do need to remix that song. And yet, I ignored this task for over two weeks. So I ask myself: What would I need to do to make "remix song" more attractive to do? And then the brainstorming begins. Once that's complete, I reenter the task onto my list (reworded to increase motivation). For example, I may renter the task as:

*open track, and listen for new ideas

And that's it. Hope that's helpful.

You can find quite a bit about mind-mapping on the web. It can be done on paper and there are also a large number of computer programs to facilitate the process. Basically, you start with a circle in which you write the main idea you are mapping and then draw branches out from there with ever smaller and more precise concepts on the branches as you get further from the circle. In this instance, my middle circle says Someday/Maybe and the lines drawn from there are Family, Fitness, Finances, Friends/Fun, and Stuff. Then from each of those are lines drawn out with ideas of things I would like to do or accomplish in each category. From there additional lines might have the next action required to make it happen.
It just helps me to keep my someday/maybe list more organized and useful.

If you have the time or the inclination, it is well worth reading one of the many books the Buzans have written on mind-mapping. As with AF, they have some simple but specific and important rules (that go beyond the bones Alan mentions). The brothers Buzan don't agree on the importance of these though :). For example, we are advised to use colour, images, lists, curving lines not sraight, a limited number of branches etc. I can't explain here why these are important, but they are. They have sections written on what is NOT a mindmap too, to make sure you're really harnessing it properly. My favourite book on it is 'The Mind Map Book'. Very comprehensive. I have been referring to it for years and do not attempt an essay without going back to it. One mindmap I did for an essay, I really liked the look of so I stuck it on my kitchen wall. I was later invited to submit that essay in a competition, which I won. I find mind-mapping as powerful to me as the shield Haephestus made was to Achilles :P. Sorry for random obtuseness :)
