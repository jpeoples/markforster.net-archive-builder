Hi Jacqueline

This is where I would use separate lists in OneNote (but whatever system suits). For me all follow up or pending email gets filed in a separate email folder just called AutoFocus and referenced for action within AF but given the volume you describe I would have a separate section in OneNote with a tab (separate list) for each person/department and list the items as a follow up reminder system.

Personally I would populate those lists from AF and add a regular task to "action follow-ups" . So, if I sent an email to Fred requiring a response I would add a task "add dd/mm email to Fred to follow up list" Adding the note to the list would enable the task to be crossed of my AF list. When I hit the recurring item "check follow up lists" I would then cross off anything on those lists already actioned and follow up whichever were appropriate. In effect those lists would be my "Follow Up" Project Management System, enabling me to see status on any given person or department without trawling through many AF pages.

The standard way of following up with AF is to put "Jim called back?" or "Jane replied?" or whatever. As you scan the list, you can get a good idea of what follow-up has been done or needs doing.

Another method which was suggested on this forum is that every time you send an email which requires a response you put a code such as [w] (for "waiting") in the email and write a rule which sends a copy to a special "Waiting" folder. You can also write a rule that brings up the reply to your attention. I find this works extremely well. By looking at the folder on a regular basis you can see which emails are still awaiting replies.

I put all my emails into related project folders (related to a project) or reference folders (everything else that needs to be kept).

If the email requires some action, then I apply a red-colored flag before I move it to the folder.

If the email requires follow-up, then I apply a blue-colored flag before I move it.

I already check most of these project folders regularly (i.e., the project is already entered into AF) and clear out all the flagged items (taking action on reds, initiating follow-up on blues).

If I'm not sure that the folder is already in AF, then I'll add "Check Project X" or "Check Reference Y" to AF.

After I take action, I remove the flag.

Works great. It keeps everything where it's supposed to be. And it organizes action by project, not by whether or not I need to follow up, etc. I.e., I am clearing all emails related to Project A at one time, rather than doing all my follow-ups, regardless of project, at one time.

The only thing this process requires is email folders, and some kind of label or flagging functionality. You can do it in Gmail, too, with the optional star feature (many kinds of stars).
