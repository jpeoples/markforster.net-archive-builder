Measuring effectiveness of AF, DIT and other time-management systems

Author: Jaroslav | Created: 2009-04-5 22:47 | Last Activity: 2009-04-8 22:48

Original Post

2009-04-5 22:47
There have been a lost of posts in various threads lately about possibly combining aspects of AF and DIT, with others countering that either AF or DIT suits them better, or even makes them "more productive". This has led me to think about how one might actually evaluate or even quantify how well a particular system works, either in general, or for a particular person.

In a work context you could perhaps imagine a scenario where as a manager you insist that your group uses a particular system, and then over say a three month period quantify the productivity of the group by some standard measure (personally in practice I don't think this would actually tell you much apart from producing a meaningless number, but that is a different story). Anyway, this approach is inappropriate for those of us who have sufficient control over our lives to choose our own time-management system.

As Mark Foster has so lucidly stated, there is only a certain amount of work we can do in a day, so by changing time-management systems, we are primarily changing which tasks we choose to do when, and which perhaps we choose never to do. Afterwards, how do we then evaluate that over a given period we have made more or less the "right" choices for us at the time? We might feel more in control, or more productive, but in fact still be spending too much time on the "wrong" tasks.

Any ideas about how to think about this?

Hi Jaroslav

I think a good way of measuring productivity would be based on an analysis of procrastination vs necessary tasks. For example, if I define "work" as any necessary task, whether it be checking email, a significant work project, laundry, tax return etc, i.e. a task that under any system has to be done, and say assume a working day to be 10 hours (ie to allow for home tasks as well as work tasks) then an analysis of how much of that 10 hour day is spent on necessary tasks will give a good indication.

If for example, I was just "busy" for those 10 hours, and, when I had decided to do project work, procrastinated by doing the laundry for 1 hour, checking email for 1 hour and watched TV because I was bored for 2 hours, at the end of the day I would "feel" I had wasted 4 hours and only "worked" for 6. In fact I would have had 80% time productivity because 2 of those procrastinating hours were actually spent on necessary tasks, but just not the tasks that I felt I "ought" to be doing. (In fact actual productivity would probably be a little less than 80% because of the boredom and guilt factors causing a slowing down in the work).

With AF I would be more likely to work effectively on tasks from my lists. If working on necessary tasks equates to productivity there is a strong likelihood that my productivity will be closer to 100%.

The question as to whether they are the "wrong" tasks should actually be addressed by AF. If an item is necessary it needs to be done. Doing that item is productivity. We will only be non-productive if we are doing unnecessary tasks and AF is designed to ensure that we do not do unnecessary tasks by means of the dismissal procedure. The concept of "wrong" seems more to be tied to a perception of importance. Doing the laundry is as necessary as doing a tax return. Watching TV is generally not. TV watching is fine - at the right time but not as a procrastinator.

Thanks for all the helpful ideas.

I certainly would agree with Canta and Mark Foster that the real test has to be in terms of the goals that you accomplish over a given period, with the same amount of effort. That period does need to be longer than a day, my guess would be an absolute minimum of a month, and realistically three or more months. So, to compare systems you would need to set up two fairly similar sets of goals, work at them for three months in turn and then evaluate to what extent you have met them. I presume that this is what Mark Foster did at least informally when he was developing AF, and came to the conclusion that AF led to him achieving a higher proportion of his goals with the same effort, or the same proportion of goals with less effort. I wonder if anyone else has tried this kind of experiment?

I think however the situation is a little more complicated in that most of us are pretty bad at having a clear set of consistent goals. In Canta's analogy, we don't want to reach one destination, but three or four simultaneously at opposite ends of the country. Whilst this won't be solved by a time management system, I did find that Mark's books, and my subsequent implementation of a DIT like system made it much clearer that it simply wasn't possible to achieve all my desired goals given the number of hours in the day, and I had to cut down on my commitments and goals. I think Mark writes about this very clearly in his book, effectively explaining how there is feedback from the DIT system back into your overall goals. In Canta's analogy, many of us would probably accept a different destination in the price of the train was a lot cheaper, or the journey a lot quicker. I find this feedback more difficult to obtain in AF. Certainly there is the dismissal process, but this works at the individual task level and at the moment I find its not showing me which overall goals right now might be unrealistic and should perhaps be dropped. And, if I do drop such a goal, how does that fit into AF, given Mark's repeated advice that we should enter all possible tasks that crop up?

Jaroslav:

Actually what I recommend with AF is that you should not start off with a list of goals, but should let them emerge from working the system. In other words you let the system take you where it will (bearing in mind that the system is only bringing out what is already inside you). Now obviously this is going to work very differently in different situations. Some of us don't have much control over what our work goals are - they are imposed on us.

But basically the answer to your question about dropping goals is that you don't need to drop a goal. The system will just stop working on that goal.
