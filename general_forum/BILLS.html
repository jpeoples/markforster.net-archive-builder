Steve

Options that may help:-

1) When the bill comes in add the payment date to your schedule. Add a task to "check schedule" on every page. That should fix the dates in your mind each time you check it.

2) Try adding a task "review bill payment system" and when that stands out look at how you deal with bills. Look at things such as using Direct Debits, internet payment etc.

3) Be aware that AF identifies reasons for resistance. If the task has not "stood out" is there a reason that you are resisting paying that particular bill?

It works for recurring items such as "check email" or "check schedule" that you need or wish to do on a regular basis. I have "check email" on every page, and sometimes more than once if I have actioned it and feel a need to readd it, perhaps owing to a larger than normal build up. 2009-02-24 13:21
For the last number of years, I have prepaid my monthly bills. I set up an automatic payment for the year so that I stay roughly one month ahead (with one off seasonal adjustments for heat etc.). There's a wonderful psychological feeling I get when I open up the bill and it says CR bal - DO NOT PAY in big bold letters. If I did have a balance on it, I would write it on the AF list with a due date (about 5 days before it's *really* due) - sometime in the next 2 weeks or so you'll surely see it. I also get those bills that I can paid off directly to my Visa (I get points!) so there's less bills that I have to worry about. The big Visa and Mastercard always get paid off in full well online prior to the due date, and I write the date that it's due on my AF list. I also have an automatic payment going through for my Visa after the bill goes out, but before it's due of approximately the average amount I spend on a low-purchasing month. That way I could never miss a minimum payment. And when the payment is due, often I either don't owe anything on that previous bill or it's a fairly small amount.

From a strict financial viewpoint, the above doesn't make any sense because someone else is using your money for a period of time. But it's like keeping a large balance in my checking account that doesn't earn much interest, the psychological effect of knowing I could be on holiday or really busy at work and not have to worry about this stuff is worth the $10/year in foregone interest I could be earning on that money.

Six months ago, I accidentally double paid my Visa by a very large amount - a hardwood flooring purchase. I had an automatic postponed payment going through my postdated list through my bank which I had forgotten about and written in my calendar to pay the visa on a date 5 days before it was due. I was so paranoid about paying it off that I paid it twice. Which is especially embarrassing since I'm an accountant by training. :-(
