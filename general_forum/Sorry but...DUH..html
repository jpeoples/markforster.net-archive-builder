Actually there are quite a lot of differences. For example to-do lists are open lists, whereas AF is a series of closed lists, and that is absolutely key to how it works. Moreover with a to-do list you are committed to trying to do everything on it, which is one reason why such lists are problematic. One thing AF does, in surprisingly subtle ways, is effortlessly scaffold reviews of exactly what it is you need to do next in relation to any action, and helping you determine whether this is something you really want or need to be doing at all.

Calling it a to-list is therefore inaccurate: it's not one list, and it is not a list of things you are committed to doing. It's more like a series of lists of candidate actions. Moreover it is more than a list, whatever name you give to it; it's a process of working with a series of closed lists.

I wonder if those who are trying to be so dismissive of the system have actually tried it, carefully following the original instructions? It's the structure of the lists and the process of working with them that many are finding so powerful, and nothing at all like to-do lists.

To Do List - List of things to do

Must Do List - List of things that must be done

Will Do List - List of things you commit to doing

Someday/Maybe List - List of things you may do someday

AF List(s) - A series of lists that can include all of the above and more (reminders, ideas, thoughts, dates to record etc etc etc)

Apples and oranges are both fruits but are very different creatures!

AF may include elements of a To Do list, it may include elements of a Will Do or Someday/Maybe List but it is none of those things and all of those things. It includes items that may be at home in one list or another without differentiation.

The similarity is that items are placed into lists. The difference is in how those lists are used.

hey, i'm going to probably drop participation from now on, but i just want to point out why both the top comment on this topic and my own (and perhaps other critical comments that i haven't seen) maybe should be taken into account a little more. granted, maybe the top comment here wasn't terribly constructive. but blowing it off as rooted in ignorance, i think, amounts to some arrogance on the part of those who consider it worthwhile to defend the system. some points to illustrate:

1. whenever people say 'this system has nothing useful to offer me,' the answer is generally, 'go back and read the directions.' this is a hand-waving answer.

2. if the answer gets more sophisticated, it is to the effect if isaac's, which makes distinctions between 'open' and 'closed' lists, and whether or not the list carries the obligation to complete everything on it. this is a defense against a straw man: these are very specific, idiosyncratic criteria that have been pasted onto the everyday conception of a to-do list, which is much more generic. what i call a to-do list is generally a set of reminders of things to do so i don't forget; that's about the long and short of it. and i think that's a pretty common definition.

Why point this out at all? Because I think there is something a bit disingenuous in the premise of the 'system': that its rules ('scaffolded reviews') are a useful way to get in touch with the intuitive side of choosing a task. What I read from the directions (which I have read carefully, and implemented) is an attempt to systematize something that is much better left outside the system - call it 'intuition.' We make systems when we want rules to help us structure things. When the rules say 'pick the thing on the list that jumps out at you,' that is pushing you back into a discretionary approach that would have existed whether or not you were trying to implement a system called 'autofocus.'

So, maybe instead of dismissing all of the critics as 'trolls' who didn't bother to think about it, consider the possibility that the subtlety you're aiming for in this is actually something that, by its very nature, cannot be systematized. It's like Lewis Carroll's dialogue between the Tortoise and Achilles: you want to systematize something that in its essence lies above the system you're talking about. In Carroll's case, the Tortoise pointed out that you can't represent the process of inference inside of an argument that relies on inference to be true. Similarly, for Autofocus, I don't really think you can formalize a step saying "Here's how to be intuitive," and make that the heart of a SYSTEM, i.e. an orderly set of rules and processes for handling work.

Finally, when taken from that perspective, maybe you can see why some people feel a little insulted when you blow them off and say they don't see your wonderful system for the "Rolls Royce" that you think it is.

wally

The principle of open and closed lists is described in a lot of detail in Mark's last book, Do It Tomorrow (DIT). Basically, the type of "to do" list you describe above, the type that causes you to "Be stressed out from having the list stay unchanged at the end of the day" is an example of an "open list". It is a list that is continually added to, subtracted from, amended and adjusted, but effectively has no end and therefore can never be completed. A closed list on the other hand is fixed and cannot be added to, only subtraced therefore from, therefore has the ability to be completed. For example, if (as recommended in DIT) you do a list of all the work that comes in today, to do tomorrow, that list cannot be added to after the end of the day.

From a purely practical perspective one type of list can never be completed whereas the other can. This understandably has a major psycholigical impact as the attempt to complete a standard "to do" list will carry all of the frustrations of a task that can never be achieved.

There's been a lot of good discussion here, but I'd like to address a point skeptic made, that (basically) intuition cannot be systematised.

Consider a triage station at a hospital's emergency department. While there are some sorts of rules as to who is dealt with first, I think it would be fair to say that there is a decent amount of intuition involved as well. This doesn't mean that the patients are not systematically dealt with, it just means that the system has to be designed in such a way that the intuition of the triage staff is part of the system.

I think it's similar with tasks and AF, ultimately it's our own responsibility to be productive members of society. I would see AF's role in this to provide a system that allows us to deal with our overload of commitments in such a way we get done what we want to get done, and decide not to do the rest. Or to put it another way, we can work out why we feel we "should" do certain things, why we haven't done them, and whether in fact we should do them at all.

So I guess what I'm saying is OK, the system is at it's heart, a method for scheduling ones attention. Looked at that way, it's not that groundbreaking. But I also bet that if we decided to do a simple queue of tasks instead (always do the oldest first), our resistance will kick in, and the system will fall down. Same thing would happen if we list everything and just pick tasks at random (just like a traditional todo list). The brilliance of AF is it provides a way of allocating our attention across a wide number of areas, but without so much system overhead that there is resistance to using it. And not to be disparaging to AF, but I believe the productivity results we've seen from AF are not inherently from AF itself, but from the fact that people have given attention to things that in some cases have not been given attention in literally years. In my opinion the most powerful part of AF is that.
