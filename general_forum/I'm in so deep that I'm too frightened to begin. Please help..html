I'm a doctoral student in the fifth year of my program. I'm slated to go on the job market in August, which means that paperwork must be turned to potential employers by June.

Around February, an event happened that sent me into a depression that lasted months. I've since been treated for that and have been feeling good for months.

However, my momentum never returned. I have two papers that I need to finish: The first should have been completed in August, and I kept telling my advisor: "One more week, just one more week." The irony is that it's almost finished. But now that it's so late, I'm scared to even start it.

What makes it even scarier is that I have yet *another* paper ahead that I must finish this spring. That paper should have been near completion by now, and here I am barely having begun it.

Long story short, I work best when I believe I'm ahead of the game. When I think I'm behind, I get frightened and just fall further behind. By this point, I'm so scared that I haven't been to the office in 2 months (grad students can work at home) and have been avoiding work email because I'm afraid to hear from my adviser. I know that he wants a draft of this paper delivered before he has anything more to say to me.

So I get up in the morning and want to work. But I'm so frightened that it's scary to even open up the document. I immediately start thinking of all the obstacles I don't even know about that still lie ahead, and I'm paralyzed. So afternoon comes, and I end up just playing games online or going shopping for supplies for my hobby. (I've read the Now Habit but somehow this fear keeps overtaking me.)

Has anyone here ever been in this deep, or in an otherwise similar situation. And Mark, have you ever had a client in this situation? If so, what helped?

Yes, I've coached loads of doctoral students with this sort of problem. It's very common!

The secret is to set yourself the goal of doing some work on it every day. "Some work" can mean as little as getting out a book or opening a file. If you make sure you do something however small every day without fail, your momentum will soon return.

Hi Rachel, I've had experience of academic projects and I know they can easily become a big dark cloud that looms overhead. I've also tried the Now Habit but couldn't get on with it as I did not find it very flexible for real life.

The best advice I could offer would be to 1: Use a timer and 2: write down (in your Task Diary) 'spend 15 mins (or other amount) on x project/work and then stop'. 3: Stop as soon as the timer goes. 4: Gradually re-build your tolerence/stamina over several weeks by increasing the session time and amount of sessions per day.

I guess you could ask 'what is the point in only doing 15 minutes? But 15 minutes is better than 0 minutes and the anxiety of having the project not move at all. Best of luck.

I think the replies above give a lot of useful advice.
My additional suggestions would be:

1. Stop beating yourself up so much and remember to ENJOY what you are doing. You might not get this chance again so try to relax a bit and make the most of it.

2. Get a friend to mentor you. They do not need to understand the academic content of what you are doing but you could get them to help you to identify a list of tasks that still need to be done. 3. Use the online games/shopping as rewards for when you've completed a task rather than as a distraction!

4. Take a break from the original paper. Work on the paper that is due in spring first and go back to the other one later.

You CAN do this and if you really want to then you will.

Hi Rachel,

Your situation is not at all unusual and struck a chord with me. As a university undergraduate I remember feeling so far behind I was as that I was barely able to leave my room and face the world and face friends and acquaintances let alone lecturers. The more I avoided thinking about it the more frightening the backlog looked.

The really good news is that starting to list individual steps in some detail (as Christine suggests) is immediately and intensely therapeutic. However long the 'real list' is it's less scary than the fear of the nebulous imaginary 'non-list' we tend to carry around in our heads all day long nagging at us.

One of the nice things about this new Autofocus system is that you can you add a really large amount of tasks (including open ended tasks) and benefit from the relief of geting them 'out of your head' (in the terminology of 'Geting Things Done') without the feeling of instant overwhelm you get from promising to yourself that you're going to do them all 'today' or in some other short timescale.

Autofocus also allows the unconscious mind time to review the tasks 'in the background' so that they seem clearer and more manageable when you come to review them in a subsequent pass through the list.

As I write I'm gathering up the myriad loose ends of a chaotic pre-Christmas scramble into an Autofocus list and immediately feeling much more calm and in control of the busy year ahead. By the way, I successfully passed the exams I was so panicked about as an undergraduate once I'd started methodically tackling the backlog piece by piece.

All the best,

I too have a paper waiting to complete. Even though it would complete a 4 year study, I struggle working on it, because it feels like 'jumping through hoops' and I resist efforting that has little real use.

The one tip I would offer I derived from Dryden's book on procrastination. Starting a task may cause you discomfort (even the very idea of starting the task, or the idea of discomfort), so this leads to procrastination. In this way, we almost think we'll get to the task until it feels there will be no discomfort. Implicitly you've created a rule that a certain condition must be true in order for you to work on a task. This rule can be the absence of discomfort, or fear, or some other condition.

Thing is, if you change the rule, you find you can work on the task. All you need to adopt is a new belief/rule. Even if you are fearful or overwhelmed, you still are physically able to work on the task. Even though you are late, you are still able to work on the task. All you need to do is to subtly change your 'working conditions'.

People don't give themselves credit for their ability to work on something in the presence of discomfort/fear/pain. And with projects like these, the discomfort is mostly associated or present while starting the task. Once you get into it, in most cases, the discomfort disappears.

This was a big a-ha for me....

one switch in your thinking and you do a ji-jutsu on self-created resistance. Moving around resistance is btw one of the best elements of Mark Forster's techniques..

Other techniques I've used:

1. This one usually works well and is fairly easy: Decide on a SMALL part of the project that I could do, and then get together all the supporting materials I would need to do it (books, files, laptop, whatever) and then take them to a favorite coffee shop or restaurant and do the work, there, while enjoying a nice drink or meal. :o) NOTE: this could ALSO be a 7-second task if the project is extremely difficult. (Usually the first 7 seconds "breaks the logjam" for me though.)

2. Another technique which I can count on is Cybernetic Transposition, but it takes more work than #1. In the full book, the technique I use when I am really stuck is the Super Achievement Clearing Process. In the free e-course, best one to use would be Lesson #9: An easy way to Resolve your Unconscious Conflicts .. To get the free course (I hope it's OK to post this link): (don't let the name put you off ... Stuart Lichtman's book rates among my top 10 most important "finds" in life):
http://www.anything-fast.com/?fid=brochureguru

3. This free mp3 on Focusing and working with "action blocks" has also been helpful to me: http://www.focusingresources.com/downloads/focusing_and_blocks.mp3

4. Still another technique I've used is Neil Fiore's getting into the "Flow State" recording. I like the version on my very old tape version of Quality Work, Guilt Free Play, but I think some version is also in his "The Now Habit." or his "Conquering Procrastination.") This only seems to work if the task isn't TOO aversive, for me!

5. Another technique which I've just found (and which I successfully used on a very difficult task the other day ...) is "Afformations" ... basically I just asked myself a question repeatedly, like, "Why will this task be easy for me?" or "Why will I be successful at completing this?" and then giving my brain a chance to come up with the answers! :o)

6. Yet another technique for literal "piles" of stuff that I have had difficulty dealing with is one I found on this site ... I think it was called "The Power of 3 * 3" and it has been very helpful ... kind of like Autofocus for physical piles of paper. :o) hey! maybe it would also be helpful in my getting my kitchen organized too !! (ie maybe it would be a helpful technique for Things, not just papers.)

Hope something in here is helpful for someone!
