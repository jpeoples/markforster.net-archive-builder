I just had a thought. I teach kids writing using a method based on the Institute for Excellence in Writing. I understand a lot of public schools use this type of approach now because it works beautifully. I am just wondering if this method wouldn't get a fiction writer off and running too. The principle is that rather than tell a child, "Write a creative story" which does little more than cause panicked convulsions and crying fits for most, you have the child read an excellent short story. You teach them methodically how to rewrite the story. In a wonderful bit of reverse psychology, you tell them all they have to do is literally copy the story changing a few defined things (e.g., they must use an -ly word, a because clause, etc.).

Time and time again I have found when I give the kids permission to just copy the story, they DON'T. They end up changing the characters and much of the plot, too. If I had asked them to do this from the get-go, it would have been a disaster. So all of this to say, I wonder if a fiction writer is stuck, if taking a section of a story they love and rewriting it and improving on it in a few ways (using a descriptive adjective, etc.) wouldn't get them into the groove of really writing their own story? I think it could very possibly take adult writers from "I can't" to "I can."

I got Phyllis A. Whitney's book out of the library - Guide to Fiction Writing and it's quite practical.
http://www.amazon.ca/Guide-Fiction-Writing-Phyllis-Whitney/dp/0871161575/ref=sr_1_1?ie=UTF8&s=books&qid=1249852984&sr=8-1

Here's my notes on the book (sorry if it's TMI):
- spend 2 months planning, 4 months writing, 2 months revision (she spends 3 hours/day, 6 days/week)
- section a novel notebook into: calendar, titles, plotting, characters, outline

- Calendar - daily log; # of manuscript pages and total words to date
- Titles - look at quotations
- Plotting - add to before and as you write; items needn't be connected; use free-association with a pencil; 40-70 pages for ideas to develop painlessly
- Characters - names, likes/dislikes, traits/types; they will come alive when you write
- Outline - plan/map; break down material (from plotting section) into rough chapters; decide where and when each character is introduced; break into scenes; when outlining, don't do any writing

The premise is that when you get 50 pages of notes and sections, etc., you won't get bogged down in the middle and you won't waste a bunch of time. After your outline and plotting sections get done, you should be just itching to actually write the thing. When my sister and I would write novels together when we were kids they would fizzle - of course we didn't know any better. I wish she were still here and we could write together again. :-(

Even if you don't care for his style of book, he is a master of pacing and language. Same thing with Stephen King's "On Writing" - but you get the feeling that both of these guys came out of the womb with a typewriter in their hands. Koontz breaks the process down better.

Others I have that I would recommend: "Scene and Structure" - Jack Bickham; Oakley Hall is similar. Anne Lamott's "bird by bird" is a classic.

Some good advice from Oakley Hall's "The Art and Craft of Novel Writing":

Henry Miller's Commandments:
1. Work on one thing at a time until finished.
2. Start no more new books, add no more new material to "Black Spring:.
3. Don't be nervous. Work calmly, joyously, recklessly on whatever is at hand.
4. Work according to Program and not according to mood. Stop at the appointed time!
5. When you can't *create* you can *work*.
6. Cement a little every day, rather than add new fertilizers.
7. Keep human! See people, go places, drink if you feel like it.
8. Don't be a draught-horse! Work with pleasure only.
9. Discard the Program when ou feel like it - but go back to it next day. Concentrate. Narrow down. Exclude.
10. Forget the books you want to write. Think only of the book you are writing.
11. Write first and always. Painting, music, friends, cinema, all these come afterwards.

Steinbeck's advice on writing:
"Don't start by trying to make the book chronological. Just take a period. Then try to remember it so clearly that you can see things: what colors and how warm or cold and how you got there. Then try to remember people. And then just tell what happened. It is important to tell what people looked like, how they walked, what they wore, what they are.
Put it all in. Don't try to organize it. And put in all the details you can remember. You will find that in a very short time things will begin to come back to you, you thought you had forgotten. Do it for very short periods at first but kind of think you aren't doing it.
Don't think back over what you have done. Don't think of literary form. Let it get out as it wants to. Overtell it in the matter of detail - cutting comes later. The form will develop in the telling. Don't make the telling follow a form."

Jacqueline, I understand struggling with blog posts. ;-) For some reason, I can post here and blab for hours, but when it comes time to blog post I am often stymied.

My personal opinion is that it does not matter and it also does. Here's why. It doesn't matter if someone has a system that works to make them more productive AND satisfied with that productivity level. One of the things I noticed was that despite being more productive with AF1, many weren't completely satisfied with it. Therein lies a very important factor ignored by most TM experts: belief.

As a psychologist, I could suggest that a client see a psychiatrist for a prescription that I thought would help them. But if that client was repelled by the very thought of taking a drug, any benefits to be gained will be to no avail. It's the same with TM. Even if dismissal rules have a the capacity to get you working in AF1, if the individual believes the rules hamper him, the system is going to break down and most likely s/he will stop using it.

In my practice, I explored with clients why they held the medication beliefs they did. Sometimes our discussion changed their mind. I think the tendency this board has of questioning tweaks is a very healthy one. We need to make sure that someone really, really will benefit from their tweak. Sometimes a patient no way, no how wants a drug and that belief needs to be honored. In the same way, someone who cannot abide by one or more of AF's "rules" should be honored. Most likely greater productivity and satisfaction will result.

If I'm correct, then why don't we just say to people, "Make a list and use your own rules"? Because there are basic principles of TM at work in AF that are effective for the majority. Just as we wouldn't trash a drug that cured most but had side effects for some, we don't scrap the AF rules that help so many. The second reason it matters is because so many of us have no idea where to start. I remember when I first found FLYLady. I literally did everything she did in the same order, even to the point of taking a bath at night. How ridiculous, right? No. I needed a place to start before determining what aspects didn't work for me (By the way, the bath was out immediately - lol).

In order to determine the "dose" of AF that worked for me, I needed to try the recommended dosage first. AF rules are the starting dose that most of us need. Yes, some of us are too quick to adjust our dosage. But I do believe that we'll find the right treatment for our time management woes and for the majority of us AF is the drug of choice.
