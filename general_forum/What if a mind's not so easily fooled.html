I find myself using two different 'strategies' to avoid the confrontation with dreaded tasks:

(1) Either I don't write them down on the list in the first place. This mostly happens with my home/personal list, where notably exactly the tasks I fear most are missing. And it's not because I didn't think of them. Instead, I have them in my head all the time. I just can't make me write them down. Unfortunately, most of those tasks are quite monolithic and don't easily submit to 'little and often'. Which is, btw, the prime reason they haven't been done before. And not doing them before has made matters worse, and helped to generate even more fear. Which constituted a vicious circle that seemingly even AF can't break.

(2) Or, if the list only contains 'ugly' tasks, I tend to avoid using the list at all, and run myself in 'off the top of my head' mode. This mostly happens to my work list, and this counter-productive behaviour is fostered at my workplace by lots of random interruptions and the kind of very short deadlines I've already written about several times. In addition, I have a hard time to find any 'fun' or easy items to make this list more 'attractive', due to it being an exclusive work list.

In a way, this reminds me of what Mark wrote in the first chapter of "Do It Tomorrow" about the mind 'fighting back' against the idea of doing what one is resisting most. It looks like my mind knows exactly what nasties are somewhere inside the list and either refuses to having them written down or just plainly rejects the whole list.

While I don't have any idea how to attack the first problem (1) - I managed to get some long-resisted tasks done by _somehow_ breaking them down into little-and-often chunks, but some others just aren't accessible that way -, I'm thinking about abandoning AF for work and returning to DIT, with the idea that clearly sizing down a day's workload might make the difference. Those tasks aren't *that* ugly, the sheer number of them might be the core problem.

Hi Alex,

I am no stranger to that feeling of resistance. I feel your pain.

One thing I love about AF is its flexibility. With a little creativity, you can break through your layers of resistance step-by-step, even if they are baby steps.

For example, you might add a task such as "write tasks related to project X" or "write down one ugly task". At some point that task will look much more appealing than the other ugly tasks on your list! :-)

Re: resisting actually opening the book. First, think why you are resisting. Perhaps you have a pressing deadline and you already know what you need to work on?

If you simply have an aversion to doing work, try forming a habit of idly browsing the AF book. No commitment to do anything. Just take those times that you would normally check email or make coffee and take the opportunity to read through your tasks. If you're really having trouble, take this opportunity to face your guilt/overwhelm. Confront each task, recognize that you can't do everything at once, and allow yourself to NOT do that task. With enough read-throughs you might just find a task that you do want to start with.

Also try actually writing down "work on x for fifteen minutes" on your AF list, even though 'x' is a task best done in a very long single stint.

Previously I'd write down "work on x report" without specifying the time requirement element, and even though I'd tell myself I could work on it 'little and often' to get it done, I wouldn't actually do ANYTHING, because my mind knew perfectly well this was a task that could only be done in one long stretch. (Notice, by the way, how procrastinors use prison metaphors to describe their tasks!)

But now, when I *see* "fifteen minutes" actually written down, somehow the task is diminished, as is my resistance to it.

And I'm finding that once I've actually done that fifteen minutes on 'x', the resistance has completely melted away, and I'm well on my way.

Hi Alex

Jacqueline's book recommendation is an excellent one.

I know what you mean about the resistance to writing certain items down - I have had a few of those and the reluctance to write them down can be more than just resistance but a real physical aversion. Another suggestion ........... take those items and write them down on a separate piece of paper, or an index card, then just put that list aside. Don't make any plans at all to look at that list, (but do remember where you put it!) Go back to your AF lists, knowing that there is nothing really nasty lurking there, and ensure that you have Thinking Time listed. When you action the Thinking Time task, just spend some time thinking about the ways in which AF has enabled you to identify reasons for resistance and the changes you have made as a result.

That will do three things - (1) it will increase your trust in the system, and in particular it's ability to handle resistance, (2) it will be setting a precedent to prepare you for handling future occurrences of resistance and (3) it will be working on finding a solution to the items you are not thinking about on that list.

In say a month's time, take that list out and look at it. If you feel like adding one or all of the items to the list do so. If not put it aside for another month and repeat.

The reasoning behind this suggestion is the fact that, instinctinctively, you probably have a pretty good idea as to the reasons for the resistance, or alternatively you believe that whatever the reasons are it is not something you are willing to face at this present time. That is ok and is an ok response - but only for a period of time. At some point those things need to be faced or dismissed for ever. If dismissal is not an option then they must be faced. AF's approach to resistance is totally non-threatening - we are not forced to "do" anything. Consequently, the stronger our trust in the system, the more willing we will be to let it loose on our real "nasties".

Mark and others,

However, the tips about breaking the tasks down or working on it a little are beside the point, unfortunately. The tasks I had in mind with problem (1) when I called them 'monolithic' basically *are* all-or-nothing tasks. Usually, they involve some confrontation with persons, instutions or situations that I'm not able to cope with. It's more a behaviour of hiding than one of procrastinating. Those confrontations are usually seen as humiliating, dangerous, painful or the like.

As an example:
(Please note that this is *not* a real-life example of my dreaded tasks, it just happens to have the same structure.)

Let's assume you're horrified by the thought of going to the dentist (more precisely: the treatment by the dentist, any dentist). So you just don't go. For years. Nothing on earth could make you go there (of course, the pain finally will, but let's leave this option out of the picture for the moment). You know you should, but you can't.

There's no way to really break down 'going to the dentist' into smaller parts. You could take some preparational action (like making an appointment), but IMO this doesn't count as really working on the task. And preparational actions are overshadowed by the same amount of fear like the real task.

Sometimes, tasks like these involve not knowing how to handle them because of delicate consequences for relationships with your family, your employer etc.

As another example:

You have an ongoing dispute with the personnel department about a rather large contribution payment. You have been turned down by someone from the personnel department who told you your claim had expired. You know for sure that it hasn't. So you have to write a letter to the head of said department. Unfortunately, this person is (a) powerful, (b) doesn't really like you (which is not your fault) and (c) ultimately, you would have to take this claim to court which would not be beneficial for your job even if you win. You cannot find a way to write that letter, even though you know that if you bring forward your claim politely, you'll probably succeed.

Hi Alex

The examples you give explain clearly the type of issues where it is an all or nothing approach. The "problem" as such is that it is not the item itself that is the problem but the associated fear factor.

Taking the dentist scenario as an example, you are right that any action taken (making the appointment etc) is so intrinsically linked to the end result that it is not a case where "just making the call" will progress the task - well actually it IS the case that it will progress the task but that is not what you want!

In cases such as the financial claim, again the issue in that type of scenario is not the task itself but the association - for example fear of conflict, or the fact that it raises other issue regarding finances.

If you suspect that some of your "bete noirs" have other attachments, and you can identify what they may be, then in addition to putting those tasks aside in the way I suggested, you could add a task to your lists to just look at those issues. By separating the root cause from the tasks themselves it becomes less threatening. For example a task to "look at methods to overcome fear of conflict" is far easier to do than "call the ogre who is going to shout at me".

A very good book that deals with worry (which we have to the nth degree over such issues!) is an old book by Dale Carnegie called How to Stop Worrying and Start Living. One of the tips is to think of the worst that can happen - it is invariably never as bad as we have somehow psyched ourselves up to think.
