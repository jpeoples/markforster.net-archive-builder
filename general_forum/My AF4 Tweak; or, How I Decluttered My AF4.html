INTRODUCTION: A little background story

(You can skip to THE TWEAK ITSELF section which is the next post below)

I think my situation is familiar to many AF users: AF lists chock-full of repetitious tasks than we have to do again and again on different days, or maintenance tasks as Erik would call them...especially house chores. They fill up our AF lists with stuff that will never be written off but will forever be crossed out then re-written in the end of the list, yet most of
them we cannot even do now, and yet vie attention with our other tasks. And the problem is that (at least with my observations in its use) AF4 rapidly breaks down in effectiveness when you go past 200 or so tasks. Of course we can switch to AF1 which, to my amazement when I was using it before, does not break down when you put lots more tasks into it, but (for me) it does not handle urgent items very well. Then there is AndreasE's "Ping Pong" AF1 tweak which tries to remedy that,

http://www.markforster.net/forum/post/988459

but, for some reason it didn't help. After using AF4 and then Ping Pong AF1 for a couple of weeks I switched back to AF1, even though I just do not like how it does not handle urgent matters, because of the latter's effectiveness in handling large volumes of tasks.

The breakthrough came when I watched Erik Lorraine's videos. I was intrigued by his Kanban time management scheme (TMS) and tried to emulate it, but since using those pesky Post-It tags would mean I have to discontinue using my fountain pen (BOOOO!)
and also since I found that filling up "energy/time blocks" is not as important for me (sorry if I mangled up your terminology, Erik; and also, please correct me if I misrepresent your thoughts/ideas later on) I just sticked to my own AF list.

Now, something Erik said in his third video really puzzled me. He already mentioned in his second video his observation/opinion that putting all of one's task reminders ("triggers") in a list could effectively cripple that person.

And yet in the third video he then says that he does not see his Kanban system as a list, although he cannot understand why, and I think it was in his fourth video that he says he puts all of his task reminders in his Kanban system because, well, it is not a list.

And yet for all appearances, Erik's Kanban system LOOKS like a list. It may have movable items, but it still is a collection of written items...hence a list. Now I was intrigued, maybe there was something I did not understand or pick up in Erik's first video, which explained his system in detail. So I watched it again a few times, and then, it clicked.

Most TMSs like AF and GTD use to-do checklists. Erik's Kanban system, on the other hand, is a collection of <>.

http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GdGaRUklIig

The main difference between a systems checklist and a to-do checklist is that the systems checklist has a predefined goal---maintain the system---while the to-do checklist has none. Therefore each item in a systems checklist is predefined and can be used again and again. A systems checklist lists components of a system to be checked to make sure that a system is working properly. For example, in the video above, a preflight checklist is done to be sure that the airplane is working properly before flight.

In Erik's Kanban scheme, he treats the maintenance part of his time management as system checklists, and these systems include Himself, His House, His Family, His Work. The Kanban scheme highlights those items in each system that need to be addressed so that the system will function properly.

Now I realized that handling maintenance tasks like this would be very effective in reducing the active items in an AF list because these repetitive tasks would be considered by the AF system only when they SHOULD be considered, not before. I then began thinking of a way of doing this efficiently, because I quickly realized a method in which I have to write each maintenance task from a list into my AF list would consume too much time.

How effective was the resulting tweak? My AF1 list immediately shrunk from around six or seven pages to just TWO, and therefore I shifted to AF4 which I felt was more effective in this situation, and I never looked back since.

THE TWEAK ITSELF

A. How I did it: Setup

1) My AF list is in a Rollabind notebook, so my Maintenance Checklists are in a separate section of my notebook. However, I think it is also quite feasible for users of fixed page notebooks to have their checklists at the BACK.

2) In my AF list, I marked each repetitive task to help me consider them better, then I considered the different systems that they may be in. I eventually got these categories:

Morning House Upkeep
Midmorning House Upkeep
Afternoon House Upkeep
Evening House Upkeep
Daily Unscheduled House Upkeep
Weekly/Occassional House Upkeep
Room Upkeep
Computer Upkeep
Mental/Social/Emotional Self Upkeep
Physical Self Upkeep
Professional Upkeep
Car Upkeep
Time Management Upkeep
Miscellaneous Upkeep

3) I wrote each category of tasks in a separate page in my Checklists section, and I also indicate the page number in the top right. The page numbers are important, as you will see.

4) I then crossed out the repetitive tasks from my AF list. And boy, they were A LOT!

5) Finally, I entered each category into my AF list, enclosing them in <> to distinguish them from other entries, and beside them the page number. Thus:

<1: Morning>
<2: Midmorning>
<3: Afternoon>
<4: Evening>
<5: Daily>
<6: Weekly/Occ'l>
<7: Room>
<8: Computer>
<9: MSE Self>
<10: Px Self>
<11: Prof'l>
<12: Car>
<13: TMS>
<14: Misc>

B. How I did it: In Practice

1) For reference, let us look first at a scan of one of my checklists:

http://i994.photobucket.com/albums/af63/nuntym/test/ScanImage002.jpg

and here are the meanings of each symbol beside each item:

http://i994.photobucket.com/albums/af63/nuntym/test/ScanImage001.jpg

(I apologize for my atrocious handwriting :blush:)

2) I have to prime each category before use. That is, I have to mark out with a ∙ each task that has to be done. I can do this in two ways. One is to set aside a time to mark through each category. Another, and one that I find I am doing more often, is to do the marking when you are processing a checklist, as detailed below.

3) I then process my AF4 list per the original rules.

4) Once the AF4 points out that I need to do a Maintenance Checklist, I look at the page number and go to that checklist. I then process it as I would a page in AF1, with the first pass an overview, the second pass to mark (∙) those that I need to do, mark with checks (√) what I have already done, and mark with crosses (×) what I had failed to do before but cannot do now, and the third pass picking out what I should do now from all the items by marking it with the first part of a check/cross (\).

5) Once I finished a task, I complete the check symbok (√) beside the item, and (if needed) write the date I need to do it again, then I remained in that checklist to see, using the AF1 rules, if I need to do something else from those marked with a ∙.

6) If I run out of space to mark a task, I just cross it out and rewrite it on the bottom.

7) Once I am satisfied that I finished all I want to do in that checklist, I go back to my AF list, cross out that Maintenance Checklist item, and rewrite it at the end.

And that's it! I hope this will help somebody.
