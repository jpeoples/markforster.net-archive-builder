I have a few questions regarding timeblocks, scheduling and DWM. I do a mix of scheduled work and creative work. I would love to be able to schedule my time and have it magically work out the way it does for Mick Cool in DIT, but I can't ever seem to make it work.

Example: Today I had a client call scheduled for 9am and I blocked off an hour. I called and got voicemail. Left a message reminding her of the meeting and then went to work on my DWM list. 20 minutes later, she returns the call and we talk for an hour (her phone lines were down due to an ice storm). Now my day is off by 20 minutes and I'm late starting the next project block on my schedule. I can't not take the call or cut off the client early when things like that happen, especially when the delay was weather related. I am a freelancer and client calls = money. I try to work with the client as long as it's reasonable to do so.

My second issue is with creative work. I would love to start off every day by doing only creative work for a few hours. I can block off this time (and reschedule as needed when I have to take a call). What I can't determine is if it makes more sense to block out time for creative work and then cherry pick the "creative" tasks from my DWM list, or keep those items on a separate list and refer only to that list when I'm working on creative stuff.

Same thing for client work. If I send an estimate to a client for 6 hours of work and he agrees, then I immediately block off the time on my schedule so it's booked. Should I then dump the tasks that need to be done for the project into my DWM list or keep them separate and refer to them when I get to the time blocks?

I think the reason I struggle is that often times I block out 3 hours to start the project only to find out that one hour into it I need to clarify something with the client so it gets put on hold. I can't "map out my day" because there are so many variables. Case in point...today we are having a snowstorm and my kids are home from school which changes my routine for the entire day.

On one hand, I like having things mapped out so I don't overbook, but on the other hand, my "map" can get blown out of the water the minute I step into my office. Even "buffering" between time blocks has failed on many occasions. Given the advantages of AF1-4 and DWM, etc. What is the best, most flexible approach to scheduling issues like these without the fear of overcommitment?

Hi Gretchen

I think you've raised some important points here and I'm sure you will get different opinions on what's best.

My way of handling project-type tasks that have fixed deadlines is to time-box them. I treat them as the 'Big Rocks' in my week that I really want or need to achieve. For example if I'm preparing a set of teaching material that must be delivered in three weeks and needs about 8-10 hours work, I break the task down into sub-tasks of about 2-3 hours and map them on my calendar over the next two weeks, to make sure I can complete the work on time. It reduces my anxiety level, just knowing that I have time to finish it!

If I get a competing request for a block of time I've set aside to work, it's much easier to say to others - sorry I'm not available, or at least (if it's something I can't avoid) look at my calendar and see where and how I can re-schedule my scheduled work. It helps me to see if I'm getting over-committed as well.

I only schedule 2 - 3 of these self-appointments a day and try to have plenty of unscheduled time for working my list as well. The key thing I find is not to over-book my week but leave plenty of buffer time, as delays and interruptions are inevitable. The downside, if any, is having to manage work which lives both in my calendar and my list but I don't find it a problem in practice.

I don't put these scheduled tasks on my AF list but on a project checklist. I keep my list for tasks that don't have a fixed time of the day associated with them and I work the list when I have no appointments.
