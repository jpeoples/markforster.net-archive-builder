Imagine you are walking down the street. You look frequently at things that are close to you because they may trip you or bite you. You look infrequently at those things that are far away because they mostly aren't as urgent as the close things. As long as you are looking around at all, the balance of where you look and what you choose to take action on is a natural one--it doesn't require conscious thought on your part to accomplish. If there's a crack in the sidewalk, you step over it. If the sidewalk is closed for repairs, you may change course from half a block away.

AF2 provides a structure for "looking around" in our lives. The entire list is reviewed at least once a day, and a subset of the list is reviewed before choosing each action to do. These reviews keep list items fresh enough in your mind to allow your intuition to make sensible priority decisions from moment to moment without strain.

Items about to be dismissed are usually not urgent, mission-critical items that absolutely must be done immediately. That's why they're at the end of your list, out of the way of better things to do. You read these on-notice items at least once a day, during your morning read-through of the entire list. More frequent review would be wasteful.

I know you are worried about dismissing something important. If you follow the rules, though, I think you will find that most things work out OK.

Remember: dismissing an item doesn't mean that you aren't allowed to do it anymore. You can always massage it back to life in a few days and give yourself another three weeks of procrastinating on it before you have to dismiss it again. :-)

I hope this helps.

David,

you asked whether there should be a rule that one should look at those tasks that "are on notice" for dismissal first and really see if something stands out before starting at the end of the list.

The more I think of it, the more I am inclined to say that I look at the item and decide depending on the urgency of each task that "is on notice" for dismissal (and not decide depending on the "standing out" nature of the item). It can't be "immediate", because if it was immediate it wouldn't be written on my list and I'd already working on it. If it's "same day" urgent or due within the next few days, I'd schedule it on my calendar to be done today or at least started today. And if it's "everything else" I'd ignore it and look at the next task that "is on notice" for dismissal. Depending on the result of this process I'll either follow the rules of AF 2 or ignore the rules until it's possible to return to normal procedure.

So, my personal answer is: Look at each of those items and decide depending on urgency, not on "standing out".

2009-07-6 15:02
Two thoughts:

1. David, I don't see anything in the orthodox AF2 rules that implies you can't put tasks on the back burner for a while, or that implies that dismissal means something isn't important. As long as "review dismissed tasks" is an item on your list, there is literally no danger of important-but-back-burnered being neglected.

2. I'm not trying to be argumentative, but in an important (slightly philosophical) sense, anyone who says they CAN'T trim down their workload is, technically, not speaking the truth. You always COULD choose to trim your workload, if you were willing to pay the price. The price might be letting someone down, or risking a poor job evaluation, or, if you wanted to trim your workload down to zero, poverty. I'm not suggesting you ought to pay any of these prices, obviously that's none of my business. But AF is (almost uniquely among productivity systems) VERY GOOD at denying the user the opportunity to fool themselves that they can do more than they can really do. And the dismissal phase, it seems to me, is the part where that becomes most clear. So if you're committed to more than you can really manage, AF will keep reminding you of this, day in day out -- whereas many suggested tweaks to the system have the effect of smothering those reminders.

When you're in that situation you have three choices, logically speaking: a) reduce your workload; b) suffer the discomfort of an AF system that feels ill-suited to your needs because it's reminding you every day that you're overstretching yourself; c) develop some tweaks to reduce the discomfort without reducing your commitments. Option c) may be a perfectly justifiable choice for busy and stressful times, but I do think it's worth admitting that it is, in a way, a self-deluding one.

In using my old AF list to transfer to AF2, I've found that I have very few items at the front of the list that are "on notice." I mark those with a post-it flag (usually one of those sign here arrows) & since they are usually repetitive items (shred old files for example), as well as close to a month old, I choose to do those items first before working my list from back to front. As I've stated before, I also put flags on other items that stand out as I go through my daily review. This not only keeps me focused without having to search for things, but it also makes sure that I do what is urgent and get those items out of the way.

In AF1, I found that working front to back caused me to not want to put items on the list at all (b/c I wouldn't make it to the end of the list), but with AF2, I'm willing to add a lot more items and getting a lot more accomplished! My desk hasn't looked this neat in months! :) On Friday I added ~100 new items and crossed off about 1/2 of those as well as quite a few older items.

For me, daily review is key to the whole success of AF2 to keep those urgent and about to be dismissed items in front of me without them being forgotten. Everyone has their tweaks & way they work the system. Some use different colors, some use flags, some use calenders & ticklers. Finding the way that best works for you to get everything done is key.
