Alan's fantastic description of a paper-based "AutoDIT" ( http://www.markforster.net/forum/post/1686045#post1687131 ) made me see similarities to a system I had tried before but couldn't get to work:

Namely, a DIT system in which unfinished TODAY pages simply became a backlog of AF1-style pages. If nothing on TODAY's page stood out, you'd cycle through this backlog of AF1 pages, following AF1 rules, till you come back to the TODAY page. Or something along those lines. (I'll refer to this as "DIT+AF1_backlog").

I think at least a dozen of us have mentioned trying to get that kind of system to work -- including Mark Forster -- but we all kept running into various problems.

So, I was trying to think, what makes "AutoDIT" different?

The only feature I see that's different is the "NEW" page -- which might more properly be called the "MISC" or "CATCH-ALL" page.

This is the page that we use for stuff that we don't know what to do with. We don't want to commit to doing it today or tomorrow, and it doesn't really make sense to assign an arbitrary future date to the item. So it goes on the CATCH-ALL page.

DIT+AF1_backlog didn't have anything like that -- urgent items went on the TODAY page (below "the line"), future items with a specific "I can start now" date would go on a future diary page, and everything else would go on TOMORROW. Thus, everything would carry an implicit commitment to take action on a certain date.

The CATCH-ALL page gives you the means to avoid that implicit commitment when such a commitment isn't appropriate. Maybe that's why AutoDIT works better than DIT+AF1_backlog?

Any thoughts?
