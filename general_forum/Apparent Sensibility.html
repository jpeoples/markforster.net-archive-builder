AF4R, as Mark practices it, seems to me to have a quality not seen since Do It Tomorrow: It feels natural. The point being, I don't feel silly explaining what I'm doing and why.

The other systems appeal to time management junkies who examine a system, try it, and learn whether it works. But few systems can be explained quickly, in every day language, and appeal to people who say, "why not just write things down and do them?"

Other systems, I don't think you can begin to explain without having to immediately discuss "weird" stuff like:
- rewrite at the end
- go through the list in this fashion
- enter everything for next month, then do some today.

=================
But for these three, the essence is easy to communicate:

Do It Tomorrow. You're busy today, so rather than let new stuff interrupt you, note them for tomorrow to work on.

Gerry's Ultra Simple Guide. Take notes, and highlight things that need doing.

AF4R: Keep 3 lists and scan through them regularly:
a) Things you might work on, and
b) Things you do regularly, and
c) Things you are working on. Try to get these done.
