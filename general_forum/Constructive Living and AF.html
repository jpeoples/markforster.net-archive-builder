Just some opening comments. The _Handbook for Constructive Living_ is his best work to date.

Rainer, regarding reality, this is a difficult subject, but the nuances are not too appropriate for the practice.

If you would like to discuss this in depth I would love to. It would be a long conversation about the lack of sophistication of Eastern thought when dealing ontology and the folly of most Western thought regarding the same.

But in short, to think in terms of subjectivity or perception as a starting point is a non-starter although that is how most Westerners would approach the nature of "reality". That is not to say that such phenomena do not inform the world, since they are inherently part of it, but they are derivative states which rest upon a more primordial relationship of the world which resists reduction.

But I think going down this route would probably be unfruitful for getting at what Reynolds has to offer although this understanding of the underpinnings does inform my practice of both AF and CL. Which might become a bit obvious in the discussion I am having with Alan.

In short, Reynolds lacks a sophisticated definition of such ideas, partly due to the prejudices of his own tradition coupled with the near absence of it in the traditions he is attempting to bring to the West. Thankfully such a thorough investigation into such matters has a small return on your investment.

Reynolds is attempting to use this non-reductionist view of the world (which includes you and has a rich and non-trasparent relationship with each other most of the time).

"Reality" becomes can be what you understand subjectively, but if you diligently practice CL, you might find yourself doing your part in world in less than transparent manner (for the most part you already are.)

What fun to see CL referenced here. I've been a devotee of Reynolds' "water" books and the Handbook of CL. (Many of his books are available for reading online from his web site.)

One of the CL mottos is "Accept your feelings, know your purpose, do what needs to be done." Rather similar to the advice "eat when hungry, sleep when tired." I believe that would be Reynolds' definition of reality. He cites the founder of Morita therapy (one of the two therapies that form the basis for CL) as monitoring his temperature when he was sick. He would do physical work till his temperature reached a certain point, then do non-physical work until it climbed to a specific mark, and then would simply rest when it reached a higher mark. At each point, he was using reality as input for his decisions and actions.

(I think about Mark Forster's oft-related measurements for his weight, web pages, timed bursts, etc, as being similar reality measurements.)

I suppose the AF list could be construed as a reality-meter of sorts -- these are the tasks I have promised to do. How many have I done or can I do? Do I feel frightened or shy about doing any of them? Does it need doing? If so, then I do the task feeling frightened or shy about it, because reality tells me it needs to be done. If it doesn't need doing, then I don't do it.

That's a VERY simplistic interpretation of AF through a CL lens, and do NOT decide CL's merits based on this explanation. It's just the best I could do in this off-the-cuff response.

Insofar as dealing with reality and chaos, I think Reynolds hypothesizes you're on your way to an important interview, and then you get a flat tire, and then a policeman tickets you for out of date inspection stickers, etc. At each point, reality is handing you a challenge, and you have to decide what is your purpose in each moment (which in this hypothetical is constantly changing--get to the interview, change the tire, don't yell at the policeman :), accept your feelings, and do what you need to do.

Thanks for reminding me of CL and Reynolds -- I enjoyed remembering that stuff.

2010-04-4 21:17
The full quote is helpful in better understanding this whole approach, so I thought I would post it here:

"One of the main tenets of Morita Therapy is that our internal experience (feelings and thoughts) is basically uncontrollable by our will. If we feel anxious about going for a job interview we can't necessarily make ourselves feel relaxed and confident. If we experience doing our income taxes as frustrating and tedious, we can't just snap our fingers and suddenly find the task satisfying and exciting. Most of the reasons for procrastination have to do with "internal barriers," like fear, anxiety, indecision, perfectionism, etc. I call these barriers the Demons of Inaction. Traditional therapies generally suggest that you must conquer such demons through various strategies such as insight, self-talk, motivation, or increased self-esteem. But Morita Therapy offers a set of tools that is less about conquering and more about co-existing with. Rather than vanquishing your anxiety about the job interview, you simply take your anxiety along for the ride. Western therapy suggests that we must exorcize these demons before we can take action. Morita therapy recommends that we accept their presence, as unpleasant as that may be, and move forward anyway. If you can learn to do this, the demons lose much of their power and many of the causes of our inaction naturally dissolve into constructive effort."
