I am enchanted with the new AF. However, there is one thing I don't like, and more than that, I don't understand why it should be so: The paradigm shift from the "let dismissed items in peace for some time and reenter them only after a week or longer"-rule in AF1-3 (and in the original AF1 rule set, there was put very much emphasis on this rule!!) to the new "dismiss and decide almost immediately what to do with the dismissed items, either re-enter (reworded) or delete"-rule of AF4.

I have to admit that in the beginning (AF1-era), I felt uneasy with dismissals for a very long time. It was only when I went through my old lists after months and had a look on what was highlighted, I suddenly realized that really most of what had been dismissed had been RIGHTFULLY dismissed - tasks that appeared far more important at the time I entered them that they were with hindsight. So, I came to peace with dismissals, and I started to like the fact that AFs dismissal processing was creating a "someday/maybe"-list automatically, without any additional work besides the simple highlighting.

I know for sure that were I forced to decide about the fate of these rightfully dismissed tasks IMMEDIATELY, I would have re-entered most of them, thus creating unnecessary work for me!

So, I won't adopt this part of the new rule-set. I will simply dismiss the backlog/closed list when appropriate (on no task standing out on first pass), highlight the remaining tasks and leave it alone: This appears more natural, too.

Apart from that, AF4 "is it". A real stroke of genius.

Reply by Mark Forster

2009-09-5 14:21
Damien:

I'm not quite sure that I understand your post, but here's a summary of what happens with dismissal in AF4:

1) You visit the Backlog and no tasks get done: Highlight all the tasks in the Backlog and convert the existing Active List into the new Backlog.

2) Continue through the new Backlog until you reach the end of the Backlog.

3) Return to the beginning of the list.

4) Cross out all the highlighted tasks. They may be discarded or re-entered, etc.

5) Continue working through the Backlog and so on.

Reply by Olaf B

2009-09-5 18:29
I'm a long time AF1 user. AF2 and RAF didn't appeal to me, but this new system I like: it eliminates the artificiality of the single-page sublists.

I basically use it as an AF1 list with two pages: HEAD and TAIL. The HEAD is the last, open page, never dismissed. The TAIL gets dismissed if no action is taken. I tried it and already finished my first (short) backlog. No dismissals this time.

The thing is: I always liked the AF1 dismissal rules. Dismissed items would stay around forever. That allowed me to put stuff on the list as I thought about it. Of course, I couldn't do all of it NOW. But I liked the way AF sorted out what I decided to do. The rest stayed around. It was dismissed, but nothing was really wrong with it, apart from the fact that you cannot everything at once. AF4, on the other hand, says to handle your dismissed items (almost) immediatly. So I decided to handle the dismissed items the AF1 way.

Well … so I thought. The more I think about it, the more I believe the AF4 way is right.

Why? Let me explain. Some information doesn't belong in a todo list. Some people wanted priorities, but I always thought priorities don't really belong on the list. That doesn't mean you shouldn't set priorities or think about your goals. But once on the todo list, all tasks are equal. Priorities distract from the autoFOCUS part.

Same thing with the indeterminately dismissed items. Dismissing in AF1 partially regains focus, but only partially. AF4 seems to force you to decide what to do with the TAIL, if you don't work on it. Are the tasks unclear? Are they still urgent? Or is this not the right time to do them?

Handling the AF4 TAIL is more work, because you must decide what to do with the dismissed items. On the one hand, that is a big drawback. You have to maintain an extra list with ideas and someday-items. On the other hand, the focus is clearer. We will see how this will turn out.

AF4 already made me do some tasks I wouldn't have done otherwise. I still don't really know how to feel about that.

Hmm, I have never felt that AF (any iteration) helps me "defeat my nature". In fact, I find the opposite -- it helps me "make the most" of my nature. The AF rules are designed to accommodate human nature and the psychology of getting things done, rather than opposing it.

Yes, I have to "make myself" pick up my book and start working through it, and sometimes I just want to start working on whatever it is I just want to do right now. But I have been learning more and more to just use the system (whichever version it happens to be at the time), because it is such a small price to pay with such a great payback.

AF has a tremendous "pull" to it -- I really don't feel myself having to force myself to use the system. Even when I do just start doing whatever I want without referring to the book, I feel it "calling" me, and I usually heed that call, because it's just so much more FUN (and productive) to use the AF list.

In the day and a half that I've been using AF4, that feeling of "fun" and "pull" is even stronger than with previous iterations.

And one nice thing about AF4 is that you really can just jump directly to whatever is on your mind, by adding it to the end of the open list, and just not having anything else "feel ready to be done". If you've just started working the Closed List, you can touch one item very quickly to avoid dismissing the whole lot, and skim through the rest (or just flip past them), then go to your Open List, skip through everything to the very end, and behold, you are now working on whatever it was that just popped into your head (or got thrown onto your desk by your boss). (*)

Maybe the fact that one is allowed to do this, will bring out the perversity in our natures and completely undermine the whole system. So far, I don't find that happening. And I am certainly the kind of person who will naturally gravitate towards doing anything I can to undermine the system. :-)


(*) The only time the rules prevent you from doing this is when you happen to be working through a bunch of highlighted items on the closed list -- the rules say you must deal with each of them somehow. If you've got 20 or 30 of them, it can really slow you down for a bit.
