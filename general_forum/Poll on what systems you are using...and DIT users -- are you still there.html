What is the point of these systems?

As I see it, they have 2 fundamental functions:

1. Organize
2. Motivate

The Organize function is necessary because it's hard to keep more than "seven plus or minus two" items in our awareness at one time. I notice this happening to me all the time. I get emotionally hijacked by one thing, and then realize that there were a lot of other things on my list that I completely lost all awareness of.

I used to think that the manner in which things are organized made a huge difference. I no longer believe that to be the case. I do think it's important for me to get stuff "out of my head" and written down, because of the 7 +/- 2, or "limited RAM" problem, mentioned above. But whether the lists are flat, or hierarchically structured, or have lines dividing them into sections, no longer strikes me as important as the main function of getting stuff written down.

There is some controversy over whether a list can Motivate. I have found that a well-structured list is highly motivating for me. A poorly-structured list will be much less motivating.

I am not saying that the list's structure is the only--or even the most important--determinant of motivation. Obviously, if you have a job that is consistent with your long-term goals and with your values, you might find yourself more highly motivated. But, the way a list is structured can make a difference. If it can increase my motivation to do something by 10-15%, I view that as significant.

I agree with Mike, Mark, and a thousand other psychologists and gurus. Action precedes motivation. If you wait till you feel like doing something, you have a problem. It works the other way around. Do it. Then, after you start doing, you start to feel motivated. Another way to express this, which I learned from he-who-cannot-be-named, is that I can feel one way and act the opposite. I can feel like avoiding the ugly task on my desk and do it anyway.

With all that said, I heartily throw myself in Seraphim's camp. I love, love, love, DWM. My particular implementation is still Walter's Excel DWM.

With regard to Organization, my list stays manageable in its size. I currently have 74 active items. The list itself is subdivided into sections, each representing an expiration date. The items that are due to expire at the end of today constitute a section that has particular salience to me and gets a lot of my focus.

With regard to Motivation, the expiration dates really keep me on my toes and honest with myself. Could I be better? Yes. Do I still procrastinate? Yes. Are there times when I worked on X, when it might have made more sense to work on Y? Yes. But, overall, I have been spending a lot more time focused on the more important stuff and a lot less time on the other stuff. I have let a lot of things that I put on my list drop away. After more careful consideration, they either were not that important, or I just was not clear on exactly what I was trying to accomplish.

What I have found is that one of the keys to making the system work is to write on my list whatever I am about to do, right before I do it. If I am going to go on markforster.net, that's OK, as long as I write it on my list first and then highlight it as the active task. This keeps me honest. And Walter's system provides me with a record of how much time I spent doing everything.

In summary, just DOING it, is the solution. There is no substitute for doing it. But DWM (in Excel) is the best system I've ever used. At this point, I am not looking for anything else. Perhaps my imagination is too limited, but I cannot envision a system better than the one I have.

I'm using "AF1Dit", i.e. AF1 combinded with DiT.

Described as follows - it's pretty simple:

_Structure_

1st thing each day when I open my AF1DiT-Device (Mole - pocket size. 2 pages = 1 AF page) is:

I write tomorrows date and beside a dashed line
e.g. "6/16 - - - - - -"

I close the dashed line form the day before (todays date)
e.g. "like: 6/15 -----------"

Stuff between the closed line and the doted line is stuff for today (closed list)
Stuff after the doted line is for tomorrow (DiT).
Stuff after the doted line with an ! at the beginning is "same day urgent", although its in the section for tomorrow (=must be done today).
Stuff before the closed line is AF1 Stuff and will be handled as described by mark

Example if today is 15th, June:
...
task AF1 - area
task
6/14-------(closed)---------------
task
task AF1 - Area
task
task
6/15----Todays list (closed) -----
task
task
task
task
6/16- - Tomorrows list (open)- - -
task
!task (same day urgent. Must be done 6/15)
task
task
task

_Processing New Items_
New items go to the end of the list (Tomorrows list)
If they are same-day-urgent I put an '!' in front

_Process_
1st
Cycle the list after the dashed line searching only for '!'.
Do '!'(=same day urgent) tasks, if you are in the right context or move to the appropriate context and do them there. Alternative: Schedule them for the same-day in your calendar.
Stuff which has no '!' will be ignored.

2/
Cycle between the closed and the doted line (today stuff) till nothing stands out or can't be done due to context reasons.
Stuff which couldn't be handled or doesn't stand out will be done in an later session or become AF1 stuff the next day.

3/ Continue processing stuff before the closed line (AF1 area) in AF1 manner. Start where you left the day before. (I use a post-it to mark this position.)
____________

+AJPM+

I am now back to AF1 since a few weeks ago...*sigh* there is no beating simplicity, I guess.

BUT, I think I have found something even more simple...so simple in fact I am scratching my head how come I haven't encountered or thought of this before. The only requirement is that you must have a list of tasks ready; aside from that, it will work on any existing list of tasks, whether electronic or paper, AF or DWM or GTD or whatever.

The steps (using paper):

1) Scan your list of tasks and pick one. You may use the rules of whatever time management system you are using, or you may treat the task list as one big whole and scan through everything.
2) Cross out that item, then write it on something that is separate from your task list. It may be a piece of paper, the back of your notebook, an index card, etc. As for me, I use a 1 1/2" x 2" Post It and stick it on the front of my notebook.
3) Scan for and transfer TWO more tasks using the above rules. You should now have three tasks. Let us call this the "Microfocus" (MF) list.
4) Do those items in any way you want, but ONLY those three items.
5) If there is a urgent task that has to be done now, cross out one of the tasks in your MF list, write the urgent task there (if you have time; you can still add it later then cross it out; as for the importance of writing what you have done in the MF list, see step # 8 below)
6) Cross out each task once it is done.
7) You can still add tasks at your big list of tasks, but resist the temptation to add to the MF list unless the task is urgent
8) Once all of the MF tasks are crossed out, re enter recurring tasks into your big list.
9) Repeat

I think it works for me because you consider only one very small list at a time. Even with the rules of AF/DWM, the mere fact that you have more than a few tasks per consideration can produce overwhelm. Also, it facilitates choosing of tasks: from the big list, you eliminate or lessen the temptation to choose items because they are easy to do; all items have similar difficulty in WRITING them down on a separate paper. Then when you go to the MF list, choosing a task is not so important anymore because the choices are so few! You do the easy or difficult task first, it does not matter; you be doing the others right after it.

Anyways, I hope this helps some of you.

Hi Nuntym,

I'm doing something similar with my DWM list. My list is very long, so I can't easily scan through it and choose three items. I do this instead:

(1) I work through the list as per the regular DWM rules. If something "stands out" then I try to work on it, little and often. If something "stands out", but I just don't feel like working on it right this second, usually because I am distracted by minutiae and this particular item is larger and needs more extended attention, then I flag it as "important" (I'm using Microsoft Outlook, so I just set Priority = Important, but you could do it with paper by putting an exclamation mark next to the task or something).

(2) After a pass through the list, mostly spent acting on minutiae and refreshing my memory as to what's currently on the list, I then work through the list again. But this time, I only look at the items flagged "important". With Outlook, this is easy -- I just filter the list to show Important items only.

(3) After I work through this list, I then show all the tasks again, and work through it as normal.


I then alternate between the full list and the filtered list. This helps balance my attention so I cover the little things as well as the more substantial things.

This idea was an outgrowth of Mark's idea to flag something for "must do next pass". I often had 10-12 items flagged as "must do next pass", and I wanted to look through those to see which was really most important / urgent. The flag came to mean "this is important, do it as soon as you can".

Sometimes I start the day focusing only on the items already flagged "important" -- this functions kind of like the "current initiative" idea.
