Jabbe:

The number of items up for dismissal is far from random. The great advantage of AF2 is that it imposes order on random tasks. This also applies to dismissal.

Bear in mind that you are working with a list that was produced under a different system so the distribution of tasks on the early pages will not be as it would have been if you had been using AF2.

Under AF2 the number of tasks which are put on notice for dismissal is dependent on a number of factors of which the most important are:

a) How well randomized the tasks were when they were entered into the list. This is why I recommend entering tasks when you think of them or when they come up in the normal course of work. Dumping a large block of backlog tasks into the list will probably result in them being dismissed in large blocks too.

b) What percentage of time was spent near the beginning of the list. If tasks were well randomized when entered they will tend to be broken up into fairly small packets. When there is time available to move back through the list then these packets will be further broken up or eliminated altogether. When there is not time to move back, the packets will remain larger. This means that dismissing tasks is very responsive to the work situation.

In general therefore the AF2 dimissal rules have the following results:

1) A large number of backlog or dumped tasks will be rejected, thus preventing the system from being overloaded.

2) When there is little time to deal with anything but urgent items, dismissal will move quite quickly which allows you to prioritise your workload as you begin to get out of the urgent phase.

3) When more time becomes available dismissal acts more slowly - in fact may well cease altogether.

Hi Jason

I do not dismiss many tasks as I genuinely feel that most need to be done but the reason for that "doing" may vary in importance. For example I may decide I can't dismiss "empty dishwasher" but in the grand scheme of things it is not an immensely important task. Something like "write report" may also not be able to be dismissed. The big advantage of AF - and the dismissal principle, whichever version you use, is that, by being a rule, you have to examine why the item is on your list and why it is not being done.

If you genuinely believe the task must be done the reasons for not doing a task will usually fall into a limited number of categories.

(1) Item cannot be done because the time/location is not right. In that case dismiss and/or set up a reminder.
(2) Waiting for someone/something else. In that case rephrase as a follow up type item to monitor status until the conditions are right.
(3) Procrastination. If the only reason is procrastination then there will be a reason - and possibly not specifically related to the task at issue. For example a 2 minute telephone call may be resisted because of a fear of conflict, post may not be opened for fear of the contents, a report may be resisted because of lack of confidence in ability. AF is amazingly helpful in these scenarios - the resisted items cannot just be ignored in a never ending list - they "must" be done or dismissed. That very rule becomes the catalyst to make us look at the reason for our reticence - ignoring the rule removes that major benefit and turns AF into just a to do list.

Hope that may help?
