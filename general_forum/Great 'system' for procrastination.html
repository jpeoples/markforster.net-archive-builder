I am very disappointed, and hesitate to call this a system. For me it ends up in one long To-Do-list where it is hard to find what I should do next. Being a very active procrastinator, it only increases my unease of mind. This is how I feel.
I have say 10 pages with items. I have sorted out some items on each page, lots of easy wins, doing the obvious things that I can easily do. That's fine.

If a couple of heavy tasks happen to be left on the same page, they tend to be closed, since I don't feel like doing any of them, since I have in my mind that there are some other, less heavy things, more important(?) somewhere else, that I could 'feel like doing'.
The closing of pages with 'non committable tasks' makes me uneasy, and I can't trust that the system keeps what is relatively most important any more.

I am stuck. Easy wins I have done away with, so I was happy for a while, but now I don't trust my system any more.
I have to look through my closed pages, to reenter a few of those items. When they are done, it's back to zero again. The idea of splitting up the list only ends in suboptimizing. I'm doing some things, but I don't think it's the best use of my time.

Jabbe and Simon:

Since you are having such problems with procrastination, here are a few suggestions:

1) If you can't get the hang of "standing out", then just give yourself a rule that you must work on at least one item per page before moving on to the next page. (You can do more than one item if you want to)

2) With the more difficult items that you are resisting hard, cross them out and re-enter them at the end of the list as easier first steps so that they too become "easy wins". This counts as "working on an item".

Jabble:

One aspect of the instructions that is very important here, and that I have the feeling is not fully appreciated, is that you *don't have to finish a task* to cross it off and re-enter it.

I tend to have difficulties with procrastination myself, and once only a limited number of tasks is left on a page, they're all the hard ones and I don't feel like doing them. But the rule mentioned above gives you a way out. What I do is take one of those tasks (most of the time, you'll know on which one you need to progress), and just find a simple thing to do that still creates some progress (like making a list of steps to completion, or starting to draft an email, or booking a meeting, or just jotting some ideas on paper). This removes the resistance. Even if you stop after that you'll have made some progress, can re-enter the task at the end and move on to the next page. And often, just starting doing this small action, you'll find you continue working on the task without even realising it.

If you didn't already do so, I strongly recommend you read Mark's article on this site "I'll just get the file out", which explains this approach. I've been known to procrastinate quite a lot, but this really works for me, especially as it's backed up by the rules of the AF approach (I prefer to call it an approach rather than a system).

Hope this helps...

AF isn't for project management, it's for getting things done. When I'm working the system I end up doing work on projects that are important but I know I would have otherwise put off for "more important" stuff. But AF (and GTD for that matter) runs on the philosophy that everything needs to get done. The beauty of AF (and advantage over GTD) is that I spend very little time categorising tasks lists (I only have three separate places: work/home, my parents' home, and errands) and I was able to start right away, it also lets me capture ideas that come into my head without the associated of stress of "how do I categorise this into the system" - it goes into the system, when I see it next I either take action on it if I can or I put it somewhere I can.

I keep my project lists separate as it will have lots of things that can't be done now or are dependent tasks. Next actions or near actions go into my list. But here again, I don't worry too much whether it's strictly a next action a la GTD, but if it's near enough - if for some reason I need another task to happen, I'll re-enter it with the task that needs to get done first (or if that prior task is on that page, do that task). I find that AF is less about working out exactly what you have to do before you do it (like GTD) though you can do this if you like, but it works very well with less defined tasks - if you need to refine it down, you will. If you don't, you save time.

While I still have room for improvement, I am finding I'm consistently doing more work on things before they are due and chipping away at stuff I'd normally ignore through extreme procrastination. I never finish my closed lists anyway so I'm not missing out on that satisfaction! I feel better about myself because I do far less procrastination (and will hopefully eliminate it all together once the system finds equilibrium - ie I've caught up with my backlog, readjusted to a more sustainable workload and find myself regularly working on things before they are urgent).

Jabbe:

One of the things I’m am finding from the Beta Testing is that people who import all their tasks into AF from a previous system tend to have problems. It’s not that the system itself can’t cope with the tasks. It’s that the people themselves tend to get symptoms of overwhelm because they are giving themselves too much stress right at the beginning to be able to learn to work the system correctly.

So what I am recommending (and will incorporate in the revised instructions) is that one should start Autofocus just by writing down the first few tasks that come into one’s head, and then add tasks as one thinks of them or as they come up. That way the list grows “organically” and one is actively engaged with all the tasks that have been entered. That greatly reduces the feelings of overwhelm.

Hi Jabbe,

The point of AutoFocus (as I see it) is to keep you "automatically focused" on what you need to be DOING right now. Not everything SHOULD go on the AutoFocus list(s). In fact, the immediate fallout of runing the list as described, is to flush out the "someday/maybe" items. If they were put on the list, and if your book is a bound one (as Mark uses) you will have a history of highlighted "someday/maybe" items that you are not going to work on until ... well, someday ... maybe.

If you know in advance that an item is "someday/maybe", simply do not put it on your list ... or put it on your list and dismiss it immediately if you wish to keep all items in that same book. My own personal preference is to do my planning (Vision/Mission/Objectives/Goals/etc.) on the computer in some simple word processing program (used to be MS Word, is now OneNote). My planning document does not need a lot of frequent reference. I do it only once a week. That planning/replanning exercise produces "actions" that get put on my list and the list is worked for a week. Thus has it always been (well, for the last 40 years or so anyway ;-)

There is absolutely no reason that everything MUST go into AF. However, I can see that AF will accommodate everything ... one way or another. And there is something to be said for one place for everything.
