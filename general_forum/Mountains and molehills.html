AF2 has helped me to embark on a chore that I have been putting off for 18 months. I have a bag that's been damaged by airport baggage handling machinery. I've had to glue it back together.

It's taken me 35 minutes. Over the 18 months since the bag was damaged, I have probably devoted 350 minutes to thinking about repairing it, wondering why I don't repair it, scheduling days when I'm going to repair it, concocting good reasons not to repair it, and looking for ways to avoid trying to repair it, most obviously by buying a new one . (But luggage that's any good is surprisingly expensive, and I don't like any of the examplesI see in the shops.)

I can understand that one might resist embarking on a project that might prove time consuming or awkward or painful. But this was none of those. Spending more time resisting something than it would have taken to complete is clearly maladaptive. Why then do we do it?

Hi Nicole
The answer is simple. The negative traits you attributed to this task or the fact that you always had more pleasant options available, caused you to never choose it because there were no internal or external influences to enforce it. The other options available always trumped choosing this one. See, the negative attributions created a resistance barrer to ever choose this over other "options available" without any other governing principles or rules in place to force the choice. AF2 simply provided you with a governing rule to break the resistance barrier.
