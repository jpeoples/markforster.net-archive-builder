I don't think too much has changed with AF4. Even with AF1 I found that, yes you can throw anything at it, but it's usually not worth it. There are two main disadvantages. The first is that you loose some of the review effect of dismissal. Dismissal still helps you to regain focus. When you dismiss something, you can evaluate what went wrong. But if your list consists not only of things you want to work on now, but everything you might or could do, nothing really went wrong and dismissing a good portion of the tasks is to be expected. The second problem is that you cannot realistically clear out your backlog/your old pages when they are full of maybe-tasks. This made dismissal a constant threat and I started to work 'tactically', i.e. working on something only to avoid dismissing. Some people find this effect helpful. Not I. Too often this led me to do something that was not really worth doing or to start something that would be dismissed eventually anyway. I would *loose* focus. Like Alan Baljeu I sometimes put ideas on my AF list, but then the first action on them is to move them somewhere else.

If I think of a task that needs to be done, and it's clear to me that it needs to be done LATER, and I have my tickler handy (i.e., Outlook tasks), then I just add the task and set the date and it's done. When the task comes up in Outlook, I evaluate: ready to do now? (move to AF4) or better later? (postpone the date) or maybe, just delete since you've realized it's not needed? or not sure? (maybe postpone, maybe ove to AF4 for sifting)

However, if a task comes to mind, and I don't want to think more about it right now, or Outlook isn't handy (computer is frozen or whatever), then I put it in AF4.

When I come across a task in AF4 that I realize is just clogging up the works because I don't want to work on it any time soon, then I move it to the tickler, to appear again in a week or a month or whatever.

So, I think AF4 still functions just fine as a "catch everything" list -- as long as you remember you also can and should use a tickler as well.

Reply by Mark Forster

2009-11-18 9:45
Just to clarify my comment, the thing which really kills AF4 is starting off with a huge dump of tasks from earlier lists. That means that the initial closed list (backlog) takes weeks, or even months to clear. This is contrary to the intent behind the system which is that it should be fast-moving.

My recommendation is (and always has been for all versions of AF) that you should build the list up gradually by entering things as they come up. That way you ensure that the list is realistic and current and doesn't die of constipation.

Although it's possible to move directly into AF4 from previous versions without re-writing your list, that should only be done if the old list was lean and fast-moving. In other words if you were having a problem with the size of your list already, don't expect AF4 to solve it!

I've also always made the point that tasks which can't be done or don't need to be done over the next few days should not be put on the list but should be scheduled. The reminder should normally be to put the task on the list, but of course can be to do something at a specific time (e.g. call Bob at 10 a.m.)

Hi Margaret

I think the issue you face is pretty much exactly the same issue we all face in deciding what to take on. I expect that most of us have many more options than we can possibly handle. Somehow, we have to determine our priorities.

AF is a great help in sorting out what we are to do. It can help us to keep a broad range of possibilities in view and it can help us to discern constantly during each day just what actions have real value for us.

I might add that the idea of using some activities as rewards for making an effort on others might have its dangers. I tend to require each action to offer its own rewards. (e.g. doing something irksome produces peace of mind; mopping the floor allows me to enjoy the comfort of a clean house....)

I think it does no harm to have a large number of items in the someday/maybe category. But we need to remember that there must be limits to what we give energy to and that there is a real skill in discovering our true priorities.

What I've decided to do is to use AF4 all day, rather than doing daily recurring tasks outside AF4, and I think I've found a way of actioning them by using a combination of the Pomodoro technique and increasing bursts of time, but hopefully still within the AF4 rules. The important thing is that I add these tasks at the end of each day (and therefore at the end of the (open) list) so that they are next to each other: Housework, Paperwork, Record-keeping.

Then next morning I start working the AF4 list at the point I left off the previous day, and I usually find myself moving quickly to those three items at the end of the list, and I feel ready to do them. I work on each for 5 minutes, then for 10 minutes, then for 15 minutes, rotating round each one until the particular task is complete or I feel I've done enough of that task for that day. At that point I carry on as normal with AF4. (I also use the Pomodoro technique of having a 5-minute leisure break at the end of each 25-minutes' worth of work, to add to the fun).

I find it more satisfying to work on the three tasks as a unit using the 'bursts' method, as it keeps me more focused on getting them done for the day (so that they are off my plate and I don't feel them nagging at me) than if I were using normal AF4 rules. If I were using normal AF4 rules and working on each item then re-adding it, they would end up interspersed with other unrelated tasks that came into my mind and were added to the list, which is what's been happening up till now, and which I've found demotivating.
