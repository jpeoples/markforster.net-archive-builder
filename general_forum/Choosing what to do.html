In the late 1990's the BBC made a TV drama called THE MISSING POSTMAN (starring James Bolam). It's about a postman who decides to deliver the mail personally to various addresses in the UK (instead of the through the normal system). He gets to Scotland and stays for some time with a lady who had a simple way of choosing what to do. She would write a task, job etc on a slip of paper and place it in a big jar. When she wanted to choose what to do she would dip into the jar and pull out a task. Seems like a good way to make a choice when choosing is hard to do.

2010-04-24 16:45
I tried this actually on my AF1 list for about a week last winter. I numbered all of the open tasks and then used Excel to randomly choose a number. Even if it chose an extremely unpleasant task, I applied "little and often" -- ie had to take at least one small step toward moving the task forward.

It worked surprisingly well for the first three or four days -- a lot of things I'd been dreading or postponing were accomplished, and also there was no feeling of "overwhelm" from a long AF list because I knew eventually everything would get done (on some random day in the future).

In the end though I dropped it because of (a) the old problem that completing one task inevitably leads to two more (and so the list just got bigger and bigger), and (b) looking back at that week, it seems mostly filled with "busy work".

Basically you're bypassing your rational and intuitive side, which can be a good idea for a few days if you're locked in negative mental patterns (eg pessimism or fatigue). But eventually a gulf opens up between what your rational and/or intuitive side want to do, and what the random system is choosing for you, and that actually creates a new source of stress.

Eventually what you need to do becomes really obvious, and you don't need the training wheels of the random list any longer.

2010-04-24 18:38
Found these techniques helpful in more "trivial" group decisions. A dozen or so of us at work usually take a longer lunch off campus on Fridays. Trying to decide were to go would pretty much start as soon as the work day did. And more than a few times the group would split and end up going to two places instead of one.

Then I asked everyone to write down their top five favorite places to go on Friday. I made a list of the results, noting how many times a choice was selected.

Ended up with about 20 suggestions within those 100 or so votes. Wrote a script to "randomly" select one of those suggestions with relative weight given to the popularity of each selection.

RIght before lunch, we all get together and hit the "roll" button and see where we are going and go.

This old saw, I find to be exceptionally dangerous. In some sense it is true and has been written down in the Western tradition since Hesiod's _Works and Days_. The little truth it does contain is rather obvious and not very illuminating. But the moral implications can end up rather monstrous in my opinion.

The metaphor is faulty anyhow. Sometimes what you sow does not come to harvest due to conditions outside your control or you fortuitously find a crop spring forth you did not plant.

"That ye may be the children of your Father which is in heaven: for he maketh his sun to rise on the evil and on the good, and sendeth rain on the just and on the unjust."
