Paper has three huge advantages:
1) it's easy
2) it's always available
Electronic has these advantages:
1) editing
2) searching
3) automated functions (tracking, etc)

The big disadvantage of electronic is it's easy to overcomplicate things. If you have a tendency to get lost working on systems, paper may be better simply because this system is fixed in place for you, and you can get to actioning.

I always find that as time goes on systems tend to get more complex. You may be at a point where you need to simplify to the easiest thing that works for you. I said in a thread last year which got mixed reviews that once you do a bit of AF with a touch of GTD and a pinch of Covey, what you have done in essence is created a whole new system. Now that may be fine if in fact this is what you need to be productive, but the first thing I would try to do is reduce the complexity of your system.

As a general comment not intended to be a criticism, I personally don't think time management systems solve nor are they meant to solve procrastination problems. They solve organization problems. Tinkering with systems is a sign of procrastination, but a new system will not solve it. I am not sure I have the procrastination cure, but this awareness may help. Good luck

2010-01-13 13:30
I'm an extremely 'digital person'. About a year ago I even stopped printing scientific articles; for environmental purposes, but mainly because digital annotations were so much more useful (Skim and OSX system-wide search are a godsend). My calendar is digital, even my note-taking is often digital. I keep paper tot a minimum.

Having said that, I've made a conscious decision to use AF with paper. With GTD, this was too cumbersome, and I used OmniFocus (and before that Things). With AF, it was finally possible, and I can say that it's been a very smart decision for me.

There are two reasons why I would advise a paper AF setup: separation and psychology.

Concerning the first, I've found that computers, as awesome as they are for storing and retrieving, have a tendency to make everything blur together. I've become such a power user that in the time span of five minutes I can 1) go into itunes to select a song, 2) open photoshop to edit some file, 3) while it loads open gmail and check new messages, 4) click on a link in this email, 5) 'watch' the link in the background while opening my task system app 'du jour', and do a bunch of other things.

I find that it's verry difficult to open the task 'system' and focus solely on that, disregarding mail popups, msn messages, song changes. Even if I turn all that stuff off, it's literally one keypress away (system-wide shortcuts), and hard to tune out. Our brains aren't made to handle that. With a paper system, I have to physically move my hands from the keyboard, pick up a pen, open the book, move aside the laptop, and focus on the list. World of difference. Sometimes I even move to the couch, away from the computer.

Concerning the latter (psychology), I find that it is greatly more satisfying to cross off a task on paper, because it's solid. I take more time to think of phrasing my task. I have an archive of old tasks that I can look back on in satisfaction. Text on a computer is just to easy to move around, add, etc. It's too fleeting and insignificant. In fact, research seems to support this, find that, among other things, note-taking on paper makes things stay in your head better than digital note-taking [citation needed due to laziness].

So really, it's just one reason: psychology. Unless it's too impractical to use a paper setup, AF(4) is so simple that there's now little excuse to opt for digital.

I found the written list functional, but:
- I felt I was spending too much time scanning lists of closed tasks for the few open ones and
- you need a small list you can carry everywhere. Not a huge problem in theory, except I tended to rely on the small lists and forget the master list.
Having said that, when I was disciplined, this worked pretty well.

I am now back with an Outlook task list, (synched to Toodledo on my new iPod Touch).
- allows me to drag mails from a mailbox review
- main view is active tasks only, in increasing order of date modified
Disadvantage is that the list does tend to grow, with similar but not quite identical tasks coming in from a number of sources. I have a "prune list" task that goes on from time to time. Nevertheless, if you have a lot of mail, I would recommend that you try this. I haven't changed it for months, which is my main metric for a process.

YMMV, of course.
