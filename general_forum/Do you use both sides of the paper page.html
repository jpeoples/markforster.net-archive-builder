I thought about doing the facing-pages thing but didn't because both my work and home lists are currently running 4-12 active pages -- but mostly because I want to keep my work life and my home life as separate as possible. They bleed together enough as it is.

So, I use a Levenger Circa notebook, compact size with an upsized set of rings and three sections (work AF, home AF, and a third section for general notetaking).

The Circa system, by the way, has another really nice side-effect. As I finish pages, I just remove them and add more blank pages as necessary. This way, I can limit the overall bulk of the notebook by not carrying around pages that are no longer useful.

On the video, you can see that Mark uses both sides of the paper. His book looks very neat and tidy.

I only use one side - the right - I use the left side for writing general stuff like:
- projected monthly costs for my reno project
- time guesstimates for the reno
- packing list for my recent ski trip
- financial doodling / projections
- place to put various post-it notes
- measurements for appliances I want to buy or room sizes
- library book list
- grocery lists
- confirmation numbers for payments
All very miscellaneous stuff.

I started off using both sides, but I lost track of where I was more easily with both sides and it helps to focus me - my attention would wander over to the other page when I had my book open when I used both sides.
Also, if you want to eventually clip/fold over the top to mark where you are with active pages, you can't do that if you used both sides.

I use both sides of the pages. I use graph paper and write my list beginning on the third square to the right. When I'm working on something I mark it by beginning the cross-out line as Mark suggested. When I finish the task, I cross it out the rest of the way. When I have a number of tasks crossed off on a page, I can do a broken vertical line between the first to squares of crossed out tasks (as someone on this forum suggested). This allows the unfinished tasks to jump out more clearly.

I also use a Circa notebook, but have taken out a divider between my home and work lists and now have "one list to rule them all." So far, it's working for me and I don't have to think about which list to add something to (like errands).

I have two sections: The front is AF. The back is other lists (gift ideas, books to read, etc) and journaling. Like Steve, I also like the ability to discard used pages and insert blank ones as needed. If I ever need to set up a separate AF list for a project or want to divide my work/home list again it's no problem.

I am also using a Circa notebook, the smaller size one, and using both sides of the pages. I like the Circa paper as it is thick enough that highlighting does not bleed through. I've also started keeping some "extras" in my Circa AF book, like my Wish List, Things to Buy, Dreams, Project List, some Financial Info, etc etc. I really like the flexibility of Circa and being able to easily add or take out pages/cards... it can also be very compact (unlike a 3-ring binder system, even a small one.)

I tried the down/up approach for a while. Then I gave up and went back to a mixed list.

I work from home and tried it during a period when work was light, so I would have about a third of a page or less work items under the line. I didn't like only having 10 or less items on a virtual work page. I also didn't like the fact that I read the list downwards, but had entered the items from the bottom up.

Since then I have taken on a lot more work, and have switched to a separate notebook for work as I really need to block off time. Also I am taking my work notebook into meetings and want it to look professional! I may switch back if I get another light period (although hopefully autofocus will help me prevent that), but don't think I'd try the down/up approach again. The benefit of one list is that if procrastinating when I should have been doing paid work, I was more likely to pick a "house" job than just spend time on mindless surfing.
