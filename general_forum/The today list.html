SF3 works well, and doesn't require this formality, but I think it's a valuable addition. (Other approaches are possible too.)

There are things that need to be done today, but don't need to be done now. I don't mean things you merely "want" done. I don't mean to make plans (as in DIT) of things you hope to do. I don't mean making a list like the "Will I Do this today?" technique. Things I hope (but don't need) to have done today just go in SF. I'll get around to them when I get there.

In short, whatever appointments are for today, whatever daily routines exist, whatever weekly routines are scheduled for the day, and whatever you are expected to have done today:

Write these on a page. There should be few items. Whenever I flip an SF page, I glance at these. I may just do one (immediate) or add any to SF column 2 (soon).

Why: Because these aren't urgent, may not be what I want or ought to do first thing, but they shouldn't be forgotten. I could write them directly in SF, and normally I'd come upon them. But if something comes up which will cut my day short, I know the things I *need* to cover and can take care of them.

Alan,

That's a good point; I think to a great extent the Calendar is a virtual COL3 for SFv3. Such that;

COL1 is:

(a) the currently active subset of your Backlog that you have available to select as NEW in progress work.

COL2 is:

(a) The current in progress work list
(b) Urgent inbound queue that must be in progress based on the way it arrived in your world.
(c) The space remaining in COL2 is your count down timer telling you an estimate of how long you have before you must close the window of opportunity to act on items COL1.
(d) The list of items you must action to some degree before you can review any other section of you backlog; or process future action on previously acted on items from this page.

COL3 is:

(a) Represented by your Calendar and Ticker in a virtual sense
(b) Must be considered when selecting any COL1 or COL2 items to action next.
(c) Can be ignored while one moves forward a page
(d) Must be actioned by the end of the day -or- re-negoitated


In my personal world COL2 item (c) is the major motivator to whittle down COL1 and it's very effective. COL3 is essentially a throttle that prevents me from activating too many COL2 items when it's not appropriate. I hadn't noticed that second dimension until you poised you point.

I do that when my big list gets overwhelming, but usually weekly rather than daily. I also include a place to record time spent on little-and-often tasks and anything from the big list that needs to be done this week. I usually review the big list while creating the weekly list.

That way, all my truly urgent and important tasks are on one page. I work through this page each day before opening the big list. I treat it like AF to decide what to do when and for how long. I don't necessarily do all the tasks, but each one gets considered and I can see which little-and-often projects are getting less (or more) attention than the others.

Once I'm done that list, I can go to the big list knowing that everything is under control.

Every day or two, the big list gets reviewed for things that should be moved to the weekly list. If I know I won't look at the big list for a few days, I copy over everything that must be done before I expect to get to it again.

If things get really hectic, I create another layer covering only a few days.

I find this weekly layer reassuring. I know there's nothing important and urgent lurking on another page of the big list.

New tasks that can be done any time get added to the big list. If they need to be done during the week they go on the weekly list.

My weekly list gets anything that would go in C2.

The list starts with a line for each "little and often" task (LAOs). When I work on one of those, I record the day rather than recopy it. Next are tasks I expect to finish in one session; those get crossed of when finished.

That's the ideal list. In reality, I sometimes add a LAO at the bottom.

Creating a form on the computer and printing it out each week didn't work. I always put too much on the form, and projects changed almost every week. It works best if I create it each week, thinking about whther I really expect to do each task that week.

When making the list, I know I have to be able to trust it has all the information I need to make time decisions for the entire week. Deadlines and start-dates are noted. If I don't know how long a project will take, it becomes a LAO so I look at it on the first day. The first task is, of course, decide how much I need to work on it.

I don't need to list things that are done more than once a day, but if they did I'd find a way to put it on the weekly list.

I work the entire list in AF mode -- read it all first thing in the morning, then do what stands out. If nothing is more important that day, LAOs get priority. Experience has shown that they will be neglected otherwise. I've already decided that everything on the week list is more important than anything not on it, so I have to finish the week list before looking at the big list.

My big list is complicated. It's between systems. It's a combination of AF1 and DWM with some DIT, with the remains of an attempt at SF. Part of it is a datebook where I write tasks on the date I next want to do them. I review this when I create each week list.

I used to keep a grid of weekly tasks, with weeks across the top for several months, but tasks changed too often. Same with a grid of daily tasks for the month. The week list is a good balance for me.

As for your list, why do you keep it at your home desk if you never go there?

My kids' lists live on the kitchen walls. The permanent list has morning, afternoon and bedtime routines. They're pen on paper, not etched in stone. The other is on a whiteboard and changes often.
