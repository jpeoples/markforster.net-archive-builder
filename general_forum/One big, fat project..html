2009-01-25 19:22
A writing project is due in 7 days. I am behind. It will require a minimum of 56 concentrated hours to complete it in time and have it be at least acceptable.

Here is the situation.
I have 4 active pages in my AF notebook with about 140 tasks outstanding. One of those 140 tasks is "finish the script (writing project). This one item is by far the most important work activity I ought to be putting my focus and all my time on. And here is where I am having difficulty feeling the effortless motivation I felt using AF for the first three weeks of the beta. Suddenly, an apparently huge wall of resistance looms before me.

One idea is to chunk down all the little steps I will have to do to complete it. The question is, what level of magnification should I use. I could say, task-write outline, task-write first draft, task-write second draft. The wall remains high with that level of magnification. The other extreme is to go to the end of my AF list and write; task-write 1 sentence.

I know very well the steps of the process required for completing these particular writing projects.
1. outline
2. first draft
3. get notes from readers - usually ends up being about 60-70 actionable notes per draft.
4. write second draft
5.get notes
6 polish
7. submit
8-nth. Notes -Write- Submit rinse and repeat 3-5 times.

Yet, the more I break the project down into these smaller phases of the process, the more unmotivated and depressed I feel. Perhaps I need to turn the magnification up higher, get to that write a sentence/write a scene level, make a single entry in the AF book and see how it goes.

I already tried a day of having a separate AF notebook for the project itself but I quickly became resistant to the whole list of tasks.

In terms of importance and priority, getting these big writing projects completed and out the door is the single most important activity of my work. Nothing happens, no money, no forward movement, nothing at all until I get the written blueprints down on paper.

I guess I am looking for a method of using AF on these big projects that maintains the organic momentum I find in using AF for other areas of my personal and work life. I believe the answer resides somewhere in the mix of "little and often" and the "optimum level of magnification of tasks" in the AF notebook. I just haven't found the trick on this one. I look forward to hearing if other's have found how to get the most out of AF with big, all consuming projects.

Hello Stark:

Your looming deadline horrifies me. Especially since I’ve been in the same situation, many times.

1 Your time estimate is PROBABLY overly optimistic. Speaking for myself, a realistic estimate would be 84 hours (1.5 x what you estimate) and more likely 112 hours (but then, I’m “slow”.)

2 That you are focusing on “what level of magnification should I use" ("I could say, task-write outline, task-write first draft, task-write second draft") is, to me, very good evidence that your resistance is, well, let’s just say “very high.”

3 How you write--how you start, how you revise, how many drafts, whatever--is entirely up to you. Me, I just have “mind dump”, revising, and final draft (looking for typos, cutting out redundancies, formatting for submission--all that stuff that takes WAY longer than we like to remember).

4 I recommend a mind dump, or whatever you want to call it. I call it that because it’s the place I start and it’s an easy way TO start and it recognizes that I’m collecting, in no order, without editing and without censoring, any and all thoughts, questions, snippets from various sources even remotely related to this writing.

5 So, you could write “script mind dump” at the end of your list (or, recognizing it’s beyond urgent, write it on a separate card.)

Then, step away from this blog and everything else and set your ktichen timer for a “short” period. For a project like this, I personally define “short” as at least one hour. (I’m more likely to choose 2 hours) but it could be truly short, like 30 minutes and start writing your mind dump.

Cross it off, write it again, set the timer again and repeat until you have enough to revise (which means, you know have a “draft” and it’s going to be easier--if still enormously time consuming--from here.

6 Just doing a mind dump is doing. It is an example of the short and often Mark Forster talks about. It’s gets us off our “but . . .s” and gets us into the project and it harnessses the power of our unconscious to start working on the project instead of working on the problem of why we’re not doing the project.

(The other trick I use, besides the “mind dump” characterization, is to set myself the goal of writing the WORST script, etc or the WORST first draft. I’ll confess I fail at this goal because it’s usually surprisingly good, but it gets me going because there are no expectations and thus no fear.)

As Carol Lloyd, a writer about creativity says, “Doing is significantly different than not doing. Doing not only “gets things done’ it teaches lessons you cannot possibly learn theoretically and can loosen even the most stubbornly entrenched feelings [of fear, doubt’.”

7 But you know all this already. You wrote: “I know very well the steps of the process required for completing these particular writing projects.” And you also wrote: Suddenly, an apparently huge wall of resistance looms before me.”

You can’t really structure this resistance, which is what you’re trying to do with fiddling with how to break down the description of the “to do” steps in this project. You need to dissolve it. (Or do an end run around it, or whatever metaphor makes sense to you.) Starting--doing--will do that.

To overcome this resistance, set a timer and start writing.

As others have noted, I've never set a timer and now wound up engrossed in the project when the timer went off. (A variation on this is to to intervals--e.g. 45 minutes working, 15 minutes resting, 45 minutes working, etc, but I may need more than 15 minutes composting time so I just make sure I'm shifting my focus completely and then going back to my project.)

So, to repeat: set your timer!

Good luck!

Stark:

You are talking about something that is going to take you 8 hours a day. There's no way you should be attempting to control this through Autofocus.

The best way to deal with a project which needs concentrated work like this is to isolate yourself from all distractions and block off 8 hours per day in your schedule. Refuse to allow yourself to do anything else other than the project during those 8 hours.

Use Autofocus outside those 8 hours for the other tasks you have.

I think it's important to say that my advice to block off 8 hours per day and not to attempt to control the project through AF was directed purely to Stark's situation where he (she?) has procrastinated to the extent that he *has* to put in that amount of time each day.

It's not the only way I would advise doing a writing project in more normal circumstances.

There's already been some correspondence about the best way of dealing with projects like writing, piano practice and so on which need a considerable amount of time. Most people find that the best way is to make their writing/practice periods time specific, but some people find other ways are better for them. It's a matter of experiment.

One way that can be very effective is to use reverse psychology and say "I am not going to spend *more* than four hours a day on this." If you're going to resist something, you might as well make it worth resisting!

Finally, remember a writer is a person who writes, not a person who thinks about writing.

I so empathise with Stark and Trish. I am not a writer, but I am doing a postgrad and I am a decade behind where I should be, and so my 'script' or my 'novel' is my thesis.

I am resisting because I'm afraid of failure, afraid of success (what will I do afterwards?), a perfectionist, and I'm overwhelmed. Perhaps there are other reasons of which I'm not yet aware. My tutor tells me he is yet to meet a writer who actually enjoys the act of writing.

I've been reading about 'self-handicapping' recently. This is different to self-sabotage because you don't deliberately trash your efforts (not turning up to the exam), you just introduce an artificial level of difficulty (not beginning revision early enough, so you never do quite as well as your ability would ordinarily suggest).

Apparently it is caused by problems of self-image, self-worth, and self-esteem. In the research I saw, it tended to affect most those students whose natural ability put them in the top 10% of their class. Basically, you think you're a pretty good student, so you can get away with "good enough" despite minimal revision. Because you haven't tried your hardest, you get to protect your inner belief that you are amongst the very best in the class without having that inner belief (which may be embedded at the very core of your psyche) ever really challenged.

So I guess the answer is to build your reserves of self-esteem; divorce your sense of self-worth from your achievements; and diminish the 'threat' that your script/ novel/ thesis/ equivalent of exams is having on your psyche.

Hi Stark
I recently finished a floundering project using AF.
Not as big as yours,but important nevertheless career wise.
Here's what I did:
1.List all the subtasks on index cards and add them to the first active page of the AF notebook
2.Then treat the index cards and the active page as a single list
3.Work on stand out items on the cards as usual,crossing off,reentering,rephrasing and dismissing etc
4.Add other tasks to the cards as they come up.

What works with this method is that you can CHOOSE your index card project tasks over the others on the list or indeed over other project related tasks.And it is this freedom of choice that neutralises my natural tendency to resist things and motivates me.
Also,by crossing off things even after you've made a small amount (any) of progress AF allows me to counteract my tendency to perfectionism.
It is these 2 (lack of choice and perfectionism) that paralyse me when I'm overwhelmed.
Ideally if you have AF working well you will avoid crises like these cos youll have worked little and often on things.
But I believe it CAN be used to facilitate the actual input needed in crises like yours.
Good luck
