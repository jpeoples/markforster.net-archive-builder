I journal. I try to do the morning pages, as per the Artist's Way, although I'm not absolutely consistent. Avrum,

I always thought dream analysis was dumb. And I still do, the way some people do it. I don't believe that dreams have a universal "language", and yet there is a kind of universality to some elements. I think that the closer you get to basic emotions in a dream the closer you get to a "message" of some kind. I do believe that Freud had it right when he uttered that famous cigar line.

I think dream work is most successful if you (as a therapist) don't analyze it but just ask the patient what HE thinks it all means. After all it is HIS dream so HE is probably best qualified to say what it means. It is also likely that he will miss the obvious point of it some times, but sometimes all it takes is a few well phrased questions.

As for my dreams, they seem to come from two sources that I can think of right now. Either I'm struggling with something in my life and I'm getting feedback from my subconscious; or I've been trying to solve some kind of intellectual problem ... and that really amounts to the same process so maybe it is actually only one source.

Sometimes I'll wake up thinking: "WOW, what a bizarre dream that was. Who knew elephants could fly Boeing 74s's" I will wonder about that a moment and then forget it. Most often, I'll remain in a quasi sleep state as I am about to wake up ... it seems for hours while some long thing plays out. It is those that are the most interesting to me. They usually have the answer to something I've been puzzling over. If I don't keep them going as I wake, they are lost. So I start writing immediately, going over and over the story line until I'm fully awake and the story is now part of my waking sate and I can properly analyze it.

Interesting stuff.

Jim hit the nail on the head, I think.

One other thought: do you think you might benefit by a traditional goal setting exercise? I benefited greatly from following exactly the program in Allan Lakein's book. I did that in the very early '70's and it has made all the difference. I have updated my long term goals list every year on my birthday and I consult it monthly when I do my monthly review.

In case you've not read about it it is quite a thorough exercise. It takes several days of work (on and off, leaving some time for percolation) to get the list in good shape, then much longer to hone it to a fine edge ;-)

I won't describe the exercise here (unless you really want me to) but the output of it is a prioritized list of your long term goals. Being who I am (of course ;-) MY output was that list broken down by time period into the following periods:

- Lifetime
- 20 years
- 10 years
- 5 years
- 3 years
- 2 years
- 1 year
- 6 months
- 3 months
- Next Month
- This Month
- Next Week
- This Week

As you can see, I spend a lot of time in the present looking at goals which drive my current task list, but those are derived from the longer time periods. My weekly review centers on the last three buckets and my list of current projects and actions. My priorities are top down and kept in alignment by my monthly review which looks at the whole structure and concentrates on the yearly and monthly time frame.

So, as a concrete example:

At the top I might have broad generalizations about living an adventurous life. The I ask myself what I mean by that and it would generate some goals in the 20 year time frame (like maybe "Climb Mt. Everest ;-). But what would I have to do by the 10 year mark to make that happen? And so on. As it gets more near term (one year or less, mostly) I am in what would be called the "project" level. And projects have monthly milestones which I can only reach by completing some things this week or next ... and then I'm at the action level. So, what I am going to do today is governed by my long term goals or life's necessities. For example, my schedule for today is:

- Read for about four hours (which is in my lifetime learning program, in general, and some specific sub goals in particular ... such as languages and history)

- Go to hardware store for materials for project which will improve my home which leads to happiness with my current living environment as well as equity appreciation which leads to long term financial goals

- Mop the kitchen floor -- which I can link back in a similar way but really just comes down to those pesky things one has to do ;-)

O.K. I hear the chorus laughing that no one plans their life that far in advance ... after all, things change and who would want to plan for what might never happen? Well, I do, and I can tell you that my lifetime goals have been remarkably stable and I'm on track for most of them. Some have now changed in scope a bit. (Age takes it toll ;-) But in some incarnation or other, my goals have either been accomplished or I'm on track to accomplishing them. Now, when they are hauling my remains off to a hole in the ground somewhere will they be saying "Poor sap, he enver did reach his goal of climbing Mt. Everest!"? Yes, I'm sure some will see it that way. But I'm reminded of Feynman ... "Tuva or Bust!" I really got that! LOL ;-)

The whole point of goals is not to reach them, but to journey toward them.

Just some early morning ramblings ;-)
