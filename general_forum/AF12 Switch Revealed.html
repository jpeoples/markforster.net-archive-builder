I've tested my new method of combining AF1 and AF2 now for long enough to confident in letting other people have a crack at it.

Here's how it works.

You always start the day in AF2

How to switch from AF2 to AF1: AF1 is entered as a task on the list. You go back to AF1 whenever that task stands out in the normal course of working AF2. Delete the task and re-enter it like any other recurring task. When going back to AF1 you always start from the beginning.

How to switch from AF1 to AF2: You switch to AF2 whenever you come to a page in which no tasks stand out for you. The switch therefore takes the place of dismissal in AF1.

Dismissing tasks is carried out at the beginning of each day in accordance with AF2 rules.

If for any reason, no switch has been made to AF1 during a day, then the following morning the task "AF1" should be deleted and re-entered at the end of the list. This is to prevent the task from getting buried. [I haven't needed to use this rule yet].

That's it! Apart from these rules, both AF1 and AF2 are worked exactly as before.

Just to remind you:

AF1 is described at http://www.markforster.net/autofocus-system/

AF2 is described at http://www.markforster.net/blog/2009/6/27/autofocus-2-time-management-system-af2.html

A sample AF page is given at http://www.markforster.net/blog/2009/7/23/autofocus-2-sample-page.html

A few comments on the above rules:

This switch changes the dynamics of both AF1 and AF2. AF1 becomes much less draconian because dismissal is according to AF2 rules. AF2 tends to concentrate on the last few pages and there is less tension pulling you back to the beginning of the list. However what is lost in each system is more than made up by the other system.

MartyH:

<< I don't see how it fits in with step 2, "Work backwards from the last item looking at each task in turn until a task 'stands out'” and your admonition to start at the end of the list each time. >>

I was drawing a distinction from what happened in AF1. If you were near the beginning of the list and something near the end of the list became urgent, then you could not go directly to it without doing at least one task on each intervening page. This made it necessary to have a separate "do it anyway" rule for urgent items.

With AF2, if a task in the early part of the list becomes urgent, then it is open to your intuition to make none of the intervening tasks stand out. So you don't have to do any intervening tasks.

With the new AF1/AF2 system this is still the case because if you are in AF1 and an item becomes urgent somewhere else in the list, then it is open to your intuition to find no tasks it prefers to do on the next page, change to AF2 and then move to the urgent task in the normal AF2 way.

So what I meant is that the next task can be any task on the list regardless of position without needing to bend the rules.

2009-07-27 17:22
Jessica:

<< then they could end up all game for dismissal at the same time >>

We need to be clear that the threat of dismissal is intended to encourage you to do them - not discourage you!

But dismissing an item is not the end of the story - it's a signal to make you examine why you have been so reluctant to do that activity. So if you found that you had gone swimming, but hadn't found any time to do weights, ride your bike or go for a walk, then you might query whether you really have time to do all four, and whether you'd do better just to concentrate on the swimming.

In any case, even if you do put them all in the list in one block, they will get split up as you do them and re-enter them.
