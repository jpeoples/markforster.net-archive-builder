Mark's systems are very flexible, so do what works best.
1. Do you have to break it up? Depending on the process and the project, you might get along just fine with just naming the project and calling the whole your task.

But if you get stuck on the big item:
2. Just write down the first thing you need to do. Or any thing you need to do on that big item. Or several things. Whichever is easiest for you.

It's usually not effective to try to list every detail that needs doing up front, so don't.

Reply by Mark Forster

RogerL:

The mistake a lot of people make when breaking large jobs into smaller chunks is thinking that they need to break the entire project down.

In some cases this may be a good idea, especially if you want to write a checklist for someone else to follow or to ensure you don't forget any steps of a procedure.

But in the majority of cases, it is enough to write down just the first few steps as Alan says. As you progress, you can break it down further.

Good question & it got me thinking:

The Pomodoro Technique may help here. If a task is less than 1 pomodoro (standard time is 25 minutes but can be altered to suit the user) then add it up with other tasks to make it up to 1 pomodoro, for example 'inbox to zero session'.

If the task is estimated to be greater than 7 pomodoros (7 x 25 minute sessions or approx 3.5 hours) then break it down, for example 'prepare report for presentation next week'. This could form part of a larger project like 'win acme contract'.

Personally, I find the Pomodoro Technique very difficult to use as a system, however I do feel this rule of thumb for task estimation and breakdown is useful.
