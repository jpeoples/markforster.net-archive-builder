Reply by Mark Forster

2009-05-11 23:17
Of course what we are all doing is trying to find the right system for us, and therefore developing your own rules about dismissing is perfectly legitimate.

However it's important to remember that when you change a rule you also change the way your intuition approaches the list as a whole. If your rule is that you will only dismiss an item if it relates to your current context, then that is going to affect how you approach that item on previous passes through that page, and consequently how you approach the other items on the same page. And because your intuition is looking at the list as a whole as well as the page you are currently on, the rule change will affect the way you approach every item on the list.

Reply by Mark Forster

David:

The answer to your specific query is that the situation you describe never arises for me because my list stays in one place - on my desk. When I'm away from my desk I am not working on the list (though I may be working on something which is on the list).

Nevertheless of course situations do arise when I am not able to do any of the tasks on a page for some reason or other. In which case I dismiss them. They will have been on the page for quite some time - I could have done them when I had the chance. I didn't - so out they go!

Note that by sticking to this rule, I am making it more likely that I will pick the items when I *am* able to do them - because I know that if I don't do them now they may get dismissed next time round.

If I were in Avrum's situation, I would consider having two lists, one for home and one for coffee shop, especially since there seems to be quite a clear divide between them.

If that weren't practical for some reason, then I would dismiss tasks according to the rules. Even in his circumstances the tasks will have been on the list for quite a number of days before they are in danger of being dismissed.

Reply by Mark Forster

2009-05-12 19:23
David:

My practice when attending conferences and such like is to have a separate notebook for travelling. I would only put into it those tasks which I need as part of the travelling (e.g. finalise conference presentation, book table for dinner, confirm return flight reservation, check email, etc.), plus specific projects which I have decided I will work on during the conference - which means I will take the necessary documents with me.

During the conference any additional tasks get added to the list as they come up. If tasks come up which I can only do at home, I make a note of them in my notetaking notebook (i.e. not my travelling AF one) and transfer them to my Home AF book on return, plus any uncompleted ones from the travelling book.

I find this works far better than carrying my main AF notebook with me all the time - as I said earlier that never leaves my desk.

2009-05-14 21:56
I follow the dismissal rule strictly, as Mark recommends, because I don't like changing the psychology of the way the dismissal rules work.

And I agree with Mark, that usually you wouldn't even consider dismissing a page, till you've had many opportunities to take action on those items.

That said, I do sometimes have the situation where something really does seem important, and I *wish* I could work on it, but don't have the tools handy, or am in the wrong place, or something like that.

In those cases, I sometimes take some tiny insignificant action, and move the item forward.

Let's say the item is on my home list, and it's "Call insurance company", but it's 10:00 PM and they are closed. The item "stands out" but I just can't take action. And I really can't dismiss this item, since it has too many ramifications. So, there are lots of things I can do.

I can rewrite the item "Call insurance company", and also note the phone number.

I can pull out my insurance file, and look it over, and put it in my backpack to carry around with me as an extra reminder, and then cross off the task and add it again to the end.

I can add the item to my office list as well, so maybe I'll see it during the day when I am more able to call them.

I can make a "to call" list, and then schedule a block of time on my calendar to make all those calls during the day that I tend to forget until it's already after hours.

I can think over the problem and ask myself why I have resisted calling them for so long, to the point that the task is facing dismissal. And then rewrite the task with some special emphasis, e.g., "Call insurance company or you'll never get reimbursed for the $1200 they owe you!!"

I might also put the item on the calendar, or into my Outlook reminders, as an extra tickler.

The idea is that the item really is "standing out", so it seems OK to me to take these kinds of actions and not dismiss the task.

Personally, I had a lot of trouble with this issue, because I resisted keeping two lists (work and home), to the point that I felt that AF wasn't working for me.

The idea of keeping a single list is very tempting, but for me it was hard to manage. So I gave it a try, and my problems were solved. This is how it works for me:

* Both of them are always with me (I actually use a file that I can access from everywhere). The items on my "Work List" aren't necessarily work-related, there are LOTS of things that are personal, but I can do that when I'm working (paying a bill through the internet, et c).

* Many items appear on both lists, and this proved very efficient for me, especially when considering that my home list is rather neglected when compared to the other one.

* Sometimes I write in my work list "Review home list", and I when I reach that task, I give the whole home list a read, without performing anything, and without dismissing. It's just a read to verify that I'm not missing anything important. If that happens, I schedule a proper time to process my home list when I'm there.

* Since I started using two lists, I can dismiss without fear. In fact, I learned that "dismissing" is an amazing tool. Sometimes, when I reach that point, I actually feel relief, since the tasks I'm "freezing" were causing me guilt, and this way I can take a little distance from them and forget them for a while. But this is another topic anyway, just wanted to point it out.


I would suggest you to try keeping two or more lists. You can always go back if you want to anyway.

Walter
