I have been evolving an adapted system that is working well in my work situation, incorporating elements of short No-list and long list systems that work for me. Comments welcome!

The broad approach is that (a bit like Seraphim) I want to include elements of short/no-list systems, which for me bring focus and energy, and also a long list / seedbed approach, which I need to keep track of everything over a period. Here it is:

I keep all my work tasks on a simple Word document. Working down from the top, there are several sections:

1. Today’s date and a short list of tasks intended for action today.
This can be freely added to at any time.

2. Completed items section, to drag and drop (or write) tasks into as they are done.
This I like to paste into a Completed items diary at the end of the day, as I find it helpful to be able to look back over what was done when. So, this section starts each day empty.

3. A short list of repeating items to be copied into the day section each morning, eg check calendars for forthcoming period (incl my wife’s!), check all yesterday’s emails and meeting notes have been processed, review list as below.

4. A long list of tasks. New items can be added to the top as they arise. This gets reviewed every morning at minimum, and items that stand out are moved into the brief action list at the top. If day items are not cleared (I try to avoid this, but unexpected things do come up), they are moved back to the top of the long list at the end of the day. Items that are not moving for any reason show themselves by gravitating towards the bottom.

And that’s it! It’s working well for me (I seem to be clearing away a backlog for the first time in a while), I’m appreciating the short focused list, a bit like the will-do list in DIT, as well as the catch-all option to track things that may take longer to come to the fore (or be dropped). Not sure how similar to Seraphim’s Serial No-List this is, if it is it’s an accident! Plan is now to continue for a period (a non-Lent challenge) and track how it goes.

If you're using Word for Windows, a very quick way to move paragraphs up and down is to click inside the paragraph, press Alt+Shift+up arrow or down arrow. Apart from Ctrl+S and Ctrl+Z, it's the combo I use most often in my day job as a tech writer.

That key combo also works for moving rows up and down inside a table. MUCH easier than cut and paste.

I like the idea of this hybrid document very much. If you give each section head a style of Heading 1 (or whatever heading level suits you), you can use the Navigation Pane on the View tab to quickly hop around inside the document.

It seems very close!


<< 4. A long list of tasks. New items can be added to the top as they arise. This gets reviewed every morning at minimum, and items that stand out are moved into the brief action list at the top. >>

If you change this rule slightly, it will be very close indeed.

First, instead of reviewing every morning, you could do it like this: cycle through the Today section over and over till nothing stands out. Then take a single pass through the Long List and either take action on tasks, or copy them to the Today section.

Second, if there is anything left over in the Today section at the end of the day, you could just move it to the top of the Long List for later processing, and start the next day fresh with a new Today section.

These two changes would make it pretty much identical to Serial No-List, unless I am missing something.
