Go AF4. I have not read DIT, but I understand that AF4 is very similar to DIT's method. I think AF4 is a very slick method and reject the older ones as obsolete. I propose you print out the AF4 method, learn its basics, and then read DIT and substitute AF4 where appropriate.

Keep in mind that the AF methods are about lists of tasks, and the DIT book discusses more than just lists so it isn't all replaced.

I think the key to making any of these methods work is to remember that you are always free to skip over a task. If your mind is nagging you to get to something way down the list, let it pull you along until you get there. This means you can always do what you want to do, only the List will stop you if you see something really important (that you forgot about) along the way.

Just go with AF4. It is simple and makes good sense and the odds are that you'll find it a good fit. Don't load it up with tons of stuff. Just grab some kind of notebook and start writing down what you have to do ... draw a line at the end when you are done and bingo, you now have a filled Closed List followed by an empty Open list. Now follow the instructions, adding incomplete and new things to the end of your Open list. It will make sense as you work the list ... it makes much less sense to hear it described.

If you want, you could watch Andreas' excellent recording of Marks day long working of the list (speeded up to hyper speed ;-) That makes it all clear.

I'm inclined to throw in a slightly dissenting opinion.

AF4 closely resembles the original Autofocus (AF1), which is why, as has been already pointed out, you can get a better understanding of the underlying principles from the original (AF1) instructions. Compared to AF1 and AF4, AF2 and AF3 (RAF) very strongly stress urgency. They look like aberrations to me, and therefore I never quite understood why Mark came out with them (although I remember the discussions that resulted in 'reverse mode'). AF4 is much closer to the spirit of Autofocus, and I came back to use AF (in a way) with AF4, which is probably the undisputedly best version of AF.

However, one size won't fit all. It all comes down to your specific work (and life) situation. If your work is driven by urgency, RAF (AF3) is worth a look. AF (all versions) is probably at its best when you're working alone (even more so, from home).

Personally, I had many problems with AF (stopped using it after AF1) because of me being a member of a team/hierarchy with a highly structured workflow with much back-and-forth interaction and many very short deadlines (less than a day, but longer than 'immediate'), while still having a lot of important, longer-term 'background' projects. It's the latter ones that have always been the major problem for me (with any time management system). Another problem I keep having with AF is that most of my tasks are quite complex and not easily broken down into 'little and often' chunks, because it takes way too long to 'get into it' every time.

To me, DIT seems to play a lot nicer with the timescale of those deadlines. It's more structured than AF, and it has different lists for different types of urgencies/deadlines ('tomorrow', 'today', 'immediate'). In addition, AF doesn't really accommodate 'Waiting for' and 'Agenda' items (both are GTD terms) very well, while both are easier to incorporate in DIT (IMHO).

That being said, I would recommend to give DIT a fair chance. It might fit your work, your life or your personality just fine. OTOH, DIT takes quite a bit of discipline to stick to it in the long run, and many people (me included) tend to 'fall off the wagon'. Procrastination-busting seems to be where AF shines most.

Recently, I started to tinker with a combination of DIT and AF4. AF4 looks like a good candidate to tackle the DIT 'backlog' (not to be confused with the AF4 'backlog'). DIT should give me some up-front structure, while I still wanted to use the strengths of AF(4). However, I soon started to experience a dominance of 'DIT mode' over 'AF mode', much like I (and others) experienced with some additions to the original AF1 (like an 'urgency card'). It's too early for conclusions, though.

Closing remark: As has already been stated, DIT is more of a complete 'system' than AF, so many elements and principles of DIT can be used in conjunction with AF.

As far as the Autofocus systems go, AF4 is the most developed and balanced. For most people it will be the best. Do it.
