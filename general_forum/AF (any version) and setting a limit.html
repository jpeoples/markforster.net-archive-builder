>> I really don't get how putting them on the list helps ... but I know that it somehow does.

Perhaps it's because so many of our decisions (particularly when we procrastinate) are made unconsciously. Knee-jerk, mindless websurfing is an activity, usually triggered by anxiety or unpleasant emotional state. AF provides a number of tools to increase mindfulness, and helpful guilt, vis-a-vis procrastination.

1) You need to stop and consider your next action/activity.
2) You write it down
3) You engage in the activity with full awareness (because of step 1 and 2) of what you're doing

Now, the above isn't a panacea for a career you hate, an abusive relationship or a methamphetamine addiction. But it can serve as a catalyst to reconsider how you're living your life. And in my line of work, self-reflection is the first step towards change.

Leon, you said:

"it is possible to complete many small easy tasks in a day but these may not be of a high value or that important."

I'd look at my task phrasing very closely if you find this happening. One of the keys to getting those larger, high value tasks done (if you resist them) is to break them down enough so that they are as easy to do as one that's easy just by the nature of the task. I've done this both on-list and off-list as a checklist. At work, I have fairly large projects on my list and I tend to use checklists off-list a lot, more to clarify my thinking of how I want to approach doing the project but the process of writing it also gets you absorbed in that project and somehow wanting to finish it off (like you want to pick up everything on your grocery list because you don't want to go back to the store). Half the time, I abandon the checklist part way through and just keep on working and don't even realize I've done so, but it's served its purpose. Breaking the projects down within the list doesn't work well for me at work, but works very well at home for some reason.

Part of the beauty of AF is that (if they're on your list - and they are on your list, aren't they?) you eventually *have* to finish those projects - or say that you aren't going to do them. So try tweaking your list with phrasing, granularity, even structure of what goes on the list or tweaking yourself, but don't tweak the system.

The one thing that I have missed in doing AF because I've worked so "little and often" is that it doesn't feel sometimes like I've even done a project. Back when I renovated my last house, I would put in 8 hour days doing a major project and finish in a day or a weekend where now I'm working on renovating a bathroom almost daily and it's sometimes hard to see the accomplishment (and that's a visible project - it's even more true when it's not so visible at work like developing system improvements). But the truth is that I had to push myself pretty hard to put in those 8 hour days and now I don't really have to push myself at all.

If you don't know when to stop for the day, try adopting something like Neil Fiore's "Unschedule" and book yourself an imaginary appointment to watch that movie at 8 p.m. with your wife. Or stop when it's time to put your kids to bed and the rest of the night is free. Unless you have an odd work schedule, the stop time for work is when you leave to go home, isn't it? If you're self employed or work at home, you can set yourself a stop time of 5 p.m. I would think?

It does work on the basis of number of tasks completed but also identifies days in which overall numbers have increased or decreased, as well as percentage of tasks actioned. I know what you mean about "completion" but when you think about it the vast majority of tasks are not actually one off's but require repeat entry. One thing AF has taught me is that productivity is not about task completion, but about time being spent on "productive" action. The very act of putting tasks on your list creates your table of productive actions. If they turn out not to be necessary, the system will deal with them by review/dismissal. The more you use AF the more you find that the quality of tasks changes - you end up with a pattern of tasks that - to you - are productive. The biggest mistake we make - and the reason for a lot of procrastination - is that we see certain essential tasks as "unimportant" and therefore feel that time spent on them is "wasted". The fact is that it is often those seemingly unimportant tasks that pave the way for the doing of the "important" stuff.

Hope that helps ...... :-)
