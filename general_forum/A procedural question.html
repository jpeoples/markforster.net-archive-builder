This is a question about recurring, low urgency tasks, such as checking e-mail, --something which I can do obsessively if I'm not careful.

Do people put tasks like these on their lists?

It might be a bit silly to have to keep going down my list until "check e-mail" stands out. It might be easier just to check it as soon as the impulse arises, and be done with it.
On the other hand, having to scan my list before checking my e-mail might reduce my desire to check it. And I might find myself doing a more challenging and interesting task instead.

Any thoughts on how to deal with tasks like these? (For instance, a task like checking in on this discussion group. which I often do four or five times a day....)

Steve,

I think what goes on the list depends on what you need prompting to do. I.E. would you put "put on my socks" , "tie my shoe laces" on the list? I doubt it. However, if you had a severe neurological problem you might ... because it might not get done otherwise.

I don't put "check email" on my list because I don't operate in an email driven environment. The worst that would happen if I failed to check it for a few days is that some people might wonder what became of me ;-) If I did operate in such an environment, then I'd go to some kind of "push" system where it would be beeping at me constantly.

I do a lot of reading and researching. But that is so important to me that I don't forget anything so nothing goes on an AF list. I just have time blocked in the early AM and go to work then. What I do in particular is driven by what I was last doing and I know what that is so I just let it flow. I do keep extensive notes and some of those notes are about other things to read ... BUT, I don't AF them because they will mature on their own. IOW, when I review my notes I'll see that I wanted to read something and I'll do it, or schedule a task to buy the book or whatever.

Steve:

That's an important point you make. It's also one which was very much emphasised in my book "Do It Tomorrow". What we are trying to do is to get away from the random impulses which keep people answering email or web-surfing all day and instead to deal with these subjects systematically. So it is indeed important to put this type of action on the AF list.

If some of you have a compulsion to check this Forum all the time, you can imagine what it's like for me! But I keep the whole thing well under control by dealing with Forum posts only when AF says so. The same with Email. The same with Facebook. The same with Twitter.

On the rare occasions I allow myself to deal with these things without AF "approval" it's sometimes hours before I come back to what I'm supposed to be doing!
