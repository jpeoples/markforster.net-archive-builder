I'm intrigued how the mounting pressure to do something about an obstinate task leads to lateral thinking about the problem at hand.

I'd added to my list "Recontact Sarah". I haven't seen her for some time, during which she's married, and so changed name, and moved. It will take a little effort to track her down. But I did get a card from her announcing the birth of her first child, so that will tell what I need to know, if only I can find it.

Going through the last few years of personal filing is an unappetising task, so I have procrastinated on it until it's the last item on the page. Did I really have to do this tedious thing now, or choose to consigning recontacting an old friend to the rejected tasks list?

And then I realised that another friend probably knew the necessary information. One quick email to him, and I had a repy quite soon afterwards.

This makes for an interesting contrast with GTD. I've never been an adherent of that, but as I understand it a key idea is to write down the next action for each goal. Doing that, I would have written "Find Sarah's address in files". It would have taken a leap of imagination to escape that tramline once committed to in writing. AF's encouragement to refactor the question has the opposite effect, deferring definition of the next action as long as possible, and leaving more room for the discovery of creative alternatives.

It's ironic that the deferral I'm enthusing about here is itself a kind of intentional procrastination.

All the above comments are very helpful. This is a sharp divide between GTD and AF. Despite the fact that both of them subscribe to the principle of analysis, which urges us to divide big things into small, digestible pieces, their implementations of that principle differ markedly (sorry about that).

The NA concept forbade phrasing items vaguely. With AF I usually phrase my items by some vague reference to the overall project. Then when it's time to action that item I shape that action to fit the time available, my energy level, etc. In AF the plan is to do _something_, but what that something is, is usually not predetermined. In GTD the plan is to do the next required physical action.

In reality, the restrictions on NA formulations were almost impossible to fulfill. Any action can have an infinite number of descriptions. Is it "Kill John" or "Flex my right index finger"? How we describe it is extremely sensitive to our mood at any given time. Is it "Call potential customer," or "Open telephone list to find customer's phone number"? It all depends, and AF allows much more sensitivity to this indeterminacy.
