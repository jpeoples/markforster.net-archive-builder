The reasons for how different personal management systems are structured are various. That creates a feel and a flow of work different for each of them. But there is a universal underlying reason to the creation of every system: People want to be reassured, feel safe/confident that what they do is the best thing they could now. It stems from a purely emotional place. The caveat for this is that things must be compared to one another. So there is a tendency to hoard things which is natural.

With this in mind, many find it difficult to let something go for it diminishes that sense of stability and confidence in the system. If you are not afflicted by that "condition", then all the better for you. You don't have to wait to dismiss anything ;)

Now once people get comfortable with a system like AF and the auto-dismissal parts, letting go of that rule creates a sense of unbalance and the system cannot be trusted anymore because it's like having none in the first place. In other words, people leave the hard decision of letting something go to the system (they don't do it themselves). If something bad happens because of that, them it's how they used the system that is at fault, not an error in decision. Next time, they would probably move a little on those uncertain things to keep them alive.

It also brings people to taking the time to take a hard long look at what is going to be dismissed and why; to readjust themselves and their commitments towards these things. Something that is seldom done in this fast paced day an age. Once you are comfortable and feel safe and secure with a system, why want to jeopardize it's effectiveness and efficiency by letting a rule go...?

I'm not a hoarder but my wife is. She buys new dishes, I throw the old stuff out :) I'm tending towards the minimalism way of thinking, she doesn't. It's not her fault or mine. My father was like me and my wife was raised to hoard stuff. The roots of these feelings run deep and it is perhaps too easy to try and ascribe a logical reasoning to an emotional condition. And let's remember that intuition itself is influenced by both!

Let me see if I can be plain...

The general populace is told by anally-retentive people to "just write down what you want to do today and start with the first item and work till you get to the last one." This is an arbitrary structure imposed onto doing tasks.

Along comes Mark Forster – the brilliant one (my sincere sentiment). He challenges the commonsense of the anally-retentive, saying, "Yes, write down all the things you need to do and let your intuition guide you on the order in which you do the tasks – do those that 'stand out'."

In our enthusiastic embrace of this new breath of fresh air, we write down everything we can think of "because the system will guide us." But, a small problem arose. Because of our excitement, we have written down everything – even things we never plan on doing. After a while, we are faced with the problem of those tasks that just sit there and sit there and sit there.....and we pile up pages and pages of mostly done tasks. At some point critical mass is reached ending in an atomic explosion of procrastination.

So, what do we do? We place an arbitrary structure onto these lagging tasks, boldly saying, "Here is what you are to do in order to process these lagging tasks. It's all here in three easy steps!" As the systems are tried, they begin to suffer the same problems as writing down a list of things to do and starting at the beginning, breeding new method after new method of dismissal. All because of an arbitrary structure that has been imposed onto these lagging tasks.

My point – my only point – was to ask why would intuition not be the answer to the arbitrary structure of dismissal as intuition was indeed the answer to the arbitrary structure of processing tasks in order beginning with the first.

I believe and propose that intuition is indeed the answer to dismissal. I don't believe I've seen a compelling argument against it. In fact, I think arguments against it unwittingly become arguments against the entirety of the AutoFocus Systems whatever the stripe.
