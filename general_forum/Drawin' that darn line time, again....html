Reply by Mark Forster

2009-06-28 12:07
Quleczka:

The whole point of the dismissal process is that it encourages you to move back to the beginning of the list, rather than spend all your time on the last few pages. There are two ways it does this:

1) Certain items are on notice for dismissal. So to avoid having to dismiss them you need to move to the beginning of the list. While doing this you have to look at every item on the way, so that helps to keep the entire contents of the list in your mind.

2) In order to avoid having to dismiss a large number of tasks at once, you need to keep the tasks well broken up. Again that encourages you to move back so that you can chip away at any large blocks. For instance I have a block of 5 items nearly at the beginning of my list. If I don't do some of them today, they will all be on notice for dismissal tomorrow. Since they are all items I want to do, I am encouraged to do some today and some tomorrow, rather than have to do all of them tomorrow (which I might not have time for).

I think a small, but important issue was clarified in this thread for me today. Working on any one or more item in the block saves it from dismissal. There is no necessity to complete all of the items in the block, although there is an incentive to do so.

For eg, I have a line drawn under a block of three open tasks, above and below of which are completed tasks. The act of completing the lowermost task only for that day moves the line upwards to the other two open tasks, which are now threatened for the following day. Does that sound right Mark?

By the same token, broken up tasks expose fewer blocks for dismissal, as Mark mentions here.

A lazy Sunday spent on AF 2 found me furiously adding tasks to the end of the list. It is so much easier to do this, knowing that they will tackled soon. I find myself breaking down my tasks a lot more, which makes the resistance to them lower. I added 36 tasks and completed 38, but a lot more focus on the urgent stuff that comes in and requires attention fast. Once I got a grip, moving further back became easier, and finally reached the threatened task. I saved it by breaking down the project into bite sized bits and moved it to the end of the list. Overall, I am noticing a faster pace.

On the subject of intuition, I initially thought that I need to be pretty sharp about what stands out to me because its a dead stop at that task and then back to the end of the list. But in practice, I found you can be wrong and get away with it, because you are constantly reviewing the list from the bottom up. If you missed it the first time, chances are your mind will engage it when you pass through it again. A lot more forgiving.

All in all, I'm having a great start.
