2009-04-16 9:46
Christine is pointing at DIT's biggest weakness: usually you should do everything that comes your way the next day, with exceptions of course. If you can't find the time you will have to rewrite things or constantly check if there are incomplete items on the previous days. This translates to a rather high maintenance of the system itself.

This is one of the big advantages of AF even if used as a paper system: you only rewrite things once you brought them forward in some way.

But these are all small points, in comparison both are very competent tools for managing your workload. Just check which one works best.

In DIT I used to use the same system as the one I now use for AF: put a cross on a page if it is finished and put a circle round the cross if there are no active pages before it. That way you can see instantly whether there are any earlier items.

Klaus:

It depends what you mean when you say "I always rewrote the tasks that didn't get completed". If you mean you re-entered them after doing some work on them, then that's perfectly ok. DIT supports a "little and often" approach in the same way as AF does.

If though you mean that you transferred tasks you hadn't started, then the danger of doing this is that you can't keep track of how many days backlog you have. You can keep track of this in other ways of course (such as a star system for every day you finish all your tasks), so it's not a huge problem. Re-writing the tasks can have the effect of impressing them on your mind, so there are positive things to be said for it as well.
