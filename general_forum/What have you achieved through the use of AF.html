I don't think I'm necessarily more effective at work, but the contrast between my work life, which worked well and my home life, where I procrastinated constantly has been a source of anxiety and depression for me for many years.

In the 4 1/2 YEARS I've lived in this house (which I purchased with the intent to renovate and sell at a profit) prior to AF I accomplished the following:

- painted 2 ceilings
- painted two rooms
- painted the front door
- finished the front steps
- hardwood flooring purchased and sitting in the garage
- lights, paint, shelving, various other stuff, bought but never used, sitting in the garage or storage room

In the 3 months since beginning AF on January 5, I have accomplished the following:

- large office bookshelves painted - lots of moulding so a fairly time consuming job, also one wall with fairly intricate paneling
- two sets of stair railings primed and painted
- new dishwasher and 2 toilets installed
- new garage door opener installed
- carpets removed from 1/2 the house (and carpet tacks too!)
- several upstairs rooms painted and lower bathroom as well
- laundry room cleaned
- furnace room cleaned
- casings and baseboards removed from 4 rooms and halls
- popcorn ceiling removed from 1 large room
- highly annoying mdf that was screwed AND glued down pulled up from front door entrance
- most of the basement painted (about 1200 s.f.).
- hardwood flooring acclimating in the house, until I find the nailer I'm looking for
- currently working on the main bathroom vanity, I have the old one 1/2 pulled apart

Other things accomplished at home include:
- about 25 trunk loads of donated stuff taken to goodwill or the recycling center (about 1/2 of my book collection too which amounted to a lot of boxes) and at least 10 garbage bags full of clothes as well as all my "baby stuff" disposed of (my baby is 8 years old)
- a bunch of stuff disposed of on freecycle
- a neater home with the main areas clean or within 5 minutes of being company ready
- fulfilled the request of a 2007 tax audit which I had procrastinated on for 5 months prior to AF but got finished within 2 weeks with AF (had to wait for stuff to come in the mail)
- current year taxes done and ready to push the button to file online (I don't want to file until my 2007 audit is cleared) - in contrast to last year when I filed late, but justified it because I got a refund
- several ski and snowshoing weekends with friends and family (none this winter prior to AF)
- entire DVD collection (~300) catalogued and put into binders
- watched 2 seasons of Dexter - had the first season for over a year...
- started knitting a scarf for a good friend (I've had the supplies for over 2 years)
- I've been the first in my neighbourhood to have my sidewalk shoveled rather than the last - sometimes I'm even out there before it stops snowing :-)
- a much cleaner car
- I've spent more time with my kids and it's been time that's focused more on them
- sent my dad's birthday card on time vs. prior years where I would phone him (probably a couple of days late) because I procrastinated

I do think the qualitative ways my life has improved has been more important in many ways though - I would say my desire to have a glass of wine (or two or three) to relax at home has been reduced to 1/10 of "normal". I haven't felt cranky or depressed once since I started using AF and this was an almost daily occurrence before. In fact, I think I avoided being at home as much as possible. I've been far less introspective in the last 3 months, stopped planning more than I've been doing, have had days and even whole weekends where I didn't escape into reading. I now have a desire to finish things I've started and not go along with the novelty of something new - just to the point of buying the supplies, but not actually doing anything with them. I previously could never *make* myself do housework etc. except for Saturday or Sunday mornings, now I do a bit every day and don't resent it and don't feel overwhelmed by it.

I should mention that since starting AF, I have been working one full time job as well as putting in about 15 hours a week on a consulting contract - and a 1.75 hour daily commute. Prior to this, I only had one job, where I occasionally worked overtime, but not on a regular basis.

Gad, in comparison to that I blush ;-)

I have not done anything particularly different, I have just done more. There were a number of areas in which things kind of "drifted" ... getting done, but not necessarily on a timely schedule. That is more under control now as I am continually reminded and catch things before they get out of control.

I have a better understanding of what is on my plate and manage it better. IOW, I had not realized how many things I was trying to do and so felt disappointed when things did not get done. Now I see how much there is and understand that there is just too much on my list and am able to logically decide things to eliminate. (In this regard I guess I have one step added to my system ... sometimes when I go through a page I just cross off (dismiss) something even though it does not need to be dismissed by the rule. I just realize that I am NOT going to do it so I don't let it hang around a minute longer. (Decisions are our friends! ;-)

Finally, my overall productivity has improved. I had planned to keep metrics on AF and here is where I anticipated using them, but I decided to trash that Idea some time ago. I realized that the metrics would not help me in any way and I did not like the overhead and the complexity it added to the basic simplicity of the system. I had started adding start and end dates and all of that. I gave it up along with my beloved tags ;-)

To summarize, AF has provided me with two things: increased productivity and better understanding and control.

I'll play! Been doing Autofocus since Feb 1 after many years of GTD. Here's what I like about AF--

Much less set up time, no more breaking down each item to it's "next action" and no more determining if it is a next action, someday/maybe, etc. No more contexts for me either. I use to spend about an hour a day on "processing" alone-- now practically zero.

No more weekly review. Going through my open pages once a day or so and keeping a separate project and goal list obviates my need for a weekly review (something I had a hard time doing anyway).

Difficult tasks get done quicker. Do a little, cross it off, write it at the end. It works!

Simple tasks get done quicker. I used to fill guilty doing "simple" tasks when the more complex (and usually more important ones) did not get done. I would sometimes fall into a state of overwhelm where its relief was procrastination. Now I just move to a task that does not show resistance.

And lastly-- I like paper. Who would have thunk? I am a messy guy with horrible handwriting, who bough a Palm III when it first came out intending never to have to write reminders and such on paper again. But I love it. I use a standard spiral notebook, have no method towards what pens I use and write as messy as I want. It's liberating! Plus I feel a bit like a mad scientist when I look at my notebook from afar. I think my next notebook will be a hardbound lab book to complete the mad scientist motif.

I have not achieved any task in particular that I wouldn't have without AF. This is because I have been taking courses, so the majority of my important tasks have hard deadlines and not completing them is not really an option :-) However, I feel I have achieved some mental breakthroughs:

1) Not all tasks need to be done. (Unfortunately this doesn't apply to coursework ;-)
2) I do not need to plan every detail of my day in order to be productive.
3) In fact, planning (and many other "organizational" tasks) is often not worth the time I put in. Even my filing system has simplified.
4) I have limited time and energy during the day. I accept this, and work with it. This has given me an incredible sense of inner calm compared to the stress and frustration I used to feel.

More often than not over the last few months I have not had much discretionary time (or energy...) for AF due to a huge number of urgent tasks - assignments, presentations, meeting preparations. And yet I find myself applying the AF philosophy everywhere now. Project due tomorrow? I look at my project list and use AF to get through it. Need to clean my place before guests come? Create a mental to do list and AF my way through it. You could say I am just planning on the fly but it is deceptively organized and efficient compared to what I used to do (plan, then re-plan, then re-plan again, then panic). And it feels organic, natural. Perhaps I am a hippie at heart...
