What does “Busy Life” mean?

Let me start by saying it again: “AF is an excellent idea, beautiful out-of-the-box thinking, a great system.” Let me add: “Mark Forster is by far the most original and interesting time management thinker today, in my opinion.”

I read with great interest all the posts that have been added to the thread I started, questioning the universality of AF. I suggested it would not work well for very busy people. Most of the commenters tried to convince the other readers and me by claiming how extremely busy they are and how well the system works for them nevertheless.

In my opinion nobody who has the time to read and post here, let alone beta-test AF, is truly very busy.

As I said before, I am semi-retired and lead what I call a leisurely life, yet I am too busy to beta-test, too busy to even to read more than maybe one percent of the posts here or to try to understand how this forum software works (erratic and unpredictable to me). Yet, compared to the times when I was REALLY busy, I lead a leisurely life.

Really busy people do not spend their time reading or writing forum posts or beta-testing any system however promising it may be.

This said, AF certainly lends itself very nicely to many people, and we all have to be grateful to Mark Forster for the time and effort creating it and bringing it to life.

But AF is not universal. And, quite frankly, I do not see why it should. Would that make it any more beautiful, or would the lack of universality make it any less attractive to those who find it useful? Every person is different, everybody’s life situation is different and keeps evolving over time. And everybody’s time management system should be tailored to their needs.

Today I use DIT with a few elements of GTD thrown in; it works well for me. When something misfires in my system, I have a look and adjust it. Maybe one day my situation will have changed sufficiently for me to consider AF.

In the mean time I hope that we will not see the quasi religious, dogmatic battles that have have been fought by some adopters of GTD about is universality and superiority.

I wish Mark Forster and you all the success with AF that you deserve.

Just my very humble opinion.

Chris

Hi Dean,

You Said:

"Main goal of any system is to get (right) things done."

I guess you mean that SHOULD be the main goal. Often systems tend to evolve to feed off of themselves. GtD seems to be one of those systems. It is so complex that one ends up doing GtD more than the tasks that GtD is supposed to help you do!

You Said:

"Originally many people got interested in GTD since one gathered *all* things from
psychic RAM and decided concrete next actions etc."

I, for one, got interested because it offered a way to dig out from a bunch of backlog ... and the initial steps of dumping it all into one big pile and working it down with a simple triage to end up with a big filled trash can and everything else filed, made lots of sense. What did NOT make sense was that "psychic RAM" garbage. People go way too far with metaphor and get lost in them, IMO. The human brain works very differently than a computer so it is misleading to try to explain mental processes as computer ones. Writing things down makes sense without the added baggage of the "psychic RAM" metaphor. Getting things out of your mind has limited application ... it is often useful to have things IN your mind for your subconscious to work on while you do other things.

You Said:

"However, GTD requires lot of work/maintenance. You first need to setup a system, collect and process everything, putting right contexts etc. I know, some people can get pay off from that. But still, it doesn't get things done (easily)."

Much of that work is required no matter what system you use. I found the idea of contexts to be wonderful ... until I found it to be nonsense ;-) I mean, the idea is one of those that SHOULD work well, but did not, for me at least. I have very few "contexts" as most of what I do I can do anytime. There are some things that do require a place and time, but I still don't see them as "contexts" but as "projects". For example: "Build a deck". Now that is not a context but a project ... true, I have to be home to do most of the things, but then that is so obvious that I don't really need to make an @HOME context in order to know that I can't pour footings if I am not at home! I mean, really now ;-)

For me it makes the most sense to group tasks by project, not where or when I do them. I need to be out running errands in order to pick up 2x4 lumber for the deck, but I'm really not apt to do that in conjunction with a run to the grocery store. I'll most likely make a special trip for it and take a truck! There are many common sense things that get squashed by the inflexibility of the GtD system. (As for how I do the same project in the AF model ... I just have the task "Work on Deck" on the list, then I pull out my project folder which has the plans, materials lists, reference material, etc. and see where I am ... then I grab the tools and a bottle of cold water and go outside to screw down some boards ;-) I tend to make use of blocked time more than AF would dictate. "Work on the Deck" would occupy me for several hours, the time being dictated more by my tolerance to the heat and humidity than my time management system! (This also made me an outcast from the GtD groupies ;-)

Hi Christine,

I don't know what makes one person stall in a cluttered environment and another thrive. It would be fascinating to research. In most areas of my life I'm a "clutter person". I've given a lot of thought to that and I have identified a mental process that causes/allows that: I simply don't notice it. Honestly, if I drop a sheet of paper on the floor I can step over it for weeks ... it is not bothering me so why should I bother it? LOL ;-)

OTOH, my desk MUST be clear. As must any "work" area. The first task in building a deck is to lay out the tools ... and the last is to put them away. When I worked in an electronics lab, my workbench was always orderly. When I worked in an office I would not go home for the day before I cleared the surface of all paper. There is something about a "work" environment that demands order. When I get "stuck" I will pull out a can of desk spray and a rag and actually CLEAN the desk. That seems to be my way of getting serious about things and might be my way of metaphorically enacting the task of "clearing the decks", as it were.

What baffles me is the question about why desks and work spaces must be cleared but other areas run wild. Someday I'll figure that one out ;-)

As to the whole "psychic RAM" business ... I get parts of that idea ... just not the metaphor nor the obsession over it. I have long had a notepad out to scribble random thoughts on to keep them out of my view as I was working. That makes sense to me. What does not is the obsessive list making so as to avoid the risk of thinking about anything ;-) For example: I posted elsewhere today about how the mind handles time and memory. David Allen, and his true believers, would have a collective heart attack at the notion that one would try to do something by remembering what it was. Yet most people do that all the time ... and live lives outside of mental institutions ;-)

It has been conjectured that the invention of the printing press and widespread availability of reading and writing began the decline of our memories. We used to have to remember things. The news used to be sung from village to village by those with memories that would put them in the various halls of fame. Once we no longer needed it, it began to decline. My big problem with the GtD obsession with NOT trusting ones memory is that it tends to destroy memory and, ultimately, one has to remember to write it down, look it up, and even where the list is kept. How will we do even that once memory has been finally and completely destroyed? I'm not sure how much of an exaggeration that is.

Speaking for myself, I scribble "reminders" so as to keep the mental chatter down. I don't need those notes for anything, I'll remember them again when needed. I also tend to work from memory a whole lot more than is politically correct to admit in some of these time management communities. I do it because I can, and because it works. At the end of a day I can pull out a list I consulted not once and find only a very few things not accomplished. (I'm talking about a list of things I honestly intended to do, not an AF list of unfiltered everything.)
