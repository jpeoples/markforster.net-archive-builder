Andy,

I too have been struggling with the system. Using DIT lingo, I have had a hard time integrating recurrent and same-day tasks. I feel stressed knowing that there are still some open recurrent or same-day tasks to do and they are not on the current page. I then scan the page for something to do so that I can get off that page and onto a page with one of those recurrent or same-day tasks.

But I do recognize that this system is devilishly clever. I daresay brilliant. It ingeniously uses a very basic principle of behaviorist psychology: no reward until I do something that I know is important (but maybe not urgent) first.

So, yes, I am struggling. Yes, I am feeling stressed doing AF. And, yes, I can see that it might have enormous potential.

Standard DIT made work a bit of a game. Would I get my list done by the end of the day? If I did, I'd get a point and add another day to my winning streak. But it was a rather gentlemanly game. I could stack the deck by making my list short. And, of course, if it was getting close to the end of the day, I could do very little work on a large number of tasks. The thing was that I could waste 45 minutes (or 3 hours and 45 minutes) and, as long as I got the list done, get a victory.

AF is a game at 10 times the speed of DIT. Sure, I can goof off, if I put it at the end of my list. To get there I will usually need first to review MANY outstanding items that I have on my plate. By the time I get to my goof-off item, I don't feel like goofing off for too long. It's a lot harder to fool myself into thinking that I can relax because I'm in good shape.

Now, in a strange way, these recurrent and same-day tasks serve a role not unlike goof-off items: they are the motors that drive the system. I am on page 15, and the bank balances that I must examine each day, and which I have not gotten to yet, are on page 12. I am starting to worry because it's been a very hectic day with computer malfunctions, telephone equipment problems, and urgent in-bound phone calls. It's starting to get late and I still have this open recurrent task. It's time to bear down and work the system. I do a little bit of this and a little bit of that. Like an American football player, I slowly work my way down the field by making one play after another. Eventually I make it to the end of the list and then back to page 12.

Am I happy? Not really. I just wanted to look at my bank balance when I wanted to look at it. The system forced me to confront everything else that was on my plate. But does it advance my long-term good better than alternative methods? I am willing to entertain the possibility that it might.

Will I stick with AF? That remains to be seen. Monday and Tuesday, I wasn't doing it correctly and they were horrendous days. Wednesday I did standard DIT and reviewed Mark's clarifications. Thursday and Friday I did AF. It made me uncomfortable. But many games are not easy the first time they are played.

One thing I like about the autofocus system is that it forces you to apply some common sense. I think a lot of the qualms and queries people have about the system seem to stem from the idea that we must treat the system as gospel and are not *allowed* to do something that's somewhere further down the list when we're currently on a different page...

If someone rings you who is on your list to call, but on another page than the one you're working on, would you say, no, I can't talk to you now because calling you is on my list further down? You wouldn't do that with another time management system either.

Mel has summarised this beautifully in her post under "A common sense approach to AF" http://www.markforster.net/forum/post/624100 and Christine B also gives some good examples under "Urgent items on a later page" http://www.markforster.net/forum/post/624014

The list is not there to stop us from doing things we need to do. It's designed to make life easier, allowing us to be less of a slave to our daily task list while still making sure that everyting we need to do gets captured and will get done at some point. No more and no less.

I wonder if there is an issue here about understanding what sort of list you create under AF (which I'm enjoying experimenting with by the way, and finding that you do indeed need to work with it to develop a better understanding of it).

Traditional to-do lists are 'must do' lists but because they are also open lists the upshot is you never get to the end of the work you must do. DIT introduced the idea of a closed list, and achieved closure by batching together all the work you 'will do' tomorrow. Now with AF you still have a closed list, with enclosure achieved just by working off one page at a time (there is nothing magic in using one day as the unit of closure). However whereas with DIT the list was of items you 'will do' that day, with AF the list is of items you 'might do' in the near future (deliberately vaguely defined). This seems to me a fundamental shift from DIT. It means that unlike either the traditional to do list or the DIT list, entering an item on an AF list does not mean you are committed to do it. Of course you might feel strongly inclined to do it when you enter it but one thing the system does is test out the real strength of that commitment, crucially when it is being tested against other things you might do. Now, an item that you feel inescapably committed to doing at a particular time, or one that you feel totally committed to doing right now regardless of where it is on a list, does not fit into this system. The answer is to do just what Mark suggests: if you overwhelmingly feel the need to do an action right now, just do it. If you are committed to actioning something at a particular time, then ensure you have a system that will remind you at the appropriate time and when it reminds you then just do it.

It seems to me that this difference in the type of list you are working with in AF is important and perhaps not been fully brought out in the discussions I have read - although I haven't read them all. It perhaps accounts for the struggles some report with the system, because their expectation is still to create lists of what they definitely will do.
