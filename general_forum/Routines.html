I'm a big fan of routines. I think they are essential to sanity ;-)

Generally, my routines are those which I don't do often enough so that I might forget, or those where the cost of forgetting is high. So I don't have much in the way of housecleaning routines any longer. I do have packing lists, though, because I will forget things if I have not noted them down.

Oddly I have a list of salad fixings because I so often have forgotten to put in the X that I just bought because it sounded so tasty when I was in the store. I tend to record recipes because I forget how I did it when it worked out so well. I also clip the preparation instructions from boxes before I trash them. Some stuff lives in the freezer so long that when it comes out I can't think what to do with it!

When I was younger ... MUCH younger ... I had a routine whereby I put certain things in certain pockets then all I needed to do was recite an incantation (list of items) and slap each pocket in turn to assure myself that I had it. I never forgot stuff and it reduced the fuss factor to nearly zero.

Another thing you might consider is "lessons learned". It is an idea I picked up working with the military. After every mission or project they produce a "lessons learned" report wherein they describe what worked and what did not so the next time someone has to do it they can avoid the same mistakes. So after a trip, when unpacking I note what I took that I did not use and what I forgot that I needed. Next trip, I can be guided by that.

Checklists: Yeah, but no.

I've been discussing this (elsewhere) and have been trying to put into words why some of what is being called a "checklist" is not.

The example in that article of the building was not a checklist ... it was a PERT/CPM plan. Neither is it a To Do list. As I often carp .. words have a meaning. A PERT/CPM plan includes dependency and time, not simply tasks ... not even tasks and priority. It is a very different animal from a checklist. You can't simply check off "turn over keys to new owner" at the start of the project ... it is one of the LAST things you check off!

A pilot's checklist IS a checklist. It is a list of things to be done that he CHECKS off, in order, before being satisfied that a particular task is done. A recipe is also a checklist. A grocery list is a sort of checklist, but it is not ordered so we don't commonly think of it as a checklist. Also, a grocery list does not pertain to a single thing ... you may be shopping for dairy and meat and veggies. It may be for lunches or dinners. It may include things for a party. It may include different stores. It is not a single "project", IOW. (And no, it is not a single project just because you are going to buy all those things. ;-)

The story about the rock band is interesting, and clever, but it is NOT a checklist that is being referred to but a CONTRACT. Now I would argue that there are probably places in that contract that would CALL FOR a checklist, but the checklist is not the contract any more than the M&Ms are.

A To Do list is not a checklist ... it is a To Do list. I guess we could debate the exact definition of a checklist until the cows come home but clearly a To Do list is a different animal. It is a list, to be sure. But it might be prioritized or time blocked or scheduled and one thing it lacks is continuity. One does not use the same To Do list every day. Checklists are reusable. One does not use a To Do list for items that are not part of a larger thing ... a checklist is used precisely for that. A To Do list is a bunch of things related ONLY by the fact that you intend to do them. A checklist is a bunch of things you MUST do to get a certain well defined outcome (like land a plane on its wheels without crashing it and getting people angry with you) and the items on it are almost always permanent and done in a sequence. I.E. you always do the same things in the same order in the same way to land a plane safely.

That is the real power of a checklist and why it is used. You work out the bugs in a procedure and then by following the steps exactly each time ... you get the same result -- the result you want.

Elsewhere I described my "every" lists. I have one page which contains the following lists (in very compact format:

- Things I do EVERY DAY (both I do by hour and those not assigned a particular time)
- Things I do EVERY WEEK (by day of week)
- Things I do EVERY MONTH (by day of month)
- Things I do EVERY YEAR (by date)

The last includes things which are not due EVERY year but on the same date every so many years. (Like "renew passport" which comes up every 10 years ... next in October of 2013 ... and "renew driver's license" which comes up every forth year, and "renew car registration" which comes up every other year, etc.)

At a glance I can see what is on the horizon while not having to leaf through a calendar to notice them. I might ALSO put them on the calendar so I'll see blocked time, for example, and not schedule something to conflict.

If you are smaller format based (as I am, mostly) you could make up an index card for each category and need only four of them ... and they also come in pretty florescent colors ;-)

I have found it very useful to put structure around my day something like this (in the spirit of absolutely clear terminology, "X:XX" below means a particular time every day, such that the X:XX on one line occurs earlier than the X:XX of the subsequent line; and "checklist" usually refers to a list of required tasks that are best followed in sequence, which was originally written down but eventually took the form of an unwritten habit):

X:XX AM: Get up (follow the "get up" checklist)

X:XX AM: Outside (follow the "outside" checklist, such as (1) Turn on drip system, (2) Feed dogs, (3) Open chicken coop, (4) Walk and pray, (5) Turn off drip system)

X:XX AM: Work (required meetings/appts and/or DWM)

X:XX PM: Errands and/or meetings and/or home/family (pre-scheduled activities consisting of required meetings/appts and/or DWM and/or being available for whatever family needs present themselves)

X:XX PM: Put children to bed (follow the "bedtime" checklist, which may not qualify as a strict checklist, since some of the tasks are optional or can be done out of order)

X:XX PM: Evening routine (follow the "evening" checklist for wrapping up and winding down and getting ready to retire for evening)


In all these cases, the use of the checklist helps make the tasks in question automatic, requiring no thought or decisions, which is what makes them so useful. They improve efficiency and efficacy and consistency, and provide a framework that works like an old shoe -- it just does its job without being noticed, to allow you to focus your energy and attention on the things that really need them.
