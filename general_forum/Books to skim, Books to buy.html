a) the therapist is trained to NOT get caught up in the content, but rather, to appreciate the process of the session
b) to provide a holding enviornment where an individual, or family, begins to experience themselves, and others, in a brand new way.

For the most part, friends and family are unable to do this for us. In general, they have a vested interest in homeostasis, because they GET something from our inability to change.

Re: Moises and article on writing, speaking and thinking about positive events:

Interesting studies, it's worth noting that the results/conclusions from the studies require nuanced interpretation.

<>

I don't think the studies indicate that. It's how you think, write or speak that influence how you feel. In her experiments she did use different 'how's' and compared the groups. It wasn't that conclusive that for example, writing about a positive life event causes worser feelings, it's just that in comparison to the other experimental groups, lower feelings were reported. There is some indication that writing about a life event in an analytical fashion isn't as good as talking or thinking about it and in general doens't boost positive feelings . Like wise writing about a negative life event seems to improve ones feelings, but repetitively thinking about a traumatic experience may do more harm than good.

It's not as simple as 'think, write or speak' about negative events to feel better and don't think, write or speak about positive events or you might feel worse...

in fact, people that have just experienced trauma usually do worse when they have to speak, think or write about what happened shortly after they have experienced the trauma. And as the paper mentions, it depends on how you think, write, speak . Thinking repetitively about a positive event seems to have a positive effect according to that particular study. (gloating can be quite pleasurable can it not!)

The interesting thing is that people don't tend to analyze positive events. Part of the reason is that there is no reason to and the other reason is that people that feel good process information differently compared to people that feel bad. If you feel bad, you tend to process information systematically, i.e. detail oriented. If you feel good, you tend to process information more superficially, using heuristics. I think the lesson in that is if you want to feel good, don't analyse!
