I'm using Omnifocus on the iPhone for my Autofocus list, and it is working brilliantly!

Omnifocus was originally designed for GTD, so it incorporates a lot of functionality that really isn't needed with Mark's system. I just ignore the parts of the program I don't need, and try to keep things as simple as possible.

I have set up a single project called "Autofocus" where all my tasks are entered. By default, new tasks are added to the bottom of the list, just as Mark suggests in his instructions.

The most difficult part was duplicating the "page" concept which is at the heart of the Autofocus system.

At first I tried creating "divider tasks" - that is, tasks which had just dashes or asterisks to show where a new page began. However, I didn't like how it looked on the screen, do I ditched that idea pretty fast.

Fortunately, Omnifocus incorporates a feature called "task grouping" which lets you group several sub-tasks under a top level task. So now I just have top level tasks called "Page 1", Page 2", "Page 3" etc., with my actual tasks grouped underneath those top level items. I review them one by one, as Mark suggests.

For me, using Omnifocus for AF offers many advantages over paper.

• You can set start dates and due dates for tasks, so there is already a built-in tickler system within the program. This helps reduce clutter, because tasks/projects with a future start date are hidden from view until you need to begin working on them.

• Tasks due soon are highlighted in orange, those due today (or which are overdue) are highlighted in red. This provides reassurance that you will not overlook something urgent.

• Dismissed tasks can hidden by moving them to another list, thereby reducing clutter and making it easier to focus on what needs to be done. Pages where all tasks are completed/dismissed are also hidden from view.

• Routine items are easy to track because you can set up repeating tasks. Again, this helps reduce clutter because the tasks are hidden from view until they become due. It also eliminates the need for rewriting.

• Projects are easier to manage, because you can group subtasks/notes under a top level task.

I tried using Appigo's Todo, but I simply could not duplicate the "page" concept which is so necessary to make Autofocus work properly.

The only downside to Omnifocus is that it is relatively expensive for an iPhone app ($20.00), and even more so if you pair it up with the desktop app (a further $79).

I'll post some screenshots to Flickr soon so everyone can see how it works.

All I'm doing, with Omnifocus, is maintaining a tickler system within AF itself, so the task/project automatically pops into view with plenty of time to spare.

I just wanted to put in a good word for Things, which has an iPhone app as well as a desktop version (and which now syncs beautifully – there were some hiccups during the beta phase). See my earlier postings (and Mel's) here:

http://markforster.squarespace.com/forum/post/623232

for details on the (simple) way I've implemented Autofocus in Things, and a suggestion from Mel about another way of doing it; and note that my second posting there points out a simpler way of accomplishing one particular end than I'd hit on when I started the thread.

I'm not claiming that Things is better for Autofocus than Omnifocus or any of the others, as I haven't tried any of those; just that Things is lovely in itself, and is easy to employ for the purposes of (uncorrupted) Autofocus. (Without very careful analysis, it looks to me like it has all the advantages of Omnifocus, too.) And I'll just add three other notes here: (i) The way I handle the "tickler" aspect of the system is to schedule tasks for the appropriate day. When the day arrives, the task is automatically moved into the Today list. I then begin each day by simply moving anything that's shown up on the Today list that morning onto my active page. (It sounds as though this is equivalent to start dates in Omnifocus. Things has due dates, too, but I don't use them.) (ii) In line with Mark's amendment about "same day urgent" items, I've started tagging such items "same day". It's then easy to drag them from one page to another until they're done, as I move through the pages. (iii) Things is cheaper than Omnifocus; it's $49.95 for the desktop version, and $9.99 for the iPhone app.

I'm not affiliated with the makers of Things, incidentally; just a fan.

Hi Martin, I think Things is a terrific app, and I did experiment with it for AF before plumping for Omnifocus. However, I wasn't happy with having scheduled/tickler items pop up in a different view and then having to move them to the correct page. I have a lot of routine/repeating items, some of which repeat several times a day, so having to check to see whether these have popped up and then move them to the appropriate page was a hassle. With Omnifocus, everything is kept on the same screen, with tickler items popping on to the last "page" automatically. It may seem like a small difference, but I wanted to keep my digital implementation of AF as maintenance free as possible.

The other advantage, for me, is that Omnifocus also supports subtasks - so an item like "Prepare tax return" contains all the next steps needed to complete the project. Once I've completed as much as I feel like doing or is possible for today, I simply move the entire project forward to my last AF "page", perhaps setting a new start date of "tomorrow" so that the project can be hidden from view until then. Of course, individual items can be plucked from within the project and listed as separate items on AF pages should it seem necessary.

I have discovered that most IPhone task/list apps can be set up to be used for AF. Remember the Milk, Toodledo, ToDo by Appigo. The benefit in using these more complex apps is that you can put due dates on those tasks that truly need them. Of course, the risk is that you will overdo it with the features that just muddy up the system.

I have been thinking about a way of integrating AF with LifeBalance since I have so much data already entered into it and I love the calendar part of the system. I also enjoy seeing how all my activities fit within the larger context of goals. I think I have a solution that would be applicable to other more complex task managers (again, with the warning that using this approach MAY reduce the effectiveness of AF; I plan to test it this week).

What I have done is created an Autofocus goal in LB with each Page as a subtask and all tasks as further subtasks for each page (LB works like an outliner if you're not familiar). When I am working on AF tasks, I will go directly to the outline rather than letting LB tell me what to do. One disadvantage to LB for AF is I can't see a count of how many items there are per page, though 4 screens of items is 32, the number of lines in my notebook I used originally. If I can deal with that, here is the benefit. When I COMPLETE an item, I will assign it to its real life goal and give it the true effort I put into it. that way my pie chart will be accurate, but I don't have to slow down the input of tasks by trying to determine this ahead of time. In fact, I think it's possible, that adding this information after the fact (something I actually enjoy doing) will be like an extra bonus reward for doing the task. I like to see if I'm spending too much time in one area or another--like spending lots of time trying to fix my AF system that isn't broken. LOL

Let me just say that I could really be fooling myself here and I may return to my trusty tadalist or to another app, but I'm giving it a try for now.

After experimenting with my lists in LifeBalance, I found I just couldn't cope with the lack of numerical accounting of items per page. That is what I love about the digital vs. paper approach. It's motivating for me to see that number go down (and I don't have to keep count myself!).

Right now I am using ToodleDo, but I'm using ToDo by Appigo on the IPhone as my interface. I love that when I mark an item done, it gets crossed off in color (I'm very into color for some reason) and it drops to the bottom of that page (just as it does in tadalist). The things I really love so far about ToDo is any items I give a due date too are listed at the top of that page. I don't really pay attention much to it as I'm working through my pages, but I like that it's structured that way. Due dates (which I'm really only assigning for true must-do on said date tasks) also mean I can look at a Focus or Hot List while I'm working through my list if I'd like. I am tagging true errands as well as a safety net in case I do forget something I could be doing.

Finally, what I'm MOST excited about using this method is that I can look on Toodledo with a Pro account and see how many tasks I'm entering vs. how many I'm doing per day. I LOVE that! It is really motivating me to be able to see that if I do X number more tasks than I enter on a given day I should be able to clear my current task lists in X days. I also have the option of assigning completed tasks to goals (lifelong, long-term, short-term) after the fact and very quickly online.

I wish I weren't a fiddler, but I'm hoping this may be my list fiddle and I'll be playing beautiful music with my lists from here on out. LOL
