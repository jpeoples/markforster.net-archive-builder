two spiral pads (one for the office and one for at home) and a calendar book (all paper).

I use a cheap spiral pocketsized notebook as inbox device. I carry it nearly always with me. That's the reason, why I put errands on the backsite of the list. I also carry a pilot g-2 (pen) in my right trouser pocket (clip outside - pen inside).

2011-02-22 12:19
Paper. More precisely, a coat-pocketable notebook (same height as A6 paper but slightly narrower). A problem I've always had with my to-do systems is neglecting to actually use them. One part of that problem is simple (lack of) physical presence. Books or binders that are bulky tend to get stowed away out of sight. Lists on a computer are entirely out of sight until called up. The current notebook so far has managed to be reliably present. (The one downside of a smaller notebook is that it only gives around 20 lines per page. I combine facing pages into a single "virtual" page.)

With this notebook always present, my second important time management component, the calendar, could actually be on a computer. It currently isn't, but that's not an important choice.

In another thread, I've described a precursor to the system I run in that notebook (called AF Advance). I don't currently recommend trying it as described.

To my thinking, it is important that the AF1-like systems, like the one I'm using, are not a substitute for a calendar or a clock. They work best with tasks that are ready to do now, but at the same time not so urgent that they have to be done immediately, or at a specific time.
