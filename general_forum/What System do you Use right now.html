After a brief flirtation with AF4R, I'm back to a digital DWM setup (which really functions more like DWM2 since there are no "pages" to deal with). The date-dependent dismissal meshes well with my personality and work habits.

I also make heavy use of calendar reminders as a kind of tickler - if there's something I know I can't address now I put a reminder in my calendar for the earliest date when I think I WILL be able to deal with it. I do the same for occasional repeating tasks (like reviewing dismissed items or remembering to put bleach in the furnace humidifier once a month).

Appointments are in my calendar. Everything syncs between the web, my laptop, and my iPad. On the off chance I don't have web access and don't have my iPad with me (it happens... rarely, but it happens) I can at the very least add items to my list via SMS, even if I can't read it.

I am not sure I can even call what I do now a system. I have a spiral college ruled notebook and I write everything in it I need to do. I date the page in the margin each day and I constantly review it to see what I think needs to be done and I do it. I am writing business tasks front to back and personal back to front so everything is in one place. The notebook stays at my desk during the day and I take it home at night in case I think of a work item to add and to add personal stuff.

For notes, I have gone to using simple legal pads. It adds the step of having to sometimes review the legal pad to add things to the spiral notebook, but I found ripping notes out of the notebook and separating that extra piece of paper annoying.

For me I am not a rules person, so I never got along with automatic dismissal and other concepts like that. The only thing I try to adhere to is to write tasks as something that can be done in a day or less. so goals and other longer term projects are broken down into something I can do today. This keeps things moving. So build a house is not on the list, but call the architect for a resume is.

At work, I've been ignoring my lists and focusing on the main item on the project schedule. As urgent stuff arises (bills, requests from spouse, etc.) I just do them immediately. Meanwhile lots of other things suffer - but most of it deserves to suffer anyway. :-) When I finally get back to my list I'll probably find 50%+ of it no longer relevant.

Hmm, that's what usually happens when I have lots of overtime demands at work, and lose a day or two because of migraines. There's no time to think about it -- just get the main things done. 80% of the "recurring" items just don't get any attention at all. The 20% that really need it (like feeding the cats and watering the garden and paying the bills) do get done, because they are ingrained habits and because they HAVE to get done.

Problem is, when the pressure lets up, I feel kinda lost. Right now, I have a little breather and used it to clean out my in box, and to catch up on this forum, and to think about whether I should get back to my list, or start a new one, or just wait for SAFv3. Or, find a way to artificially create the pressure that helps me really focus on what's really important. Or, realize that it's OK to go at 110% for a while, get a huge amount of work done, and then lay back for awhile afterwards.

Is it better to keep an even keel at 80-90%, pretty much all the time, with an occasional break? That seems to be the dominant model in the Western world, at least in the USA. Keep up a fast pace, always working (or least making the appearance of being busy), and take an occasional week or two for vacation.

Or is it better to go at 110% or 150%, neglecting sleep and basic needs, for several weeks or months, and then slacking off to 20-30% for another few weeks or months? Some Mediterranean and Slavic cultures seem to follow this model. Maybe it's because of the particular agricultural background of the culture or something.

Both models seem to work. E.g., in the "space race" the Americans followed the first model, the Russians the second model. Both accomplished amazing things, both accomplished many "firsts".

My periodic fits of migraine headaches seem to knock me out periodically, so I tend to follow the 150% / 30% model whether I like it or not.

I'm hoping Mark will chime in and say "SAFv3 addresses all those issues", as he has many times with previous pre-release keep-them-in-suspense discussions. :-) (And he's always been right about that, by the way!)

I use a calendar to plan committed time and to feed timed tasks into AF. I then use a tweaked version of the original AF1 for selecting what to do during uncommitted time.

Both the calendar and the AF list are on paper. Using paper heavily constrains a system compared to digital flexibility, but to-do lists on a computer have always failed for me, after some time. Their lack of physical presence seems to make them too easy to just ignore.

I have applied two tweaks to standard Autofocus, one minor and one major.

The first and minor tweak could be called "batched activation." It came to be because I liked both Autofocus and the basic idea of "Do It Tomorrow" and greedily wanted to have both in one system.

The Rules: On the left margin of every page, tasks are marked by a vertical line drawn with a yellow highlitghter. As new items are added, they start out unmarked and are ignored during task selection. Activate new tasks in batches by extending the yellow line downward. If you only activate new tasks once every morning, you have "Do It Tomorrow".

I don't know if "batch activation" makes the system better. I started AF with this tweak so I didn't have to decide between AF and DIT and then just never discarded it.
