I love the distinction between action and activity on the current home page of this site. From my own experience, I know this is absolutely true. And from some statements here in the discussion forum I have the impression that those who wrote them simply still cling to activity in order to avoid to take real action – and once you're in that mode, you shy away from simplicity. I know it from my personal history of procrastination and my periods of obsessive methodology. It's the complicated system that appeals to you, the system with the sophisticated file system, a zillion tags and reference systems, the system that needs a sophisticated software solution and - most important of all - is able to keep you busy the whole day organizing your tasks without any of it getting done. (Most of what I read from GTD adepts on the net falls into this category, too.)

Imagine some bad guys came into your house, put a gun to your head and set you a deadline: Until then-and-then, you will have to complete this-and-this tasks, otherwise they will do nasty things to you, your wife and your children. And it's no joke, it's real pressure. You ABSOLUTELY have to get these things DONE.

How would you do it? Would you really start up any piece of software and begin by feeding all these tasks into it? At least I wouldn't. If I were in real pressure, I'd grab some large, cheap notebook, some pen, jot everything down that's in my mind and then I would simply START DOING THINGS.

The way you act once you're under real pressure is the way of acting that suits you – even without the pressure.

I think the main point is to become aware of one's clinging to activity. Once you let that go, you're half there.

This is exactly why (or at least one reason why) I like Autofocus so much. It is a very natural system. It takes the gun-to-the-head approach, and systematizes and extends it to cover all of your discretionary time.

Granted, "WHAT DO I HAVE TO DO NEXT SO THEY DON'T SHOOT ME???" is a bit different from "do whatever stands out", but somehow the two concepts seem to be connected at some deep level. In both cases, one would gravitate to the task that you CAN do next, and that FEELS RIGHT to do next. There's just more pressure in the gun-to-the-head case, to TAKE ACTION NOW.

With every other TM system I've used, whenever there was any extraordinary deadline pressure, or some other kind of overwhelm, I'd be forced to abandon the system and go into "emergency mode" to deal with the overwhelming situation: make a short list, bang through it as quick as possible, don't think about anything else. When the dust clears, I could go back to my TM-system-of-the-month, but it would always be difficult to remember how it all worked, how it all fit together, and where I should start again.

With Autofocus, dealing with these overwhelming situations is actually pretty easy, and fits right into the system. I can just add all the pressing tasks to the end of my list, and then start working on them right away, jumping straight to that page. I stay focused on them, working the AF system as normal, till the crisis has cleared. And then, I can relax and just start working through the system as normal, treating all these new tasks (if any loose ends remain) as any other task in the system.

I am quickly finding the easiest default behavior is just use the AF list. Easy to capture new tasks. Easy to review existing tasks. Easy to get started up again after a week full of meetings or emergencies. Overwhelm and pressure actually drive me to my list now, rather than driving me to panic and abandon my system.

What is AF? It's a system for writing down and doing things.
How do you do AF? Follow Mark's instructions.
Why is it useful to do AF?

This question does not have an easy answer. AndreasE's first post in this thread helped me identify why AF is so good.

First, let's figure one reason which is not a reason why I do AF.

I don't do AF because it is the best way to organize lists of stuff that I have committed myself to doing. There really are much better ways to organize this stuff. Various kinds of lists, outliners, and project planning systems are better than AF.

I don't do AF because it is the most efficient way to keep track of my commitments. It's not.

I do AF because it's the best way so far to keep me motivated. AndreasE asks us to imagine someone putting a gun to our head and threatening us and our family with murder. OK. I am imagining that. Now the gun-wielding threatener tells me to do something. What will I do? Will I start surfing the web? Will I decide that now would be a good time to sort through those old photos in my bottom draw? Will I tell myself that I'll do just what this guy with the gun says after I wash the dishes? No. I will probably try to do whatever this guy is asking me to do. If the assignment he gives me is highly complex and will take many days of sustained effort, I will use complex software with tags and means for tracking dependencies.

What does AndreasE's thought experiment teach me? It teaches me that AF is probably of no use to me when there is a gun at my head because AF's key virtue is that it can keep me working actively when there is no gun at my head.

I am highly motivated when there is a gun at my head. Fortunately (unfortunately?), I have never worked with a gun at my head. And that is the problem. It's hard to stay motivated over weeks and months when the gun is gone.

Why is AF good? AF is good because it keeps me motivated even when there is no gun. Be sure of one thing: if someone put a gun to my head, the last thing I would be doing is AF. (Let's imagine using AF in the presence of the gun. "This guy wants me to drive him to Decatur. OK. I'll put that at the end of my list. But right now, I am still on page 26. Let's see, this assignment that's due in six months is standing out. Let's work on it for a few minutes.")
