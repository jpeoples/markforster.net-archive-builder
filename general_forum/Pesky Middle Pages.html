Hi Ron,

It sounds like you might have a "sticking page" - is there one page that you get to in forward mode where nothing is standing out on a regular basis?

If that's the case, I would take the opportunity to really look at those tasks and break them down to find something to do that you can do a little of, and re-write the task at the end of the list. Or you can take one of the tasks and break it down into more do-able pieces at the end of your list and count that as working on it. Or, if it's something you're unsure whether you're even going to / should be doing it, write a question mark at the end of it and turn it into a choice of whether to do it or not. If the answer is "yes", then write the tasks to complete it at the end of your list and keep moving forward in forward mode.

In AF1, I sometimes would come to pages which had only these big, ugly tasks left, and I would take the one I had the *most* resistance to - and break that down into non-resisting types of tasks at the end of the list (as a kind of checklist) so that I could keep moving forward rather than dismiss the page.

I wouldn't change where I moved forward from though. I think many of us have found that where we are tempted to tweak is where we need to think about our list operation. Have you thought of going through your list and doing some pre-dismissal? Maybe what's on that page or pages isn't something you really want to be doing right now?

I hope this helps - could you explain more about what happens when your forward mode breaks down?

One more thing Ron - if you write the reason down for *why* you want that task done, it might help to break down your resistance. Just one good reason should do it. If the reason is stupid (eg. Ron:

Also, don't forget the little known aspect of AF (i.e. "raf") -- you can scrap items off your list at anytime, and you can target them directly and anytime whether in FM or in RM. They *don't* have to be above the line and put on review first.

I think some of the genesis of the various "tweaks" we see to the system, whether in this thread or others, might stem from forgetting that Mr. Forster envisioned this *additional common-sense approach* to getting items off the list when they don't belong, or they are hampering movement.

I don't think he envisioned someone having to wait until the system effected the removal of an item that was no longer relevant or an item one no longer had any intention of doing. This would be equivalent, say, to a proponent of GTD not doing the next action on a project because he hasn't written down the next action. That wouldn't make much sense.

I tend to think if one keeps this common sense "rule" in mind, then the discussion of how to "pre-filter" what one puts on the list (i.e. the "grass-catcher" discussion) loses some of its relevancy. You just put stuff on your list. Later, if you see it and it doesn't belong on the list you take it off. In the *meantime* you might run across it in the system and skip it, or action it, or it might be put on review.

In your case, if you keep seeing items in the early part of your list and you you aren't doing them, and you *aren't going to do them*, then you *don't* have to wait for the line to sweep across the early pages in order to get rid of them. You can just get rid of them. Presto! Now when you go into forward mode they won't be holding you up (i.e. kicking you back into Reverse Mode) and you will be able to continue on, eventually getting to those "middle pages".

Thoughts? What say the other forum members and practitioners of AF? Have I fairly presented Mr. Forster's approach?

--Joseph

Hi Ron

Yes, Jacqueline is right. The number of open pages can become an issue as it takes time to move back or forwards (and particularly forwards) when there are only 1 or 2 items to a page. The thing to remember is that, as you clear tasks the number of pages gets less and less so you start to move more quickly through those middle pages.

Two practical things that have helped me:-

My stats spreadsheet is set to show 100 pages of AF on the screen, in 4 blocks of 25. When a page is cleared it is shown in a different colour so I can see at a glance which pages are open and closed and how many items remain on each. Being able to literally "see" a pattern I'm sure helps my subconscious when moving through. I naturally think of my pages in groups (51 to 75, 76 to 100) and clearing those blocks have become mini-goals which I am sure helps tasks to stand out more easily.

Secondly, my "digital version" in OneNote enables me to move completed pages to another section so that it is easier to look through 25 active pages rather than say 70 actual pages to locate those 25.

The thing with reverse mode is that each new page, by the time you fill it and start a fresh one, will normally (for me at least) have less than 10 items still active (and usually less than 5). I find that new tasks can get cleared fairly quickly, as can more recent items, leaving me more time available to move back. I also find that my phrasing of tasks is now slightly different. I am now more likely to put "project x" than "project x - step 1", "project x - step 2" etc and to keep those steps, or next actions separate from my list. It is then easier to address "project x" for as short a period of time as required and to then move it forward. Sometimes if I am working fairly intensively on a project in bursts throughout the day, it may well appear on a page 4 or 5 times, but if I had it down as say 10 seperate tasks and that project itself did not stand out, I would have a set of resisted tasks (and not necessarily together in one group) that would begin to block my progress.

Hope that may help a little?

One trick I use to deal with items that are getting 'stuck' and thus holding middle pages open (because I keep 'skipping' over the items every time).

I pick one of these items and say "its time to examine why it is never popping up as 'ready to do'. I put my diot beside it. I think (or write) about the teas; why did I think it needed to be done in the first place? why might I be resisting it? Can I break it down into simpler steps? Can I 'make a start' by getting the file out (or whatever)?

This qualifies as 'working on it' even if I dont actually do anything else.

Then I can cross it off (as I did some work on it) and decide what to add to the end of my list as a result - I might decide to re-enter it, modify it in some way or break it down - or I might decide to leave it behind.

This trick works a treat for me and helps me close those 'pesky middle pages' sooner.

Laurence:

Regarding the "easing of the rules". Wouldn't that "ease" you right out of AutoFocus?

It *seems* to me that if you can just cruise down your list in forward mode, page after page, looking for something to do then what you really have is just one giant "to do" list and you are looking for something to do on it. Just like our ancestors did B.A.F. (Before Auto Focus).

The Forward Mode rule creates a closed list. If nothing feels ready to be done you don't go to the next page. . . so you had better do something (even a teeny tiny something) to see that next page. It *forces* you to work.

And, don't forget, if nothing on that page is ever really going to be actionable (for whatever reason) you can just delete all of those items and then continue on to the next page.

But in the above two scenarios, unlike with easing the rules, there is an AutoFocus engine that is driving us to action or decision making.

Just my thoughts.
