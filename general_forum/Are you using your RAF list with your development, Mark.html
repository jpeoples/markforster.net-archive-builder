2009-09-2 10:31
I may have taken a left turn somewhere along the line but ...

I made the decision to categorize tasks in only two ways:

- Those I am committed to doing this week

- All others

I can choose to see the "all others" as a backlog but I simply put them all in the "someday/maybe" bucket. After all, if I am not going to do them this week, what does it matter what I call them? During my next weekly review, I go over that list, sort it out, and select those things that I will commit to doing during the next week.

This has had some dramatic effects:

- My list is very much smaller than it used to be (about 10 pages this morning) and so I get through the list much more quickly ... several times a day, in fact,

- I am much more focused because I don't have so many fantasies on the list. I dismiss them right away so they don't get in my way,

- Not CALLING it a "backlog" helps me a great deal. I don't even like the term. And what does it mean? There will always be more to do than there is time to do it in, so does that mean I'll always be "backlogged"? That is not a helpful construct for me. It represents a category of "quasi importance". Things I need to do but am not willing to commit to doing, but I still feel I need to do them more than other things. HUH? That is a foggy way of looking at things, I've decided.

Moreover, in thinking about this, I noticed that all such descriptions end up referring to time in some way. Things are important mostly with respect to when they will get done. It seems, to me, simpler to make a binary decision ... will they get done now ... or NOT. Rather than WHEN will they get done ... now, or a little later, or a little bit later than that, or a lot later or ... I think this realization was triggered by that discussion about someone's new software that requires estimating a completion date. I wondered why that felt so repulsive to me. It was a schedule without a schedule ... daft! Now I better understand why that approach is wrong.

I can't think of anything I've lost by taking this approach. If I really do have more things I MUST do than I CAN do, calling it a backlog does not change that fact. Recognizing what I WILL do is more pertinent than any other consideration. So I wind up focusing on what I WILL do and dealing directly with that.

So basically AF is working to manage my time among tasks that I have decided that I really will do this week and, of course, continues to act as a grass catcher to collect junk that drifts on by.

As to the list "rules", I'm finding that it matters a lot less than it used to. My list is so short now that it makes little difference if I'm working forward, backward, or cherry picking. I tend to work from the end, but that may be more a matter of habit than anything else.

My dismissal rules have also morphed. Now I continually look at tasks with the question: "Am I STILL really committed to doing this task this week?" I'm still surprised how many times I get "NO!" for an answer. Something changes, I guess, and the urgency or importance drops so I dismiss it right then and there. It will probably go on again when I review in less than a week. Or maybe something else will tickle me to put it back.

One more thing, I'm liking that suggestion of putting a "!" in front of tasks that are added back after being dismissed. That really does serve to make me think about why I'm not doing it. Why it is happier being moved from one list to another but not to be acted on.

Just some musings this morning ;-)

Hi Jacqueline,

My filtering is to get the list down to a manageable size and to keep it uncluttered. So at 10 pages, with 20 items per page, that is just 200 items ... BUT ... most of those are completed now. Some pages have only two or three tasks left on them.

I am not yet monitoring the list with metrics, so I can't tell you much about the dynamics. I think I added about 15 items to the list today, and completed about as many. I expect to close a page or two tomorrow.

The method does not matter so long as:

- The list is of a manageable size

- It is easy to read ... I.E. is not cluttered with things that you are not going to do in the current review period

- The rules for processing are not burdensome ... (RAF is, for me, just on the ragged edge of having rules so complicated that the list becomes the focus and not the work. As I said, with my smaller list, I just brows it in any order that strikes me and don't worry much about forward or reverse mode or complex dismissal rules. If I won't do a task, it gets dismissed.)

- It is dynamic enough to keep up with your needs (that's why my grass catcher function of the AF list works for me ... a lot of things coming on to the list just occur to me ... but most of what occurs to me is nonsense ;-)

My grass catcher aspect is, as I mentioned, just to keep me from using a separate notebook for that purpose. So long as I dismiss them instantly, there is no longer a problem.

I think it is probably good that you collapsed the two books into one. Once you have sensitized yourself to A vs. C, it ought to be just as good ... and with one fewer books.
