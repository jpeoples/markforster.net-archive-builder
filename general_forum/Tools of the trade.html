Alison Reeves wrote:

I'm trying out SAF and am using the old stalagmite / stalactite method for managing work and personal in one book:

Write work-related tasks from the top of the page, going down.

Write personal tasks from the bottom of the page, going up.

When they meet in the middle, draw a heavy line.

Do this on both Column 1 and Column 2.

Otherwise, process as normal.


This allows you to skip the out-of-context tasks if you want, or skim them as needed.

And it allows you to carry around a single book.

I wouldn't worry too much about home / work division.

That's not to say mix them all together, just recognize that no system can be perfect. Life isn't like that. Rather than never being happy with a system that won't do100% of the job, use one that works 95% of the time and allows us to do whatever is needed the rest of the time.

2010-11-16 16:49
My own preferred solution to the Workplace/Home problem (which I don't often have to face myself) is two books.

The tasks should go in the book according to the location where they will be done, not whether they are work or personal. So in Cricket's example "Call Doctor" would go in the Workplace book.

If there are tasks which can be done either at home or at work, then they should be in both books.

If you want to keep your Home book at home and your Workplace book at work, then you can write selected home tasks on a piece of paper for work and enter them in your workplace book when you get there (and vice versa). That will also prevent the two locations spilling over into each other too much.

2010-11-16 17:15
I prefer the stalactite/stalagmite method over two books, because:

(1) For me, it's not so much about location as about focus. I work from home most of the time, but still need to maintain focus on my work tasks during work hours.

(2) I don't need double entry if the task can be done in either location, as is required if you use two books.

(3) On days (rarer and rarer) when there isn't so much pressure at work, I can be a little more flexible in my focus. Having both tasks appearing on one page (but separated by a line) helps prompt me to "switch locations" as needed, by showing me the whole range of tasks that can be done, not just work. Normally I just suppress the home tasks but on light work days I can lighten up on the suppression a bit.
