Having really only got back on track with AF this week I decided to work to the end of my list using AF1 and start AF2 today. That gave me a chance to read the instructions, catch up on the comments and experiences of others, and get a feel for how I imagined it would work. I had already decided I was going to run with my old list as it is an old friend!!

The issues I had picked up on, and on which I shared concern, were really those stated by Mike and Seraphim, regarding getting back to the beginning of the list and I read the suggested tweaks (such as the index card approach) and also Mark's response and found Mark's post on trying to combine both systems really helpful. Knowing that Seraphim has even more active items and pages than me (as at last night I had 59 active pages and 362 active items) I felt I had a clear picture of how AF2 would work and that I would need to artificially find a way through all of those pages.

What I found was not what I expected (which illustrates the need to try something before making assumptions!!) I genuinely found that the incentive to "rescue" the potentially dismissable item on Page 29 (my current page is 92!) did draw me back quite easily and I was surprised how many items appeared a lot more attractive than they had been in AF1. It also highlighted to me the number of duplicated and repeated items still lurking within the pages.

The number of active pages had not particularly bothered me within AF1. However, the need to stay current had left me doing "just one" item on too many occasions, with the result that pages hung around for far too long. AF2 will enforce action or dismissal in a way that could be avoided in AF1. I am also finding that I can more easily "stop when the time is right". If there was something that had an urgency I would often work longer than I wanted to on it, because it could take me forever to get back to it. With AF2 I can stop and start a lot more easily, and also break tasks down more easily.

There is a greater incentive to clear pages because going back from P92 to P29 is BORING!!! There is also no need to keep adding routine items such as email to every page to ensure regular attention and I have been surprised by how many tasks that have languished for weeks are standing out.

OneNote still works well as my "digital" option. I have had to change my procedure for update slightly but that is no problem and is actually easier. My stats spreadsheet will work just as well and I am looking forward to comparing progress.

So far I am loving it! :-)

Hi Diane

With recurring items you would cross it off and readd it at the end of the list. That keeps it current. So if for example you enter your QB data weekly, by the time you action the task it will probably be one or two pages back in your notebook. Crossing it off clears the item on say P10 and then you can readd it on P12 (or whatever your last page is). You will keep seeing the item each time you go through the list, ready to be actioned when it next stands out. If you left the same item uncrossed on the list, at some point it would just end up being dismissed once it reached the front of the list.

Hi Diane

Sorry for the delay in replying!

I think with recurring items it would depend on the frequency of occurrence. Most recurring items, eg make coffee, check email etc may be done several times a day and the crossing out and reentering will give an indication of frequency. It also depends on how quickly you are entering tasks. I would not necessarily cross out the task and reenter it on the next line for example but would if it were several lines down the page. However, it strikes me that something such as neck exercises which you may want to do quite frequently may be better worked with some sort of regular timer at a defined interval.

There haven't been any official tweaks to the dismissal system although several have been suggested. The general rule is that the line is drawn each day you use AF. If you only worked the list at weekends that would give only two lines - the more you use the list the faster it becomes. The main difficulty people have had is when there is insufficient time to work back to the on notice item and it is an item they really feel they must do. Personally if I believe it must be done then I will do it but that is not according to the rules.

I think what you are referring to with large tasks is where the steps can all be written down and actioned as you go along more easily. For example, on page 3 you see "upgrade PC". You start this (crossing it off) and write the steps as new tasks, eg do backup; download software; install software; reboot PC etc. As you then return to the last page you can action those steps easily in succession, without them being spread all over the list or being many pages away from your current position.

What I have done with recurring tasks (daily or weekly) was suggested by Mark in some other thread. I set up Remember the Milk (or any other task manager) with the task name. Then when I pull up that task manager (which is daily with work) I write it on my AF2 list, then mark it complete in RTM.

For example, I would write "Journal" in RTM, but know that it means "Write Journal task on today's AF2 list). I find this is a good reminder to do it daily, but my list doesn't get too cluttered with stuff for tomorrow or next week on the list.

I've been doing the same kind of thing with future tickler items. It goes on the calendar when I will work on it. And the calendar item gets added to my AF2 list on that day or just before.

Well after nearly three weeks of AF2 I am finding my approach changing again! The contant returning to the last page was quite a problem at the start (and I still have a similar number of open pages so that is not impacting my approach) but I am finding that the return to the end AND the reading backwards up the page is becoming the norm. My "tweak" of keeping the closed unit benefit by working a page before returning to the end has just drifted away. I cannot say that I am returning to the end after "every" task actioned but certainly very very frequently.

I have a much greater desire to complete so many of the older items; they are standing out more easily (and I am recognising it!) and I am identifying reasons for procrastination more easily. Because under AF1 I had gotten into a pattern of doing tasks "because they had to be done" and therefore could not be dismissed without consequence, rather than because they were standing out, I was bypassing some of the most important elements of AF - and hadn't even recognised that I was doing so!!

As for the dismissal process ......... I find that I know instinctively when I draw that line whether I will action or dismiss the item/s. I can also look at upcoming on notice items and know that I will get done some of those things that I have procrastinated on for months, even though I have no desire to do them "now". So far I have dismissed 9 items and actioned 19 "on notice" items but the earliest of those on notice items was from 11 February! I am also consistently clearing older items, making more and more inroads back through my lists.

As for number of items - on only one day since starting AF2 have my number of items increased - and that was only by 2 ..... and it was a "Frantic Friday" :-) Although the red line is chomping through those early pages at a steady but relatively slow pace, the number of later items being cleared will inevitably make it speed up shortly, and at some point my list will stabilise. My personal opinion is that using my AF1 list was absolutely the right thing for me (although I realise maybe not for everybody) as it has avoided the concerns about items being dismissed too soon and has not influenced me to restrict what I add to the list. Those early pages have given me the breathing space necessary.
