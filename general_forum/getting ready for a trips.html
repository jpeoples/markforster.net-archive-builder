It comes down to two answers:

- Use AF as written for it and trust that your intuition will manage it or,

- Keep a separate "Things to do before my trip" list.

I opt for the latter with reminders on my calendar. You could also put reminders in your AF list as well. Personally, I find that trip things (unless you travel very frequently and are set up to travel) are out of the ordinary ... or maybe that is wrong ... they are so IN the ordinary that you scarcely think about them. I suspect that is why they tend to get overlooked. Why you wind up in Timbuktu without a toothbrush, for example. Anyway, I find that thinking through my daily activities helps me generate a list of things to consider for my trip and writing those on a list is helpful.

Now, to put this in context, I am just a tad paranoid about this. Some years ago, I drove to another city for a business meeting. I packed up and was ready to go early. (I traveled frequently so I had this all down at, you have to understand.) Just a few miles outside of my destination I was reviewing my next To Dos.

- Get to the City
- Find the hotel
- Unpack
- Change into my business suit ... OH CRAP!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!

I realized at that moment that I had left my suit hanging on the hall tree to be put in the car and never took it out and PUT IT IN THE CAR! Trust me, this was my many hundredth trip and I was no amateur at this whole business ... something just distracted me that day and ... well ... it was not a fun time.

So factor my paranoia into this whole business before you decide ;-)

Generally I will treat trip planning as a project or mini project. I used to do a lot of business travel and maintained a "Travel" file which contained a lot of reusable information - eg City Maps, specific info about hotels, packing lists (depending upon locations and time of year!) Client Contact Details etc. As most of my travel was in the US I kept a bag of US related travel items ready to go, including such things as plug adapters, US hairdryer, US stamps, travel stationery supplies etc. Basically, anything that could be prepared in advance was done so.

I also had a general check list (eg passport, tickets, company literature, set video etc) and then only needed to prepare a checklist for anything unique to the current trip.

Even though I do not travel so frequently I still have packing lists and an overnight bag packed and available so that I can literally go somewhere immediately in the case of an emergency - or perhaps when I've just had enough of my backlog - now there's an idea ....:-)

I think a checklist of things to do/action is vital for any trip but this can easily be prepared and actioned as part of a general "trip planning" task within AF. By the time the trip gets closer then most things will be prepared, with just a final checklist for day of departure tasks.

Yet another interesting post. Checklists are essential for me and I am finding I can integrate them with AF.

I travel abroad from the UK for 95% of my work, some assignments are in developing or recovering regions, so I use checklists a lot. This is so I can always have the right kit, supplies, spares with me to carry out my work, even if the baggage handlers manage to send my checked luggage somewhere else.

Like Christine, but not as much as her, I use OneNote and am moving some backlog and project stuff out of my AF book (Moleskine, medium size, hard cover, one end for work, other end flipped over for personal), but I still jot down checklists and items that may have be considered for checklists in my AF notebook.

This is because I only use the right page for AF entries and processing. The left-hand pages are used for important information, even notes on key meetings, IP settings for Internet access abroad and checklists.

So for my next assignment (Egypt next month for the month), I have a travel checklist on a left-hand page already set up and being amended as necessary. By processing this list, I can add outstanding items to the end of my AF list, eg get repeat medication to last the duration, replace Energizer USB recharger. My travel checklists have sections: travel/wear en route, cabin bag, and checkin case (hardshell to minimise damage and theft).

Thanks to this forum, I have picked up all sorts of useful tips, and because I seemed not be making progress at one point, I realised I had transgressed by entering too many backlogs and other "baggage" in my AF list at the start.

This has made me move such items out to OneNote along with projects/major tasks, BUT I list current project titles on a left-hand page. So I can do what others on this forum have advocated: put the next action (or more, if appropriate) for a project on my AF list (steps and next actions/tasks are listed in OneNote) for AF processing.

Similarly, I have crossed out backlog items from early/old pages and written them on a separate left-hand page from where they can be transferred one or more at a time and placed (again!) on the AF list like any other.
