My first AF notebook got filled up a couple days ago (it was a half-used notebook that had only 40 pages left).

So I just started another one.

At first, I was treating the two notebooks as one big AF list, and cycling through them both. I.e., when I come to the end of the last page of Notebook 2, I start back at the first active page of Notebook 1. When I finish all the pages in Notebook 1, I then continue to the first active page in Notebook 2. Etc.

But today I decided to try it differently -- let's just count all of Notebook 1 as "dismissed", and focus on Notebook 2. I've added a new task to Notebook 2: "review Notebook 1".

It is already helping me to move faster and stay more focused. I'm afraid I might forget to do important tasks that were still active in Notebook 1, but maybe AF will just take care of that automatically. I'll let you know how it goes.

Seraphim:

What I'd generally recommend when starting a new notebook is to transfer all the unactioned tasks in the old notebook into the new notebook, while keeping the page structure by drawing lines to indicate where each page ended in the old notebook:

Sort CDs
Prepare Friday's seminar
------------------------------
Prepare newsletter
------------------------------
Tidy bureau drawers
Learn video editing software
Z-pen Quickstart Guide
Thinking Session
------------------------------
Tidy under Bureau
Clean car
Arrange to have coffee with C
------------------------------
and so on.

The page structure is a very important part of the workings of AF.

Lynn

Mark's guidelines above are to retain the page structure which is a vital part of AF but I can see why you would not want to write out 250 tasks in that way!

It seems that realistically there are only two options - (1) to run two notebooks until completion of the 250 tasks or (2) to re-enter them. In fact re-entering may not be such a daunting task - it will be less than 10 pages and writing out a page may only take 5 minutes or so. My suggestion would be to rewrite the tasks but you do not need to do that in one go. When you are ready to start the new book work out how many pages you need for the old items (remember to include space for a line between each "page" as per Mark's recommendation), and add a task to "add old items to new notebook". You can then add the tasks gradually, knowing you have enough space, and start you new lists say 8 or so pages in.

Reply by Mike

2009-03-25 18:39
Giulia,

If you do it that way, you'll lose some of the power of AF. In order for it to work, you have to dismiss items that remain when the others on the page are done. If you simply move them forward, they are not dismissed and can hang around for another whole cycle of pages. If you want to consolidate, keep the page structure. IOW, put the remaining items from one page as new items on a blank page, draw a line indicating the page boundary, then add items from another consolidated page, draw a line, etc. Don't put anything else on those consolidated pages. Then in your cycle of pages, when you come to that consolidated page, treat each "section" as a separate page (because that was what it was before consolidation) and dismiss if you don't do at least one item of that group.
