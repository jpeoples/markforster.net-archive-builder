You go girl! ;-) ;-)

What's not to understand?

- One long list
- A line demarking a "closed sub list"
- Loop through the closed list until you can't do more
- Do one pass through the open list portion
- Go back and start looping through the closed list again
- Lather, rinse, repeat ;-0

When you make a pass of the closed list and don't do anything (like AF1 here)

- Draw a line at the end of the open list portion making it the new closed list
- Add any closed list items you want to save to the end of the NEW open list (after the line)
- Trash the OLD closed list

It should work fine with your prioritized goal oriented list as well.

The KEY points, as I see them are:

- It is ONE LONG LIST (with a "closed" and "open" section)
- - You process the first in a continual loop
- - You process the second in one single pass

- Switching is automatic
- - From closed to open when you find nothing to do
- - From open to closed at the end of one pass

- There is now only one dismissal rule
- - You dismiss ONLY from the closed list when nothing is done on a pass
- - You get to KEEP dismissed items if you choose

- You declare the open list "closed" when you invoke the dismissal rule

IMO, it is by far the simplest system yet. No forward and backward. No different dismissal rules. No entering dummy reminder tasks to switch directions. My cats could do this one. (But alas, the only item on their list today is to sleep until dinner time ;-)

Give it a shot! ;-)

Jacqueline:

If you "jump" to an item on the Active List, then what you have done in effect is to say that there are no tasks in the Backlog that feel ready to be done just then. That is actually the signal in any case to move to the Active List.

The important thing now is to continue from the task you have just done. So if it was early in the Active List continue through the Active List. If it was the last item on the Active List, return to the first task in the Backlog.

So, as you can see, you didn't cheat at all!

I think this is how the book "The Paradox of Choice - Why More is Less" (which I read yesterday) relates to time management: having more choice in what to do does not lead to happiness with our choices most of the time. Having more options makes us question those choices we have made. Of course, we don't THINK it will, we think we'll be happier with lots of choice - but the science proves us wrong.
