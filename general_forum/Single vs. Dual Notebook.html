I've been using AF since the beginning of the Beta program and (since I work outside the home) have taken MarkF's advice to setup a separate notebook for each of the two locations. This has been working very well - I love the system.

My question: Has anyone (who works away from home) yet tried merging their two notebooks? Any pros and cons? I'm currently using a small moleskine reporter's style (flip from the top) notebook and if I treat the open page as one long skinny page, I can put about fifty items on a "page". As I'm using it now, I started from the back for home and from the front for work, but I'm thinking that keeping it all together might be adequate.

I love the small reporter's style notebook as I can keep it in a shirt pocket for instant note taking - and a couple of paperclips easily mark the spot where I'm currently working and the spot to add new items. However... whenever I'm ready to capture a thought, I find it oddly irritating to have to decide whether to open to the work or to the home side.

It seems that with a sufficient number of lines per page I shouldn't have to make a conscious decision at the point of ideation - as I can eventually get to everything in its correct context.

I just started to experiment with having different books integrated. I need three of them: things to be done in the office, things to be done at home and things which I can do everywhere (i.e. for which I have to use the laptop or which are of general natures). And I work both from home and from my office.

Starting point was to have different pages in one notebook. Then I could just skip the non relevant pages. But the disadvantage is to find the right page which might be buried somewhere within the currently 10 open pages. My new setting will be: Split the page: Most tasks are general (not bound to office or home). Thus, I will start on the left side of my notebook (small moleskine) with the general tasks, on the right side top with the home tasks and at the right side bottom with the office tasks. If the left side (general tasks) has been filled, I go to the next left side in the book - unless the right side is still not used. If it is still empty then I use it also for general tasks. If the side which is used for office or home specific tasks is filled, I go to the next available right side in the book. It should be quite easy to mark the correct pages in the book (one for general, one for location specific tasks).

When cycling through the book the tasks on a page are general (at least about one half), and about half of the rest is location specific for the location where I'm currently in. Of course, some of the tasks are not doable in that moment - this might become a problem when the page should be dismissed - but I will see, I anyway haven't dismissed anything so far.

Sounds more complicated than it is - sorry for the long winded description.

A month on and I'm finding the single notebook to work well. I do have tasks that I extract onto other lists, such as for projects, but that falls more into my concept of project planning anyway - the difference being that now I would be more likely to treat those lists in an AF way than as a to do list. The entry of those items to the project list comes from AF as does the task to action the project. For me that keeps everything in the one list very easily.

With regard to work vs home items, I find that items naturally stand out according to where I am or what "mode" of working I am in. For example "Update Family Tree" or similar leisure tasks have been standing out at appropriate times - evenings or weekends - and work tasks during the day. Margaret raised this question above. However, from my experience so far, if an item from the "other mode" stood out I would "trust the system" and action it. Obviously I am home based so that is not a problem for me - if I "update Family Tree" between 9-5 no-one will sack me - similarly I can easily do accounts work on a Saturday without having to go to an external office. I suspect that if I was working in an external office "sort sock drawer" would be unlikely to stand out - even if it did hold more rational appeal that whatever I was currently engaged in :-)

I am finding that the system itself really does balance everything far better than I could myself. That's certainly not to say that I haven't had some difficulties, primarily due to deadlined backlogs and also to limited discretionary time on occasion. however the system has cetainly enabled me to deal with those eventualities in a far, far more efficient way than I would ever have done without it. It takes some time to put the degree of trust in the system that is needed in those circumstances (we have probably built up a significant habit of panic and giving in to the headless chicken syndrome), but even in those scenarios the system does work .......

I've had a change in my job, where I now have to keep a stricter schedule (we got reclassified as "non-exempt", i.e., hourly, whereas before we were "exempt", i.e., salaried).

I decided to use this opportunity to switch to separate books -- one for work, one for personal.

I am finding that it is much crisper, much easier to stay focused on what is really important in that particular context, and that my pages fly by much more quickly.

I did like having it all in one notebook, so I could stay mentally tuned to the items in the other context, and use the AF system to actually prompt me for when it's time to switch contexts.

But now I am thinking it may have been better to do it with two notebooks all along -- and just block out the time on my calendar for when I'm doing which context.

Also, having two notebooks is not the nightmare I was thinking. I keep them both handy all the time. So, if I'm "working" but have a personal thought, I can still just jot it down in my personal AF book very easily. It's working great.
