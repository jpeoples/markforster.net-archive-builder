Hi,

I'm in a state of flux. I use insights from Mark all over the place: current initiatives, processing lists on Autofocus principles, listing work tasks on Do-It-Tomorrow principles ... But I've also used principles from Zen To Done, so I've now got a hybrid system.

I keep a calendar for scheduled items. I block off a section of the day for work (I'm self-employed), and I keep a DIT list for work items. For other tasks I maintain a DWM-style list.

But I find a lengthy list so discouraging that I've radically shortened it by bringing the dismissal date forward to just one week from the entry date. Having things hanging around for a week is quite long enough if they don't get pushed forward. (I've accordingly renamed the list from DWM to OWM, for One Week Method.)

As a result, the number of items on the list remains in the 20s, which is quite enough for me.

I felt I had to introduce the Zen-to-Done Big Rocks principle to get a sense of priorities, so I began defining a number of the most important items to do for the week ahead - a few each in the areas of personal / family / work. Sure enough, after a promising first week, this crashed in flames. I really hate * anybody *, including me, telling me what to do!

But I still think I need the Big Rocks principle, so I'm reintroducing it on strict kaizen principles - "each step so small I can't fail to take it". So, defining a single Most Important Thing in each area for the coming day; redefining them as they get done; leaving greater ambitiousness for the future. The MITs appear at the top of my OWM list on my phone.

It'll be two or three weeks before I pronounce judgement on this particular recipe.

Chris

After developing four or five systems, I continue to come back to the most simple one I developed, the Steno notebook method. I hesitate to even use the word develop because all it really entails is writing what needs to be done in one steno notebook and sticking to that one place so I always know where to look for what I need to do. I do use groupings by project followed by a list of what I need to do or who I need to call. I let my mind pick the priority as I cycle through the pages. Writing in one place along with a simple a-z filing system have allowed me to be more productive than anything else I have tried. I'm still using a daily 'focus' list each day with up to 3 MITs (most important tasks), nowadays in a spiral bound notebook on the right hand pages.

The top of the left hand page is then used for an AF-like list of ongoing tasks that aren't day specific.
