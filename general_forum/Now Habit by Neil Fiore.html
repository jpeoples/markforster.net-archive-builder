Try it an see. I very much enjoyed reading The Now Habit and found that it just did not help me at all.

Mark's Do It Tomorrow, on the other hand, helped me enormously from the very first time I used it.

Just off the top of my head, one thing both books have in common is the use of a kind of "reverse psychology." Fiore has you block out all the times when you commit yourself to NOT WORKING. It's all very clever. You know that you will have time devoted to not working, so that when you are working you won't be anxious that your leisure needs will go unmet.

Forster's reverse psychology can be found in some of the self-talk he suggests we use. "I won't write the XYZ report, I'll just open the folder and take out the paper." Forster has me tell myself that I am not going to work on some overwhelming project, I'll just do a simple physical task.

Like I said, DIT worked for me and The Now Habit did not. Different strokes for different folks.
