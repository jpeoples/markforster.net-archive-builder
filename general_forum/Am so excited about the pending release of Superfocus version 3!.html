The trouble with the published SAF is (to quote Mark) "It can become rather directionless with a lack of proper balance between the two columns on the page."

I'm not sure what the "proper balance" is, but you (and I) can easily get stuck trying to decide what's next.

I think the problem with GTD is the complexity of organizing distracts, AND the actual organization somehow doesn't fit the way the mind works. I think the problem is a clash between what you think you should do, and what you feel ready to do. When these two are different, doing the former builds stress. Doing the latter doesn't. AF makes the choice easier.

Bill Jensen, author of Simplicity (Making Work Work), follows this theme in a slightly different context, that of the modern office. He proposes that every delegation of work should include, rather than a detailed spec of the work, these three elements:
Know - what the receiver needs to understand
Feel - information affecting how the worker should feel about the work
Do - what this person should make happen.

The point is that a modern knowledge worker can be much more effective when the mind and heart are in tune with work expectation, so communicate this way. This level of understanding also empowers the employee to deal with the inevitable obstacles that arise.

For you as a user of a TM system (like AutoFocus), whether you are assigned the work or define it yourself, you control when and how it gets done. This work is best accomplished when your heart and mind are in tune. AF presents a mix of tasks so you can pick one that fits. When GTD processes the tasks, often only your mind is geared for action. AF4R's division of labour presents the same potential flaw if you get yourself stuck in the Unfinished page.

Re GTD
Honestly, I don't see GTD and AF as competing models. To me, they address different concerns completely. GTD is excellent at helping you define the tasks that need to be done (or *can* be done) but doesn't really address the actual execution of those tasks. AF and its derivatives manage the *doing* without paying much attention to filtering the tasks from the clutter of information that come our way.

Looking at GTD from that perspective will-- at least for me-- boil the process down to a couple of questions:
1. Is this "something to know" or "something to do"?
2. If it's "something to do", what can I do *right now*?
The answers to that second question go on my AF list to be processed accordingly.
