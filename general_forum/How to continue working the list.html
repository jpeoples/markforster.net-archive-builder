Hi Eugene,

Good question!

I've only worked with AF1 and never kept a record of where I stopped on the page (ie. the last item worked on), only what page I left off on. I don't think I would have known which item I left off doing, so I think the answer for me when doing AF1 was to start off again at the top of the page you left off on.

FWIW, I usually started the day on the page AFTER the page I worked on the night before (as long as I'd done something on it). ie. I would go quickly through the page in the evening from the last task done, nothing remaining stood out, I would move the page marker to the next page and leave the list for the remainder of the day and start on the new page in the morning (which is starting from the last item left, I guess).

In AF1, it likely shouldn't really matter if you start in the middle of the page or the top because you're circling around the page multiple times?

If you have little discretionary time isn't a system with low overhead like AF good? The question becomes can you keep up with your work. As the AF list grows or shrinks you can see how you are doing. In DIT we had the audit process to assess this. If you are in a busy patch then its fair enough that you fall behind however if your busy patch is permanent what are going to do. Work more hours (hello burnout), work more efficiently (hello time management system) or cut work out. The latter is difficult when you are under pressure and more so when your boss is difficult. You've got to choose priorities (ie what you'll do in the next few days) and agree with stakeholders. Alternatively list everything and divide between must do, should do and could do. I can tell you all what won't help. Spending hours researching and choosing a new time management system, especially GTD. By the time you've listed your 100 open loops and the next actions you'll certainly feel swamped.

Which brings me to another issue (sorry if I am repeating a recent post), the idea that a project can be reduced to quick actions like call peter, meet susie, book room etc. Some projects require you to think what needs to be done which takes time when you haven't done it before and you don't know what to do next. Some projects just require hours of hard slog and you've just got to face it. Don't be seduced by thinking there is an easy way, sometimes there isn't.

Then I just work on each one via a standing out method. With the little stuff that I have a problem with (what Knaus calls maintenance procrastination items), they have their own section. That's just because I didn't really realize before that I don't do that stuff (like cleaning or getting the oil changed in the car) because I filter it out as unimportant. But I was doing a lot more of that kind of thing with AF - too much for my own comfort! :-) So I want to spend a bit of time on that stuff, but not to the exclusion of things that I know will make more of a difference.

In January, I'm moving to semi-retirement, so we'll see how the method holds up or if I go back to pure AF (v.1). I know there's an aversion here to working based on previously determined priority, but I can't help it, I've done it for so many years that I feel discombobulated if I don't do that - like I'm doing something wrong. And it's worked so well for me that I hate trying to fix something that isn't broken.

I'm actually a bit puzzled about what Jacqueline says about not being able to work AF unless one has lots of discretionary time. All time is discretionary unless one has no choice at all about what to do. So being in a meeting isn't discretionary time and a true emergency isn't discretionary time but just about everything else (in an office context) is. So if you have two urgent reports to get ready by close of play, then that is still discretionary time because you still have to use your discretion about which report to work on first and what other tasks need to be done as well as the two reports in the time frame.

I find AF4 extremely powerful in situations where I only have a short time to do a lot of work. If one is floundering, the solution is not to abandon the system but to focus it. You do that by cutting everything out of your list which isn't essential to the goals which you are currently working on.
