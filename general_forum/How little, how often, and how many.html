I don't recall discussions on the principle behind "little & often". My understanding is that if you do one thing only, or spend all your time on a few things, everything else falls apart. So instead, if you do a little bit on each thing, nothing falls apart. Then you repeat doing a little of each.

But it's often the case that you can do bigger tasks more effectively by concentrating on them for 30min or maybe even a few hours. And smaller tasks are more effectively done in 10 minute bits than in 30 second bits.

I'm thinking the most effective strategy is to daily do little bits on a good number of things, and lots on a few things. I think it's less effective to pick up those few things lots of times, doing only a little bit each time.

But I'm really not sure.

Little and often applies in two ways in SuperFocus and 3T:

1) Medium to large sized tasks can be worked at in several goes over a short period in Column 2.

2) If these tasks are part of a larger whole another task from the whole can be entered at the end of the list.

For example, reading "War and Peace"

"Read Chapter 1" takes several goes, which are controlled in column 2.

When that is finished, the next task is "Read Chapter 2" which is entered at the end of the list.

So the whole book is read little and often.

Small tasks, e.g. "Select which book to read next" etc can be done in one go.

Hours worked per week = number projects x sessions per project x time per session.

If one number on the right goes up, another has to go down. There's no way around it, unless you change the number of hours in a week.

So, yes, if you have an hour a day for projects you could do 5 minutes on 12 projects every day, if 5 minutes were effective. You could also do a full hour once a week on 5 projects, or a full hour all 5 days on one project.

For projects, my Goldilocks zone is 30 minutes per project, at least every other day. I have 90 minutes a day, so that's a whopping 6 projects. 5 is more realistic. When a system is filled to capacity, it can't react to changes. If nothing happens to the extra time, I can do an extra session or relax. (Or I can fool myself into thinking I can handle an extra project and watch them all suffer.)

Less than 30 minutes and I don't get into the project. The first 5 minutes are usually the least productive and messiest. 30 usually finishes a defined chunk. Also, if the sessions are too short I spend too much time moving between them, putting away one file and getting out the next.

If I miss more than a day I get out of the habit. The light at the end of the tunnel gets dimmer and seems to recede. Also, the file and equipment gets buried. Twice in a row is often good, but it can leave me feeling so virtuous that I skip, not just one day, but several. See previous comment about receding light.

In the term "little and often", the word "little" is relative. What it means is working at something regularly rather than in one huge effort (usually at the last minute).

The classic example is piano practice when you have a weekly lesson. The teacher will encourage you to practice your exercises for a relatively short time every day, rather than have a huge session the night before the lesson. In this case "relatively short" probably means half-an-hour daily, rather than two to three hours in a panic.

In the case of a concert pianist "relatively short" would probably be more like four hours.

On the other hand clearing your email little and often might only take ten minutes.
