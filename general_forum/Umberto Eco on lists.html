Hi,

Thought people might enjoy an interview with Eco on the value of lists throughout history. He has some great comments about the psychology of list making, such as

"The list is the origin of culture. It's part of the history of art and literature. What does culture want? To make infinity comprehensible. It also wants to create order -- not always, but often. And how, as a human being, does one face infinity? "

Full interview here: http://www.spiegel.de/international/zeitgeist/0,1518,659577,00.html

For a skeptical view of Eco's opinion of lists, here's a link to a fascinating (to me) blog, "Taking Note," by an academic in the history of philosophy: http://takingnotenow.blogspot.com/2009/11/umberto-eco-on-lists.html

The blog writer, Manfred, prefers Susan Sontag's depiction of list-making: http://takingnotenow.blogspot.com/2009/11/sontag-on-lists-and-collections.html

And he adds a nice coda with a quotation on collecting: http://takingnotenow.blogspot.com/2008/10/collecting.html

BTW, Manfred Kuehn (Boston University) is a devotee of a Windows-based wiki-outliner called ConnectedText. He wrote an interesting testimonial to the program here: "Some Idiosyncratic Reflections on Note-Taking in General and ConnectedText in Particular", http://www.connectedtext.com/manfred.html

His blog is, to me, absolutely fascinating as he draws in references from historians and philosophers on making and taking notes, he loves outliners, and he seems to try out every note-taking or wiki or outlining app ever released for Windows. I browsed his blog archives for two whole afternoons and wanted more.

Apologies for the firehose of information! One thing just leads to another...Now if I could only devise an SAF for making, taking, keeping, referring, and retrieving notes, my life would be complete! But then what would I have left to obsess about? :)
