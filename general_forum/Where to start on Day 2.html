Wolfgang:

Actually, your post made me think about the time overheads involved in switching tasks when I was using the time-burst technique in DIT to get through a large volume of tasks. In AF, this has virtually disappeared for me because of the little and often approach it cleverly weaves into the system. For eg, a project may involve getting the files and software ready to tackle it (the time overhead), so that is all I do, before crossing it out and putting it at the end of the list.

When I reach the end, it seems like a fresh start, and I feel amazingly motivated to get started on the project. Now, you will note, that overhead has not disappeared, but simply broken up. And the next pass will get you to a running start on it.

With time bursts, I used to get frustrated with the artificial time cut off, when I really wanted to spend a bit more time on a task, or some times less.

I have virtually given up using any sort of timer, and now let my own rhythm dictate the time I dictate on a project or task. And AF has worked well under several heavy work load volumes coming at me in an unexpected way last week.

Not sure if my post helps you in any way on your projects, but I think if you allow the mental context switching to be a single item you do to kick start your project, I think you may find that you are mentally geared to start work on it on the next pass. Not to mention the fact that you will be quickly knocking off a few low level items if you are eager to get to it at the end, further preparing you to concentrate on the project as some smaller mosquito tasks may have been done along the way.
