2009-10-19 13:30
Switching from AF4 to AF2 for a time crunch of one or two days can be combined with having a separate list in this way:

Add a new item at the end of the open list and call it "List for time crunch" (or something like this).
Add the items of your new separate list, but more indented than the "List for time crunch" item.
Leave several lines empty to add new time-crunch items if necessary.
Add the item "End of time crunch list" at the same level of indention as the "List for time crunch" item.
Add new non-time-crunch items.

E.g., the end of the open list would look like:

Work on Project K
Work on Project B
List for time crunch
-- item # 1
-- item # 2
-- item # 3
(empty line)
(empty line)
(empty line)
(empty line)
End of time crunch list.
Work on Project F
Work on Project H

Then work your list in the AF2 way.

Whenever I did this I didn't care about dismissal for those one or two days.

A word in support of AF3 (or AF2)
So many of the discussions here are about great progress with AF4 that I may be in a minority. But an alternative perspective may be worth it if there are others like me, who haven't been able to get in the flow with AF4. I'd vote for keeping all versions of AF as options, as individuals' personal styles and work situations do vary. I've finally realised that AF4 doesn't fit with my circumstances or with my inclinations, not sure which, and it was with some relief that I've switched back to AF3 last week.

AF4 makes perfect sense to me in theory, and I expected to follow it. But after several weeks of trying, I've realised that AF4 seems to require more discipline and a more methodical approach to working through the list than I'm able to sustain.

Typically my time available is in short bursts between meetings or other commitments, so I need to keep scanning the list to know I'm picking out the most important thing to be doing at that time. I need to be able to note down quick reminders about urgent things to do when I get back to my desk as well as the bigger tasks with future deadlines. So when trying to do AF4, after scanning and picking out the must do now items, (or the items on the separate list of things I actually need to do next), I lose track of where I was 'supposed' to be up to in the main list or even which list (closed or open) I was in; this is because I can't tell when I am just scanning quickly to pick out any 'must do now' urgent items, or making a proper pass through the list where I need to follow 'the rules' for moving to the other list (or not), or for reviewing/ dismissing. There seems to be an overhead to working the list which doesn't flow for me.

With AF3 (or AF2), none of this is a problem - it allows you to keep on top of current items, look out for things further back which are becoming urgent, and forces you to deal with the oldest items gradually day by day. It suits fragmented work sessions, keeps you responsive to what's going on now without having to work 'off list', and it doesn't disrupt the dynamic of the list if you don't strictly follow the process but dart about to make the most of opportunities which present themselves to get things done. It just seems lots more flexible and simpler to me. Just my view of course.

By the way, thanks to Mark and everyone on this forum for your thoughts and ideas about getting everything done. It's such an invaluable resource for anyone interested in learning to be more productive.

Hi Jane. I'm reading through your explanation of your work patterns and it makes sense. AF2/3 have a bias towards urgency, and if you have a lot of urgency it will help you get there.

AF4 however has a bias towards *preventing* urgency. If you can get to a point where very little requires immediate action, AF4 will keep you there by first rubbing out your backlog before these tasks become late, and second giving you a chance to work repeatedly on tasks that have become important just now, by re-inserting at the end of the list, so you can move a few things along quickly.

As for losing track, may I suggest a paperclip attached to the current task?

A few points about the above discussion:

1) I don't think that jumping between AF4 and AF2 is a good thing. I envisaged that it might be a acceptable if one had a sudden unexpected period of urgent tasks... but I saw that as an exceptional case. If you work is always predominantly urgent then you would be better off sticking with AF2. If you have specific times when you need to work urgently (like clearing your desk before going on holiday) then best to use AF2 with a special "Must Do Before Going on Holiday" list.

2) AF4 is perfectly capable of dealing with a normal degree of urgent items. As you will have seen from my demonstration, the aim is to keep moving quickly through the list. You should avoid getting stuck endlessly in the closed list. Also avoid dumping huge quantities of stuff into your list. It should reflect those things which you are actively working on - not everything that you think you might get round to "sometime".

Reply by Mark Forster

2009-10-20 12:46
One further thing:

You should not put anything on your list which does not reflect your current commitments and goals. If your list grows too large to deal with, then you should review your current commitments with a view to cutting them back to a manageable size.

I do think the initial AF4 instructions are slightly incomplete, and Mark's explanations in-forum help to complete them. There is also a need to handle things beyond the immediate commitment, and AF4 doesn't address that.

I keep Maybe Later lists of possible future commitments from which I may borrow new tasks to put into AF4. I process tasks following AF4 principles:
1. Everything goes to my active list.
2. Later as I pass by a task I may evaluate it. If it represents something I'm not committed to doing soon, I either delete it or move it to one of these Maybe Later lists.
3. When I dismiss the Closed list, I scan the Maybe Laters for things I want to add.

Several people had expressed sentiments like Alex:
<>

Honestly, I think this simply means you misunderstood the rules. The rules tell you to work on something if you feel like it. If you're feeling stuck in the closed list, that means you feel there's something more important on the other side and you go there: Pass over any task you feel is less important than what's ahead.

Then if you come back to the closed list and find none of these things are important now, dismiss them! And guess what: You will now be "stuck" processing the tasks you previously complained you couldn't get to. And for those old tasks you really don't want to throw out: You re-enter them in your new open list, and they will sit there waiting for you to come back.

It all works very smoothly provided you keep your lists to a reasonable size.
