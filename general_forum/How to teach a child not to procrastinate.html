Author: Jacqueline | Created: 2009-07-27 14:17 | Last Activity: 2009-07-28 23:50

Original Post

2009-07-27 14:17
My 21 y.o. son has been helping me with the renovations on my house and I get him to run errands for me etc. I was putting a list on the fridge of tasks for him to do that week (he is working a part time job). I haven't been nagging at all, thinking the shortage of money would give him incentive to work on the house with me. Consequences, right? I pay him about 1.5 times per hour (cash) what he makes at his part-time job (taxable) - so in reality, almost double.

He's moving about an hour away to go to University in the fall. Yesterday I told him it would probably be best if he moved out August 1st (he has already rented a place where he's going to school) as he wasn't helping me anyway. His plans were to spend a couple of days a week at his new place (doing what, I don't know - brushing up on his Jon Stewart/Stephen Colbert expertise?) and the remainder helping me. We agreed that if he didn't put in 25 hours / week minimum working for me, I would take his car away if he couldn't pay me for it in full (he's paid half but doesn't have the money to pay for the whole thing).

My concern is that he will end up going to school (and in life) and procrastinate as he's already taken 2 years off after his first year of University. He also tends to avoid things like paying his cell phone bill or student loan - when he has the money - which makes no sense to me. Then he avoids them when they call instead of telling them that he's paid it. He doesn't even open his mail!?!

Is this procrastination and avoidance normal? Is it something most kids just grow out of? I just worry because I have three brothers like this and these kind of habits have made a mess of their lives (incredibly intelligent but no motivation).

Is there some way I can get through to him or do I just let him screw up and take the consequences?

I too have dealt with this and was told by a professional the sooner in life you make kids deal with the consequences of their actions the better off they will be. So for instance kids in middle school when the grades don't count for college is a perfect time to let them hand in the paper you told them to edit which they didn't, not bring them the homework you knew they forgot. Let them get a tardy because they forgot to set their alarm etc. The hope is when the consequences are real, they will have learned they need to perform. It is hard as a parent to sit back and watch kids not do what they need to do. The other reality is that some people are more organized, motivated and driven than others.

Best of luck

The only piece of advice I'll offer is the following.... rarely do cognitive solutions work for emotional problems. And... without knowing the whole score (read: family dynamics, major changes - positive or negative), most advice will prove to be ineffective.

Parents (er. all of us) enter the first session of therapy with the hope/fantasy that the "expert" has a quick-fix, one-size-fits-all solution to their dilemma. While it is important to not deflate the fantasy, it is also crucial to educate the client about the age/stage of their child, family dynamics, and the process of therapy.
