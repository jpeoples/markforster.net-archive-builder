Today I lost my paper organizer. I was walking back to the car from the grocery when I noticed that my notebook was not with me. I came back to the places where I was before, thinking that I left it somewhere, but I never found it. I asked some of the employees there but no one seemed to have noticed nor had anyone returned it. I however had to leave because the groceries were needed, so I just went back home, hoping that somebody turned it in.

I drove back in a daze. The thing is, there was nothing much of monetary worth there, but all of my plans, all of my projects were there. I have my old organizer notes in my room but they were scattered and unorganized, and most of them are outdated. However, what was I to do? I lost my AF notebook. Deal with it, I told myself.

I was not able to do much this evening, I just did my chores. After all of them were done, I looked for the grocery's phone number and called them. Drats, no one turned in the notebook. Oh well, I said to myself, it's gone. I was ruminating in my head whether I should be buying a new one when I opened my email.

And lo and behold! Somebody emailed me about my organizer! I called the number and I was able to talk to the finder of my notebook. She said she found it in a part of the store I never went to. I assume that somebody stole the notebook from me (it looks dignified and expensive looking, you know) thinking it had money inside, but finding nothing of worth there just left it where my savior found it. Anyways, we set up a meeting tomorrow morning for me to pick it up.

Notwithstanding the hopefully happy ending to my adventure tomorrow morning, it got me wondering whether this notebook has practically made it a crutch for me to live life, as Erik Loraine said in one of his videos. It disturbs me that losing a notebook with little monetary value could potentially bring someone's plans to such potential chaos, and wondering the wisdom of it all.

Or maybe I shouldn't have brought it in the grocery.

Or maybe I shouldn't have bought such a beautiful notebook.

I dunno.

If only it is not so hard to back-up a paper notebook like we could a smartphone, PDA or laptop. Ahh well.

Sorry for the rant.

God bless!

Sorry, I should have been more explicit.

As I said in the video, if you actually need a crutch, then by all means I believe you should use it. I mean if you break a leg and don't use a crutch, you won't go far.

On the other side if you don't have a broken leg and use a crutch, then you are effectively crippling yourself. Good use of habits tend to highlight some things that can be treated as crutches from then on.

One could of course argue that since habits are a kind of crutch, that it's just the same thing but only changing shape. I tend to believe that it's the fundamental way that human being gets orguanized and it has been like that starting from our prehistoric past. We are geared towards it and I feel it more as a natural state then a crutch.

This sounds as if it could have been one of those situations where you draw on the goodwill you created by having such good systems in the first place, like when you fry your hard drive at work and the company backup recovery fails.

1. dump everything you can recall on a new list, and keep it handy to add things as they occur
2. contact your contacts, tell them what has happened and ask for their patience. Share what you are working on for them and ask if they need anything else.

Which leads to a useful question: "have I built up the goodwill I would need if MY system were to go down?". Hmm.

Every time you enter a new page in your notebook, make a photocopy of the just-completed page. So, in the worst case you'll only lose the tasks you've written down since the last photocopy. And, since those will be the most recently written, it will be easier to recreate. This will probably keep your losses to a minimum.

That must have been awful! I felt that way when learning to use a PDA, and again when it started to die, even though one of the benefits of a PDA is how easy it is to make a backup on the computer.

I lost my purse notebook one time at an office. I must have been talking rather than paying attention. Fortunately they called me the next day. Normally I treat that book the way I treat my wallet -- in my hand or in my purse. Not even in my shopping bag -- my purse.

My other lists and notes live on my desk. If I need something from them while out, I make a copy.

The only exceptions are the shopping lists, which lives on the fridge (we add to it as we get low on anything) until one of us goes shopping. I have one list for the grocery store (which I go to frequently) and one for all other stores. When I combined them, every week I had to copy the non-grocery items to the next list.

Yes, lists can become crutches, but I prefer to think of them as baskets. GTD has it right -- you let the list hold thoughts for you so you can concentrate on what's in front of you.

I can recreate them well enough if I have to, but it's easier to do it right once.
