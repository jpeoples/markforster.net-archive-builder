A NYT article about intuition got me thinking about AF. The article was about Airport Security and what they do besides undressing people in front of metal detectors:

>>A well-trained eye and ear — emphasis on the well-trained — can pick up signals a metal detector cannot.

“We started off thinking, What is it we do better than anybody else? What’s the advantage we have? And it’s that we see two million people every day. We know what normal is,” Mr. Hawley said. A good behavioral detection officer has developed the skills to separate normal airport anxieties from fundamental signs of “hostile intent,” he said.<<

http://www.nytimes.com/2007/08/21/business/21road.html

In AF, the recommendation is to "trust my subconscious" and read a page until one item "stands out". Very well. But my first weeks with the system were not pleasant: I always had the suspicion that I was completing the wrong tasks, even if my subconscius was obviously pleased.

At some point, the cat was obsessively groomed, the floor looked shiny and I had answered every conceivable email, re-conected with long lost friends and repaired the copy machine at the office until I dropped all the lists, finished that ugly work project I had been avoiding, and re-started my AF system with a few changes: I separated work and private tasks (left page private, right pages work) I had a separate project list on my computer, and I reminded myself in AF to look at it from time to time.

In this "planning mode", I also created more granular plans of the bigger projects, entered deadlines into the calendar, thought about priorities and "milestones". then, I entered a few items in the AF lists and updated the status of a few projects.

We tend to think that intuition is always natural, that the "gut instinct" knows best. Unfortunately, not in every case: The intuition of a child to run across the street, the risky behaviour of a stock market trader.

This time I had a different experience with AF: Somehow my mind was much more willing to let goal-related items "stand out". This was not only a matter of motivation.

To trust the subconscios maybe is not the end of the story. The ability to autofocus might need training and experience as every other activity that relies on intuition.

The instructions for AF are straightforward and helpful. But for people with complex commitments, regularly thinking about the bigger picture of their work might be an integral part of the system, to develop a more educated intuition, so to say.

M_N:

You mean the instructions should include something like:"DO include plenty of creative tasks such as Think about, Investigate; Discuss; Review"?

To be quite clear, AF is not intended to rely on intuition only. It is intended to balance the intuitive and rational minds.

Your experience starting AF was interesting. To someone reading what you say like myself, it stands out that most of your difficulties came from "that ugly work project I had been avoiding".

I don't know enough about what actually happened, but if you were working strictly according to the AF instructions, there should have come a point where you either had to do the work project (or at least start on it) or dismiss it. If you dismissed it, then that should have been the time to work out why you were avoiding it and what you could do about it.

In the event, what appears to have happened is that AF actually caused a crisis in your mind about the project, which resulted in your doing it anyway. Was this the triumph of rationality over intuition or the other way round, if every part of you was screaming "I must do that project"?

In fact my experience, and that of other people on this forum, is that following the AF process is an education in itself.

When Mark says "In fact my experience, and that of other people on this forum, is that following the AF process is an education in itself. " that is SO true. And it carries on - it's not a case that you wake up one morning and think "I know everything there is to know about AutoFocus, what it does, how it works in every scenario, what systems it works best with, what colour pen to use, etc etc etc" In fact the moment you think you know what is going to happen next ..... it doesn't. The more I test the system, and try other things around it, the more it surprises me.

Some days it is easier to trust the system than others. Some days I get in a flap about something or other that I really "ought" to do. Somedays I will think - "aha, if I do this and put stuff into AF this way, I will get those flappy things done within the system" Piffle! What normally happens is that I get more and more frustrated - resent the things that I am doing even though they are on my list - and the only cure for this very sad state of affairs is to go back to "what stands out", and what stands out genuinely rather than because I have tried to force it to do so by telling my subconscious that it has big neon flashing lights around it.

As was posted elsewhere, it's getting back to the items standing out being FUN!

When I took on my first employee I stressed that one of the rules of the company was that we must have fun. We worked hard, we planned hard, we got stressed, we had successes and we had failures. But we also had fun. Then one day she came to me and said, "Chris, I'm not having fun anymore" That was quite a defining moment - a moment that required a complete rethink, not so much on what we were doing, but on how we were doing it.

So how does that compare with AF? AF is not just a system for getting things done and definitely not just "a To Do list" :-) It is, as Mark says, a system to balance the rational and the intuitive. It is a system that provides Focus. It is a system that enables us to do what is important for us. THAT is where the confusion often lies ...... our intuition knows what is important to us - but that may be very different to what we, or others, have told us is important, and it is then that there can be a real battle. If our deep seated desire is to go and raise sheep on a Scottish island and we are currently working in a high tech City industry, we will never be comfortable with AF because it will not let us forget what is really important. If we are determined to stick with the high tech job at any cost (and I mean at any cost as opposed to a means towards the island croft) then only a structured system, a system to do, will work.

Sure we can use AF to do, but that waters down it's full impact to the extent that it will start to behave like a To Do list and it's real benefits will just evaporate. To repeat what Mark says, "following the AF process is an education in itself"
