2009-03-11 20:04
I've been using one notebook since the beginning, and it's been working for me. However, I wanted to try the multiple-notebook method. So now I have three notebooks: Work, Home, and Creative (for purely fun projects to do in my actual, spare time).

Last night, I was feeling good about the advantages multiple notebooks could have, and looking forward to trying this out. But, today, I find myself feeling major resistance to using any of them. I've actually been working without my Work notebook today. I've been doing stuff, but I'm already starting to feel that antsy, out-of-control feeling. :-D

A part of me wants to go right back to using one notebook immediately. Another part of me still thinks separate notebooks would have lots of advantages, so I should try to stick with it for at least a while and see if the resistance will fade as I get used to multiple notebooks.

For those who use separate notebooks, what do you like about it? And, for those who've tried both, which did you choose to go with and why?

I have a Work book and a Home book. I do this because my Work (like most people's) is totally separate from my Home life: in a different location, with different tools and people available. And I'd prefer to forget about work when I leave the office.

At home, I mix chore tasks and creative tasks in one book. I can't imagine having separate books being effective... how would I know which book to pick up? I would get stuck in that dilemma, and thus not pick up either. When you use one book for both kinds of task, you scan the list of tasks on a page and let your subconscious mind decide whether now is the right time for chores or creativity.

Personally I have been surprised how often it chooses to work on a chore instead of a creative act -- but once that decision is made the chore stops feeling like a chore, and becomes quite pleasurable.

2009-03-12 1:52
I personally use two notebooks, since I've found that my work and home tasks are best kept separated.

But one thing I really liked about having one list is that it allows AF to manage your context switching for you.

This is one thing I could never do well with GTD -- I'd have all my tasks organized into different contexts. And let's say that right now I'm working through my "@Computer" context. Well, there's enough work in that context to keep me busy for several decades at least. So how do I know when it's time to switch to "@Calls" or "@Home"? Well, I need some external prompting. That usually meant blocking out time on the calendar -- but I always resisted it.

And I know why I resisted it. Let's say I blocked off a couple hours for @Computer. But I've got a nagging feeling that there's a really important phone call I should be making. Don't remember what it is, but it's really nagging at me. So, I ignore my self-assigned time block for @Computer, and switch over to @Calls. Turns out the call could have waited, but while I'm there, I keep having this nagging feeling that I should be working on some other project -- switch to @Boss or @Home or whatever. But I can't remember.

So, I switch again, and realize I *do* have an urgent item I need to discuss with my boss. So I go into @Boss mode, and while I'm talking with him, go through the rest of the items I've saved up in the @Boss context.

Meanwhile, my @Computer time block has evaporated, and I've still got tons of work to do in that context. When am I ever going to do it?


With AF I never had this problem. Everything is there on the page together. I may be "at work", but all the personal stuff is standing out. Maybe that means I should take an extended lunch break to blast through some of those things. Or maybe it even means I should schedule some time off to focus on my personal stuff.

Or maybe I'm working on the computer, but the only thing on the AF list that stands out are the phone calls. OK, switch to phone call mode, make a phone call or two, and then see where AF guides me next.

GTD never did a good job of helping me decide when to switch contexts. But this is an area where AF really shines.

2009-03-12 11:12
I prefer and believe it's better to have a single list, but in my case it's proving problematic.

I find myself in a situation where I work or stay out of my house a lot, so when I'm back, I'm exhausted and I don't even want to open the notebook. That makes me neglect many tasks that can only be performed there.

Next day at work, going through the list, I realize that most of the tasks can only be done at home, leaving only a few that I can perform at work (I'll call them A/B/C). Many times, I just don't feel that I have to work on any of the three at that specific moment, and know that there are many that I'd rather perform, that are on the next page. A, B and C are not ready for dismissal; I just know for certain that I'll work on them a few hours later.

This is when I find some distortion. Many of the tasks that are on the next page and would allow me to delay A/B/C just a bit are not there because the home-only tasks are firmly taking their place, and they won't disappear. This leads me to either dismiss A/B/C, or to start working on them even if I don't "feel like to". But if that page would have been full of work-only tasks, there's a big chance that during its life cycle, A B and C would have been completed or started.

I could easily fix the situation by using two notebooks, but by insisting in using only once, I realize how severely I am neglecting my home-tasks. I knew that I was delaying a lot of stuff, but not on this level. By going through the lists again and again, I realize that there is a huge imbalance between the things that I do out of my house, and inside of it. If I'd use two lists, I would surely ignore the "home" one and be productive on the "work" one. Using only one kind of encourages balance.

Now, for the system to work I can choose between splitting it in two, and accept that I won't be doing a lot at home, or to keep only one and start distributing my time/energy in a better fashion. Haven't decided yet.
