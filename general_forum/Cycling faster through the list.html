With all of Mark Forster's systems, I have always struggled to cycle through the list fast enough. If I don't cycle fast enough, I can end up "chasing the end of the list" and never revisiting the beginning of the list. Even if I do cycle through from time to time, infrequent cycling means infrequent exposure to the items in my list. My list will work better if I am constantly exposed to its contents; the subconscious mind will always be processing them in the background. Also, infrequent cycling means I won't trigger the "dismissal" rules, which means I will be lacking one of the key ingredients of Mark Forster's systems.

Here are some rule modifications that have helped me cycle through the list faster, in order of most effective to least effective (in my experience at least). These modifications assume one is using the standard SF3 rules ( http://www.markforster.net/blog/2011/2/10/rules-for-superfocus.html ).

1. OVERCOME YOUR FEAR OF DISMISSAL. Seriously. Fear of dismissal can cause you to "game" the system and play tricks to prevent a page from getting dismissed. Reflecting on dismissed items is one of the most powerful, even life-changing, elements of Mark Forster's systems. Don't deprive yourself of its power. Let "standing out" and "dismissal" do their work.

2. Set a time limit to get through each page so that you will cycle through your notebook at least once per day. E.g., if you have 12 active pages, and 8 hours of discretionary time per day, then set a time limit of 8/12 = 40 minutes per page. Set a timer, and move on when the timer rings. If you haven't actioned anything in Column 1 yet, then dismiss everything left on the page. I don't LIKE using this rule, since using a timer just feels "kludgey" and awkward. But since it *IS* so effective, I do use it if I seem to be moving too slowly and need a kick-start.

3. Enter urgent items into Column 2 on the NEXT page instead of the current page. (If it makes you anxious to put it on the next page, because you think you won't get to it quickly enough, then go ahead and put it on the current page -- but your default should be to enter it on the NEXT page.)

4. When you come to a new page, take action on all items in Column 2 before looking at the items in Column 1.

5. Use a notebook with more lines per page. (This did seem to make a slight difference when I moved from a 22-line pocket notebook to a 5"x8" 32-line notebook. But only a SLIGHT difference. If you like the convenience and portability of a pocket notebook, I would not recommend switching to a larger notebook simply in order to get more speed. I am planning to switch back to a pocket notebook after I finish up my current 5x8 notebook.)


Here are other ideas I tried to increase cycling speed -- they didn't seem to help. (They DID help reduce "overloading" Column 2, but did not seem to make any difference in cycling speed):

+ Make daily Column 2 tasks into daily habits so you do them as part of your daily routine instead of in SF (e.g., checklist at beginning of day)

+ Use notebook with FEWER lines per page. In my experience this made the cycling time slower, not faster. But the difference was only slight.

+ Block off a few lines of Column 1 on every page (so that Column 2 will have more lines than Column 1). This didn't make any difference to cycling time.

+ De-commission Column 2 tasks back to Column 1, especially if priorities have changed. This helped keep Column 2 "leaner", which did increase cycling time, but it's a bad habit to get into, if you are following the standard SF3 rules. It takes away the whole reason behind Column 2 and eventually Column 2 loses its "kick". (If you *like* that idea, then you can try Mark Forster's latest variation -- http://www.markforster.net/forum/post/1453964 ).


I also tried this idea but it seriously disturbed the "flow" of the list -- it just wasn't "SuperFocus" any more:

+ Act on no more than 1 item in C1 during each visit to the page. This might work in an "emergency" but I would not recommend it as a general habit. It is just far too constricting.


Has anyone tried any other minor rule modifications that preserve the "flow" and overall effectiveness of SuperFocus, but help improve the cycling speed?

I always have a problem envisaging what it is that causes crawlers to crawl because it is so alien to my own way of working the list. And I often wish that I could stand over someone's shoulder so I could tell them what they are doing wrong.

I think it's important to pay particular attention to two of the rules. One is dismissal, which as Seraphim says above is a key element of the system. I can't stress enough how important this is.

The second is "standing out" or "feeling ready to be done". The whole idea is that your mind should act intuitively within the framework of the rules, not consciously. If you find yourself making calculations like "if I do three tasks on this page, I'll reach x in time to meet the deadline", you are not working SF properly.

However just because you are working "intuitively" doesn't mean that you have no control over what you are doing. You can instruct your intuition so that it achieves what you want it to.

In particular you need to instruct your intuition about what speed it should be going at. There is a continuum between CRAWL and RACE, and you should be considerably nearer the RACE end than the CRAWL end. It's actually no more difficult to decide to speed up while doing SF than it is to decide to quicken your pace when you are walking down the street.

This is a general reply as too many people have made the same points to answer them individually:

1) If what you are doing produces the results you want, then it doesn't matter what speed you are moving at.

2) SF is a system for controlling discretionary time. If you don't have any discretionary time, then the system isn't going to work. The solution is to increase your discretionary time. There are many ways of doing this, but the place to start is the number of meetings you have.

3) I've never been a programmer so I don't know whether it's actually the case that taking a break means that you have to start again from scratch. It would be contrary to my experience in every other field of mental activity, where taking a break improves one's thinking. However if it is the case then I suggest that the best way forward is to take programming out of discretionary time and make it a scheduled activity.

4) If you don't follow the dismissal rules, you can forget about making SF work.

On programming -- I used to think "little and often" didn't work for this. But over the last couple of years I found that's just not quite true. Both AF and SF have given me plenty of practice finding ways to mentally bookmark my programming work, so it's not so hard to stop and start up again. And like Mark said, "taking a break improves one's thinking".

Programming can be like an addiction -- you develop some code, you try it out, it works but not quite, you tweak it, it works better but still not exactly what you want, you keep developing, keep tweaking, finally you are happy with the results. This process gives you immediate feedback, but like a gambling addiction, the results are not 100% consistent. So you keep developing, tweaking, testing till you get the results you want, but even when you're "done" it NEVER feels really perfect, you KNOW there are better ways to abstract the code into reusable modules, genericize it better so you can reuse the modules in other projects, etc. And that keeps drawing you back.

This process is perfectly descriptive of addictive behavior.

The best way to train a dog (so I've read) is to give it a reward for the desired behavior about 60% of the time. This is actually far more effective than giving it a reward 100% of the time. Programming is like that. If you wrote some code and it always worked the first time, it would be mechanical and boring, rather than addictive.

I only do some programming occasionally, in Perl or XML development or VB macros -- I'm not a full-time software developer, so maybe it's different for you full-time pros out there. But AF / SF taught me that a really big part of the "programmer's high" when you feel like you are "in the zone" is really a kind of addiction, like I tried to describe above, and not a real working necessity. AF/SF can help you get over that habit and work effectively doing "little and often". I've found my code is actually done much faster and with better quality this way. But then again, I'm not a full-timer so maybe it doesn't really apply...

I think the "time boxing" that doesn't work for you is what I'd call "scheduling." That is, designating a certain arbitrary time of day to do Project X. That is as unappealing to me as it seems to be to you. Its arbitrariness and pre-calculatedness is the exact opposite of SF's "standing out."

What I call "time boxing" is simply that I will set a timer when I start working one of these projects. I have time-boxed in the past, and it always runs great until I start disrespecting the timer. That's when it falls apart. The other problem is that feeling of "what now?" in between time boxes. Before SF, it wasn't always clear how to fit something gainful into those in-between times.

Btw, for any "Pomodoro" fans out there, my only objections to The Pomodoro Method are that the 25-minute box is just not long enough, and I don't want the entire day to be time-boxed. So you can think of this whole idea as modified Pomodoro, injected with performance-enhancing SuperFocus, if you want. Though to me, SF feels like the heart of the system.

I've only dabbled in programming and novel writing, but I've definitely felt the heady, addictive rush that Seraphim describes, and how hard it is to get back into the flow after a break.

A good exercise is to push yourself as far out of "giant teetering house of cards" mode as possible.

Try to keep the house as small and stable as possible. Take a few moments to look at each card before you add it. Does it have to be added to that house right now, or can it sit in a stack on the side (written in a notebook)? Can you turn the house into a tidy, stable stack before going home?

Doing one card (or at least one room) at a time helps me focus. Rather than chasing threads all over the place, leaving half-debugged sections everywhere, I can do one smaller section at a time and actually finish them. Program flow and calculations are easier to read without all the error and exception checking, and can be done with a horrid user interface. I write a bare-bones routine that works, and make a note of what format the data needs to be in.

It's also easier to come back to a project if each room is small and stable. I can write a pretty data-input section and I don't have to remember a thing about the calculations to do it. Then I find the user can't give me the data I assumed, so at least I didn't waste time doing all that error and exception checking.

So I invoked Trick #2 (see #2 in my first post at the top of this thread) -- "give yourself a time limit to get through each page". I allowed 20 minutes per page. I thought this would work pretty well since most of the pages were "mature" and didn't have many tasks left on them.
