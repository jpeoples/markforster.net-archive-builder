In the "It's been a month" thread, Mike wrote an extensive treatise. I've selected salient bits for discussion

"""The problem is ME, not the iPhone or the app. I'll enter one context (like sleep, for example) then the next time I think about it (I realize that I'm watching TV or doing housework, for example) I'll click Eternity and see that I've been sleeping for 37 hours! WTF? It is not so much that I need to know where I spend my time, that is going to be very helpful, of course, but that I am not AWARE.

I'm not willing to give up on Eternity. I have to draw a line somewhere and being AWARE of my context ... really that I am CHANGING contexts ...is so basic to TM that to fail at that simple thing is unacceptable.

I look at it this way: before you can change anything, you have to be aware of where you are, where you want to go, and what steps need to be taken ... then monitor how well you are doing. That is pretty basic change theory, right? All control is a negative feedback loop. Simple operations research stuff. So if I can't get past knowing when I am changing context, how am I EVER going to be able to affect ANY other change? It is like the pilot not being aware he is going off course because he does not think to look at the compass. I don't do that when I fly. I don't do that when I drive. How can I accept that situation and give up on recording the context switches everywhere else in my life? That is the very name of my whole issue with TM in the first place.
"""

My response:


Interesting Mike. Sounds like a job for lifehack.com to solve. It seems you only need to develop one habit, somehow, although I can imagine there's more to the problem. On the hack side:

One thing I tried successfully was to set my watch on repeating countdown timer. Every 30 minutes the watch would beep and I'd think, "why's that beeping?" "To remind me to be aware of context." And then I thought, "What am I doing now?", and recorded that in Dave Seah's Emergent Task Timer.

Other suggestions:
Suppose every time you turned your iPhone on you saw your current context. Maybe tape "what are you doing?" to your phone as a perpetual reminder :-). Or embed this question in your Todo list (make it the title of your list) assuming you have an established Todo list habit. Or suppose a timer was built into ToodleDo so that selecting a task starts a timer and leaving the task to look at other tasks stops a timer.

As a chess player, I've become aware of the difficulty of managing my own focus there. One challenge I've not mastered is to be aware of my opponent's plans. It's too easy to get caught up in thinking I'm going to do this and this and that, completely forgetting what my opponent has some ideas as well.

Point is, it's very hard to think two things at once, and doing such makes the task much more difficult. I'm not sure how well people can master such though I think it's worth the effort. Possibly the only good way is to develop one of the two things into an automatic habit, somehow.

If anyone has good answers, please share.

I wrote on this very topic this morning on a thread initiated by Norman. At the time, I was unaware that Mike was also writing about this.

The first time I faced the issues we are discussing here was when I read the DIT book and tried to implement it. I struggled with the habit of writing something down before I did it.

That meant it was OK to surf the web as a displacement activity for grading papers, as long as I first wrote it down before I did it.

This is hard. It's a burden. But it bring me back to my list again and again. It forces me to confront all the things that I've deemed important that my brain has chosen--in the heat of the moment--to forget.

This, for me, was the problem with most of the AFs. It was just too awkward to shift quickly from one thing to another. With DWM, I can do whatever I want whenever I want to. There is enormous freedom. There is only one caveat: first write it down.

And that brings the mindfulness, or awareness, or whatever you want to call it. That is the habit that I want to cultivate because that is the habit that gives me efficiency, balance, and self-actualization. While the payoff is high, it takes extraordinary discipline.
