I am following DWM as is since it was released. My first reaction was "nothing can ever be better to me than AF4", but of course I did not listen to it --- that had also been my first reaction when I was using DIT and AF1 was announced. DWM has worked great for me, and (or because?) I just use it day in day out. As my notebook is about to finish, I am eager to start fresh with a new one. I think I like mass dismissals as well as minor day dismissals.

However, as I feel a bit sluggish, I tried the "Will I?" trick today (see below). It worked. I intend to keep this frame of mind. :)

The "trick" is briefly described on this article:

http://scienceblogs.com/cortex/2010/07/will_i.php

An excerpt:

"But that's not what happened. It turned out that that the "Will I?" group solved nearly 25 percent more anagrams. When people asked themselves a question - Can I do this? - they became more motivated to actually do it, which allowed them to solve more puzzles. This suggests that the Nike slogan should be "Just do it?" and not "Just do it"."

By the way, I really enjoy Jonah Lehrer's blog. I am a lurker in this forum, by the way, not a ad-bot. :)

Thanks for the link, Natalia. Very interesting article.

Maybe this idea of 'intrinsic motivation' is the reason why so many of us find AF and DWM more effective than traditional systems. There's been quite a lot of discussion on this forum about resistance. Some people have mentioned that they tend to rebel against doing anything they've been told to do. There is much less resistance with AF, because working the list is more like making a series of choices than following a set of orders. So it could be said that the AF method indirectly elicits intrinsic motivation. Mark has always been clear that AF is not a 'To Do' list, but perhaps we should now call it a 'Will I?' list?
