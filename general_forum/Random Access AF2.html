As I mentioned in David Drake's "Any testimonials . . ." thread, I started Random Access AF2 (RAAF2) today.

AF2 is constituted by its rules:
1. Go to the last item on your list (i.e. the most recently entered task)
2. Work backwards from the last item looking at each task in turn until a task “stands out”.
3. Do the task in whole or in part.
4. Re-enter the task if necessary.
5. Repeat steps 1-5


RAAF2's rules are
1. Look through your list however you feel like.
2. Stop at a task that “stands out”.
3. Do the task in whole or in part.
4. Re-enter the task if necessary.
5. Repeat steps 1-5

Note the this does not affect the start-of-day sequence which is sequential, not random.

My reaction thus far is very positive. It is not a huge difference, but it is significant. In AF2, I would do a quick item. Then I would go back to the end of the list and work towards the front. I would do another quick item. At this point, I didn't want to go back to the end again, since I just did that one minute ago. Furthermore, there was another item I wanted to do on my current page. But, dutifully and tediously, I would return to the last page and again work my way to the front.

In RAAF2, this problem is gone. If I want to do something else on the current page I do it. If I want to go to the front and work the block that's on notice for dismissal I do it. If I want to go to the last page and work on urgent recent stuff, I do it.

I am finding that I actually look at the items more carefully now. With AF2, after the 10th time, I was barely looking at the items near the end of the list, because I had been through them so many times already.

Of course, I don't have the kind of quality control standards that Mark has. I have only tested RAAF2 for 7.5 hours. Time will tell if this meme will replicate or become extinct.

I don't think you need to give this method a fancy name like RAAF2. It's a method that's been around for decades, possibly centuries, and is known as a "To Do List". If it works for you, fine. But I think I should point out what the disadvantages relative to AF2 are:

1) Far from being "a problem" the constant movement in AF2 from the back of the list is what makes the system so sensitive to changing circumstances throughout the day.

2) The dismissal process in AF2 sets up a tension which brings the whole list into focus. However this only works if you are moving from the back of the list as per the AF2 instructions.

It's not the dismissal process itself that's weakened, it's the effect the dismissal process has on the rest of the list. If you are working the list according to the rules, the dismissal process sets up a tension between the beginning and the end of the list which ensures that your intuition is able to prioritise the entire list correctly - urgent tasks and less urgent tasks. Random access doesn't have this affect (not for me anyway).
