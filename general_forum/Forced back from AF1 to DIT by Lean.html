Our team recently underwent a "Lean transformation wave", aiming at reducing waste, optimising efficiency, and improving client satisfaction and team morale. One of the changes resulting from this wave is the introduction of a "Daily Whiteboard", where the entire team gathers around a whiteboard for a 15 minute meeting ervery morning.

One of the key components of this whiteboard is a section where every team member is expected to write down the activities he's planning to work on that day. I initially expressed concern at this. As I was using AF1, as a rule I didn't know at the end of each day what exactly I'd be working on the next, even knowing that the system would see to it that the important things got done. So clearly this is a context that seems to be incompatible with any AF-style task management system.

Before adopting AF, I'd been using Do It Tomorrow (DIT) for a few years, almost since the book first came out, and I was very happy with it (although I like AF1 even better). Because of the next day focus of DIT, it seems a natural choice in my current context. So I purchased a new calendar (I use all Mark's systems on paper), and since my return from vacation last Monday, I've officially switched back to DIT.

However, I regularly add a task on my next day list called "AF list", in an attempt not to drop all activities that were being managed via my AF list. When working on this task, I temporarily switch back to modified AF1 mode: when I check off an unfinished item, I either add it back at the end of the AF list, or I write it down in my DIT diary for the next day.

Pending question for me is if I'll maintain an AF list in parallel like this (mainly for the smaller bits), or if this process will gradually clear all activity from the AF list and I'll be back to DIT only in a few days or weeks...

I'd welcome any observations, comments or suggestions on this.

"Write down"? Don't you guys like talking with each other? :-)

My rule of thumb as an Agile and Lean software development coach is that each to-be-done task you bring up at the morning meeting should be something you estimate will take two or more hours to accomplish. Otherwise, the tasks will be too fine-grained, and you'll spend the meeting telling everyone precisely how many pencils you intend to sharpen that day.

So that ends up being no more than four tasks discussed per person--probably more on the order of one or two tasks discussed for most people.

In that kind of situation, I would skim my AF1 list before the meeting and put paperclips next to any more-than-two-hour items I wanted to complete that day. Then I would copy only those items onto the board for purposes of discussion, and just keep rocking my AF1 list from minute to minute through the day.

Dan, of course we talk. That's the point of the whole exercise. We're just required to update our list of activities for the day before the daily meeting where we discuss them. Also, we typically note down 3 to 5 activities, mostly representing one hour of work or more, or work to achieve an important milestone.

Both, making a selection from the AF list and marking these items to me seems to invalidate the whole AF1 approach, as it shortcircuits the mechanism where your intuition helps you choose what to do next. Working like this reduces the whole AF list to a kind of master to do list, which is something Mark warns against if I remember correctly.

Therefore, I feel DIT is more compatible, as there you build your list of activities for the next day during the current day. But the risk for me is that this predetermined list will induce resistance and generate procrastination, which is something AF1 largely eliminated. On the other hand, as you publicly announce what you intend to achieve, this is a strong incentive to actually do it to avoid having to admit defeat during tomorrow's whiteboard meeting...

Finally, it occurred to me that I could handle the AF1 list as my DIT Current Initiative, meaning starting each day with a lapse of time working in AF1 fashion on the list.

But it's still early days (second week of back to DIT), so i'll see how things evolve.

Try asking yourself "If the day is average (or just a bit better), which tasks do I expect I'll do?" when you review your list before the meeting.

Are you allowed to add things to the list as the day goes on, like two hours solving an urgent problem?

What about the occasional "Clear 10 small items in 2 hours."

Think about the goal of the whiteboard and the new system as a whole. Chances are the boss is implementing a system he doesn't yet fully understand. It's easy to focus on the system rather than results at that stage. Is it to embarrass people into finishing things (which will backfire), or help people select priorities and stay focused -- something you already do using DIT and AF.

Did a book come with the new system? It might be worth reading. You will probably pull different ideas from it than your boss did. Think about how your current system addresses the same things, and if the new system addresses anything your current system lacks. I suspect the meetings will do things for the group that each member doing DIT in isolation can't do.

I suspect the two systems will be complementary.
