I haven't mastered the subject but I have found the timed interval trick helps. If I'm of a mind to do something, but also intimidated by that thing, I tried using a timer. Work 5 minutes. (Anybody can do 5 minutes, it gets you started.) Break 5 minutes. (Clean up stuff maybe, or find some tunes.) Work 10 minutes. Break 5. Work 15, break 5, work 20, break 5, work 25, break 5, work 25, break 5, work 25, break 5. (25 is a plenty long interval without break.), work 15 (getting weary), break 5, work 10 break 5, work 5, stop.

Result is more than 2.5 hours of hard work, effectively the same as 3 or 4 hours of non-stop work because you slow down when you don't stop, and this is work I really was reluctant to start.

This may not apply to you but my spirit of intentionality is a bit different. I keep the scut corralled and get it off my plate only partially for the reason you mentioned....looking forward to better work and pleasures ahead. But my strongest motive is because I want my mind with as few negative distractions (the nagging) as possible to create a better mental environment for creative flow.

Secondly, I advocate for flexibility blended into my work day for obvious reasons. I also am flexible with the duration of timed bursts based on the anxiety or physical pain levels to set the timer. If the anxiety or pain I'm experienceing is high, I'm OK with setting it to 5 minutes. If I'm taking a mid day break, I set it for an hour. The timer method can both launch work and restrict it. It also restricts break times.
YVMV
