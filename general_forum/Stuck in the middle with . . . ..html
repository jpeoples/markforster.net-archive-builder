It's almost exactly a year since I downloaded what became AF1. Into my seventh decade, I have tried so many different personal productivity approaches/techniques/whatever, from MBO to Seven Star, from Outlook to GTD, and beyond.

By and large I KNOW that AF, and AF4 in particular, is the best or better solution for me (I'm self-employed and sometimes operate from feast to famine). This forum has been great as I have used to wonder if I was the only one with a severe procrastination issue - now I know I'm not alone!

Longer serving members of this forum may recall a few of my earlier posts in which I described how I have adapted AF to my work and lifestyle, which is working abroad in "Strange Places, (with) Questionable People" (to paraphrase the BBC's John Simpson in one of his books) on short-term assignments in the water sector (in a non-engineering capacity).

The quest for the ultimate solution for ME is possibly one reason for my procrastination in many areas. I have found myself over the year oscillating between paper and digital solutions, and have not yet settled on one approach for a reasonable length of time.

Have any other members found a modus operandi that combines the paper and the computer/PDA approach? I have grown attached to my Moleskine notebook and gel pen, yet I've looked at loose-leaf solutions as well. All the while, I use Outlook and have toyed with other digital solutions, especially as I also use a Windows Mobile PDA.

In my latest incarnation, I have set up AF4 in ListPro (tried MLO, GTD, Michael Linenburger;s approach, I think I abhor the overhead of some digital solutions). With ListPro on both my notebook and HTC Windows Phone, I am able to shift items around from Current to Backlog and capture in Deferred (Context). The last one is because I acknowledge that some tasks cannot be done in my current context, eg when away on assignment, I cannot do certain Home and Home Office tasks.

By using the following labels, I have found I can group tasks in a logical alpha-sequence thus:
BACKLOG/CLOSED
CURRENT/OPEN
DEFERRED (CONTEXT)
LTBW (let the bastard wait)

Right now, I think I'll use my Moleskine notebook on right-hand pages to jot down new items as I think of them, then transfer to ListPro later in the day (and cross them off in the notebook); the left-hand pages remain to to be used for notes in meetings, telephone calls, doodling.

I use Outlook to set up reminders and to schedule hard-edge tasks, meetings etc.

Any of this sound familiar, or daunting?

I picked up a tip in the early days of the forum, or maybe it was in one of Mark's books, that helps me when I HAVE to make progress on an unattractive task:

I have a kitchen timer in my home office (on the shelf above my standing height desk, almost eye-level), the timer is a digital one available from Maplin in the UK.

(1) Set it for 5 minutes and work focused on the ONE task
(2) When the alarm goes, reset it for 10 minutes and continue
(3) Again, reset either by doubling the previous increment or just adding 5 minutes to the previous spell
(4) And so on until you've made meaningful progress.

After five cycles, one has done a minimum of 75 minutes (5 + 10 +15 + 20 + 25) or a maximum of 155 minutes (5 + 10 + 20 + 40 + 80). I find this helps one get "into" the task, make meaningful progress if not completion, increases satisfaction, decreases damage to self-esteem etc.

I think it's called a Selami Technique, but the slices get progressively thicker.

It works, now to put my money where my mouth/keyboard is . . . . . . and get an obstacle out of the way before a hard-edge commitment later today. I have allowed Christmas and our two granddaughters to be an excuse not to settle into some tasks, although either reason is deemed acceptable 9especially the latter!).

AF4 is working so well for me that I'd encourage anyone 'stuck in the middle' to first watch the animated version (found in "AF4 News" under Most Recent Postings) then try following it precisely for one week, on paper not electronically. Make yourself follow it without fail for one full week - no cheating, experimenting, or shortcutting. Try to move as quickly through the lists as Mark did using the little-and-often principle.

Use Outlook or something similar (something SIMPLE and QUICK) to record reminders. But stick with paper as much as possible. For different contexts use pages in the back of whatever notebook you use.

Write everything down. Don't let yourself stray into doing some task that you haven't written down first; tasks such as taking a quick look at some website or your email. Write those distracting items down when they occur to you and don't succumb to the temptation to do them until you reach them on your AF4 list. Be disciplined and hard on yourself for one week - be disappointed when you stray, then get back to AF4 until your week is up.

Watch the animation another time or two during the week and emulate it. Know the rules so well that you could teach AF4 to someone else.

Do it.
