I started with AF in February and it worked out remarkably well. What appealed to me was the simplicity of writing my tasks in a notebook. Nothing more.

Now, I have scanned the forum and found many people having problems with dismissing, getting through their active pages and handling urgent tasks. Interestingly, these are exactly the things I found AF handling exceptionally well. Let me explain why:

Firstly, I spend most of my time on the last page. This is the work area, this is what I am doing right now: the automatically established focus. Reentering tasks, adding follow-up and new tasks, I often stay on the last page for a whole day and more. Peace of mind is retained, because I know I have everything else noted down on earlier pages.

Every once in a while I loose steam. That is when I return to the first active page. Going through the active pages is a realively quick process. It is also more of a review than a work process. »Standing out« for me means looking for tasks that are urgent or important or somehow ›fell‹ from the last past. Then I do some work to get them going (again) and move them to the last page.

At this stage I also dismiss pages. This is very important for me. Without dismissing I feel I would procrastinate, do unimportant stuff only to ›save‹ a page. Only when I dismiss I can focus on the important things. So I dismiss everything unless (a) it is more important than what I am doing right now (i.e. is on the last page) or (b) I have promised someone to do it.

Some of these things are urgent. Fine. So I start it, it moves to the last page and this allows me to focus on it as long as necessary. Urgent stuff had alway been somewhat of a problem for me, because I would loose sight of the more important things. This is where AF helped: sure, I might loose focus on something I worked before, but I know I will pick it up again once I move though my active pages.

David,

usually, I start with the last page. This weekend, however, I did not use my list. So, tomorrow starting with the first page seems like the right thing to do. But most of the time I do not get stuck in the middle of my book. As I said, going through my active pages does not take very long. For me, it's a change of pace. Let's say I come upon a book I have to read. Now I don't sit down and read for two hours. (I might do that once it is on the last page.) Rather, I jot down some questions, read the table of contents, skim through the introduction, think about some more questions, etc. Then I move the task to the last page and proceed (most likely to the next page).

Christine,

at first the AF approach seemed very unstructured to me (but I confess, that also somewhat appealed to me). I think the reason AF works so well for me is that using more complicated systems, I never had the feeling of being in control, there always was another list to review or a reminder file to check.

I forgot to mention an important modification I made: I use the last (full) page and the "very last" (half-filled) page as a unit: that is my work area. Every time the very last page is filled, the focus shifts and tightens a little.

Altogether, AF allows me to be as unorganised as possible, while still providing indispensable structure:

- the ability to focus on my work as long as I want (the last page);
- the ability to shift focus (radically) and still return to the previous focus (it remains in the active pages): this works for urgent tasks as well as when I am bored or tired;
- the ability to add new tasks as they come in without the need (but the possibility) of working on them right away;
- the ability to retain tasks on my active pages without the need to commit to them instantly;
- the ability to dismiss anything I do not get around to without loosing (it remains highlighted)
