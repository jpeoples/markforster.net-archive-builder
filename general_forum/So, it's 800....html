To your query, I would have to ask: who's in charge of your time and energy? You or your AF list?

The AF list is a tool, and a very useful tool, but a tool that goes into the drawer when I'm done with it or don't need it.

But yes, I do have "read a chapter", "read a comic," "watch a Dr Who on Netflix", stuff like that (we'll take it as a given that these are relaxing activities :)

Doesn't matter if it's 4 pages away or not. If I'm tired and ready to relax, I'll do it. I know there are lots of tasks left to do on my lists, but I have to call it a night sometime!

Sorry if I misinterpreted your post. It appears the AF method has been working for you, so that's great. I would recast your problem as one that writers have: do they still keep writing when they're feeling hot, or do they do as Hemingway advised and stop in the middle of a sentence, so you can pick up where you left off the next morning? The latter method -- finishing the work day before your tank is empty -- leaves you primed for the next day. (Some of the productivity mavens call this "parking on the downhill side.")

Sounds like you're leaving yourself enough energy and interest to start off your next AF session with gusto. So I wouldn't worry about it. If there's a task that's *really* important, it'll make itself known.

I love seeing how AF automatically gravitates towards what's most important, and helps me overcome artificially/psychologically induced resistance and stress (as opposed to stress because you REALLY have to get something done right now!).

For example, I was really feeling nervous about a certain deadline-driven task today -- really feeling some pressure -- but it was a kind of "external" pressure -- not the "standing out" kind of feeling. So I just said to myself, let's skip that item, you can't concentrate on it because you're so stressed about it, and just go do something more fun. And so I did. And then I realized that the stress I was feeling was really just nervous pressure, and had no real basis. Nobody needs me to do that task right now. I still have plenty of time before the deadline. So, I was very glad I just followed the AF system. It made me realize how much these feelings of stress can be purely psychological.

My whole point is that AF helps you to sort out all those those nagging thoughts, pestering "I really should be..." thoughts, etc. The psychology of this method is really quite fascinating.

So, ... back to your point about doing something you just *want* to do. Personally, I'd throw the book on my AF list, and let the list help me figure out why the question makes me feel so stressed out. Maybe there is something else your unconscious mind is prompting you to do instead, something else that's really more important. And then, when the book "stands out", I'd spend some time reading it!

Not that this is the only answer! It's just what I would do myself, and I'm sure you'll get lots of good suggestions! :-)
