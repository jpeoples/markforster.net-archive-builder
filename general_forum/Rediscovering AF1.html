I noticed that in Mark's blog posts about his upcoming TM method, David Drake and Brian apparently went back to AF1 and was having a good time using it. I actually had never tried using AF1 on paper but only electronically (which sucked). So, being curious, after realizing that my own TM method was a failure:

http://www.markforster.net/forum/post/1090159

I tried AF1 using paper and pen, but with the provision that it has to be compact, i.e. I can put my notebook in my pant's back pocket. And that means one thing: I had to use a memo-sized notebook (or as others would call it a tickler notebook).

Now I realized that when you open a spiral-bound-at-the-top memo notebook, you can treat two facing pages of it like they are just one page because of how the notebook is constructed. Therefore, using the 5"x3" memo notebook I got from Staples as an example, its 15-line-a-page capacity becomes virtually 30 lines a page. Using this idea, I started doing AF1. Here is a sample of a "virtual" page (and my atrocious handwriting):

http://i994.photobucket.com/albums/af63/nuntym/IMG00023-20100510-1220.jpg

And what do you know: it works! For the past four or five days I have used my notebook almost continually, especially since it is so compact. And it is much more motivating for me than DWM or AF4, since I am focusing on one constantly sized virtual page at a time.

Thank you, David Drake and Brian, for reintroducing AF1 to me! And thank you very much, Mark, for making all these TM methods for us!

2010-06-5 13:33
Wooba:

<< When it feels like the list is slowing down (which usually happens for me when it gets to around 10 pages -- which is nowhere near 300 active tasks) I aim to dismiss more ruthlessly than I normally would. >>

1) My own opinion is that it's not the number of pages which is the critical factor, it's the willingness of the person using the system to work "little and often". See http://www.markforster.net/forum/post/642846

2) There is a difference between "weeding" and "dismissing":

Weeding is the deletion at any stage of tasks which are no longer felt to be relevant. Tasks are crossed out and forgotten about. Weeding is the result of a deliberate decision.

Dismissal takes place as the result of the rule that if at least one task is not done when a page is visited (except the last page), then all the remaining tasks on that page are dismissed.

From the above definitions you will see that you can't aim to dismiss more ruthlessly (unless you weren't sticking to the rules in the first place). Dismissal should happen automatically. What you can do though is to aim to weed the list more ruthlessly.
