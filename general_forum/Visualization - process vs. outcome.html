Hi Zane,

I think one of the things about BFL or any of that type of short term program is that it gets you enthused to see just how much you can accomplish in only 12 weeks. If you break it down that's ONLY 40 measly hours of working out - and what's that? What kind of loser can't do that? (I'm talking myself into it again here ...) :-)

You have pictures of people who start out kind of pudgy and out of shape and look awesome after to model yourself after; and you have a very reasonable diet where you're never hungry and get to indulge once in awhile. And once you've been working out for just 2-3 weeks, you start feeling how strong you're getting and that's motivating as well.

Somewhere I've got Bill Phillips' original magazine editorial where he first started promoting the BFL contest to give away his Ferrari. I'll dig it up and post some tips, because I do remember it as being phenomenally motivating.

I didn't sign up and send stuff in etc., but I did keep with the program for a couple of years afterwards (switched jobs, worked crazy hours and let my weightlifting go) - I do miss having that muscle definition though.

Smileypete, Yes - I noticed the same comment too and thought of an olympic bobsledder I heard speak once and he had said the same thing - they dream of the medal, but they truly visualize on the process more.

Zane,

I was thinking about this further, and I think a key component of enthusiasm is the level of belief and certainty you have about the outcome. In something like BFL, you KNOW if you complete those workouts and the way of eating as prescribed, you WILL achieve those results. It's virtually impossible not to, unless you're an extreme endomorph (in which case you'll have to adapt the program, but will still have decent results). It's like following a recipe out of a cookbook - follow the steps and the result has to occur.

Here's a good article on it:
http://www.rockyourday.com/the-wrong-way-to-think-about-your-goals-and-how-to-fix-it/
