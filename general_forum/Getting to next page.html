One thing to keep in mind about AF is that you only have to do one thing on a page before moving on to the next one. And you only have to do that one thing for as long as you feel like, which can be a few minutes.

So, if you feel you've been stuck on page one for too long, this is what you can do the next time you pull out your AF notebook:

1. Choose an item on page one.

2. Work on that item for as long as you want. (Could be 5 or 10 minutes.)

3. Cross it out (and add it to the end of your notebook if it isn't finished).

4. Move on to page two.

And, to keep from getting stuck on another page, you can set a limit on how many items you'll work on on each page before moving on.

You should also examine why you got stuck on page one. Was it really because items on that page kept standing out to you? Or was it because you felt you *should* do them?

Hi Sqril

You are right regarding wording of tasks and that wording will inevitably vary from person to person. Sometimes with a task like reading, just putting it on the list once will be enough to give it the kickstart it needs - sometimes it will need to be added again but the correct balance will become clear as you get used to the system.

As far as projects are concerned, they are a discussion area in themselves. As Mark said in another post, a book on time management will be very different to a book on project management, and he differentiated between managing the project and managing yourself in the project. AF handles the latter; for the former you need some form of project management system which, at least for larger projects, would be outside of AF.

As a general rule, I define a project as anything out of the normal routine that consists of more than a few odd tasks. As a result it will normally come accompanied by "baggage" in the form of files (digital or paper), other paperwork, research, contacts, physical items etc etc. Consequently those "things" need some sort of system to control them, whether that be a computer folder, a filing cabinet, a box or any combination of the above. I personally manage my projects within Microsoft OneNote as that looks like a notebook (or more like a Filofax style organizer) but can include all manner of digital files as well as text, and I also use it to reference and keep track of any physical elements of a project. The structure of any of my projects is actually quite simple - a summary of the project, an index, the relevant files and/or links as appropriate and finally a list of tasks. It is that task list that then impacts upon AF.

Some projects may require blocks of time, for example a research project (Family Tree) or a writing project (Book/Thesis etc). Others may be better suited to ad hoc tasks or groups of tasks, for example planning a business trip, or producing a set of accounts. With either type, I will have a task list within my project management system but will action that list within AF, either by means of a time block (actioned when it stands out) or by adding individual tasks into the system.

I hope that explains things more clearly. As I said it is a subject in itself and one I could expand on at great length :-) , but hopefully that gives an indication as to how project management can be integrated quite easily into AF.
