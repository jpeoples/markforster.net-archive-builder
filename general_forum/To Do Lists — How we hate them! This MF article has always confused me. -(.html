Here's the article:
http://www.markforster.net/to-do-lists

Here's a part of it:
"Have a separate place to record new ideas. When you think of something new to do, don’t write it straight onto your to-do list. Items should only go on your to-do list after they have been subjected to rigorous assessment as mentioned above. On the other hand it’s important not to lose new ideas, so keep a separate list of “possible” actions."

This sounds like Mike's grass catcher concept?

This also doesn't make sense to me - the positives make sense, the negatives don't:

"The advantages of a to-do list are obvious: everything is prioritised; you know exactly what to do next; things which are urgent and/or important get done first; items don’t get lost; and you have somewhere to write new things as they arise. What’s more it’s ideally suited for computerisation. What could be better?

There is only one disadvantage of a to-do list. It doesn’t work!

There are two main reasons why it doesn’t work. The first is that you never get more than a third of the way down the list. The second is that for every item that you cross off the list, you think of another three items to go on it."

This has always bugged me because this isn't how I (or most people I know) use a daily to-do list. Actually I don't know anyone who uses a daily to-do list like this. I just write down what I plan on doing for the day or for a segment of time. If I don't get more than 1/3 of it done, it's because some major disruption happened. I also add very, very little to it - if anything, just keep some time open to deal with today-type interruptions.

Maybe someone - Mark? - can explain this to me?

"Don’t rewrite your list every day. Keep the same list going. That way you will become acutely conscious of how long items have been on it."

I don't know about this - Penelope Trunk says:
"I rewrite the list every day by hand. Because if something on the list is not worth taking the time to rewrite by hand, it’s not worth taking the time to do."

I personally like to do lists. They solve my biggest issue which is forgetting to do something. So knowing I have a complete capture is a benefit to me. What I see in Mark's post sounds a lot like how I observed a coworker operating with the Franklin-Covey page-a-day organizing system. Each day he'd rewrite tasks from the previous day, mark them ABCD priority, and work on a third of what was on the list. Of course intelligent people can deal with a flawed system, and correct their behavior to make it work.

Penelope and Mark agree on the value of dropping stuff. Penelope uses 'laziness' as a cue to get things dropped. Mark uses 'ignored too long' as a cue to either drop or action stuff. Seems to me the latter has more power.

I think there are three categories of tasks that often get confused. 1. There are tasks that must be done on a specific day. 2. There are tasks that must be done, but not necessarily today. 3. And there are those we would like to do. Maybe. :-) Mixing these together on one list can be confusing, at least for most folks.

Category 1's are what David Allen said were the only tasks to go on a calendar/daily diary. The biggest problem seems to be in category 2. Email tasks are in that category for me. I used to leave all my email-related tasks in separate folders (or worse my inbox) and wait for them to stand out. I lived in fear that I would miss deadlines on some of them and occasionally did. As I've written before, I am absolutely thrilled that Goodtodo has managed my email tasks by allowing me to send them to a specific date (i.e., rather than have a waiting on category, I send a receipt for a purchase to a date when I expect to have received it by and only see that task/email on that day). I've had an empty inbox every day since using it and have even gotten to many fun email tasks (watching videos, reading links) that used to be trashed when my inbox was jammed.

So how should one deal with the other category 2 tasks? Organize them by context a la GTD? AF them? DIT them? Prioritize them? Develop routines for them (as for housekeeping)? My personal experience is that any of these methods can be helpful save for mixing them up too much with category 1's or category 3's. Doing so is what makes me feel overwhelmed and takes my eye off category 1's. I often take a category 2 task and convert it to a category 1. For example, I have a speech due on Saturday. What *must* I do today to be fully prepared? That's a category 1 that is on today's Goodtodo.

I have continued to do all categories of tasks as they stand out without using a list. But I also add category 1's and 2's to Goodtodo. The way I avoid overwhelm is by reviewing the day's list and redating or deleting tasks that aren't going to get done today. Thus I convert every day's list to a will-do list that requires no prioritization--very much in line with Mark's philosophy. I also have a DIT kind of focus. If I get an email today that needs a response, I send it to tomorrow's Goodtodo list. When tomorrow is here and I can respond to it, great. If I know I won't, I redate it to a time I most likely can. My goal is to do at least one thing tomorrow on projects that come in today. If I can't, I don't sweat it, but at least I have a guideline for working.

Alison, the only way it works in a busy context is if it's blatently always in front of you so you can't forget. A yellow notebook sitting in your view space will get you to look at it regularly. If not, put stronger reminders in place until you become regular. A flashing neon sign.

Mel, I'm with you on separating the will-dos from the maybes. But when do you get to the "like to do maybe" list? For a time I have been separating my stuff into "working on it" and "not working on it". The "working on it" stuff is my AF4 list. The "not working on it" stuff I will want to work on someday, like when I get some current projects finished. Every new thing goes there. I'll dismiss them if I never want to work on it, or I'll activate them by doing some work. Each time I scan the AF4 open list I also scan the not-working-on-it stuff to see if I want to start working on anything.
