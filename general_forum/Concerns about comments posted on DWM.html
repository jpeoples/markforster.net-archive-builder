This is my first post so in that sense I’m a lurker (see other posts for definition) but I have kept a close eye on all the AF developments and the subsequent discussions. In fact I’m a keen user of all systems developed by Mark Forster.

My reason for this post is to ask all lurkers and regular posts to take a step back and have a reality check.

Since the launch of DWM some of the comments and the tone of those comments are giving me some concern, Is everyone remembering what we have here? Essentially we have 1 – 2 – 1 (free) coaching with to me, one of the best if not the best time management gurus around.

Mark is definitely up there with David Allen, Stephen Covey and David Taylor and in my opinion he is beyond them because

a) he is not stuck in one system or rigid in his beliefs
b) he accepts he is not right all the time
c) he is not trying to sell me an organiser, a pen or bizarrely on one web site essential oils!

He has given us one of the simplest T.M. Systems with AF, free of charge and all you need is a cheap notepad and pen – you can add all you like in terms of moleskine books, computer programs etc but all you really need is a cheap notepad and pen.

He has asked that people test it so AF became AF2, AF3 and AF4. All free of charge, all open to constructive criticism and always being developed.

I came to Mark’s website after reading all 3 of his books. DIT is commonly mentioned in posts but I find it does not work as a system for me although I take from it 3 things

a) building in a buffer so as not to interfere with what I am doing today
b) using checklists where appropriate (I want to be Mick Cool and not Joe Slobb!) and
c) the current initiative principle

In my opinion the better system is detailed in Mark’s first book ‘Get Everything Done’ with the principle of regular, focussed, sufficient attention and I see more of this principle in all versions of the AF system then I do DIT.

In fact AF is to me GED’s regular, focused, sufficient attention developed to make sure you capture everything. I think that’s why David Allen’s GTD is often quoted in AF posts.

(His second book does not relate to time management and is not really relevant here but it’s a fantastic revolutionary way of looking at your life but its not for this post.)

I have been using AF1 since it started, failed very quickly with AF2 and AF3, settled for a while in AF4 and have just recently went back to a variation of AF1. What suits me for a typical day best is

a) 30 minutes on current initiative from DIT (if required)
b) 30 minutes on routine tasks in the office
c) AF1 (or 4) with no more than 15 minutes on any activity before moving onto next item (from GED).

I mention this to demonstrate that I use a bit of every system Mark has developed. I am grateful for all of the work he has put into developing those systems, ongoing changes and continually trying to create a better system.

I am however concerned about some of the language and tone of comments since the launch of DIT2/AF2 (now DWM) will make Mark stop continually trying out new ideas and systems on us. Let’s be honest Mark must enjoy doing this as he’s not making any money out of it or us, but how long will it last?

A quick scan of the comments comes up with “this whole thing has become a joke”, “not impressed Mark”, “completely bewildered” and “sheer madness”. Mark responds to those comments gracefully but again for how much longer?

So that’s my input (back to lurking again!) On reading the posts since the launch of the new system (which again costs next to nothing – an A4 diary!) I think the emotions have been running a bit high but we should all take stock of what we have here – the most down to earth, non dogmatic TM expert asking us to try out a system he has only been pre testing himself for 3 days and giving us back virtually real time feedback and advice. Can anyone think of another T.M. expert that would put himself out there like that?

Regards

Hi all - I agree with Skeg. It brings to mind two analogies.

First, road-rage: a driver sitting cocooned in a car, who thinks another driver has done something wrong, may interact with that driver in a very different way than if he/she were standing face-to-face with them. In a similar way, some people posting on a forum say things they would never dream of saying if they were face-to-face with someone having a conversation - being "invisible" can lure people away from the niceties which make up good manners.

Second, imagine that you are keen to learn to play tennis, and a top-notch tennis coach has offered to teach you every single day for 1 month, free of charge, with all the back-up you need. On your first session the coach explains the basic techniques you need to learn to become a good player and asks you to practice them every day. For a couple of days you do exactly as he says, but then on the third day you start thinking maybe his techniques could be improved, or are plain wrong, and you decide you'll make a few tweaks here and there, maybe use a badminton racquet instead to see if that's any better, maybe change the scoring system so that you only need a score of 30 to win a game. How do you think that's going to work out for you in your bid to become a good tennis player?

So how about getting behind Mark in this enterprise: how about testing the system as it stands, taking into account any amendments he himself suggests, for a reasonable period of time, so that the feedback he gets is useful. It is quite likely that a (mainly silent) majority of people on the forum are already doing just that, and reserving judgement until they've worked DWM for long enough to see its advantages or disadvantages. It goes against some people's nature to blindly follow instructions from another person without continually questioning the method every step of the way, and no doubt there will be some useful ideas that Mark can take from their comments, as well as from the comments of those who are testing the system as Mark launched it.
