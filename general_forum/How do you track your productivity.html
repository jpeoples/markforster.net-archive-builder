I used to work at the US Department of Labor in Seattle, mainly on projects to encourage a culture shift in state and local agencies towards quality, customer satisfaction and continuous learning.

I was the resident troublemaker and oddball thinker in an office full of very talented but fairly linear thinkers.

And some of them would wonder to my teammates if I actually did anything. Because most of the time it didn't look like I was sitting at my desk focusing.

And I'd wonder about it as well.

One day when I was feeling particularly guilty, and suspecting that I was wasting the taxpayers' money, I asked myself a very different question:

"If I hadn't been here the past six month, what would be different? What wouldn't be here"

It was long enough ago that I don't remember the details of what difference I'd made. But as soon as I saw it in that light, I started to think that I needed a raise. The value I added was quite dramatic, and when I look at my experience there I can say happily that Uncle Sam got a deal.

So these are my questions when I'm wondering if I'm being effective, productive or just slacking:

1. In the last six (three, twelve, two) months (days, weeks, years) where have I come and what has changed?
2. If I hadn't been here, what would be different and what wouldn't have happened.

The trick, of course, is learning to self-regulate how you work and live. Do you focus on what's meaningful? Are you happy and enjoying life? And do you have patterns and structures and rhythms that support you in being able to get stuff done easily. If you can answer a resounding 'Yes!' to all three, you're good.

And it's always a great exploration to elevate all three of these to the next level.

Great question you posted TK, really got me thinking, thank you!!!

performance metrics for myself (and probably for other knowledge workers reading this) really don't make much sense But I do keep track of three metrics that calculate automatically from my DWM/AF spreadsheet. I like to look at these three on a daily basis.

#1 % complete-- Tasks on list complete/total list.
#2 net gain/lost-- Number of tasks added and not completed today-- if it is a negative number I am closing out more tasks than I am adding.
#3 "Real tasks" done. The number of really significant tasks (usually work projects) that I completed and did not reenter.

Again, these are really silly numbers in the grand scheme of things. But I do think playing against yesterday's score on each three metrics help to motivate me to stay on list.

From the bat, I must say that I don't believe that there are "metrics" easily applicable but there are ways to ensure good effectiveness in what you do.

First, I think that effectiveness is directly related to value. If you gain a lot of value from one activity compared to another, then it is more effective for you. The problem is, the type of value is quite different from task to task and from person to person. But there is 2 built in processes in my system that assure that I do the things that have the most value.

One of those is that I limit. I simply limit the amount that I'm willing to do in a day. Let me give you an example and explain the concept. If I give you 10 different cakes (and let's assume you like cake) as choices for desert but you can only choose one. You will, without fail, take the one that will give you the most enjoyment.

On top of that, that value is compared with all the other things you didn't eat. So the idea that value is also derived from the comparison with another thing comes into play. Therefore I always have a couple of choices in my system to make me choose, hence derive value for each thing and pick the one that has the most and I can only do that. All of Mark's systems have this stamp too!

If you doubt the power of limiting yourself, try Twitter. It's a wonderful tool to learn to write concisely and with value. You only have 140 characters to send your thoughts or a message. So I usually start by typing what I want to say and then cut, rephrase, restructure and change the grammar of the text to fit snuggly in those limits.

Ex:
Everybody should go watch the A-Team movie; it's a "must see"!!!
it was so hilarious... I really laughed when the guy tries to jump-start the ambulance with the defibrillator!

Would become something like:
I found "The A-Team" movie hilarious; it's a Must-See...
Watch for the guy trying to jump-start an ambulance with a defibrillator!!!


By the way, my backlog too has a limit so if I want to put something in, I have to do something or throw something out! And if I only have 30 minutes for my emails this day, I will find the most effective way to do them quite fast.

Then, I have most activities divided into categories where I can not only know what type of mindset and energy I'm going to use but also which value I'm shooting for in those activities:
Rest/Relax: Did I spend time really focussing on the now.
Maintenance: Is all I committed to in the "important, not urgent" quadrant.
Projects/Learning: Have I got a real boost in creativity and fire to move forward.
Physical: Did I get a good workout for my condition.
Fun/Entertainment: Did I totally escaped reality for a while.

Since the categories are defined by values, they are their own determinators of Effectiveness. And since I always leave time to learn in my day, I often look at what I do and how to do it better.

But for this to work, you need to master having stuff on your list and not commit to it. This way, those things don't bug you and you commit only to the most valued things.

As for a "metric" for the system itself: since I manage my energies during the day, if I find that in this particular time of year, I do more creative things then budgeted, then I simply re-budget :)

Erik,

Thanks for the rant! :-) This stuff is really hard to measure. Peter Drucker, who coined the term "knowledge worker", wrote many books on the topic of measuring productivity when there aren't any widgets to count.

For example, see Drucker's book "Management in the 21st Century", or just google "drucker knowledge worker".

Drucker was the first to frame the question, but he didn't get very far in defining real metrics. But I haven't seen anyone else get much farther than he did.


Lately, I've been trying to get myself to think in terms of "ROI" -- Return On Investment.

The Investment is the effort invested, where "effort" is roughly equivalent to "blood, sweat, and tears".

The Return is the positive impact -- am I getting the results I want? It can also be a measure of leverage -- how much "blood, sweat, and tears" am I reducing for other people, or for myself later on.

I am finding it's easiest to figure out a metric when I am dealing with an ongoing activity, rather than a one-off task or project. For example, I spend some time on "get out the vote" efforts, distributing candidate literature, etc.; and helping coordinate other volunteers. I struggle with how to measure our effectiveness, and to decide where we should focus our effort.

Then a friend recommended a book, Lawrence Grey's "How to Win a Local Election", which had the key insight that most activities can be translated directly into votes. For example, a personal contact (door-to-door precinct work) generally translates into 1.5 votes -- you influence the person you contacted, plus some of their relatives and friends, minus a percentage of people who don't like your message. Putting up a sign for a candidate might generate 2 votes. But it can take 10 phone calls to get 1 vote.

This means that my team and I are really a "vote factory". Favorable votes are our widgets. There are other important things we must also do -- like decide which candidates we want to support! -- but the purpose of all the precinct work, signs, and meetings is to generate those votes. Otherwise it's just busywork.

This example has helped me think of ways to measure other things that I do on a regular basis -- any kind of maintenance activity or ongoing project. The trick seems to be to define the desired outcome in measurable units.

The "SMART" goal method is similar on the surface -- "make sure your goals are Measurable". But for me, this is a little different. This process is more iterative, more agile, and less top-down than SMART.

Wow some great ideas are emerging! I agree with Erik that each kind of activity has a different kind of measure.

"Worked on a task 2 hours" is not a measure of productivity, though "Worked this week for 30 minutes" might be a clue why something isn't progressing. A Good Day? It's virtually impossible to classify each day as good or bad. Some days are obvious, but... One day is too short to measure productivity. Sometimes you do much work but complete nothing. You can compare one week to another, or one month to another. It's not so much the quantity of things done, but rather a subjective impression of whether one week was better or worse than another. Look at the collection of completed work, and the impact of that work, and decide whether it's good.

Task-Specific Success Measures. For creative work, you could track the amount of time on a project, and grade the work ABCDF. Or count money earned or people's responses.
For routine work, just measure how long it takes. One week you clean the house top to bottom and it takes 4 hours. Next week you clean bottom to top and it takes only 2 hours.

I have to provide a weekly report to my boss. It usually includes issues I've struggled with (and therefore need assistance or top cover) as well as accomplishments.

As I start with the previous report and delete completed items, I am starting with a list of outstanding tasks or problems which sets the stage for the next week. Any changes get made in blue as the week goes on, so it show progress on items as well as new items either completed or identified. (Identifying an issue is progress as well for me.) The more blue, the better. It is completely separate from my DWM list, but helps to keep me focused on items that are important.

So this report that seemed like a waste of time when I first started doing it, can be a motivator. If I've been good about updating it daily and it doesn't show much progress, it motivates me to work harder and produce real progress during the remainder of the week. When little is accomplished for the whole week, it serves as motivation for the following week.

While it doesn't track productivity from a statistical standpoint, it does clearly show progress (or lack thereof when things hang around in the report without it). I'm forced to really look at whether an item hanging around is something that will get done or not and goes beyond the little and often that lets it remain on my DWM list. I am overloaded, so there are always choices about what isn't going to get done. Sometimes no good choices remain and my boss sees that about the same time I do. That allows for him to provide assistance for those things he wants done because he can see what won't get done without it.
