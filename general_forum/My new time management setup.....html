Hi Folks,

After working for so long with all of the different time management systems -- GTD, DIT, AF, DWM, and then back to AF1....I am going to try something a little different. One of the things I have had problems with is having one long list of everything at work together -- no matter what the system. Standard contexts in GTD did not work that well for me. So now I am going to try something Mark posted some time ago, but with a new wrinkle.

I wear so many different hats at work. I have significant administrative responsibilities and all of the endless busy, but important work associated with being a chair. Then I am a researcher, with an NIH-funded laboratory with research assistants, grad students, dental students, undergraduate students, post-docs, etc. Then I have all of the manuscripts and book chapters I am writing. And let's not forget teaching. And more committees. Ah, the life of a professor.

SO what I am going to do is to start to keep separate running lists under separate "Areas of Focus", a GTD-type term. This way, I will be able to monitor better each of my areas of responsibiltiy to make sure I am keeping some balance in my work. How will I work those lists? Does everyone remember Mark's post entitled "The simplest time management method?" Here is the link:

http://www.markforster.net/blog/2008/11/17/the-simplest-time-management-method.html

I will use Mark's approach on each of these lists. I will use my intuition and blocking off time for areas as needed and work off of those particular lists. If I have a few things left above the line on one list, and they no longer seem important/stand out....I will use an AF approach and dismiss them and then redraw the line.

Urgent things that come up....well, as Mark has always said, I will use my common sense on things, and if something comes up and needs to be done, I will just do it. Tasks with hard due dates will be tracked in Outlook 2007. I will keep a separate projects list and of course, project support materials. I will still use entries like "Work on Project X".

At any rate, I am going to give this a try. Oh....and apart from the hard due date items....this will all be on paper in a nice notebook with divided sections.

I would love to hear comments, concerns, suggestions, from colleagues here....

Best wishes,
-David

I have known for a long time that I am overcommitted, and may well be in competition with David for the number of overcommitments. :-) I think I still hold the record for total number of open tasks on my AFx lists - I think it was over 1400 at one point. :-)

The problem has always been trying to gauge which particular commitments are causing the trouble and what to do about it.

AF1 was great at helping me get a sense of "balance" and to hone my intuition to decide when to switch from one commitment to another, when to switch from one context to another, and when to take a break by doing something "light".

But with AF1, my list just kept growing. It was possible to keep adding stuff, with no real mechanism for whittling down the list. Individual pages and their tasks could get "dismissed", but for each dismissed page, I had 10 new pages full of tasks. The growing size of the list made it very difficult to keep up with urgent tasks -- I couldn't cycle through the list fast enough to make sure I saw the urgent tasks frequently enough.

AF4 did a better job of presenting me with the urgent tasks more frequently, but it didn't help reduce the growth of the list. It had the same issues as AF1 in that respect. Every time I finally shut down my Closed List and started a new one, the new one was always longer than any previous one. Before switching to DWM my Closed List had reach about 30 pages, I think, still about 30-40% uncompleted on average.

DWM has the best mechanism so far for flagging when you've simply got too much stuff going on, too many commitments. The stuff will simply drop off the list if you don't take care of it, and if you find that really important stuff is dropping off the list, then that's the flag that you have too many commitments. This is working well for me -- it's helping me figure out exactly where the problems are.

I really like DWM because it preserves the best parts of AFx (the "standing out" that brings together your intuition and your intellect, and the freedom of "doing whatever you want, for as long as you want") while giving you a much more effective "dismissal" function that allows you to diagnose your overload much more effectively. I am finding it works especially well when combined with the "current initiative first" rule that Mark suggested.

I have always tried very hard to work these systems without tweaks. Constantly tweaking, besides taking up lots of time, also ends up with confusion. You never really know if you've made things any better.

"Constantly tweaking, besides taking up lots of time, also ends up with confusion. You never really know if you've made things any better."

I'd like to contradict. People are different, and what works for the one don't has to work for another (this is even true for basic things like food - not two human beings have exactly the same metabolism!). And although it is difficult to know what is better BEFORE you try it, it's relatively easy to know once you SEE it - or here: EXPERIENCE it.

All you need to do is to stick with a tweak for several weeks. Once you've got the habit, you FEEL whether it works for you or not. (You just have to ignore any theories why it SHOULD work... ;-D)

And you know that you have found the system that really works for you when you notice that you are no longer that much interested in other systems. That's how it went for me: Over the last year, being here, following the evolution of AF, I have worked out a system of tweaks that I use meanwhile for several months, and my urge to visit the AF forum (where at times I looked up several times a day) has almost disappeared. Plus the urge to testdrive new tweaks has vanished as well, because everything runs just smoothly, even in periods of heavy workload and tight deadlines.

So, it didn't end up with confusion - on the contrary!

Or in other words: You look for tweaks as long as a basically good system doesn't fit you 100%. It's like trying a new piece of clothing on: If it doesn't fit everywhere, you feel uncomfortable - even if it's a good piece of clothing. What do you do? You change it here and there... and one day it just fits, and you stop thinking about it! You just wear it.

David

I've met a handful of people that I considered good at managing their time and whom I worked with closely enough to see how they did it. This is what they had in common.

1. Most tasks were dispatched as they arrived. They were responsive and decisive.

2. They were elegant in adding what they could quickly and passing the batton on. There was no putting it on a list/pile to think about.

3. They were very clear what they wanted from others. They tended not to follow up. However if others didn't deliver they were direct about the impact that had. Sufficiently direct so people didn't fail to deliver a second time.

4. They didn't use a list based time management system. Every few days they made a list of the 5 or 6 big projects they had on the go. Not detailed plans just a short list of the project names.

5. Projects requiring complex scheduling of resources were delegated.

I think DWM adds well to this for the myriad of tasks that we think of to do but don't necessarily have to do. DWM is a good place to catch such tasks and there's an expiry date.
