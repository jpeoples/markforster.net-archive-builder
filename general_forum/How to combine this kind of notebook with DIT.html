Something I've been fancying with for some time is a special kind of notebook that some German coaches recommend which they call a "Superbuch", literally translated as "super book".
That's a hard-bound note book (any kind will do) where you write *everything* (except, maybe, long journal entries, and it does not serve as your calendar). There you collect your tasks, notes from telephone calls, and a lot of other stuff. There are no pre-written dates, and you either append a new day's notes where you finished the day before, or you start a new page every day. But a day's notes might fill more than one page.

The nice thing is to have everything in one place, and you're not restricted in how much space a day will get.
The obvious downside to this is that it is bound to become a mess quickly. You have to actively maintain and regularly prune it. Even then, it's a little hard to identify all your tasks (colour-coding might help a little).
Some people love those books, others hate them.

The special reason why I'm posting this here is that I don't see a way to combine this note book with DIT. There is no "tomorrow" to put the new work that's coming in during the course of a day. There are no future dates to put reminders or future tasks that cannot be done earlier.

Any ideas for workarounds?

Side note: A conventional ring-binder style planner in 2-page-a-day format (with a blank page for notes every day) would come close, but (a) I heavily dislike the rings when writing on the left page, and (b) you can hardly find that kind of page layout here in Germany, all manufacturers that offer 2-page-a-day insist on putting "late meetings" (Time/system), to-dos (Filofax) or other stuff on the second page, no real blank pages for notes. And (c) they're generally bulky and huge. So they're out.

You could keep tomorrow's "work" on a separate piece of paper and then when today is finished write it in the last blank page. Of course this does not solve the problem of future dates for reminders in the future, although you could just insert a notecard or blank piece of paper with these keeping them all in one place.

This is not a unique issue, I too struggled with where to put notes, project lists etc. In GTD projects are all on lists you keep. After using DIT, what I find is that Mark's advise of putting notes in an in tray and then writing the tasks that come from the notes in the task diary is the way to go, I then file the notes in a file for the project. I keep all of my notes related to a project in a clip in the file, which allows me to see the progress being made instead of having them on random pages in a book which is hard to sort through when you need access to them. I do have a spiral notebook which has project related thoughts such as reminders of key issues, that are not date specific, or are out further than the current task diary. There is no reason I could not file these in the separate project files and create a task to review them say monthly to make sure the projects are on track.

For me the task diary containing just tasks and future reminders makes it cleaner for me.

Good luck.

There was a post a few years back on MarkTAW.com called "Cascading Next Actions" which outlined a method which I think would work well for the superbuch approach. If you Google it you should find a few links.

Basically it amounts to marking every action you need to take as you collect your notes (put a box beside the entry or some other preferred symol) and once you check it off, listing the "next action" farther down the page (wherever their is space). You phrase the action in such as way as to keep the project "in mind".

Dear Alex

I used one of these books for years (though I called it a "logbook" rather than a super book).

The main idea behind it is that it is frequently easier to find things by the date they were entered than by the subject matter.

I ceased to use it when the main means of communicating with me became email rather than telephone.

There is no reason why this type of book shouldn't be used with DIT, but I don't think it would be a good idea to try to combine it with the Task Diary. In other words it would be additional to the Task Diary, not instead of it.

I used an ordinary large hard-cover notebook and wrote in the date before the first note for each day. I didn't start a new page for each day. I ticked off each entry once any action needed had been taken, ticked the top of the page if all items had been actioned on it, and put a double tick if there were no unactioned items on earlier pages.

Best wishes,

Mark

Actually, I was thinking of another variation, which is a combination of paper and electronic solutions.

A large part of the appeal of paper is that it makes capture much easier and more flexible (e.g. writing across a two-page spread, markups, highlighting, sketches, diagrams, mindmaps). Another advantage is that it 'feels' more 'obligatory' in contrast to digital solutions which I find easier to dismiss.
A disadvantage of the 'superbook' is that it gets messy rather quickly.
Another aspect is that I'm determined to keep my address book digital and I'm inclined to do the same with my calendar (think: alarms, recurring appointments). So I'll have a hybrid system anyway.

So I'm pondering using paper in the 'superbook' variant for capture and journaling, cleaning it up regularly (daily, I hope) by entering all 'to-do' items into Outlook (where they can be separated into real commitments and 'Someday/Maybes', in GTD parlance, and real commitments can be scheduled for tomorrow or any other future date).
From Outlook I could create a 'daily plan of attack' (on paper), which considers both the scheduled appointments for the day (which are usually very few in my case) and the 'task diary' type tasks for the day.

To sum it up, Outlook would serve as the 'middle man' for pooling and scheduling all tasks. In the 'not-so-super-book', I'd start a new page every day with an overview of scheduled appointments for that day, my closed list (the clear disadvantage is that I'll have to rewrite it every day) and enough space to capture 'below-the-line' items beneath the closed list. This also gives me the opportunity to more clearly separate work and personal tasks/lists, which is something that I really want to do. I'm not an executive, small business owner or self-employed person, and I don't feel like carrying around the paper weight (not sure that this expression doesn't backfire) of my work tasks in my personal life. Most time management systems tend to fall short in this respect, and DIT, unfortunately, is no exception.
I know very well that it is the mantra of all time management literature I know of not to do that, but I really don't feel like carrying aroung a huge binder or whatever in my spare time. Here's what works for me: I carry an A5 spiral-bound notebook wherever I go. They are freely available in any stationery store, so one is not locked into any expensive proprietary system. A5 is a European paper size. US paper sizes include one close to it which I would use if I lived in that country.

Each book lasts me about three weeks. I use the front for notes. The back is my task diary. When I start a new book, I head the back pages with the dates of the days coming in the next 3 or 4 weeks or so; it only takes a couple of minutes. You really don't need to spend money to have pages neatly preprinted with dates when you can hand write them yourself.

Of course, there are things one might wish to schedule at a moment beyond the 3-4 week period. That's particularly true as you near the end of the 3-4 weeks. Those tasks I capture on the very last page, headed "To next book", and transcribe when the next book gets started.

The notes at the front are made in chronological order, and cover every subject, in the way that others have described. I number and keep all the books. When clients or counterparties have raised disputes over some matter in the past, the realisation that I can produce contemporaneous notes is normally enough to make them back down.

This scheme has a number of advantages.

1 There's only one book to carry around. (MonkeyMind asks "Why not have two note books: one super book and one task diary?". Alex W replies "I'm afraid that using two separate notebooks (logbook and task diary) would complicate things rather than improve my work." I agree with Alex W, and ask "Why not recognise that one book has two ends?")

2 There's no choice to be made about which book to write in on a particular topic, as there would be if one had separate notebooks for different subjects.

3 There's no danger of having forgotten to bring the relevant book, as there would be if one had separate notebooks for different subjects.

4 There's no choice to be made about where to look for notes written in the past on a particular topic, as there would be if one had separate notebooks for different subjects.

5 Flipping between the task diary and the notebook is a matter of turning pages rather than pushing one book away and putting another in front of you. Some Post-it tabs or similar can make it easy to get directly between the current pages.

6 (For me, most important, and the objection to any ring binder system:) The spiral binding allows the book to be folded right back, so that it it presents a small A5-sized space for writing in a confined space, such as a bus or train.

7 If you want a larger page to draw on, opening the book to present two opposing pages gives you a surface the size of A4. Admittedly, it has the spiral down the middle, so it's not as nice as a single sheet that size; but the spiral is smaller than any ring binding or similar system. (Alex W mentions "writing across a two-page spread".

8 The pages are not punched or perforated, so there's no danger of them falling out as there is in a system such as Levenger Circa. (Some notebooks have pages pre-perforated to make them easier to tear out neatly; that's an embellishment I actively don't want.)

The main drawback is that there's a small amount of housekeeping to be done when one switches from one book to the next. But that's not without disadvantages in terms of decluttering. I sometimes describe this arrangement as "my self-emptying filofax."

I tinkered with the idea of combining a notebook and calendar for advance planning for quite a while, and found a solution that works for me. Maybe it'll work for you too.

I use an ordinary yellow legal pad (not the glue top kind, but the kind that you can flip and tear sheets out of). Then I put separate Weekly calendar sheets in the back of the pad, between the cardboard back and the last page of the notebook. These sheets are for advance planning and DIT. Each day has plenty of room for scheduled Appointments, To Do's, and Reminders. I also have columns for Calls to Return and Items To Do/Reminders for the week.

I make the calendar sheets myself in Microsoft Word, and print them on regular sized paper (portrait, not landscape, so I can flip the legal pad pages up quickly on the fly to read and write).

I number the pages of the legal pad 1-50 for reference purposes. The first 3 pages are an "index". The rest are for notes and Daily Agenda/Task Diary.

I like to have everything for the day on one page. Each morning, I draw a vertical line down the middle of the first blank page in the pad: "Things To Do" go on the right side, (I draw a line under my Closed List). "Appointments" go on the upper half of the left side, "People To Call" go on the bottom half of the left side.

Items for the Daily Page come from checking unfinished items from the previous day's page and the DIT notes on the weekly calendar page in the back. I put a checkmark by everything on these sources to make sure I haven't missed anything. I also check my Notes pages from the previous day: any tasks that come up from my notes are marked w/a circle on the left margin to make them stand out while I'm taking notes. (They should've been processed to the appropriate day on the weekly page, but sometimes life happens...)

The notebook pages immediately following the Daily Page are for meeting notes, phone log, etc.

The beauty of my system for me is that I may use 1 page for notes today, I may use 10; doesn't matter. I work through my Daily Page, crossing items off, adding new ones below the line if necessary or putting them on the appropriate day of the Weekly calendar page. Tomorrow I'll go to the next blank page in the pad, and make a new Daily Page.

During the day, if I complete a task and take notes on it, I check off the item as done on the Daily Page and put the appropriate page number behind it so I can easily find the notes if I need them. I never remove any notes or Daily Pages from the pad. If I need the notes for a project or client, I xerox the page and drop it into the appropriate file. I seriously keep my old legal pads under lock and key.

My system is pretty flexible, cheap, and easy to maintain. A pad may last 2 weeks, or 3 days, depending on what I'm doing. (I'm a Financial Advisor, so I have days when I have a lot of meetings, others that are taken up by phone calls or research.) I keep about 2 months (8 pages) of weekly sheets in the back of the pad, and one monthly sheet for the month following those (I don't usually schedule myself out more than 3 months at a time). When a pad is full, I put the weekly calendar sheets in a new pad. No re-writing.

Legal pads are cheap and available anywhere. The weekly sheet I made is undated, so I xerox as many as I want and fill the dates in myself...takes 2 minutes for 2 months' worth of calendar pages. It takes about one minute to number the notebook pages 1-50.The component that takes longest to maintain is the index sheets at the front. I do these after the pad is full so I can go back and refresh myself on anything important. This is usually a 5-10 minute process.

Like I said, this works for me. Maybe you'll be able to glean something from my system that will work for yours.

I love this topic and wanted to contribute what works for me. I also use the Moleskin cashier. It's the perfect size, with no spirals, disks or rings to make it snag on a pocket or in a bag. I have a pen on the page I'm on as the page marker.
