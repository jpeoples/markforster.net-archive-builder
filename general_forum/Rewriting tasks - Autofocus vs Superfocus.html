Seraphim:

Let's leave urgent tasks out of the discussion and just concentrate on unfinished tasks.

The SuperFocus equivalent of AutoFocus would be if all the tasks you put in Column 1 read "Do some work on X". The "Do some work on...." may of course be understood rather than explicitly stated.

If you did that with all your tasks, it would be identical to AutoFocus.

It's the re-entry of Unfinished tasks in Column 2 that makes the big difference between AutoFocus and SuperFocus. The purpose of Column 2 is to force the finishing of tasks once they have been started. That means that once you have allowed a task to get into Column 2 you must finish the task as defined.

Note that tasks will only get into Column 2 if you define them other than "Do a bit of work on X". In effect, the entry of tasks into Column 2 is always the result of a voluntary decision to define the task in such a way that it can't be finished in one go.

In other words you are making a voluntary decision to force the task to be finished. Once you've taken on that commitment, you shouldn't regard yourself as at liberty to back out of it.

If you're going keep backing out of it, you would be better off keeping all your tasks as "Do some work on X". At least that way you wouldn't be fooling yourself that you are doing SuperFocus.

For most unfinished tasks that I enter into Column 2, it works just fine to rewrite them as originally entered. And SuperFocus drives those tasks to completion.

But sometimes during the course of working the task, the task demands to be changed. I've found that SuperFocus still does a great job of driving these tasks to completion. Here are examples - I think only one of them is "backing out of it", and the rest are legitimate:

- I started a project, but as I got into it, I got a better idea of what it really entails, and realized that I needed to rewrite it to focus on one particular aspect of the project, leaving the rest for later. I would re-enter the part that needs focus now in Column 2, but enter the rest of the project as a task in Column 1, last page. SuperFocus has worked great to drive it to completion but allow it to flex as needed. Autofocus allows the flex but doesn't have the drive to completion.

- I started a project, but the project changes for some reason outside my control, and is no longer a priority. I would then go through my "wrapping up" procedure, and the project would eventually end up on Column 1 of the last page. Your answer to Jen in the other thread seems to say that this is OK. ( http://www.markforster.net/forum/post/1416990 )

- I started a project, but now one or more new projects have come along and have forced me to change my priorities. I don't have bandwidth for the original project right now. I would go through my "wrapping up" procedure on this one, too, and the project would eventually end up on Column 1 of the last page. You seem to be saying that I should finish this project as originally written, no matter what, since I have already allowed it into Column 2.

- I started a project, but now realize it was a mistake to start it at that time. Bad judgment, frivolity, wanting to do more than realistic, or whatever, just got hold of me. Now I want to wrap it up and get out, and save it for later. This is the one scenario where I agree it's "backing out" without a good reason. I think it's happened only once or twice, during my SuperFocus startup when I was learning the basic mechanics, and when it happened, if I remember correctly, I ended up just deleting the tasks and putting them into Outlook reminders to reconsider in a month or so. One thing I really like about Autofocus is how you can enter a task in any particular form, however it occurs to you at the moment. And then, as you work on it, you can rewrite it to clarify what you really want to do with it. The idea becomes more focused as you continue to work. It also spins off other related tasks.

In other words, Autofocus never **requires** you to define "next actions" like GTD does. It allows you to define them as the need arises, as you find more clarity on the task or project as you are working on it.

I thought the same idea applied to SuperFocus, and that's how I have been using it.

For example, just today, I had a task called "Team XXX Action Items". Originally I meant "stop keeping your action items in Outlook -- write each one as a task in SF". And so that's what I started doing.

As I worked on it, I realized that I needed to make a distinction between my Team XXX Action Items that are tasks for which I am responsible; and my Team XXX Agenda Items, which are things I must make sure get discussed at the next team meeting, since I am the meeting facilitator. So, I rewrote the task in Column 2, "Sort out Team XXX Action Items from Agenda Items". It's a completely natural outgrowth of the original task.

Next pass, I sorted out the items into two lists, but still hadn't entered my own action items onto the SF list. So, I rewrote the task into Column 2 again, this time calling it "Enter Team XXX Action Items into SF".

Mark, it sounds like you are saying this is not how you envisioned the system working. You intended that the unfinished task gets re-entered into Column 2 each time, written in exactly the same way and meaning exactly the same thing. Am I understanding you correctly?

If yes, then how would you handle a situation like I describe here?

And in general, how would SuperFocus allow a task to become more clearly defined as you iterate on it?

Seraphim:

Your example of "Team XXX Action Items" is an interesting one, and I had to read through it several times to follow the dynamics of it.

It sounds to me that in fact the task has still remained "Team XXX Action Items", even though you have written it rather differently each time. In other words, you won't consider it finished until everything covered by the original "Team XXX Action items" is finished.

So what you are really doing with the rewritten tasks is using them like bookmarks. It's as if I had "Read War and Peace" as one huge task, but every time I re-entered it I put "War and Peace, p. 244" or whatever. You're keeping track of where you've got to in the original project by putting down the next step, but it's still the whole project that you are working on, not just the step.

That seems perfectly acceptable to me.
