Ranier,

If you are being serious. I love the ridiculously extended metaphors and similes that "Homer" just gets lost in at times in the _Iliad_. But since it is thoroughly from an oral tradition there is much tedious repetition to the eyes of a contemporary reader. (This is remedied easily with some intelligent editing of the text.)

Also, the text stands as an incredible rebuke of war and a song of sympathy for the "barbarian". Hector, probably the only sympathetic character (maybe Priam as well), has quite a touching moment with his wife before his death, the celebration of which (complete with homerian epithet) ends most contemporary translations.

"And so they buried Hector, tamer of horses."

A beautifully and surprisingly stark and silent ending to such an overwrought tale. Within the context of this line must the entire work be read, or (un)fortunately re-read.

_The Odyssey_ is more accessible to us. And who doesn't love Odysseus?

As a "story" _The Odyssey_ is more successful for contemporary times, but my copies of the _Iliad_ are much more worn. Its lyric is wonderfully beautiful, baffling, flawed, and dense. It also resists interpretation to a greater degree than the _Odyssey_.

Also if you care for other things Greek, the _Iliad_ is simply the shadow in which all else written.

If I could only own a copy of one, it would be the _Iliad_.

Been a long time since I studied either diligently, so take this with a mine of salt.
