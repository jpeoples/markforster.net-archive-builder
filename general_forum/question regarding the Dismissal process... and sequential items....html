Hi Trevor,

This is just my personal experience with doing the same kind of major home renovation that you are doing using Autofocus as well for a number of months.

The one thing I wouldn't do is to put all of your projects on the list, because you will find that you may start many projects - deconstructing etc. but not having the time to finish in a reasonable time frame because you're busy deconstructing (the first step) something else.

What worked better for me was scheduling everything that had to do with these projects except for the ones that were big jobs that I kind of dreaded and didn't have a real need to complete in a certain time frame (like removing stipple from the ceiling or glued down mdf from the floor - ugh!). Basically anything I could work 'little and often' on without making a huge mess. I used AF condensed for this and it worked the best for me of all the methods.

I would imagine you could just tackle one project at a time using the list. I just never found for myself that I made progress as fast as I could have with scheduling it.

I prefer Mark's ideas around "current initiative" for reno projects. It worked better for me to focus on doing something on a particular project (only one at a time) every day as soon as I got home (like ripping up carpet) or picking stuff up on my way home from work and then a big push to do the major time consuming parts on the weekends to finish it off.

I should say however, that using Autofocus to start working on these projects was literally a life-saver because it got me started and I did make quite a lot of progress using it (the only problem was that I was left with a lot of stuff ripped up but not finished). The AF method reduced the overwhelmed feeling that I had when looking at everything I had to do that had paralyzed me into not doing anything at all.
