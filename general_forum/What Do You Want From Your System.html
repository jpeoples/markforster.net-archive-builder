The title of this post is taken from a question Alan Baljeu recently asked.

My answer is twofold. I want my system to be a system, that is, to organize my tasks. And, I want it to motivate me to do those tasks.

In this post, I will assume that the "system" requirement of the system is noncontroversial.

From Norman's "intuition" thread, it appears as if the "motivation" requirement is controversial.

In English as plain as I can make it, my hope is that I will be more likely to do tasks that I know are good for me if I use the system than if I don't use the system. When this hope is satisfied, I find that the system motivates me.

DWM really gets me motivated. And not in the sense that Mike uses "motivated." If I understand him correctly, he says he is motivated when he feels like he wants to do something. What I like about DWM is that it gets me to do things precisely when I don't feel like I want to do them. It gets me to do them anyway.

And, of course, 99% of the time, 15 seconds after starting to do them, I feel like I want to do them.

For me, of the two desiderata--organization and motivation--the latter easily overshadows the former in importance and power.

So, to answer Alan's question, I want a system that will get me organized and get me motivated. And I would gladly sacrifice organization for motivation. And DWM is a good system for me, because it gets me to do the things that I know are good for me and that I avoided doing pre-DWM.

I don't really need a system to motivate me to do things. And even if I did, I don't think that would be successful long-term (why don't I want to do them anyway?). What I really need is something that helps me focus, and helps me avoid the twin dangers of:
1) being so overwhelmed with tasks that I can't decide what to do next
2) Getting so absorbed with one thing that everything else goes out of the window (or hyperfocus, as some others here would probably call it)

DWM and AF achieve both admirably - I can focus on the task in hand knowing that everything else is not being 'lost', and the system makes sure that I keep catching those tasks which I might otherwise forget when I get stuck into a big, absorbing task.

On top of that, they are almost minimalist enough to be not 'really' a system. This is important to me: if I ever feel that the 'system' overhead is taking up a chunk of my time, or that it is 'forcing' me do things that I don't really want to do, I rebel, and then I'm back to square one.

Moises, I think that's the problem I had with all the AF offspring eventually. At first, it was new and fun and I was motivated to see how many pages I could close out. But it wasn't long before no one cared about that, including me. The other problem was I often found myself motivated to do the wrong things. There is often a good reason we are procrastinating. AF and its descendants CAN get us considering that, but it offers very little guidance outside of pushing one's self to do things.

The more I've thought about it, my mostly listless system accomplishes what I want it to. The things that get done are those that I feel motivated to do, people are bugging me about, and/or that I remember to do. The rest probably wasn't worth doing. Again, the exception here is when there's a deadline or something I need to do for someone else that I'm likely to forget. All go on my calendar or Gootodo list. An example. When using AF, I would list "Read Book A." I would come round to it and find that it was easier than the other tasks on the list so I'd spend a bit reading Book A. Honestly, though, if I have to have a list remind me to read a book, it's just not something I want to read. In fact, the more pressure I feel in seeing that task on the list repeatedly, the LESS I want to read it.

Not trying to argue against something that works for you or others! I am just trying to understand better what works for me.
