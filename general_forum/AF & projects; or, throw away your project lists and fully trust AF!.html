nuntym <>

I'm still refining how I handle projects, but I'm getting closer. What I'm doing is in line with what Mark's posted in the discussion under "Using Resistance as a Guide" and as quoted/referenced above.

A project starts as a grouping of related tasks, or as a goal which needs tasks defined. I add a page for the project, give it a name, and reference that name in AF. Within that page I write anything I want to.

When I encounter a project in AF, I may look at its page, and then decide whether to work on it. All of these count as "Working on a project".
- Defining or rewriting its goal
- Defining intermediate goals
- Defining or refining tasks
- Planning out how to achieve the project
- (Most importantly) Working on any tasks.

As Mark says, any of the tasks may be put individually on the AF list, in addition to being on the project page, if I so choose.

This approach is very general, and can be used for other task groupings besides projects. E.g., checklists.

Would a large group of tasks for the project clog up the list and prevent you from closing pages?

Do you work better with all the project's tasks on one page? If you put them in the AF list when you think of them, they'll be scattered.

Will the next action for the project get buried on an AF page in the middle of the book and be missed on your daily scan?

Every project is different, and it changes as you work through it. The state of your list also affects how you should handle projects.
