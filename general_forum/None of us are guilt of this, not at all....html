> I'd bet big bucks that those who have a compelling "why" don't visit productivity sites.

avrum, for me that is almost true. But I would have to strike the word "why" and replace it with "pressure" or "deadline." I am currently set up with a wonderful "why" and plenty of autonomy to do it. My schedule is a little choppy and does distract me, but it was much worse in the past when I got a lot more done. The difference is that my prior productive periods had not only a lot of "why," but also a lot of "need it done yesterday."

My current lack of productivity only began some five years ago and really puzzles me. My last compelling "why" project that I completed was about a year and a half ago. It was also my last compelling "why" project that had a deadline. I blazed through it in six weeks and loved it (mostly, except for a couple of times when I allowed myself to get sleep-deprived).

One can speculate endlessly about the reasons, but my intuition tells me I am simply used to this pattern, since I was a little boy in school: serve everyone else's deadlines first, then attend to your own—if you have any energy left.

Once in a while, someone mentions "addicted to the rush of a deadline," and I know exactly what they are talking about. Adding a deadline to a tedious task energizes me, even if I hate the task. Particularly if I can throw a heroic all-nighter at it, I come away feeling extremely capable and satisfied. The experience of being that focused is something to me like "runner's high."

For me, the holy grail of time management is to get that level of focus and competence from my *own* deadlines. Or, I should say, my own priorities, without the deadlines.
