First of all, I promise this isn't a tweak! (well, not really)

One of the great strengths of AF (both types) is the inclusion of less nasty 'rewards' - meaning that you get 'guilt-free play' (in the words of Neil Fiore's 'The Now Habit') built in.

But if 'as long as you feel like' for some of the more nasty tasks means 'until you get stuck', and then you move off to a nicer task, then you've effectively rewarded yourself for your procrastination. Do that often enough and your 'guilt-free rewards' are increasingly not guilt-free at all!

What I'm doing is, when a nasty task 'stands out' (as they frequently do!), I not only mark that task with a dot, but also then look for and mark a reward task. (Actually, if the reward task is not on the final page, I'm crossing it out and re-entering, with a mark, on the final page). I then set a timer to do at least 30mins on my nasty task, knowing that I've got a nice reward task on the last page that I've committed to doing when I've successfully done my stint. I'm finding this quite a good way of pulling me through the stuck task.

I wonder whether identifying the 'next' task, as well as the one you're about to work on, might help people focus more as a general approach than as a specific strategy for the 'stuck' nasty tasks.

If I follow AF rules to a "T", guilt and mindfulness guide my actions. For example, I have "surf the web" on my list. Pre-AF days, I'd mindlessly surf the web until:

a) I had a client
b) a pressing deadline was upon me
c) it was time to go to bed

With AF, I'm "forced" to highlight "surf the web", and then reenter it as a task at the end/beginning of my list. This provides enough of a gap to help me choose to do something else, probably more aligned with my goals.

The key is to follow the rules.
