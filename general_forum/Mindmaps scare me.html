I've been thinking about mindmaps lately and mit's comment on another thread about finding them overwhelming.

Mindmaps scare me.

When someone (ie. me) who is easily overwhelmed sees the enormity of a project all in one huge unmotivating picture like a mindmap, it is paralyzing, not motivating. I avoided my house renovations for over 4 years by seeing too much of the "whole job". I tried breaking it down, did various mind maps, timelines etc. etc. Tried dumping into the AF list too - and dismissing later.

The only thing that helped was to block out the whole picture entirely and focus 100% of my attention on one small piece of the big project as if the other pieces literally didn't exist.

Even now, the surest way for me to feel anxiety again about getting my house renovated is to look at my original time-lines and monthly breakdowns of areas and things to do in those areas.

2009-04-2 11:14
A mind map is no more nor less scary than a list. If your list has just a few high level entries (like "Renovate Kitchen") it is not that scary. If you put the 400 odd steps you'll need to do on that list then it will, no doubt, petrify you. The same is true of mind maps. They can be as high or low leveled as you like. In most systems the branches below any level can be collapsed. So you can control how much detail you see. So your center node might be "Sell House". Under that you might have the nodes "Renovate", "Find New House", etc. Under "Renovate" you might have "Kitchen", "Bath", "Master Bedroom". Under Kitchen you may have some large tasks and under those some more detail. While you are focusing on the Kitchen you can keep the other areas collapsed.

It is quite easy to see just what you want to see and no more.

Mind maps don't scare me ... some of the people walking around without their meds do, though ;-)

Jacqueline,

a basic Mind Map is actually nothing more and nothing less than a visual representation of a hierarchical outline list, that allows you to fold and unfold subnodes, which correspond to subprojects. If you do Mind Mapping on a computer, it is essential that you use software that allows you to fold and unfold branches, as has already been mentioned above. Either way, I think that for a large project some form of being able to fold up sub-projects is essential, so that you can focus on just the handful of tasks that are currently of interest, rather than the 400 in the whole project.

2009-04-3 11:47
Actually, a mind map is a bit more than a visual representation of a hierarchical list. A mind map is not hierarchical at all. It is a network. One thing that throws some people off is that once you have mind mapped a chapter in a book, for example, you'll find that you have LOST the SEQUENTIAL flow that you would have captured with a Harvard outline. What you get in return is a network which shows the relationship of ideas TO EACH OTHER, rather than in relationship to the order presented in the chapter.

I think it is important to understand those two basic features: it is visual, but it is also a nonlinear network of ideas related to each other.

Mike,

to respond to your April 3rd post, you are quite correct that a Mind Map can have a network structure with cross links between items deep down the branches. On the other hand a useful Mind Map is rarely a general network (it typically has a distinguished node at the centre which is the overall project goal) and most proponents of Mind Mapping that I have come across encourage you to severely limit the cross links and largely maintain a hierarchical structure. In my experience, the cross links are primarily useful in a brainstorming session, whether on your own or in group. Even then its usually easiest to first set up the basic hierarchical branches and then think about adding cross links to represent other relationships.

Once you have all the ideas and need to develop a project plan and then time manage it, which is I think the primary topic of this forum, they can become obstructive, and indeed frightening, since in general its best to work on a large project one branch at a time, rather than dotting about all over the place. Of course this may force you to completely rethink the overall hierarchy. If your project is to renovate an empty house and your branches each represent a room, it would probably be crazy to have the wiring redone in each room independently, rather than having the electrician in to do the whole lot. You might thus want to create a hierarchy by type of activity rather than by room.

Jaroslav's post emphasises the general point that it is quicker to group one's tasks by type of action rather than by project. In the example he gives it is better to do the wiring for the entire house, rather than complete all the actions for one room at a time. In the same way it's economical in time to batch all your emails together (and the same with phone calls, paperwork, etc), rather than deal with them by project.

The implication of this for mind-maps is that once one has planned all one's projects hierarchically, you need to be able to extract the different types of actions so they can be batched non-hierarchically. This is easy to do with most mind-mapping programs by using filters.

Jaroslav,

There are different uses for mind maps, as you say. When it comes to "understanding" something, then links need to be there if they need to be there. I mean, one can't ignore information (linkages) just because it violates the rules of one's system of recording the information. It would be like limiting the scope of a project because you ran out of room on the page to list steps!

The second use discussed is of laying out some kind of CREATED system ... such as a project. In this case, the map needs to conform to the physical reality, not the internal logical connections. For example, in the filing system Christine cited, they files are physical and can live on only one place which can be shown equally well on a Harvard Outline or mind map with no cross links. However, if one wanted to show connections between the various files (such as those which have both tax consequences and are also organized by subsidiary company or something like that) then cross linking is necessary. Cross linking, viewed another way, is like "tagging". It is a way of noting that things have more than one meaning or use.

So what it comes down to is that one needs to show as much complexity as there really is, and that one needs to see. As I indicated, my use of mind maps is for research. When I read a book I don't just "read" it, I dissect it, pulling out the ideas and showing relationships between them, and other ideas I have in my own mental framework. So my maps can become quite tangled. BUT, while they would give anyone else a migraine, to me they illuminate things. As I travel around the linkages I see things that cause me to question things and set me off in new directions. And that is why I map in the first place.

I don't know if that clarifies anything ;-)
