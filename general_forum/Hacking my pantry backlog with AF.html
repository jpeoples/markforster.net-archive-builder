At the risk of sounding very odd, I am admitting here that I have a food hoarding problem. I literally have enough food in my pantries and freezers that I wouldn't have to go grocery shopping for 6 months or more. Really, how many pounds of risotto does one non-Italian family need?

My plan is to treat each shelf in the pantry, the cupboards and the freezer as a separate "page". There's probably about 25 items or so on each shelf, so the logistics work too. It's also somewhat disorganized, so there's a good variety on each shelf as well. If I dismiss, I will give everything on the shelf to the food bank or the dog or the garbage eats it.

It makes me wonder how many other things you could clear out using AF principles - too many clothes? DVD's to watch?

Books? - obviously. I'm using the method to pick my next book to read based on thinking of the shelves as a page in my "to be read" bookcase. If I don't pick one of the books, I decided that I would dismiss all of them on that shelf (ie. give them away) - I have to admit, I haven't dismissed any yet. :-)

Jacqueline,

it doesn't solve your immediate food backlog problem, and doesn't really work for non-perishable items like books, CD's and DVD's but the system we have for food, largely developed by my wife, is that new long lasting food items eg rice, tins etc, are only bought when we use one item up. So, if we finish a box of tea, we add tea to the next shopping list. For any given category, there is a certain number in the cupboard, so that when you actually do finish a box, you are not in the position that you have run out of it (except when I forget to add it to the list). Once you get this system into equilibrium, it works remarkably well.

Jacqueline,

My neighbor has this "problem" as well. We often shop together ... I have CostCo membership and she has a Sam's Club membership. She can't buy one of anything. I have noticed that there are at least two processes at work. 1) She has a scarcity mentality ... IOW, she is in dread of not having enough ... well, food, but really anything. So she is driven to buy multiples. And logic does not help, she happily throws food out to make room for more food. Logically speaking, it boggles the mind. She has three freezers filled with food then buys more frozen food and struggles to find a place to put it. And this goes far beyond food. When she used to rent videos, she would rent about eight of them ... then watching them all over the week became less fun than a chore.

2) The real sustaining problem is that much of this is now automated. I can see that she no longer thinks but reflexively grabs two of everything. No thinking is involved. The other day she was buying paper cups. They came in packs of 600 (this is a warehouse store, of course ;-) and she bought two of those packs. I asked how many a day she used and when she said "one", I walked her through the math which took her to just shy of four years worth of paper cups to be stored ... somewhere. Which is what really drives her husband nuts ;-)

If one assumes that those are the only two factors operating, it becomes clear that a change has to take place in two places: 1) On each reach for an item ask why you are buying it and will you really run out if you don't buy lots of it and what is the worst that would happen if you did run out and, 2) stop to think in the first place ... IOW, don't shop on autopilot.

All of that said, I really like your application of AF to the shelves. It will make for a good variety as you eat down the pantry. As to sales, who can really resist a sale? LOL ;-) I learned a good lesson when Ramen noodles were on sale and I overbought. Until then I did not know that there are bug eggs in all grain and in time they will hatch out. OY! Now I only overbuy sale items that are canned ... and then I make it a priority to eat them down.
