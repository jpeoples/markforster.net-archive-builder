Original Post

2011-04-15 19:18
Lately, my SuperFocus notebook has become my faithful companion during most of my waking hours. I have several routine tasks that I enter each morning (on the last page, Column 1) and want to do early, before commuting to work; e.g., exercise, process In tray, etc. Even though it's a fairly fixed routine, I like entering these tasks and crossing them off. But often, my current page is not close to the last page. So quite soon after entering the routine tasks, I redefine them as urgent, cross them off the last page, and reenter them on the current page in C2. In this way, I don't cheat by jumping straight to the last page. Still, the process seems somewhat contrived. I suppose I could just enter them straightaway into C2 of the current page, but I'm not always 100% alert in the a.m. when thinking about all the things I need to do, and it's more natural to write everything in one place before defining some tasks as urgent.

I'm considering changing the procedure and just doing a daily reset to the last page. This variation implies that one must cycle through the complete list at least once per day, which is good practice anyway.

An alternative would be to stop entering the routine things, and count on habit and memory to get them done each day. But I like entering and crossing off some tasks, especially exercise!

Following on Seraphim's post. Over the course of several months, I've had the following progression:
1. I noticed I kept neglecting to do certain things each day.
2. I made a list of routines and tried to follow them. Sometimes I did and sometimes not.
3. I examined the list and simplified it, pared it. This was hard, but valuable. Made it into a daily check list.
4. I got more consistent. I didn't always need to follow the list. I got less consistent.
5. I found sometimes it was tedious or awkward doing all the items in one spell.
6. Enter "Routines" in C2. Do it little & often.

So now routines is a "first thing" and a "evening thing", but it's not a do-them-all-now thing. Seems to be good, but maybe there's a 7th stage.

If you hadn't yet done any tasks in C1, then you hadn't started C1 and could have entered the urgent tasks directly into C2 of the current page.

I like Mark's "row of circles ●●●●●○" bottom-margin hack for counting how many times a page has been visited. I was daydreaming about possibly adding right-margin dots to each line, to count how many times a task was skipped, but decided that would slow the process down too much. But having a count of the number of visits, before a page is finally completed or dismissed, will be helpful. One could even add a rule, that if you visit a page that has N circles at the bottom, it is time to dismiss it, where N might be 12 or 20 or however many fill the entire margin. An obvious upper limit is the number of lines on the page, since at least one task must be actioned on each visit.

One could also wait to fill the circle until it is time to go to the next page. That way, one could quickly find the current page after closing and reopening the notebook, by flipping through the pages until the unfilled circle is spotted. I use the "lonely dots" technique to tell me whether I've completed a C1 task (and how many) on the current visit, so I don't need the filled bottom circle to tell me that. This technique also marks the current page, but is not as distinctive as the row of circles on the bottom.
