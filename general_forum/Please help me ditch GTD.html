JTKaspar:

Here are my thoughts.

1. Those lists strike me as essentially shopping list (even though they might not be buying lists). So, thinking analogously, how would you handle a grocery list in AF? I don't think you would put down each item on the AF list (buy milk, buy bread, buy cheese). That doesn't seem right. Rather you would slowly compile a run-of-the-mill grocery list, adding things to purchase as you go along. When it came time to go shopping you would take that list.

So. . .if you compile a "things to check out list" it would be separate from the AF list. Nothing wrong with that. Now if you need help in getting around to *using* that list, then you would put a task on your AF list: "go grocery shopping." Or, in your case "browse books/movies".

2. I wonder if the best way to handle these types of things would be to enter the task on your list incorporating the deadline. Thus you would enter "Do X before Jan 1st".

I'm not sure about this, but it seems that AF would then bring this task to your attention in such a way that you would likely get it done well before the due date. I think this is one of the benefits of AF, it staves off our tendency to procrastinate items to the last minute.

Now, if the "Do X before Jan 1st" task keeps coming up and you keep ignoring it, and it is "in danger" of being up for review over and over, but you save it by doing some work on it. . . well, it seems it will get done over time, and the due date will be staring you in the face over and over. (Probably no need to look at your calendar at all!)

Alternatively, if the task actually DOES come up for review. . . then I think it would mean you should evaluate whether it is ripe to put it in AF right now at all. Perhaps the due date is so far along, or you have so many other pressing and important tasks, that this item really shouldn't be on the radar until later.

How you make sure you get it on the radar (on the AF list) later would be something to think about.

JTKaspar:

Joseph has made some excellent points in his reply, so I'll just add a few thoughts.

I personally do a lot of reading and a lot of watching movies on DVD. So here's how I handle it.

If I hear of a book or movie that I want to check out I will put the title on my AF list with a question mark after it, e.g. Some Like It Hot? That is giving me the task to look up what is said about the book/movie on Amazon, Lovefilm, IMDB etc. That is a task that will only take a few moments, so I don't find any problem with that type.

If after checking it out I decide to order it I do so there and then.

I normally rent movies from Lovefilm. When the movie arrives (I have three at a time), I put the title on the AF list. I usually watch them in chunks of about 30 minutes, so they will get re-entered a couple of times. I keep up a fairly high turn-over of movies.

Books I treat rather differently. I keep all the books I currently want to read on a bookshelf with the last read on the left. I have a task "Reading" on my list. When that task comes up, I look along the books from the left and chose one. This gives priority to the ones I've been most recently reading.

With articles and on-line viideos, I put the title on the AF list (and if necessary where to find it) and treat it as any other task, doing it in chunks if it is long. I try to keep the numbers down - and it's essential to be prepared for them to go into Review Mode.

As someone who used GTD starting in 2001 and was convinced that it was best way to be productive (until I noticed how much time I spent on the system and not on doing) here's my advice--

Grab a notebook and just start AF. Do a "brain dump" as David Allen calls it to start off the list. Work off the list for the day. I found I was much more productive my first AF day than I was the day before on GTD.

I put everything in AF-- things I can't do now now, things that are due later, things I might do, things I don't know what to do with, etc. It all gets sorted out using the rules. No need to "process" and no need to "review" task lists.

I do keep a calendar for events and I do keep a project list with milestones someplace separate, which I review whenever review project list stands out. I also do a "weekly review" but this too only when it stands out in the AF list.
