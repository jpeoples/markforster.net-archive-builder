Kate

I have not been starting from scratch, but I think it doesn't make any big difference as long as you don't dump your mind in the system all at once. But in your situation you shouldn't have any backlog at all.

As I can work from several locations, it makes sense to me to have only one single list, and I would suggest everybody to try it. At the end, as somebody else said on this forum, "my life is one".

In my case I follow several private and professional projects, have to arrange my time according to the work of my wife, the school of our child, the requests of my colleagues and my customers, and of course my desires. What I really appreciate of AF are the simplicity and light weight and these are for me winning factors in this context.

All my tasks go on the list, and recently I have stopped using a reminder tool, and started entering on the list also those items that will be done in some future date. Till now also this is working; having them under my eyes gives me the opportunity to catch new ideas, and at the end to better plan my time.

Enjoy your return to work.

Crikey, just looked at your blog and that is a very complicated system. By the time you've calculated how long all your tasks are going to take you, you could have done 10 tasks. Must admit, I have a pretty pivotal job with a high workload so I just have to crash through things as fast as I can.

I use my own version of Do It Tomorrow but I very rarely write things down. In terms of meetings and travel I use my calendar a lot. As soon as I get a sniff of a meeting I enter it into my calendar. I make a note of what I need in the subject line or what I am waiting on and from who and use traffic lights so I can see straight away where I am up to (i.e. green is confirmed, yellow is pending and I am waiting on info, red is action and I need to do something!). i also add travel into my calendar so I know if I need to book tickets or arrange a lift, etc.

In terms of the daily routine thing, I do that too but I email those tasks to myself (i.e. check calendar, clear email, etc.). My email folders are set up as tickler folders (i.e. 17 Thu, 18 Fri, 21 Mon, etc.). Once I've done the daily task I drag it tomorrow's folder or if it's something I need to do again later in the day I will put it in my PM folder.

In the same way, when I ask someone to do something in an email, I blind copy it to myself, work out when I need an answer by then drag it to that folder. I normally give people a couple of days, sometimes a week and every now and again same day or next day.

Each morning I clear my email then clear my email folder for that particular day. It's pretty easy to remember if I've received a reply and if not, I chase the response by forwarding the email.

Hope that makes sense!

Kate,

Outlook is indeed a powerful and flexible tool. There is no practical limit to the amount of time you can spend tuning your configuration. So you need to be disciplined.

I have tried a lot of approaches and my top eight tips would be:

1. Pick a configuration, allow yourself to tweak it for the first couple of days and then stick with it for a couple of weeks. After that, decide whether it works for you.

2. Keep it as simple as possible. Use a paper system as a template. If you're using AF, don't date your tasks or set priorities.

3. Use the task list for tasks (!).

4. If you have a lot of mail to deal with, clear it in batches. move any mail that will take more than a couple of minutes to the task list (drag and drop). and use search folders for critical incoming mails and sent mails flagged for follow up.

5. If you're using the latest version of Autofocus (which is almost zero set-up and maintenance and works well for me), you will need separate lists for projects and routines. Decide where you are going to keep them. (Separate Tasks folders or One Note are popular choices)

6. Despite what I said about keeping it simple, check out the task views. I like to view my tasks filtered for open tasks only.

7. Make sure you keep a paper copy with you when you are away from the machine. Or at least something to note down new ideas and actions. (A piece of blank A4 folded down to A7 works for me. I do write quite small, though.)

8. If you are lucky enough to have a laptop and a separate screen at work, use an extended desktop across both. Try to be consistent about where your admin (Mail/Calendar/tasks) shows and where your work (documents, plans, presentations) goes. But don't get obsessive.

To illustrate what I'm talking about, there's an annotated screenshot of my setup here: http://screencast.com/t/EHFXFukWLc

It's not perfect, but has been working well for me for a couple of weeks (so I'm allowing myself a tiny tweakette). It is Outlook 2007. I had something similar with Outlook 2003.

Hope this helps,

Will
