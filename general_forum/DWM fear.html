This seems to be inherent to to DWM. As long as you are ahead of the game, it's no problem. But if you are behind, it can be a big problem.

My concern is that fear isn't the best long-term motivator, especially in this context. The first time you figure out that you can take a task that is supposed to be "dismissed" and re-enter it or do it anyway without penalty, your fear and thus your motivation will be weakened. If Mark were personally policing your process, the fear might be more effective. ;-) With enough opportunities to breach the rules without consequence, you will start tinkering with DWM or looking for another approach altogether.

My advice is to seek out reward-based motivation. So attempt to complete or delete all tasks by their "due date" for a set period and then brag about it here. Or give yourself a reward for doing so each month. Or really knuckle down and make clearing all of your tasks before their due date a habit. Do it every day whether you want to or not until you can't stand NOT doing it. This is how I made flossing my teeth a habit. Fear that I would develop gum disease or be yelled at by my hygienist (I was repeatedly) didn't do it for me. I just made myself floss and now I HAVE to. A positive change made--no fear required.

"Fear can be a superbly motivating factor in life, and it undoubtedly serves as one of the principle engines of human advance.... Yet even at its best, fear cannot be affirmative, only defensive. There's a couple hundred pages more. Regarding motivation, essentially he's saying if carrots aren't motivating enough, find tastier food to work towards, and get rid of the sticks as soon as you can. As Frank Herbert wrote, "Fear is the mind killer."

The literature comes down on the side of rewards pretty conclusively. Here's one potential reason why imo. If you are doing something out of fear, you are mentally rehearsing what you do NOT want to do. IOW, "If I don't look at my DWM list, I could dismiss that super-important task and then x, y, and nasty z could happen." I see myself not doing what I want to be doing. If, on the other hand, I focus on rewards, I see myself doing the desired behavior and earning the reward. We know that our thoughts and mental imagery are powerful antecedents to behavior. Can't tell you how many times I've thought during a tennis match, "Oh no! I hope I don't double fault!" only to do just that.

I see a problem even in thinking that we are the kinds of people that are best motivated by fear. Underlying that thought is the belief that I am lazy, undisciplined, a procrastinator, and so forth. How does this negative self-labeling move me toward my goals?

Then again, some people actually enjoy being afraid. Check out the top ten Blockbuster rentals each week and at least half of them are horror flicks. Some people would rather procrastinate a task to make it more exciting: "Will I not get it done and finally get fired?" That kind of thing. To each his own. I'm done using fear as a motivator in my life. I have plenty of other people who will try and use it for me. ;-)
