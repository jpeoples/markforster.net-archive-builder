I think will has a point.

Moreover, interruptions can be (in most cases) limited a great deal. There is a universal truth about human nature that we feel the need to be needed. It gives a purpose to our existence and this is why it is sooo hard to say no or to undo behaviors that invite interruptions.

So I think the first step is to understand that we feel satisfaction at being interrupted even though it might be irritating. Then, it is possible to shift that sense of being needed from there to actual value in terms of effectiveness of work for example.

This discussion might be interesting but I suggest that we tackle the sources of interruption one by one because they have very little in common on the way to reduce them. If I may, start the ball on this, I'd choose emails.

I have done 2 things for my emails that have reduced the inflow dramatically (90%). But before you can apply it, you have to go over the feeling that it is not acceptable behavior and that you will loose people's respect with that. YOU are responsible for what other's expectation is of how you treat their email. Don't be ruled by other's expectation of you! If you consistently do it a way, they will expect that, only the change from now to the new way will unbalance a few.

First, I put an auto-responder that says that their email has been queued and will be answered just before 12:00pm or 5:00pm. If it is really urgent, they can find me at my phone. This gives people an out if it was really urgent and also makes them think twice before calling me. If they call and was not urgent, I tell them I will get to it when I review the emails. After that people EXPECT me to act like that so they will re-ajust their use of emails towards me.

The second thing is tied to the first. Since I do my emails towards 11:30am and 4:30pm, I have 30 minutes before lunch or I leave work so I don't linger on them. I make it brief and to the point. If the want a discussion, they can schedule a meeting. I also put this at the end of the email because it is invariably 5 lines or less even to my boss (including salutations and what not):

Q: Why is this email 5 sentences or less?
A: http://five.sentenc.es

That auto responder would probably get me fired, Erik. My email is managed by switching off any alert mechanisms and allowing the task to stand out.

All other types of interruptions are off book; I deal with the phone calls, colleagues or bosses that call on me, then write the commitments arising from those interruptions into the notebook.

If the action requires immediate attention, it goes into C2 of the same page. The task I was working on disappears to the C2 of the next active page. Sometimes, that creates unease, because the matter I was working on is urgent as well. If its really uncomfortable for me to see the current task go, I classify it as urgent and enter it into C2 of the same page.

But something's gotta give: if the interruption creates a major commitment which eats into my time, I've got to be realistic. Move the task I was working on to C2 to another page, and inform the person I am working with that the deliverable will face delays.

Everything can't be urgent if it is likely to occupy more that the finite time I have at work. I've gotta bump off one urgent task for another. That how I manage my C2 task build-up and the expectations of others.

BATCHING is a terrific tool for dealing with many kinds of interruptions.

Alan, you mentioned email. This is a "low hanging fruit" -- just keep "Clear email" as a recurring SF task and do it when it stands out.

And turn off those Outlook pop-up windows that tell you about every single new email!!

This makes you process your emails in a batch, which is much more efficient than processing them one-off.

...Which leads to a great principle: Batch things whenever it makes sense! Instead of going to your boss every time something comes up, save those things up and deal with them all at one go, in a daily or weekly meeting. And train your boss to save up his/her interruptions for that meeting, too.

And BATCHING is just an example of developing systems to handle your work. Mark discusses this in DIT at great length, cf. the two mechanics and their different methods of working.

Interruptions:

1) Phone calls;
a) if callerid blank - let voicemail answer
b) if callerd recognized - if current task stopp-able answer phone
c) if callerid reconnized - if current tasks not stopp-able - voicemail - add check voicemail to c2
d) if cell phone - let voicemail answer; add listen to cell voicemail to c1 - SMS a reply instead of returning the call if possible

2) Walk ups: (these apply when you are interrupted; not when you are not busy and can talk)
a) If snuck up from behind - take time stopping what you are doing; and write down "restart" notes on a piece of paper. Make it clear to the person that they are interrupting you with there actions.
b) If the interrupting person is there to engage in chitcat; answer all questions with 1 or 2 word answers. Attempt to turn back to work frequently until they get the hint.
c) if interrupting person say "can we", "do you have time", "I need you to"; stop them and tell them you are busy and schedule time to review what they need on the calendar. You'll find out quickly how often the interruption is truly unimportant. Many many people procrastinate by playing white-rabbit around the office.

3) Meeting requests in dysfunctional organizations: When you a meeting request from someone other than your boss; accept it as "tentative" and in the respond request the agenda so you can prepare for the meeting. Allows you to plan leaving the meeting early too if you aren't need for the whole thing. Make a habit if possible of not going to some percentage of meetings that don't have agendas...if they complain to your boss just point out that they never have agenda, can't stay on topic and tend to waste valuable time. It's hard to argue with that.
