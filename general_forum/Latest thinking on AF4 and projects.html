Fredrick,

Welcome.

We have had that type of discussion before and I think it depends on how you work. I'll give you my opinion, and expect to get differing ones ;-)

My way is to put project things in project "files" (paper, electronic, whatever). This keeps my thinking clear. I can see how step 6 relates to step 18, and all of that. Then I decide what can be done now, and in the near future and put THAT into my time management system.

AF is not a PROJECT management system, though for small projects it works well enough. For something larger I'd think about it as a real PROJECT and manage that the way you normally would (Pert/CPM, hand drawn bubbles, word documents, Post-it notes all over the walls ;-) When you have TASKS coming up, be they for a project or otherwise, then a TIME MANAGEMENT system is called for ... and AF is ideal for that.

It depends on when you expect to do them. If they are tasks which can be done now, then my advice is to put them on the list. I encourage being as far advanced with any project as it is possible to be at any given time.

However if for one reason or another, it is not desirable or feasible that a task should be done now, then put it into Outlook Tasks or similar reminder system to be brought forward either for action at a specified date or for further review.

If you are comfortable with paper, you can draw your own and do it pretty simply. But PERT/CPM systems really benefit from computerization if you need to get a handle or resources and scheduling. If not, then just draw a bubble on the extreme right (centered vertically) of a large sheet of paper and put your final outcome. Then to the immediate left put bubbles for the tasks/milestones/goals/projects that must be completed just before that and connect them to the final bubble. Do the same for every bubble until you get to those which have no predecessors (can be done right now) and start working. Easy peasy ... untll you want to find out how long things will take, then the calcualtions by hand are tedious ... but still possible. (I won't go into it here, but it is just a question of figuring out how long each task will take and adding durations. But it gets tricky when you consider hours in a day, work days in a week, and multiple people working on it for varying percentages of their days).

The granddaddy of them is MS Project, which is part of Office 2007 (and 2003, I guess) if you have the whole package. There are smaller and simpler ones you can get for free as well.

Here is an overview ... you can search for more.

I too run a consulting company with many projects (large and small) across several countries.

One of the great features of Autofocus it that it helps me make progress on many fronts at the same time - instead of just narrowing my focus on the urgent and 'important' stuff. Just to clarify...

We are talking about two different things using the same name:

- Project Management, in the classical sense is very specific and has well tested tools and procedures to handle the complexity. It is used for very large projects involving a cast of thousands and resources in the millions. Like big construction and building ships. (In fact, the father of this whole thing was the guy who managed the building of the first Nuclear Submarine Admiral Rickover.)

- We are also using the term "project management" to mean "managing projects" ... this is really a different animal, and one which mostly apples to us. This is most aptly described as keeping project notes, and developing action plans then putting them into a time management system to be acted upon.

It might just pay to keep those two ideas distinct. I don't need a "Project Management" system because nothing I do these days is that complex. I do need to "manage my projects", however. I do that in notebooks, Word Documents, and then move the actions into some variant of AutoFocus.

@Greg

Don’t get me going on project management tools! We’ve used so many over the decades and none provided me with what we needed.

Everywhere I go people are using MS Project – which is a fine tool I’m sure – but it forces one particular view or model of project management on the world. If you link the tasks like you are supposed to do and then something is a day late – it moves everything all over the place as it reschedules. And it does not understand the concept of the working week – people and especially teams, tackle things on a week by week basis. They like to start new things on Monday and finish up by a deadline on Friday. MS Project reschedules things for 14.15 on Wednesday just to make it fit the ‘rules’. And woe betide you if you want to add a 3rd resource to a team but don’t want the week-long package of work to get shrunk by 33.333%! I use MS Project only where I must to keep he corporate immune system at bay.

The tool we developed allows us allocate work to teams or resources week-by-week in a visual way by moving what we call ‘blobs’ around on a grid (to make a kind of Gantt chart). It then shows, for each resource/task combination the start and end dates, how many days per week and the cost of those days. And the weekly costs by various categories (which we can define at will).The Access database is used to capture the task allocations and transfer the data to whatever corporate accounting system or project management system my clients may have.

The tool is designed to allow non PMPs (or ‘non-pimps’ as we call them) to read, understand and modify the plan by simply moving the blobs around the grid.

It doesn’t do load balancing or even automatic rescheduling based on some PERT network – that only confuses people. Instead the non-pimps get to move their blobs around at will until (a) the plan looks like it will get all the work done, (b) none of the teams are over-extended at any point and (c) the costs are within the budget.

Over time we have added some bells and whistles – but never at the expense of its simplicity. Its all very colourful too – so people love to use snap-shots in their reports and presentations. If your boss doesn’t like what the plan says – you hand her the spreadsheet and say “here, you make it work out cheaper!” Sometimes they do.

Anyway - this is straying off topic for this site.

2009-09-11 10:15
MS Project and similar use critical path analysis. If you are building a house, and you will need roof tiles in four weeks, and roof tiles take three weeks to be delivered, you'd better order roof tiles in one week, or progress will be delayed.

But most people's work doesn't have dependencies such as that. As long as the tasks get done by the due date, they can be done in any order . This point is made strongly in DIT, where the order in which you do your tasks is any you like that gets the lists done in a day. Even in more structured environments, such as software development, the task dependencies are much fewer than one might imagine. As long as a programmer codes his bit of the application, it doesn't much matter what sequence he followed to get there.

It's not just that the features that are central to MS Project aren't useful. They aren't neutral, but negative, in that they get in the way of planning. The tool will schedule on Thursday in three weeks' time the task you know you will do tomorrow. You wind up fighting the software to get a sensible plan.

For this reason, I agree that MS Project is inappropriate for the great bulk of everyday planning. What is more suitable is a tool that provides a grid, for time along one dimension, people along another, and activities at the intersections; that allows text to be written into the grid squares; and allows the grid squares to be coloured to delineate different projects; and that allows the squares to be moved around to adjust the timetable. Excel fulfills this description well, but the table editor in Word, or almost any other grid-based tool including small post-it notes stuck on a large piece of flipchart paper, will do just as well.

The most useful tip I can offer in this simple-minded grid-based approach to planning is that although planning tools typically present time along the horizontal axis, it's much easier to fit the text into the squares if the timeline runs vertically down the page, and the names of the people run along the top.

I worked with a guy on a very large project, some years ago. He continually fought the CPM system we used. On talking with him I found that he was not using the tool correctly in the first place. He would try to tell the systme when HE wanted to do something rather than let the system schedule it for him.

The way to use the system is to define the tasks and the dependencies. Next you lay out resources if that pertains. But if you are a single person it may not. If you have told the system that tasks A, B, and C are predecessors of task D; that task D must be done in a week; that tasks A, B, and C each take 1 working day ... the system will show you that you can start on ANY of the three predecessor tasks just so you get all three of them done before D must be started.

I've never had the system tell me WHICH of the tree tasks I should be doing, it just shows me that all three are ready to be started and that all three must be done before I start D. The whole point of the system is to tell you which tasks you CAN be working on and which you can NOT be working on.

At least that has been my experience. I do completely agree that PERT/CPM systems are inappropriate for all but the most complex personal situation.
