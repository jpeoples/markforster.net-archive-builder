The only reason I stopped using SF is that I began following the "Dreams" process, in which a prime mandate is "I will do it when I feel like it and not before." (Of course, this must go hand-in-hand with the vision work to avoid Drifting.)

I had come to SFv3 fresh, with no prior AF experience, and I thought it was really excellent. Mark remarked in another thread that he had hoped SF would result in a vision effectively emerging, through the "sifting" process if I understood him correctly. And I felt he very nearly succeeded with that. I always felt like it was just missing some subtle ingredient to make it really perfect, and of course each variation promised to be It.

As for the C2 troubles, of course that was the central challenge. There was so much more potential, though, even in canonical SFv3 than many of us were using. Think of how every task was "finished" according to how you phrased it, or even according to any convention you might decide to adopt when reading it. I came to take "do a little bit of X" as my convention for anything written in C2, and this went a long way toward relieving the pressure. This can be a double-edged sword, true, but one could sharply change the dynamics just by changing these conventions, and there you have a whole host of variations inside the standard canonical version without any actual rule changes. In another thread, someone reported on writing "review recent pages for urgencies" when he got overwhelmed. The rules don't tell you to review recent pages, but they do tell you to do *whatever* you've written in C2. Potential, people! Untapped!!

But there is another edge to that sword: since SF is all based on how you word things, it is also thus based on your own concepts and limitations about tasks and management.

For instance, in the middle of my SF experience, I read Mark's DIT book, and I learned for the first time that there is such a thing as "one day's work." This was phenomenal! E-mail ceased to be a black hole. I found a simple folder structure that kept today's incoming mail separate from my active/pending items, separate from my backlog, and then this affected the meaning of "email" on my SF list. "Email" came to mean "do one day's worth of email today." What a concept!! It would start in C1, then live in C2 for a while, and then back to C1 until tomorrow. This had nothing to do with SF, yet it was an SF turbocharge. Had I never learned the DIT concept, I might still be saying "SF gets really bogged down by email ..."

SF is truly a brilliant invention, and perhaps v3 is like Edison's proverbial 9,999th light bulb filament, the penultimate variation. Even if I never return to that canonical system, the lessons I learned from using it will power anything else I come up with. Its principles (such as little & often, and closed lists, "chuck anything at it," the whole lot taught in DIT) are eternal. Any system that manifests these principles will be good, and the one that manifests them just right for you will be wonderful if not perfect.

In the "Dreams" world, I am gradually settling on something SF-like, very different in outward form but much the same underneath. Of course, I can move items all over at will ("when I feel like it"), but it keeps my place in the big things while offering up a stream of little things.

It is beginning to *feel* like SuperFocus, and that is a very good feeling. If only I can figure out how to merge it with my "dashboard," I'll have it made!

There seems to be a consensus that those who dropped SF, did so because of the compulsory nature of C2.

And those who keep using SF, are successful because they have modified the compulsory nature of C2, using it as a "fast track" as Marc from Brussels describes.

That's pretty much what I've been doing, too, but with a different form factor. At home, I'm using a series of accordion folders that sit on a shelf. Each folder is equivalent to a "page" in SF. I have a red "hot folder" that works like C2, and also serves as a "bookmark" for where I am on the shelf.

First I take down my Hot folder, and work on whatever needs attention (but not ALL of it, as SF would normally have me do).

Then I put the Hot folder back on the shelf, and take down the next folder. I work on whatever I want in that folder.

If I am not done with the item, then I have a couple choices. If the item is "hot", then I put it into my "Hot" folder so it gets "fast track attention". If the item is "normal", then I put it in the last accordion folder on my shelf.

Items that don't get worked on, stay in the folder.

When I'm done working on things in that folder, it goes back on the shelf in the same place where it was. If I worked on NOTHING in that folder, then I dismiss that folder by putting it in a box under my desk. I periodically purge that box.

Then I move on to the "Hot folder" again. And then the next folder on the shelf. Then the hot folder again. And so on, through all the folders on the shelf. Then I start again from the beginning.

When I have a new task, I write it on a scrap of paper and have two choices. If it's "hot", then it goes in my hot folder. Otherwise, it goes in the last accordion file on the shelf. (Just like SF.)

One thing I really like: I can put whatever I want in my folders -- not just written lists of tasks. For example, rather than writing "Journal" on a list, I just put my actual journal on the shelf.

Likewise, it's really easy to handle mail and other incoming paper. I scan through it. I put the hot items in my hot folder. The rest goes into the last folder on the shelf. It gets processed just like everything else.

Books can also just go on the shelf with everything else -- as long as I don't put too many on the shelf at once.

Also, it's easy to group things into "projects". I just add another folder on the shelf for any new projects that come up. The project folder gets processed just like any other folder. So, it gets frequent attention, and I also am prompted to look through all the project papers and details -- rather than just seeing "Project X" on a list, which would never stand out for me.

When I go to work, I take my hot folder with me in my backpack, and maybe one or two project folders. At lunchtime I can go through them and deal with anything like phone calls or whatever that need attention. When I get back home, the folders go back in their place on the shelf.

I carry around some notecards in my pocket, and jot down new items as they come up. When I get home, I put them in the folder.

One nice thing is that I can visually SEE how much work I have. If the shelf starts getting full, it's time to get serious about dismissal and/or pruning.

It's nice having my journal on the shelf. This is where I like to think over things and write out my ideas and goals, as well as retrospective and self-corrective kinds of pondering.

I've been doing this about two weeks now and am really enjoying it. The one gap is really feeling happy with the way my personal email is worked into this.

At work, almost everything is electronic, so I set up something very similar in OneNote - borrowing many ideas from Alan.


Anyway, back to the main point -- this is very much like SF but without the compulsory nature of C2. It's even more tactile than a written list.

A simple approach to a detached C2 in a paper based system that keeps C2 'in your face' is to do one or both of the following:

1) Cut several pages (say 5-10) in half (or for more main working space, cut 40 off) vertically. This is your c1, and then keep 2 or 3 pages after as full pages of which only the right column c2 will be visible at all times. Now you have a detached c2. Can repeat this sequence for rest of notebook.

2) Fold instead of cutting as above. (This works, but in testing, cutting works better)

and for maintenance, whenever need to dismiss or move past a c2, then just fold it over.

Can vary the number of pages set for c1 and c2 every batch of pages to have enough to move through.

Just another thought of how to use standard paper based notebook form with another dimension.

If you use a loose-leaf notebook, and use "project pages" interspersed with your ordinary SF pages, you can get the same effect.

Basically, if you want a page to get attention sooner than it would in the usual sequence, just take it out of the book and reinsert it a few pages forward.

This gives you total control over the rhythm and frequency of exposure to each project intermixed with ordinary SF pages of random tasks.

Alan - I agree with you, personally the loose-leaf notebook form factor never worked for me, either. It's too clunky to move things around. And it's too bulky to carry around conveniently.

The accordion files work great for me for personal stuff, and OneNote works great at work, both following essentially the same process.

With OneNote, I thought I'd lose some key dynamic unless I counted an exact number of items to go into each OneNote section -- just like in SF, you have a specific, set number of lines per page. But this time around, I have abandoned that. Instead, I gather all new items into my "Unfiled Notes", which links to a section in my "SF" notebook. Every time I cycle through the sections in the SF notebook, I process the Unfiled stuff. Some of it gets actioned immediately, some of it gets moved to my Hot folder, some of it is deleted. If anything is left, I create a new section, put today's date on it, and put all the leftover new stuff into that section. Sometimes it's 3 items, sometimes its 50 items. It doesn't seem to really matter. What *does* matter is that it's now a "closed list" and it gets repeated attention every time I cycle through the list.
