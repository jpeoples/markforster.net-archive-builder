I'm finding that Column 2 abuse is NOT self-correcting, but rather self-perpetuating. What are you all finding?

For example, if I put just a few too many urgent items in C2, then I get stuck on that page. And I get stuck on the next page, too. It starts to take nearly all day to get through a page or two. Too slow.

Meanwhile, lots of new items are piling up at the end of the list -- getting further and further away. I start to become afraid that it will take me too long to get to them, since I am stuck on my current page, somewhere in the middle of the notebook. So, I start entering one or two of them into my current C2, as "urgent" items (using Mark's definition of urgent = "you want to get to it before you would through the normal processing of the list").

Of course that just slows everything down more, since I have even more C2 items I must action at each turn of the page.

Eventually this just crashes.

Personally, I don't find "system crash" to equate to "self-correcting". The system crashing and forcing me to start over from scratch doesn't really help. I know, because when I restart the system, I eventually fall into the same pattern. And the system crashes again!

I am trying to see if I can come up with a way to make this actually self-correcting -- meaning, if you start to get stuck somewhere, and your C2 starts to fill up, the system will automatically react and somehow start to force you along more quickly. Maybe through triggering more dismissals. Maybe through forcing you to turn pages more quickly somehow. But I want this to be RULE-BASED, not "common-sense" based. I want the SYSTEM to teach me how to stop crashing it, just by following the rules of the system.

This is why I am trying Mark's experiment for myself, of entering urgent items on C2 of the NEXT page. Mark said that would make things move more quickly. Maybe that's what a person should do automatically, whenever you find it takes more than a day to turn the pages through your whole list.

None of this is intended as criticism of Mark - not by any means - I have the deepest respect for him, and am so thankful for all the work he's done. I just want to see if I can make this system work for me more effectively, as a SYSTEM, not with frequent "common sense overrides" (since I don't have much of that, when I am buried with crises), and not with piles of idiosyncratic "tweaks" but with one or two very simple "adjustments" that follow the spirit of the original rules.

Alan wrote:
<< I've observed the cumulative effect of C2 myself. It does self correct only in that bad usage yields bad results and you learn from that. >>

That's not self-correction. It's "user" correction. In other words, the system clogs up if there are too many urgent tasks and/or unfinished tasks, so the user must change behavior to accommodate.


<< My suggested remedy is to drop most of the unfinished tasks and resume with a workable load. >>

Yes, that can work, but the problem is, the rules don't allow for that. You must invoke the "common sense rule" to operate that way. That's exactly what I'm trying to avoid. I want the rules themselves to guide me to successful use of the system.

Maybe it's an unattainable goal but still, that's what I'm looking for. :-)


<< Second, knowing the required behaviour, set your mind to operate accordingly, and the problem won't arise. >>

The problem is, this goes against three of the fundamental rules of the system:

+ "enter whatever you want into the list"

+ "work on whatever feels ready to be done"

+ "work as long as you like"


In addition to all these rules, you have to follow a whole bunch more in order to avoid C2 problems:

+ Don't enter too many urgent tasks - or handle them off-list (what if you REALLY DO have lots of urgent tasks? what if it's too complicated to handle them off-list, or you are forced to create a secondary system to support them?)

+ Don't take on any commitments that you aren't ready to drive to completion (this goes totally contrary to the "take action on whatever stands out" and "work on it as long as you like" rules)

+ Make sure you craft your tasks either as open-ended tasks that can't be finished so will always get re-entered in C1 ---- or small discrete tasks easily accomplished so they are finished in one go, or stay for a small time in C2 ---- or maybe a few largish tasks ("War and Peace") that will stay in C2 for a long time till they are done. (These are great principles, and "little and often" tends to produce these kinds of tasks anyway, but it requires much more rigor than the fundamental rule, "put whatever you want on the list")


I'm looking for something much simpler, like "if the list is bogging down for you, try entering your urgent tasks on the NEXT page, since this tends to make the system go faster".

Or "try reducing the number of lines in C1 relative to C2, since this will tend to reduce the time you spend cycling through the tasks on any one page".

These are very simple mechanical rules that can make the system move faster and reduce C2 congestion, without the need for frequent invocation of "common sense" rules or introducing new rules that go contrary to the fundamental "in the zone" concepts of Autofocus.

Don wrote:
<< I've noticed a kind of oscillation of C2 as you've pointed out, but I don't know what you mean by "system crash". >>

I mean that the system moves so slowly that you can only get through a page or two each day, meanwhile adding 2 or 3 pages to the end of the list each day.

Or, column 2 gets so filled up everywhere that you frequently enter unfinished tasks not on the next page, but 2 or 3 or 4 pages forward.

The system just doesn't work well when these things start to happen. And the system doesn't recover by itself from these problems. The user is forced to invoke "common sense" rules to get out of the problem (e.g., decommissioning lots of C2 unfinished items by moving them to end-of-list C1) or to simply start over.


<< When I got stuck on a page, I was still doing a lot of important work. I ended up finishing or actioning everything on the page before moving on. >>

Yes, I find that happens, too. The longer you stay on a page, the more often you cycle through each task -- and each exposure to a task reduces your resistance to it. So they all tend to stand out after awhile.

I often find myself completing 80%+ of the C1 items on a page, the very first time I arrive on that page. Adding new urgent things on C2 of the current page makes this worse, since I cycle through the whole page a lot more while I'm working those C2 items, and that cycling makes a lot more of the C1 tasks "stand out".

But that's really not how the cycling is supposed to happen. The repeated exposure that breaks down resistance is supposed to happen by means of cycling through the *entire list* many times, clearing a much smaller percentage each time you work through a page. This keeps the whole list fresh, and prevents you from bogging down in the middle, and being forced to endlessly chase the end of the list.

I still get lots done even when moving slowly -- much more than with AF or even DWM. But it just gets slower and slower every day.

Marcus wrote:
<<< But unless you enter everything with such proviso, you have to think about whether you want a thing explored or done *before* you enter it. You have to pre-plan your entries. >>>

I suppose this depends on what your "default meaning" is for vaguely-written tasks.

My own default is "this task means explore, develop, percolate unless otherwise designated".

Thus if I write "Project XXX", it means "think about Project XXX, explore Project XXX, do a little work on Project XXX".

Thus it would always be re-entered in Column 1 on last page.

And, I can re-enter it in a new form if I want:

+ "Ask NNN about Project XXX" (which is written more specifically and concretely and would normally be a C2 task if left unfinished)

+ "How does Project YYY relate to Project XXX?" (which is another open-ended, re-enter on C1 last page kind of task)


If I want to make sure it gets the C2 treatment, I can write it differently: "Finish Project XXX". This one would end up in C2 if not finished in one go. And this is where I need to be careful how I write things.

I do this with my email. Sometimes if I just want to do a quick check, I'll write "Email". If I want to give it the full treatment, I write "Clear email".


If you want, your "default" treatment can be "this is always a C2-type task unless otherwise designated". So, if you write "email", it would mean "clear email". If you wanted it to be looser, you could write "check some email".


I guess the Action Item for everyone doing SF is to decide: "What is my default task treatment?"
