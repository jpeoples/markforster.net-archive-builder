Ranier:

Might want to google hermeneutics and look at the wiki.

In short for now: it was an approach developed to read texts, specifically the Bible in the Christian tradition. Certainly hermeneutics had been used prior (but not really developed explicitly), but with Augustine begins the explicit discussion of how one approaches a text, again specifically the Christian Scriptures.

Augustine believed (in short) that in order to understand a text (again the Christian scriptures) two primary precepts, stands, modes of being, etc. had to be understood or taken up:

Love of God.
Love of one's fellow man.

Implied in his writing is also love of the writings themselves. In light or within the horizon of such love, only then can only fully engage in interpretation of Scripture, especially the difficult parts, which many would like to pass over or quickly explain away. (Jesus calling people dogs.) For Augustine, these "dark passages" is where understanding and relation with the text, God, and one's fellow man truly begins.

Hermeneutics over time became more and more explicitly developed especially in the 18th and 19th centuries expanding the method to texts outside the Christian Scriptures. Hermeneutics as such began to be looked at and attempted to be understood in a hermeneutic manner.

In the 20th century, a radical shift was made in hermeneutics. Heidegger applied this understanding to understanding of everyday life. To "man" how related to the world.

This insight would shake philosophy and all the social science, especially on the Continent and in the Anglo-American world (except in Philosophy.)

A so-called hermeneutics of suspicion has been described beginning in the late 19th century and early 20th century. It often suggested as a departure of a hermeneutics of charity. In other words, rather than love being the fundamental relationship of one to a text or another person or the world, the understanding of that relationship should be first put under suspicion.

The great triad of hermeneuts (practioners of hermeneutics as such) of suspicion are commonly held to be: Nietzsche, Freud, and Marx.

This is an extreme misunderstanding, for these men first and foremost loved those texts they looked at and those people they attempted to understand and the world around them. It might be hard to see at first, but within a hermeneutics of charity it is clear these men were lovers of what they wished to understand.

In fact, I doubt that any struggle to understand anything cannot be marked primarily by love, even if that love is not made explicit.

The basic tenet of hermeneutics (we are talking in the most simple of terms) is that when you approach a text, a person, or the world, you already have an understanding of it. That understanding might something like: "this is a book" or just "this is something." Nevertheless within that understanding you already bringing a lot to the table and as you read or relate further to that which you are trying to understand, that further understanding informs your prior understanding, And that new insight to your first encounter informs the next. Thus the hermeneutic circle.

This is most easily seen in texts. You read the first sentence of Proust. You have some idea what to expect before even beginning to read him. Each sentence informs your initial understanding and every prior sentence which then serves to inform your understanding of the current sentence and the next.

Thus understanding has a circular or spiraling nature. It is not one of a tabula rasa where you just learn new stuff. Or a tinker toy analogy where you just are adding knowledge piece by piece in some network of understanding governed by rules which can be made explicit (cognitive science).

This might all seem basic, but there is plenty of nuance to it. Especially when it comes to understanding everyday life.

Something like the word "prejudice" becomes a vice in our era, but without a pre-judgement no matter how clear that pre-judgement is to us, is necessary to understand anything. So pre-judgement is not a "bad" thing or barrier to understanding, but rather the very condition of understanding. As we engage what our pre-judgement has allowed us to understand in some manner, hermeneutics can then help us understand those latent pre-judgements and how they help or hinder of our understanding. But alway keeping in mind that these pre-judgements can never be made fully explicit, for no knowledge can ever be made completely explicit.

This is not bad news, but the good. For a good interpreter is always ready to be surprised, to be wrong, because he necessarily is wrong in the sense he can never understand something completely. The fullness of understanding can never be had. This allows a certain distance between the interpreter and the interpreted. With the right balance, with love, a reciprocal relationship can develop and respect be developed.

For if we are primarily understanding beings, what are we without that which we attempt to understand? To fully understand something, would be self-destruction and destruction of the understood, whether a text or another person or the world. To engage in the hubris of believing on can fully understand or a rejection of our own relation to world as understanding entities is too impoverish ourselves and the world around us.

In short.
