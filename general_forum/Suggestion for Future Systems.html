Hi TK

I am surprised you find "little and often" a weakness of AF.

Firstly, it is only an aspect of the more important principle: "Work on an item for as long as you feel like doing so". This more important principle would surely help you to overcome any shortcoming you see in "little and often".

Secondly I am surprised by the tasks you regard as "monumental" (filing, paying bills and organizing a bookcase). However, I guess your point is that if you do not do them often enough, they might become monumental.

Thirdly, I find myself resisting your suggestion that large projects and learning do not respond well to "little and often". I can agree that some projects might be best handled by starting them and not letting up for anything until they are finished, but I feel that most projects benefit from: an early start, persistent attention and timely conclusion well ahead of deadline. As for learning, there seems to be an even stronger case for spaced practice of what is to be learned (recall Mark's often repeated examples of learning a language or learning a musical instrument).

Finally, if one finds a problem with "little and often", the solution is simple: "Work on a task as often and as long as it seems to you to be likely to produce the best results."
Do you think that formulation would overcome the problems you have with "little and often?"

Hi TK,

I agree with Learning on this one. Block time for some things. Learning is a good example of something that you can use both approahes for. Take learning a language for example. It usually breaks down into things that take longer sessions and things that can be done little and often. When you are learning the rules of grammer, for example conjugating a family of verbs ... it is probably best to study the rules for a block of time and work it thorugh without distraction. But then when you are learning vocabularly, you can benefit from setting short timers during the day and working for 10-30 minutes bursts.

The large blocks also benefit from short review periods that you can squeeze in anywhere and do in short bursts.

The same approach could be taken with learning most things. Spend a block of two hours or so reading the material and learning the principles, then use short bursts for review, memorizing key formulas, dates, vocabulary or general review.

As to other things you suggest (like paying bills or filing) I find they yield best to routine. Pay bills on Saturday morning every week. File papers on Tuesday while your favorite TV show is on. That kind of thing. (Or, with filing, you could just file every paper when you are done with it ;-)

Well, I'm learning German, Spanish and Portuguese at the moment and I find the 'little and often' approach works beautifully. Remember that 'little' is a relative term here. It doesn't mean never work for more than 5 or 10 minutes at a stretch on anything. It means that regular work on a project is more effective than one huge burst of activity (usually at the last minute).

Regarding "working for as long as you feel like it", if you are so demotivated at learning German that you would never feel like working at it for more than 10 minutes then frankly you'd be better off doing something else.

The trouble with time blocking is that "Work for 2 hours on French" may be fine some days, but on others nothing will get you to start. Once you fail to do your stint one day, then it becomes more likely that you will fail the next and so on.

There is much less resistance attached to "Work on French for as long as I feel like it".

2009-05-28 8:59
Pimlseur is great. After getting the basics down of their method, you can apply them to exercises on your own.

Watching TV, movies, etc. in the language you are learning is great with subtitles or not. Understanding is not the point but becoming more familiar with sounds and structure. You will soak things up.

Also, with internet streaming you can extend this practice to listening to radio programs in the language you are trying to learn. DW does a remarkable job at this.

Finding a group of people who meet up to speak the language you are learning is a great way to improve your skills as well. There are usually native as well second language speakers. It is a great way to get over embarrassment of speaking the language.

For pronunciation of the language (my greatest difficulty), it has been shown that attempting to use the foreign pronunciation in your own tongue can help develop pronunciation skills when you don't have the opportunity to speak the target language.

Read, read, read. It builds up passive vocabulary and develops mastery of more complicated grammatical structures. Don't pick up that dictionary for every word you don't know or that grammar for every confusing syntactical structure. Just read for as much understanding as you can get out of it.

FWIW.

Let me add a caveat. Don't speak your native language ever. As English speakers, German speakers I found were eager to practice their rather impeccable English with me. I refused to answer a single question in English and eventually they would indulge my desire to speak only in German.

Norman:

The backwards method is mainly used in the early lessons. And pronunciation is about a whole lot more than just being able to say words correctly. It is excellent at teaching you the rhythm and flow of the language, and how to respond quickly too.

It's quite possible to have an enormous vocabulary and still have difficulty conversing. Pimsleur aims to teach you the most common conversational words, so that you have the basic structure with which to expand your knowledge.
