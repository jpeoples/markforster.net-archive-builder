Robert Fritz is most noted as an author on creativity and developing creativity in individuals and organizations. While reading his book Creating, interestingly there is a section on time management. It struck a chord with me as he shares some of my thoughts and the thoughts of others here on time management like keeping it simple

Basically, he states he “hates” time management and many time management systems. He shares my thought that many time management systems take too much time because they require too many forms, lists and other paraphernalia. He recommends a simple system which is to use a single sheet of paper which is turned sideways and has 6 columns. They are labeled within 1 day through within 5days and within 2 weeks. Everything you want to do is put on the form in one of these columns. Each item is given a priority of A - D. A= Imperative, B = Important, C = truly desired and D = mildly desired. Anything other than A, B or C is immediately crossed off. You then cross off the items as they are done, which can be anytime before the due date in the column. He urges you to ad new items as soon as you know about them. Fritz thinks organizing dimensionally rather than linearly is more efficient. He note the key points as:

1. Get the steps and actions out of your mind (sounds like a tenet of GTD).
2. Think dimensionally, i.e. within 2 days rather than things to do on Tuesday.
3. Keep it simple, do not spend too much time on forms.
4. Cross of items as soon as they are done.
5. Cross of items below a C priority.

He also espouses quickly writing things down to get them out of your head so they do not block creativity. Also items not done in a week are carried over into the next week and then eliminated if they are not done in that next time period. What is not really discussed is how often you do the form and what happens to something you write within one and then fail to accomplish it. I am going to try to email the author and see if he will answer a few questions on this simple method.

2010-04-21 6:58
Choice is only a problem if you don't make it at the right place/time. With the standard "To Do" list you make the choice when you chose what to put on the list in the first place. With the "standing out" lists (AF/DWM) you make the choice when it is time to act ... a point which causes the most trouble, I find. That is when things get underfoot. If you have too many things from which to choose, you can become paralyzed when it is time to DO. I found it helpful to start the day scanning the AF list to cull those things that I CHOSE to do that day. Then I would work from that subset list and find no problems knowing what to do ... they were all there written down in front of me. I just had to start at the top and work down. Falling off a log.

This is also illustrated by the sales example. If you have not chosen what you want BEFORE you walk into the store, you have a problem deciding and are subject to the sales pressures of sales people. If you decide in advance that you want a blue sweater, you don't have that problem. You can still retain flexibility and pick the red one because the shade of blue on the shelf is not what you had in mind and the red looks better. But since you went in with a goal (by a blue sweater) you are only dealing with one reference point ... other things are compared to that and not with each other. IOW, you are not LOOKING for red or yellow, only blue and the other colors only come into the field of view in terms of are they better than your first choice ... blue.

A theory of the German psychologist Julius Kuhl attempts to separate people into two groups: state-oriented individuals who focus under stress on past, present or future states, rather than options available for action; and action-oriented individuals who focus under stress on action alternatives.

There seems to be a personality dimension of state versus action orientation: State-oriented persons on the preoccupation dimension, are those who report having persistent and uncontrollable negative emotional states after being exposed to aversive events. They tend to focus their attention on a past, present, or future state instead of focusing it on the current task. Action-oriented persons, in contrast, have a better ability to “down-regulate” the negative affect elicited by an aversive experience.

State orientation differs from the constructs of neuroticism and anxiety with regard to the way negative affect is managed: State oriented individuals are not postulated to have an increased sensitivity to negative affect or to punishment as neurotic or anxious individuals do. Instead, they have a decreased self-regulatory capacity to reduce negative affect in a self-initiated “top–down” manner once it is aroused.

If this all makes any sense, then I would draw the following conclusions regarding the impacts of writing and reading to-do lists to a person’s work or life:

For an action orientated person writing and reading to-do lists would lead to a greater awareness of choices and opportunities, increased efficiency and faster accomplishment.

For a state orientated person writing and reading to-do lists would lead to more distraction, a decrease in the ability of handling the amount of information, decreased efficiency, and a state of “feeling stalled”.
