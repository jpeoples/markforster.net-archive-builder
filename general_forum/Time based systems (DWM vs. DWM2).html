Hi Nuntym,

I'm a storyteller. If I want to rehearse four times this week, I write:
> Rehearse 1
> Rehearse 2
> Rehearse 3
> Rehearse 4

(I'd probably replace many of the "Rehearse" with a squiggle to mean "same word as above.")

If it's Monday and I see those tasks, I might skip it. If it's Thursday and I still haven't done Rehearse 1, I know I have to catch up.

Or I'd write:
> Rehearse 1,2,3,4
And cross off each number rather as I do it. This way it doesn't stand out as much on the page and tends to get buried.

If I finish all 4 rehearsals, then I'm done for the week and copy it onto the next open page with a note that the group of tasks doesn't become active until Monday. I might do it again before Monday, but that would exceed my goal of 4 and I should use that time on my other goals.

If I don't finish them all, then I still enter the next batch on the next open page. I then choose what to do about the undone rehearsals.

If I really need to get in those rehearsals, I might enter 4 for this week and leave the old ones on the list, so I see it twice on my way around. Or I might enter 5 times this week rather than 4, to make up for last week, and mark the old undone ones as "will never do". Or I might admit I'll never catch up, enter 4 for this week and delete the ones I didn't do last week.

If it's a task with discrete steps but I know I'll leave it too late and try to do too many steps in one sitting I spell them out, one after the other. I also indent all but the project title. When I see the indented block, I know there's a chain that has to be done in order and I have to start work early.

> Story for November 15
>> check theme and length
>> pick some candidates and sleep on them
>> choose story
>> read favourite and check the length. (I like to fix it before learning it.)
>> make outline
>> learn first half
>> learn second half
>> rehearse 4 times (treat this line as above)

I might also add target dates to each step.

This reminds me that I need to do several sessions. If I manage more than one step per session, great!

I like to clean one room a day, but it's usually half that speed. I list all of them up front and cross them off as I do them to keep track.

bathroom,
kitchen,
front hall,
parents',
kid1,
kid2,
bathroom,
kitchen,
front hall,
dining,
living,
laundry

Bathroom, kitchen and front hall need more frequent work so they're entered twice. When I finish the cycle, I rewrite it on the next open page. I know if I'm on track from the date I started this cycle and how many rooms are done and if I'm keeping up.

This works better than writing the rooms on the calendar. It always slips, and I'm stuck erasing and rewriting or working on an old date and trying to catch up. Also, sometimes the rooms are done out of order, such as when a kid can't find something. It's done for that cycle so I mark it done.

The human mind is so weird sometimes...right after commenting on Mark's new "Super AF1" or (I guess) SAF1 on how similar it is to Alan's system, I remembered Alan's joke about "HDW" to make a system more focused on finishing than DWM. I then remembered Ilse's post above about the different task markers one can use, and it clicked in my mind...DWM, when it comes to dismissing items, is actually just "WM": you dismiss items at the 7th day mark and the 30th day mark. So what if we make a TRUE DWM, wherein the system dismisses all at the 1st, 7th, and 30th day marks?

And heck, it is easy: you can do it both in DWM1 and DWM2 (easier, I think, in DWM2 and electronic DWM1, as the today page in DWM1 is often full). So you follow all rules of DWM1 or DWM2, except

1) When you are going to do a task that is not in the last day page/section, cross it out and rewrite at the today page (for DWM1) or at the end of the list with a heavy dot (●) at the beginning (for DWM2; if it is at the last section then just mark a heavy dot (●) at the beginning). (NOTE: I think you can also do this for urgent items you want to do today, like you are planning to do it today, a few moments from now or right after what you are doing now, but I am not sure how it will affect the system).
2) When are not finished with the item but have to do something else, do not cross it out but leave it there; you can go back to it anytime.
3) When you are finished with the item for today but is either recurring or have follow-up tasks that you can do some other day, then cross it out and write it the 7-days-left page (for DWM1) or put a mark before it (in my case, an open circle, o, for DWM2), to denote a "re-entered" item to be crossed out after 7 days
4) All items with the heavy dot (●) are crossed-out the next day.

So you now have 3 entry points: Today for "To be done, or D, tasks", 7 days from now for "Re-entered, or W tasks," and 30 days from now for "New, or M tasks."

Now what are the theoretical advantages for this?

1) It gives increased focus on finishing.
2) It gives you a visual representation of how many items are "in the fire" right now.
3) It forces you to have a realistic picture of what you are planning to do today.
4) It gives a visual representation of what you have accomplished today.

There are, of course, some disadvantages to this scheme, most importantly the overhead of re-writing and possible anxiety on seeing how many you have to do today.

Besides, I have not tested this, just thought of it and wanted to put it here. However I am going to test this now, it looks very promising...at least theoretically ^___^
