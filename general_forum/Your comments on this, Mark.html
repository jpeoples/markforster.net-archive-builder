David:

It's an interesting article but Dan doesn't really give quite enough information to be able to judge how his method would actually work. As far as I can tell he is advocating some form of full scheduling. In other words you don't just write:

Email
Voicemail
Write Report
Lunch

You write:

0900 Email
0930 Voicemail
0940 Write report
1230 Lunch

There's nothing basicallywrong with this method of scheduling, as long as one remembers that one always runs behind schedule, and leaves plenty of time for interruptions. However many people's experience is that they start getting further and further behind and end up spending more time revising the schedule than doing the work.

Bear in mind that the Task Diary in DIT is actually a calendar and therefore is achieving much of what he is talking about without scheduling exact times.

2008-10-20 11:02
Alex:

What do I think of that system?

I have to confess that I have never seen any need for any of these complications.

With DIT there is no need to plan your day, no need to prioritise, no need to time anything. You just have a list of stuff to do and get on with doing it. If you have meetings and interruptions, then you just carry on with the list once the meeting or interruption is over.

DIT also allows you to make sure that your work is in balance, but insisting that one day's incoming work must equal one day's outgoing work on average.

So why all the complication?

I just write down everything that comes in during the day for tomorrow and then do it tomorrow.

If it really has to be done today, then I do it today.

If I don't get through the list tomorrow then I carry it forward, but with the proviso that I must catch up within 4 days or so.

If I can't catch up then I must look at why I can't and do something about it.

What's so difficult about that?

2008-10-20 14:05
I tend to agree with Mark. Keep it simple!

When I was working in an office environment when answering the phone and doing others' bidding was the norm, I had a simple system based around a single reporter's notebook. I would write a 'to do' list and would just keep it running - crossing out tasks as they were completed. Sometimes, to stop having to look back too many pages, I would rewrite a task later on to keep tasks bunched together over fewer pages. This meant I could work off my to do list, be interrupted and then just come back to where I'd left off. Some days I got interrupted a lot, others hardly at all. I would review things now and again (depending on how much had built up) and if some tasks were continually being left undone I would either do them, try to persuade someone else to do them (delegate/bribe), or delete them if by neglect they had become obsolete. It's amazing how even in a customer support situation like I was in, you can avoid the need to do some tasks altogether. Obviously I was careful only to do this with tasks that seemed unnecessary in the first place - if I was chased on a particular task then it would get done sooner.

With DIT then, a similar system would work but using the task diary as the default list. I would work from the list of tasks in the task diary, get interrupted, then continue on my list of tasks for that day. All the other guidelines of DIT (checking incoming work for its level of urgency, etc. and entering reminders for the future for delegated tasks, etc.) would still apply. I think this is where figuring out the basic premises of a time management system then adapting it for your own environment is so useful. No time management system is bespoke and, like a software package I used to do the support for, each person has to tweak the system to make it work for them. But, providing you still follow the basic principles then the philosophy behind the system should remain pretty much intact - which is presumably what appealed in the first place.

Reply by Mark Forster

2008-10-20 15:52
Hannah:

In DIT "same day" and "immediate" are not the same thing. "Immediate" is defined as being so urgent that you drop everything else in order to deal with it.

The example you give of answering the telephone is an example of an "immediate" action. You wouldn't normally write an immediate action down in your Task Diary because you would be too busy doing it.

However any action coming out of the phone call would not be "immediate" (unless it was an emergency). It would either be "same day or "everything else". In that case you would write it down and be forced to chose between writing it on today's page or tomorrow's).

2008-10-24 16:07
Mark,

I'm the author of the original post "Why a to-do list doesn't work" (from the Evolving Excellence blog), and have very much enjoyed following this discussion. Your concept of the closed list is intriguing: I like the simplicity of the system and the way it doesn't require the user to constantly shift around the inevitable broken appointments for his own work. There's real elegance in that simplicity.

As you suggest in your comments, I do advocate leaving plenty of time for interruptions: scheduling one's day completely full is a recipe for certain failure and frustration.

That said, I think there are three real benefits from scheduling time in the calendar. First, blocking out the time in the calendar prevents other people from scheduling you for other activities. (Nature abhors a vacuum -- and an empty calendar.) Second, using the calendar forces you to really consider how long an activity will take. Finally, just as making a commitment to doing something on a specific *day* helps ensure that it will get done, choosing a specific *time* is similarly powerful in promoting action.

Thank you for the thought-provoking ideas. I look forward to following your blog in the future.
