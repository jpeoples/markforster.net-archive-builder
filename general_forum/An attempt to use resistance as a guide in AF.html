After reading Alan's latest comment in Mark Forster's blog post "Using resistance as a guide,"

http://www.markforster.net/blog/2010/9/30/using-resistance-as-a-guide.html#comment10135934

I speculated whether I can use the "resistance principle" in AF. Sounds kinda crazy, right? I mean, all versions of AF rests on the principle of tasks "standing out" when they are ready to be done. And of course (if you had the same experience as me) most of the items that "stand out" in AF are the easy ones.

I then remembered this thread that I read AND even participated a few months ago,

http://www.markforster.net/forum/post/1173500

about interrogative self-talk. Interrogative self-talk, or asking yourself a question or questions before working like, "Will I do this?", was found by a research study to be a more effective way of motivating oneself for work than declarative self talk, or the telling yourself something before you work like "I can do this!"

I then thought, what if I made an algorithm of questions that I can use everytime I use my AF? Would that bring out the "resistance principle?" Well, the best that I got is a "want/need" algorithm. I think it is close enough. Anyways, here is the algorithm and steps that I came up with, which I have been using for the past week or so. Please note that I use a filtering/prefiltering tweak of the tasks I need to do this week (step 4):


1. Go through your AF per the rules of your list until an item stands out.

2. Looking at this item, ask yourself, "Do I want to do this?" As you ask this, take note of your thoughts and feelings.

3. Next, ask yourself, "Do I need to do this?" Again take note of your thoughts and feelings. If you can either honestly say YES to this specific question (not the one at step #2) or your feelings are at ease, then go to step #5. If you can either honestly answer NO to this specific question (not the one at step #2) or your feelings become troubled, go to the next step.

4. Ask yourself, "Do I need to do this this week?" Again take note of your thoughts and feelings. If you can either honestly say YES to this or your feelings are at ease, then leave the task alone and go back to step 1. If you can either honestly answer NO to this or your feelings become troubled, dismiss it, then go back to step 1.

5. Ask yourself, "How can I do this conclusively, concisely, and carefully?" Then using the answers that you can think of from this question, do the task until you feel you have done what you should do, then go back to step 1.


Now I will explain the rationale for each step.

a. I found that feelings are VERY important in this. If you cannot really say with your mind what your answers to the questions are, then go with your heart. I found that troubled feelings mean you disagree with it; peaceful feelings mean you agree with it.

b. In my experience, the question "Do I want to do this?" is not directly important in task selection, but rather ENHANCES the answering of the question "Do I need to do this?" You see, when I ask myself "Do I need to do this?" sometimes (well...actually most of the time) I somehow trick myself to mean that question to be "Do I WANT to do this?" However, when I answer FIRST the question "Do I WANT to do this?" THEN answer the question "Do I NEED to do this?" I have no more excuse to change "need" to "what". And let me tell you: sometimes those answers can be VERY surprising.

c. Of course if the answer to the question "Do I need to do it?" is NO then I will not do it now, but if the task was "standing out" then there should be a reason why it stood out from the rest. Most commonly it is because I want to do it but I don't need to do it now, yet in the near future I might (or not). That is the rationale for the question in step 3: "Do I need to do this this week?" As I said, I am using the filtering/pre-filtering tweak by Mike that I summarized here,

http://www.markforster.net/forum/post/1262814

Therefore, this question puts the said tweak in action. If I do not need to do it this week, I can dismiss it and review at a later time whether I can/need to do it next week. Otherwise, it stays.

d. Finally, if the answer to the question "Do I need to do it?" is YES, then I should start on it in the best way that I can manage. I found that the question "How can I do this conclusively, concisely, and carefully?" prepares my mind into focusing into what are important in doing something (as eloquently said by Erik): effectiveness, efficiency, and (as I painfully found out) care.


The results have been very encouraging. The algorithm in my experience actually ENHANCES the "standing out" of tasks, and at the end of the day, I always have the feeling that I did what were the most important things to do. It is also very humbling: it makes me realize how much my wants and needs can sometimes be VERY different.

I encourage everyone to give this algorithm a try. I hope it will be a blessing to you, and let's see if we can enhance it further.

God bless.

To expand a bit on my last comment, "Resistance" works on the principle that you go for the things you are resisting most first, while the various forms of Autofocus work on the principle that you get the easy stuff done and then you are gradually presented with more difficult stuff.

If you applied "Resistance" to AF1 for example you would go to a page, do the thing you were resisting most. But then, since everything on the page would then presumably be easier than what you have just done, where would you stop? Logically you'd carry on and do everything on the page before moving on to the next page.
