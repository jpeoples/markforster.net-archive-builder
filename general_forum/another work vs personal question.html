Much as I love AF, I continue to bump up against a specific issue: work vs. home. I've posted on this before and apologize for boring anyone!

I think I understand the guidance on when to use two lists versus one list - ie., to use different lists for different locations, not for different kinds of tasks. However... I'm fortunate and probably atypical in that I have an office I can use when I want to or need to, but can work from home when I prefer. Most work tasks can be done from either place. This is a wonderful arrangement, allowing me to choose quiet and solitude or the pleasant buzz of the office as I wish... except that it's causing me confusion when it comes to AF!

I tried two lists but this seemed an unnecessary effort, and illogical, because so many of my work tasks can be done from either location. One list feels much better. But I've realised, as I work through the list, that it matters a lot to me to divide my day into "work time" and "non-work time". In a very unstructured life, this is a key bit of structure for me.

But of course if I designate a particular period of hours as "work time", then use my Autofocus list, I'll inevitably come across many non-work tasks on my list. What I've been doing is simply ignoring the non-work tasks. But I can't help feeling this interrupts the "standing out" process, by imposing a filter on what's allowed to stand out.

Do I simply have to choose between a) abandoning any effort to designate "work time" and "non-work time", or b) keeping fixed hours for going into the office, and using two location-specific lists? Or is there a third way? Any thoughts gladly received.

Hi Ludlow: It seems to me that you are already applying a "meta-filter" if you like. Work and non-work time are clearly divided in your mind, so why not tag each task on the margin as "W" or "NW'. That way when you are in work mode, you will clearly skip the NW tasks, and allow only the W tasks to stand out to you.

I use something similar for delegated tasks to my office assistant who comes in once a week. They are peppered thoughout my AF list, and tagged. I apply my mental filter when my assistant comes in, and I discuss the items by picking out the tagged tasks.

Sometimes, I put in all in a separate task list and have an AF task "Discuss task list with ABC". Depends on what my intuition tells me, and how long the lists are.

I little inelegant or contrived, but the simple system works for me.

My recommendation for what it's worth is not to worry about when you feel you "should" be in work mode or home mode but let the tasks stand out in the order they wish. If your experience follows mine you will find a natural rhythm develops without too much input from a rational, or imposed perspective.

Hi Lefty,

I think you may have missed some of the fine print ;-)

The separate notebooks does not address "context" but "place" ... i.e. physical location. Mark uses the example of leaving his work notebook at work and going home to the home notebook. He can't do tasks on either list in the other place.

Secondly, you don't have to dismiss tasks just because you can't do them now. You only dismiss tasks when there is nothing at all you WANT to do on that page. (There is some discussion about what if you are in the wrong place and there is nothing you CAN do. Some of us dismiss and readd, others just recognize that is not the intent of dismissal and leave it there until we are in the right place. YMMV. And yes, it does add an extra step, so that is why I have chosen to just let the items sit there until I can reasonably do them.)

As to "contexts", I ran away from GTD screaming because of that insanity ;-) I did find it useful to have some "lists" for specific things such as a shopping list, a list of things to discuss with my wife, etc. But context had nothing at all to do with it. I might cull out items from my list that I will do this weekend out in the yard. That is not a context list but just an ordinary list of things to do on Saturday in the yard. I also have a "hot list" of the things I MUST do today and I put that on a Post-it on my book's cover or on an index card.

As to beta testers ... I think the whole list is a beta test group. (Unless Mark does not beta test his new idea.)

One frustration I had with GTD was not having a guided way to *switch* contexts.

For example, I am working my @Computer list. It is essentially endless. I could spend *days* working that list.

Or I am working my @Home list. Again, I have an essentially endless list here. There are so many projects to do outside, so many repairs and odds and ends I could do inside.

My frustration with GTD was that, while working my list in one of these "contexts", I would be anxiously worried that maybe I *really* should be working on something on one of my other context lists!

You could try "time blocking" for this. This works great for me if I keep it very general -- e.g., "work" and "personal". But how detailed are you going to get? Should I create a timeblock for each and every context? (@Home, @Calls, @Wife, @Outside, @Computer, @Desk, @Work, @Errands). It just gets nonsensical and totally arbitrary.

AF solved that problem for me. The "standing out" process helps prompt me when it's the right time to switch contexts. E.g., I'm at my computer, and for awhile, all the "computer" tasks are standing out, since it just naturally works that way. But then, after while, "wash the dishes" stands out. So, maybe it's time to leave the computer and go wash some dishes. This just works great. And it doesn't need any special context tagging.

Another thing.

At first, I tried using one single AF list for everything. But I started having trouble, since I had lots of urgent personal tasks at the time, and I'd find myself whittling away the day on my personal tasks. But, I am an employee, and even though I work at home, I need to be focused on work tasks for the bulk of the day. An occasional break, even a lengthy one, to work on personal stuff is fine -- but it's just not acceptable (to me or to my employer!) to give only a fraction of the day to my paid work.

So, I quickly learned that I needed to switch to two books -- one for personal, one for work. That worked much better, for several months.

But now, I'm back to a single book. I'm using the method of listing work items from the top down, and personal items from the bottom up, and drawing a line where they meet in the middle.

I think the reason it's working now, even though it didn't work earlier, is because AF has become a totally essential and dependable system for me. I write everything in there, and trust that it will get done eventually. It's really working for me, and I have confidence that the right things will get done at the right time. I didn't really have that confidence before, so I always gravitated towards the urgent and pressing tasks, which generally happen to be my personal tasks.

Now, having everything in one list again, I find it's easy to focus on the work tasks during work time, but still nice to see what other things I need to be thinking about when I have some personal time. And sometimes, those personal things really do stand out, and I realize I've been neglecting them, and need to take a long lunch break to deal with them, or maybe even take half a vacation day or something to push through some of them.

One reason I like AF so much, is it helps me balance all of this, without arbitrary time blocks or rules. It all works very naturally, without needing to force-fit anything into an arbitrary structure.
