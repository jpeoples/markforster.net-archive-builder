My Biggest Problem with AF

Author: AndreasE | Created: 2009-04-9 1:05 | Last Activity: 2009-04-17 17:30

Original Post

2009-04-9 1:05
AF is wonderful. I just follow the rules and get into a flow, and things get easy. Projects move forward, ideas emerge, things get done. I know where to write anything down - at the end of the list -, and I know where to look for the next action - on my current active page -, and work is wonderful.

But as soon as something becomes URGENT, I start falling out of the rhythm.

An example:
I have to call somebody, not now, but tomorrow or friday. I write it on the last page, which is page 23. But my current active page is page 9, and the chance to get through all active pages the next two days is almost zero.

This throws me out of the flow. Why? Because it's NOT ENOUGH to write this task down and get on with what I am doing; I MUST REALIZE that this task is time-sensitive and that I have to do something about it to make sure I won't miss to do it in time.

I can write it in my calendar instead, although it's not a fixed date. Okay. I can use the index card method and write it there (although I don't like this index card thing anymore; it feels like a q&d fix, not like something that belongs to a system), okay.

But what bothers me is THAT I HAVE TO THINK OF IT. When I jot something down at the end of the list, I am obliged to CHECK IT whether it is time-sensitive and if so, make preparations against it accordingly. This is one step too many and throws me out of the flow. In geekish: I have to unload my current (mental) process, load another and produce ideas how to avoid missing some obligatiion, then finishing this process and load again the process before. The opposite of "flow", isn't it?

IMHO a system should consist of routines that won't let the necessity for such "reparations" and "improvisations" come up.

My best "additional rule" up to now was to make the forementioned "check-if-time-sensitive" NOT when writing a task down, but in the moment I CLOSE a list. This is a step that's part of the system - you draw a line at the bottom of the page, you start a new, write "to do 24" on top or whatever - and therefore a good opportunity to go through all items to see whether some of them need additional treatment. And to draw some additional symbols to manifest that. (I have to admit I did not follow my own rule. Because I wanted to experience what would happen otherwise.)

I can hear already the mantra "Trust the system!"
Well, trust can not be demanded, it must be earned. I am on page 9, and I realized that there is an entry on page 23 that needs to be done within the next two days. Which means I have to do it out of order. Commonsense-rule, I know. It's not that jump that bothers me but that it REMINDS ME of the possibility that there may be OTHER urgent tasks (or tasks that HAVE become urgent meanwhile) somewhere on the pages 10-22. It bothers me because I see once again that the AF system IS NOT DESIGNED TO PREVENT those tasks from being entered and left alone.

Yes, it's true that AF encourages to start projects early enough so that their deadlines will most probably be met long before. But even then may it happen that something becomes urgent. And I am not sure I will know it every time.

I would like to see Mark Forster come up with a ingenious solution to that.

It is interesting that you mention this as I have been thinking about exactly the same issues and trying to find a work around!

My current workaround when things get crazy or urgent things pile up is to cull a mini-list from the AF list and work from that list but using AF rules for a day or two. I get back to the "real" AF list once the craziness passes. The mini-list might be project based (a project has just become critical), urgency based (a list of time sensitive tasks) or "context" based (I am traveling and don't have access to usual resources). This list may be a page or 2. The workflow has all the elements of Autofocus. I can pick the item that stands out but only from this mini-list. I add to the end of the list if progress is made but not completed. I expect all the items to be completed from this list before I get back to the main list. When I do get back to the main list, I just mark all the items (moved to mini-list) completed.

This has been working well but still feels like a work-around and as you mention doesn't have the same flow as the pure AF. I still have to work at it to branch off to the mini-list (although there is enough motivation due to external circumstances to create one). Someone mentioned in another thread that they use the last page to create this mini-list and work the last page exclusively. I haven't tried that yet although that may be closer to Pure AF than what I have.

Hi AnreasE,

I think I am a fairly busy fellow with a large number of deadline driven projects and tasks. I am finding AF to work well for me, but I do the following things for things that have to be done on or by a certain time:

(1) For the phone call you mentioned -- it has to be done on Thursday or Friday -- add this to your calendar as a header or note somehow. I do this in Outlook by creating an all-day task that spans two days -- so the banner is at the top -- and list the task as "Call Sally about project X?" The question mark means it does not HAVE to be done at a certain time of even the first day, but by the end of the second day. I keep these kinds of things separate from the AF list.

(2) I use Outlook 2007 for task management of things that have hard deadlines. They are right there and listed also below each day on the calendar. I do also add these into my AF lists and make notation as follows: "Complete review of task force report DUE: 4/10" I underline the "DUE: 4/10" with a red pen.

(3) I maintain separate project lists and plans in OneNote 2007. If there are tasks that I am ready to move on, I put these into AF. It is stricly a judgement call to leave them on the planning list versus putting them into my AF lists.

(4) I also create time blocks for certain important projects -- sometimes up to 2 hours of time -- and I protect that time ruthlessly -- and work ONLY on items associated with that particular project.

(5) When not doing the above -- I work off of my AF lists! I try to do this as much as possible.

This combination is working well for me.

I hope this helps some.

I have a pretty good idea of what is in my lists from my regular cycling and reviews and generally speaking have virtually no time dependent tasks on my earlier pages. What I do to keep potentially time dependent tasks flowing is, at the beginning of each week, I identify what I want to achieve that week. That will of course include any time dependent tasks. I just keep that list on my desk and work through the lists in the normal way. I don't action tasks from that list (which I personally do in the form of a MindMap) but highlight tasks if and when they are actioned (in the normal course of AF).

If a task becomes time critical then I will "just do it" but generally the tasks get done naturally. That MindMap takes the worry about missing items away, in that they are identified and I also know I have the "just do it" option available if necessary. In practice the relevant tasks normally stand out quite naturally, so get actioned easily and without pressure.

I am normally working towards the end of my list most of the time so if I am unsure where an item is I just add it (or readd it) onto the last page. I have never considered having items duplicated to be a problem.

Andreas:

I'm going to address your original post in which you ask me to come up with an ingenious answer to the problem of urgency.

Well, here it is:

TRUST THE SYSTEM

Why is that the answer?

At the moment your chances of reaching the "urgent" item (actually not that urgent) are "almost zero" - but the reason they are "almost zero" is because you are not trusting the system to deliver with that type of item.

Your lack of trust means that you are taking that type of item out of the system. As a result the system is not having to adjust to the existence of that type of item.

What you are basically saying to your intuitive mind is: "I don't trust you to get me to urgent items on time, so I'm taking them out of your hands and dealing with them separately."

So your intuitive mind says: "OK, in that case I won't bother to allow for them."

And it doesn't.

I think that comes down to what we define as a project (and perhaps that is where some of the misunderstandings on this subject have arisen). I don't see a project as necessarily needing to be a task of vast complexity requiring Gantt Charts and complicated interactions of steps. To me a project, in it's simplest form, can be anything out of the ordinary that requires a number of steps, and on which it can be helpful to monitor progress in some way, or to co-ordinate associated stuff. That is a very wide description which covers a lot of eventualities. I have no doubt that things I define as projects may not be so for others. Some things may be projects on occasion, and not on others. For example, cleaning my kitchen is a series of routine tasks. I do not see it as a project per se. You can just as easily say, "well that can be done my means of a checklist". Absolutely and so it is, but to me that is still a form of project management, albeit on a small scale.

To me a Filing System is a form of project management - it is definitely the management of stuff, but I don't see the need for a different system to manage one type of "stuff" from another. To me it ranks alongside the fact that in AF "stack dishwasher" can sit comfortably next to "Take over World". It is not the quality of the task (or "stuff") that is relevant, but the way in which it is managed.

Christine:

I am using the GTD definition of "project" which is anything which can't be done in one session. And in GTD all such projects need to be managing by listing next actions etc.

What I am trying to get away from is the idea that this is necessary in AF. AF works on very different principles from GTD, and the vast majority of "projects" can be managed perfectly happily on the task list without any additional levels of management. On the occasions when they can't, then the management of the project becomes a task like any other.

As I said above, A project needs as much planning as is appropriate for that project - no more and no less.
