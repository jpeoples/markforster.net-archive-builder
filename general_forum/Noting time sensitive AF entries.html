One thing I've found is that adding anything to the system can overcomplicate it but also I've found that adding any form of notation or tag to the left of the item can be distracting when scanning through them. I've found that adding any form of additional notation as a suffix works better, in that the items can still be scanned easily or you can quickly scan down the list to identify those tagged items.

For example, any email items that need action and end up in my list are suffixed -AFe and post items -P. That is also my way of identifying where I will find the relevant document/email etc.

First, any scheduled events get put on a calendar. Part of my routine when I arrive at work is to check my calendar. For these items, alarms or reminders are also helpful.

Anything that has a deadline also goes on the calendar. If it is way in the future, I will also put a reminder on the calendar ahead of the due date to add it to the AF list. This is for items that are so far in the future that they might otherwise get dismissed by following the rules. For those that are due sooner, they go right on the list (and also on the calendar as a safety net).

When any task with a deadline is added to AF, I put the due date to the right of it in brackets like this: [Due 2March]. That way I not only see the item repeatedly, but also see the due date. The due date is really part of defining the task.

Sometimes I add a small post it note to my bookmark for a new item that is due the same day. (The item is still added to the last page where it belongs.) That way I don't need to worry about not getting to the last page. I am inconsistent with this tweak. Often it is only for items I am afraid of forgetting or if I know I won't make it through to the last page. With enough advance notice for a task, this is not necessary and clearly goes against the instructions.

We all have slightly different styles and that results in slightly different approaches while using the same basic guidelines. Sometimes I need to use some of the above tweaks to help me through something knowing that I will drop them when they no longer are helpful. Making those choices knowing that they are temporary sometimes reduces stress that has become a temporary hindrance to performance. Personally, I see nothing wrong with that. It is just part of my own common sense rule.

2009-03-1 15:44
You can make Chandler do closed lists by setting up a new collection for each page. Or, probably more effectively, by using a note as a page.

It's not the fastest program though, and hasn't quite gone where it was expected to go when it started.

Other big note+tasks+calendar programs include Spicebird (uses Thunderbird+Lightning underneath), EssentialPIM (free and commercial versions) and Do-Organizer (not free). I have them all installed (or available as portable versions, which I prefer) as well as Outlook (and others) but don't think I would use any of them to run AF. Do-Organizer comes closest. The problem with all of them is that inputting tasks can be quite time consuming because of setting dates, options etc - and they are not designed to use a number of closed lists (though workarounds are usually possible); and using the notes sections as pages means that striking though an item takes more than a single click. They also have quite a big general overhead (though it varies a lot between them), which is fine if you have them always on but not if you will often load them just for AF.

I much prefer using a computer based way of operating AF, but believe that doing it successfully means that the solution has to be quick and light with features/interface etc that suit you well as an individual.
