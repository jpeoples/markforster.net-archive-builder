I see three (at least) distinct things going on here:

1) Tasks
2) Deadlines
3) Routines

IMO, only tasks go on the AF list. Routines (like meditate, or exercise) are on their own lists and I try to habituate those. Deadlines go on the calendar or they will not get linked to the time they are due. I'm of the mind to not make AF do more than it can effectively do. I don't have "Service Car" on AF because it is dependent on the millage. I do, however, have a routine to clean out the car on Saturday and then I check the millage ... at which point, if I figure I'm about ready to take it in, I put "Service Car" in the Calendar for 07:00 next Saturday morning. Notice that this example uses Routine and Calender but not AF. AF is to tell me what odd things need doing which are not linked to time (deadline). Now if the deadline were a long way off for a large level of effort task (like writing a book) I'd use AF and Calender and probably also Routine for that.

2009-02-24 10:07
For me it is really important to keep routine tasks in AF. Putting those routines in has enabled me to identify a lot of factors surrounding the way I do them, when I do them, why I do and how long I do them. I have always struggled with a high boredom factor with many routine tasks - that is all but eliminated within AF.

I would say that my 3 most "useful" items in AF are

Check email
Check schedule
and
Thinking Time

Check email (a) stops me checking every 5 minutes and (b) somehow has enabled me to focus better during the time spent, with the result that I have already cleared a massive amount of email backlog.

Check schedule - if I have anything to add to my schedule it usually starts in AF as "add x to schedule" and I can then see at a glance all of my timed appointments, or my "frantic Fridays" :-) Tasks like "service car" do go into my AF but only until they get to my schedule. For example my car service is due Sept/Oct - In August I will add a task "Car Service", part of which will involve calling the garage to book it in, arranging it's MOT etc. Once it is booked in it moves to my schedule and gets picked up on the "check schedule" task.

Thinking Time - this is absolutely invaluable. For me, one of the most amazing effects of AF has been the way it which it has identified reasons for procrastination, what I feel about certain tasks, obligations, goals, working methodology etc. I can choose to use AF just to "get things done" or I can choose to act on the things that I am coming to realise as a result of using AF and make the appropriate changes to move my life forward in a more productive and satisfying way. Thinking Time gives me the time and space to really examine those things and plan for the future.

I think the key here is whether a task is time specific or not. If it's a routine that I clean my teeth when I get up, then that's time specific as far as I'm concerned, and it shouldn't be on the list. I think that's pretty obvious!

However having a deadline is not the same as being time specific. My experience is that Autofocus is better at delivering things by their deadline dates, than I am unaided. So I would most certainly include deadline tasks on AF.

And the fact that something is recurring, doesn't make it routine. So things like Email, Forum Comments, studying, Twitter, Facebook, tidying the office, are all tasks on AF. And have benefited immensely from being there.

On the other hand I don't have "Read Newspaper" as an AF task because that is part of my routine over breakfast.

And as for "Back Up Hard Drive" - I've mechanised that so it happens automatically every day. I do have a task to check that it's happened correctly though!
