Well Chris, this hasn't been discussed lately. I can't say I have practical and effective goals and purpose yet, but I think I'm getting close. A bunch of random points:

Goals without self-management (time management) doesn't get you far; you need both.
Deciding your purpose and setting goals are two different things.
Regarding purpose, it makes sense that it ought to connect to your make. What are you good at? What do you like? What do you value? What is important?
As a Christian, I found The Purpose Driven Life somewhat helpful, it's only a partial answer I think.
Purpose, to me, is hard to act on. It's not usually one thing but many.
Goals are specific objectives you set for yourself. Try to choose ones that agree with your purpose. If they clash, change goals or refine your purpose.
Start small, succeed small, then go bigger.

Think about my Purpose is a recurring task for me. In that, I evaluate what I'm doing (review list) and consider if it aligns well with my conept of my purpose. I may then try to come up with new activities to balance.
Replace movie watching with something more engaging.

+AJPM+

I completely forgot until now about this technique that I am about to write. It has been some time since I had last done this until now, but I will attest to its surprisingly, and sometimes embarrassingly, candid yet accurate revelation of your innermost thoughts and desires. It is also quick, easy, and fun!

You will need: a piece of paper, and a pen or pencil.

1. Place the paper on the table in landscape orientation.

2. Think about the question you want to answer about yourself, then try to forget about it for the next steps.

3. Think of eight words, the first ones you can think of, or ones you read from your surroundings, and write them side by side at the top.

4. Pair each word with its neighbor (first and second, third and fourth, fifth and sixth, seventh and eighth) by connecting a line between them. You should now have four pairs of words.

5. Look at each pair, and think of a word that would describe their relationship with each other. Write the FIRST word that comes into your mind below the line connecting the pair. You should know have four new words.

6. Again, pair each word with its neighbor using lines, as before. You should now have two pairs.

7. Again, look at each pair, and think of a word that would describe their relationship with each other. Write the FIRST word that comes into your mind below the line connecting the pair. You should know have two new words.

8. Finally, pair the last two words, and think of a word describing their relationship with each other. Write the FIRST word that comes into your mind below the line connecting the pair. THIS is the answer of your subconscious to your question.

Now, if the answer is nonsensical, do not write it off right away. Ruminate on the answer for a while; it might surprise you.

For example, I accidentally erased the first version of this post earlier. As you can see, it was quite long, and that really ticked me off. I then thought of this weird idea of doing this technique to find out what to do next. SO I did, with the question, "What do I do now?"

The answer I got was NONSENSE. Yep, the word NONSENSE. Huh?

After a few moments scratching my head, I then realized that in my first version of this post I never did include about what to do when you get a "nonsense" answer like what I had a few moments before (literally). I just laughed and typed again, and well, you got this post. ^_____^

God bless.
