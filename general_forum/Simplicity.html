Mary in another post mentioned her old system of writing in spiral notebooks prior to finding this site. Years ago I also used to use a similar method and wrote about it in an ebook. At the company I work at now virtually everyone uses spiral notebooks versus legal pads which are pervasive in most companies in the US. I have noticed that the people at this firm are more productive than at many other places I have worked where people are writing on legal pads or not at all. I am wondering if it due to the simplicity of having everything in one place. No matter if you are using AF, the system I outlined or another the reality is all you have to do and think about is in one place, so as long as you are writing in the notebook, reviewing this notebook regularly and taking action you will be productive. I have noticed most of the tweaks I have used have not improved on the simplicity of writing in one place. As I have pulled out notes to file them and put project lists in other places, it has just served to cause me to have more places to look for things and more things to look at reducing time to actually do things and increasing the anxiety I have thinking I am not remembering all I have to do.

If Mark's new tweak incorporates a form of project tracking/management into it I will give it a try, if not, I think I will just continue on with the Simple Guide to Time Management method.

Anyone else have a spiral notebook based time management system to share?

H Gerry,

That is absolutely the KEY to any good system ... permanence and a single locus!

That said, there is some flexibility that may prove useful. My "ultimate" system was in several parts and a bit complex as it evolved over the years. Briefly it was composed of:

- A spiral notebook. This was the anchor of the system. I eschewed the whole idea of "scratch paper"! When I first started a new book I'd number every right hand page with odd numbers, leaving five pages in front blank for reference material and an index of important entries. I'd also tape phone books to the inside cover, etc. Every entry had a pointer to previous conversations and subsequent conversations so I could quickly follow the "chain". I had a system of codes for entires to speed up notation. Oh, it was quite elaborate ;-)

Every thing went into that book. BUSINESS AND PERSONAL! This was crucial to me. One book, one place ... for everything! I never had more than one place to look for something. If it was not in my book, it was not current and so was filed and I (and anyone else) could look it up alphabetically in the file cabinet.

Business cards were taped in ... they did not go into a Rolodex that would always be on my desk when I needed the card in some other city. Even phone messages if they had useful info on them were taped in there. I can't tell you how much credibility I had when I read from my notebook and people saw the original phone message taped right there. I was never questioned about the facts of anything. It saved all kinds of argument. Even over the phone, people can tell that you have it all written down in front of you in contemporaneous notes. (From time to time I also used a bound notebook. I liked that they did not have the wires to hang up on things. But I did not like that they were not hole punched so could not fit in my binder ... see below.) Oh, and periodically I'd photocopy the pages and keep them filed in the first file in my file cabinet ... just in case. (I'd put a check mark by the page number to indicate the page had been photocopied so I'd know where I left off.) When you commit to a universal notebook you just HAVE to photocopy it. (If I were doing this today, I'd scan it into EverNote so I'd have instant access anywhere if I were without it.)

- I also had a ring binder which contained my calendar and 43 tabs for upcoming info. I would snap my spiral book into that when I was on the move so I had only one thing to carry. My calendar was in this book but my TO DO list was also in my spiral book. When I planned my day I would put my meetings and TO DO items on the top of the spiral "log" so I had them always in front of me. My calendar was for planning purposes only.

- When I got a memo it might go into the ring binder if it needed to be referenced quickly. If not, it would go into a chron file so I knew were to find it ... it might possibly be photocopied and put into a project folder ... that just depended.

- My format was 8.5" x 11" because most of the paper I handled was in that format.

- I had punched plastic sleeves in the binder for things like airline tickets.

My binder with my spiral book was right on top of other things in my brief case. If I had tickets or reservations, they were in a plastic sleeve in my binder right under the date. No last minute worries about if I brought the tickets. I even had my passport in a pocket in that binder. I literally could redirect a cab and be on the next plane to a foreign country with no notice. I could buy more socks when I got there, if needed, but I could not make up for missing info.

Now, in fairness, this was in the days of old before the ubiquitous notebook computer. But I think I'd still keep to my spiral book and use things like EverNote for back up and voluminous documentation.

Jacqueline,

I agree that in general terms that is true. Productivity is about what you do, not what you plan to do. OTOH, if we are talking about a total work management system (as the original poster was) there is more to it than a TO DO list. As I said, it is about recording information so that it can be accessed quickly and not get lost. It is about knowing who said what to whom about which and when so that when a vendor says: "I never promised that." you can call him on it and get what you paid for. In an environment where many things are a mater of interpretation this can be important. Sure you write a nice contract with detailed specifications ... but there are always things which don't make it there. "Hey, you said that the response time would be under a second and it is. But you also said your system would be stable and it crashes twice a day which blows the value of that response time all to hell. This was to be resolved last week, remember our conversation wherein you said...?"

That kind of management system does not lend itself well to scraps of paper. IOW, if what you are doing is done when it is done, then a scrap of paper is fine. If what you are doing has something of a "life", then you have to hang on to information until the whole "project" is over or you're toast.

Mike, in my previous world, we had a lot of staff turnover and projects that didn't end. Having well maintained files was a crucial part of the process - if the files don't work, they're not set up or maintained properly. If you're working on the 2008 year-end, the notes for the previous year-end better be in the 2007 year-end binder / files - not stuck in someone's notebook who has since moved onto another job.
