First of all: Mark, your approach to time management is the best I know.

When I first tried to adopt a time management system, alll I knew was that it should be an easy system, so that I avoid procrastinating within the system itself (as I did with David Allen's approach) and that it should rely on scientific knowledge so that it would really work.

I've read a lot of scientific studies about time management and came to the conclusion that the best way to handle my daily tasks and to avoid procrastination (which means to me: not doing something instead of something, but doing something and painfully regretting not doing something else) is to incorporate ideas that have been used in cognitive behavioural therapy and in neuroscience, such as improving the executive function of the brain and to balance the rational and intuitive parts of the brain. And then I read about DIT and it really incorporated these ideas. I read the book and decided to give it a go and it worked smoothly!

Now, you said that you think that the autofocus system is a major improvement over DIT so I decided to have a crack at it, too. After 5 days of testing I can say that it's not really much better than DIT - just a bit different:

- the idea of "structured procrastination" is stretched to the limits in AF (which is much better than in DIT), but unfortunately at the expense of the idea of getting a day's workload done in one day. I miss the good feeling I had after having done my workload for the day. Now I really don't know when to call it a day.

- by doing my "current initiative" in DIT I was sure that I got around to do at least something meaningful that day, with AF I can't be that sure. Structured procrastination means that I get things done - but maybe just minor things. I'm a lazy person and the things that "stand out" for me when going through the list are often things that are easy or fun to do. In other words: My rational and intuitive parts of the brain are not in balance at all! My intuitive parts often win over my rational parts and I end up doing busy work instead of meaningful work.

- DIT was just one list in your daily planner: I got used to split my pages in half. The left side for deadlines, appointments etc. and the right side for my tasks (including the current initiative). Everything was there at a glance. AF is unfortunately one list + plus a daily planner, because I can't write down things that has to be done at a specific time.

If there was an easy way to incorporate the idea of structured procrastination in the DIT-system, it would be perfect for me. Unfortunately it seems that AF doesn't balances both parts of my brain at all. It seems that I actually start to what I call "procrastinate within the system" by unconsciously picking up items on my AF-list that are easy to do while I dismiss others. It's definately not a matter of how I write things down (e.g. "start outline" vs. "write article") because it worked perfectly in DIT.

Thanks for your work Mark!

P.S.: In case some sentences are pretty awkward: I'm not a native speaker - have mercy on me!

M. Romer,

Why are you dismissing things?

I would dismiss something because, with the benefit of hindsight, I thought it was trivial. Sometimes, in the heat of the moment, I decide I have to do something silly. If I write it down, and then come back to it 4 days later, I might dismiss it. But I wouldn't dismiss an item that I recognize is important to my long-term well-being, like filing my taxes.

I am planning on giving AF at least another full week. But it seems possible to create a hybrid AF. You could have two lists. One list is the standard AF list. The second list would contain recurrent, time-specific, and day-specific tasks. Essentially, there would be a mandatory list and a discretionary list. Checking in-boxes (physical and virtual) could go in mandatory. Serious work and procrastinating activities would go in discretionary.

AF consists of multiple closed lists (each page being a closed list). Hybrid AF would allow us to work off of two sets of closed lists simultaneously.

As I said, I don't plan on testing this hybrid. At least not for another week. But if you are ready to abandon AF, you might want to try something like this first.

Good luck,

moises

Because I have so many pleasant activities on my list, the concept of being relieved at being done with it is foreign to me. The one thing I do for big deadlined projects (like taxes) is to add them to a program called DoThisFirst that tells me how much time I need to spend on it each day to be done in plenty of time. I then write "DoThisFirst" on my AF list and do it whenever it stands out. You could do this with a current initiative list as well.

One problem I think people have with AF is thinking that they will forever do the "less important" things. I see this like being on a free diet. Yes, at first you will O.D. on desserts. But if you stick it out long enough, you'll get really sick of sweets and you'll go for the meat.
