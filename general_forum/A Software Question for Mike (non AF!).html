Christine,

A parable (but true, and sadly so). In my misspent youth I took my turn in the barrel chairing an operating system problem reporting committee. The way this thing worked is that users would report problems and they would be investigated and the fix would be discussed among everyone responsible for the upcoming release and marketing (who's livelihood depended on the system working correctly for the end users).

One day a problem reached the committee regarding a user complaint that a particular database product, when you asked for a report on some field, sorted hundreds of blank lines to the top of the report (for those entries where that field was blank). After much debate the database programmer exclaimed in frustration: "It works as coded." There was not a closed mouth in the room!

For years that idiotic (but perfectly understandable) response helped me make sense of the nonsense in the industry. "Works as coded." Of course, everything always works as coded ... the problem is why was it coded that way!!!!

Yes, transistors are logical. Integrated circuits are logical. The entire hardware box is logical. Even the software is logical, from the standpoint that it works as coded. But why do idiots code things that way? I could write a book about that. But why bother? Others have already done so.

I wish I could remember the title of a book I read a few decades ago about this exact problem . The author cited the case of a crashed school bus where the driver was blamed. She denied it. After much investigation it was determined that she DID shift into low gear on the downside of a hill (as safety and the law require), and as unbelievable as it sounded, her story about the bus shifting out of gear on its own WAS true. The manufacturer had decided that to protect the engine from over reving and being damaged he would design the transmission to shift into neutral in that condition ... yet that condition was one which drivers (and highway officials) counted on to save lives ... not engines.

And the beat goes on.

Re: Sock drawer.

The problem inherently (and inescapably) bound into the whole idea of bug fixes is that the changes are not reflected back to the requirements document. Most often the bug starts out with the requirements ... someone had not conceived of a situation so it was not addressed. If you don't go all the way back and then clean the mess up through each stage of the development process you'll never get it right. Something will always be working at odds with something else.

Another of my favorite examples: While working at the same company as before I was debugging an operating system crash for a computer in Spain. It happened that as soon as a log tape was first mounted the system crashed ... we spent weeks tracking it down. The solution was as funny as it was tragic and costly. It seems that when the OS was first modified to work in Australia there was a need to use both seven track log tapes and change the way the date was formatted (differently from the U.S.). Some brain trust decided that there was only one spare bit he could use so he used that one bit for both things (in a crucial OS data structure). This goofball even called it the "Australian Bit", in the OS documentation ... which, of course, compounded the problem as it did not immediately indicate that it had anything to do with log tapes or time!

When the OS was used for Spain, there was a need for the same date format (I think, it is getting fuzzy now, or it may have been for the same log tapes) in any case, they tested that flag and since the other feature was tied to it (but not wanted) the whole house of cards came down. I mean, hey, you have a spare bit lying around doing nothing, why not use it? What could possibly go wrong? Arrrrg.

All of this comes down to good practice and religious adherence to logic and documentation. ALL of which are in short supply in the computer industry.
