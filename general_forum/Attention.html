Re: David

I never did push the 'create post' button on that comment. I got distracted and thought it was too off topic anyway. It was posting it in a thread that was discussing goals and happiness.

I'm still halfway the book though but here are some interesting quotes in the chapter about productivity and flow (which you may consider an absorbed state of attention within a particular activity:

"if you ask me while I'm playing tennis if I'm happy, I'll say, 'Heck! Wait a minute...' Happiness is a later reflection of the flow, rather than the result of the experience at the time." ~ Csikszentmihaly

The interesting thing about flow is that you can also be in a certain kind flow while procrastinating. Browsing the web can be quite hypnotic, you lose track of time etc. Which segues into another quote:

"If left to their own devices and genetic programming, and without a salient external stimulus to attract them, most people go into a mode of low-level information processing in which they worry about things or watch television." ~ Csikszentmihaly on leisure

In contrast, if your work or hobbies often bring you into a state of flow, you may need to increase the 'difficulty' or novelty over time to keep getting into a state of flow. This type of attention can then even be seen as a critical process that aids or evolution.

When you think about it, flow is said to require an activity that is not too difficult but at the same time not too simple. If our work is too hard, we often display reactions of avoidance, if the work is too boring, again we often display reactions of avoidance, all be it maybe in a different flavour.

If you think about the problem with many people's lives is that we're engaged with commitments and task lists that are either too boring or too difficult. One of my earlier remarks about Autofocus (or for any time management system), if the activities on the list aren't enriching your experience, your experience is going to suck even if you're getting a lot done. I think to the benefit of AF versus other systems, is that you will be evaluating the why's of resistance which much more effect. That way you can make better decisions about what you're going to do in the future and present.

The idea of flow as being an attentional process that aids our 'evolution' correlates interestingly with today's world: it's increasing development of technology, faster change, higher amounts of information and choices have created the type of environment where you can experience continual learning and newer levels of difficulty: it's ideal for experiencing flow!

But one does need to master the attentional process and have the room to create a supporting environment for its corresponding activites. In other words, being able to do the activities that engage flow. I think one of the main issues is that people are having to keep up with an established environment that isn't at all conducive to flow, happiness or satisfying productivity.

I think the way the article Andy posted ended interestingly, talking about distractions not necessarily being a bad thing while putting a different spin on multitasking and how we're adapting to today's world. I know a lot of successful enterpreneurs with ADD that do indeed thrive (although there are probably 5 that don't for everyone that does).

Re. misinterpretation of some comments - succintness is only valuable when it's accompanied by clarity. Otherwise it sounds like a koan - and people will tend to make their own interpretations. Eschew obfuscation; espouse elucidation.

Mark, in your comment:
"people ... will take every opportunity to talk, read, fantasize, intellectualise about a subject rather than actually do it."

Talking, reading or fantasizing (or envisioning) about a subject may be a critical part of actually doing it. Those steps can answer questions you have that are preventing you from feeling comfortable just doing it - and prevent errors that you will likely make if you just do it. The key is to not get stuck in that mode. To learn enough and then move into doing. But if you're never doing things that push you beyond the boundaries of what you currently know, you're not challenging yourself enough.

I have had more problems at work with people who "just do it" rather than thinking about what they're doing - or who won't ask a question if they don't understand something rather than going down the wrong path. That's why, when I was training new people, I always took the time to explain how this job/task fit into the big picture of what we were trying to do and what I thought were the ways to get there. I wanted them to be able to adapt their process (on their own) based on what they revealed in their doing. I've also worked with some highly productive people that seem to need to talk things through with someone else to wrap their heads around a concept and what we're trying to achieve.

I've mentioned before that I don't use AF at work. I wasn't comfortable with cluttering my mind with what I perceive as "noise." I have the gift? / problem? of a difficulty with attentional shift. This was a problem when I used to manage people - until I realized that many times, my job *was* the interruptions. Here they were, babbling away (sounding like the adults in a Charlie Brown show) and my mind was still focused on what I was doing before the interruption. My point is (yes, I do have one!) - that extreme focus and attention really isn't all that and a bag of chips.

Here is the thing ... and I don't expect anyone to believe me without the experience of it ... there is "meditation" and "meditation practice" and they are two very different things. A similar example might be a virtuoso musician. Yes, he will practice the Rachmaninoff #3 to get it right. But he will also practice scales. Trust me, the best do that. And, as someone said elsewhere, the top athletes practice the basics of their sport as well. Golfers practice putting and driving, not just playing games. They do BOTH. Why? Because it simply works. It has been proven over and over. If you want to be at the top of your game, never forget to practice the essentials.

So meditating on Epictetus is a good thing. No, it's a GREAT thing. But it is not the same thing as "practicing meditation" ... per se. If it were, do you think that the Buddhist monks would be practicing so many hours a day for so many years over the centuries? They must believe that it works. That book I read on the ship last time (if anyone is interested I'll take the trouble to find the title and author) cited PET scan studies which showed physical changes in the brain from years of meditation practice, as well as functional changes which occur from meditating on something in particular.

I also meditate daily (just 20 minutes, first thing).

I do it as an expression of my Christian faith. It is not so much because I find it "useful" or that I want to "get something out of it". Most of the time I really could not articulate any immediate "benefits".

But I look at it this way: When I am sick with the 'flu, the main thing I need to do is just to shut down all activity and abandon myself to the healing power within me that eventually returns me to health. Or, with the one I love best in life, I persistently find myself giving over the best of myself to her, letting go of my own priorities, abandoning myself to her, trusting in her, in short, giving her my heart.

So too in meditation I let go of my thoughts, my planning, my worries, my sorrows, my feelings, and I trust that the reality that I encounter at the ground of my being will somehow well up and permeate my life.

I wouldn't even attempt to "prove" that it works, but I do believe that many of the things I find on my AF list are there because of the contemplative flavour this practice brings to my life.

If this sounds like nonsense to anyone, all I can say is: "Allow yourself to love somebody deeply and see if it changes you".
