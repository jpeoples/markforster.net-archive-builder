Joe:

If you can't take action on something for a week or so then what is it doing on your list? You should have it in some sort of reminder (diary, Outlook, tickler file, etc.) to be brought up when you are able to do it.

And since "standing out" means that the item feels ready to do, there's a contradiction in saying an item is standing out if you aren't ready to do it.

Reply by Mark Forster

2009-02-4 23:37
Greenchutney:

There's no great mystery about this. If you have only a couple of items on the page, you will presumably have visited the page several times - so there will have already been multiple opportunities to take action on them.If you haven't taken action on them on those previous opportunities, in spite of "wanting to do them", then what guarantee is there that you will do them even if you do defer them? My advice is to follow the instructions and dismiss them. If you do that consistently, you will be more likely to do them on earlier visits to the page.

Remember that dismissing an item does not prevent you from doing it in the future - it is intended to be an opportunity to reassess it.

However if an item can't be done for several days, then it shouldn't be on the list at all. It should be in a reminder of some kind, as I wrote in my reply to Joe above.

Greenchutney:

If the week's priorities changed, then it would seem to be an ideal opportunity to review the task, which is what dismissal is all about. AF is designed to be able to cope with shifting priorities and dismissing items is part of that process.

In AF a reminder is used as a signal to put an item on the main list, rather than that it has to be done that day.

Greenchutney;

I interpret Dismissing as you do in your post.

Although I started off with a manual system and got going with it, I have been using AF with ListPro for over a week now. So it;s easy for me to tag each item, if I want to, regarding its fate.

As I work abroad, there are many things I can't physically do while on assignment, and vice versa. But I don't want to forget these tasks as they occur to me, so they start off in my AF list.

When I review the page, I'll tag some as "Defer To Do Later". Then if I want to do them at a time and place when I am able to, I either change them to "Reminder" or "Task Scheduled" when I copy and paste the text into Outlook, which is what Mark suggests.

When Outlook reminds me I can either decide to do them there and then, or I can restore them to AF to be picked up during scanning. I see this as an example of the flexibility of AF.

In scanning the pages, Listpro (the way I've set mine up) I am able to focus on AF tasks without yet any status and see which stands out to do now.

A real example of the Defer To Do Later process are the tasks I have told Outlook to remind me tomorrow evening when I have to spend over six hours at Johannesburg Airport en route home in the UK. I've scheduled them, or have reminders set up for the time slot because i know I'll have decent wi-fi connection in the KLM Lounge, the Zambia internet access being very slow and flaky. Included in the tasks are: online VAT returns, checking my company's bank and credit card statements (I have a firewall and other security measures running always!), long emails with attachments, making Skype calls etc.

Works for me, which is the most important thing.
