I think it would be valuable if some could share what they consider to be the minimal elements that should be included in a workable system.

After trying out a lot of methods based on my previous trial and error exercises, and from comments on this forum (especially after Seraphim came out with his "AutoDIT"), I came up with eight elements that I believe that everybody has to deal with in their daily lives. (In addition to the normal to-do list, or active AF pages).

I believe that these elements (or 'types') of tasks are dealt with by everybody regardless if they have a system or not, and regardless if they are on a conscious or subliminal level. Therefore, I think that they all should be addressed.

These eight elements are:

1) Actives
2) New (capture)
3) Day Plan ("What do I have to do today?")
4) Tomorrow (must-do's + "can't get to today")
5) Routine/Checklist (Everybody has to do routine things from time to time.)
6) Scheduled items (anything in the future)
7) Project tasks (anything that requires multiple tasks to accomplish)
8) Backlogged items (undone, to be re-evaluated, clutter)

The thing that I find personally to be paramount is the Actives Page; THE place to put ONLY items that you are actually working on.

Once I realised these basics, all of a sudden my system became more simplified and workable, and I quit trying to complicate things.

Incidentally, to give credit, I think that what prompted me to finally simplify my system came from reading a response from Alan to me in http://www.markforster.net/forum/post/1649349#post1651684 . In one short paragraph, he listed every one of the points stated above (except for "tomorrow").

Perhaps you've heard this story before:

Back in the early 1900′s, Charles Schwab was the head of the Bethlehem Steel Corporation. Consultant Ivy Lee offered to increase the productivity of Schwab’s people, and he would let Schwab decide what the results were worth. Schwab agreed.

Lee taught each executive to sit down at the end of each day, and create a list. The list must contain only the six most important things to be completed the next day. Each morning, they should start the day by working on the first item on that list, and not move on until it is completed.

As the story goes, Schwab initally balked at Lee's suggested method. Didn't you need to account for unplanned things that pop up? What about setting larger goals, beyond the scope of the next day? How could such a simplistic thing increase anyone's productivity? To Schwab's incredulous objections, Lee famously replied, "Sir, if you haven't the discipline to do this method, then no other more complicated scheme will work for you."

After a few months of seeing results, Schwab sent a check to Lee for $25,000. That’s about $500,000 in today’s dollars.

Now I don't mean to make a plug for the 6 MIT method, rather I mean to highlight the discipline. I personally define discipline as a self-imposed habit that provides some desired result in one's life. It's not what you do, it's why you do it - over and over and over. Even Mark's friend practiced the discipline of not choosing what to do. I think the above story highlights (for me at least) that the system doesn't matter nearly as much as the discipline does.

I also bring this up because, as a new DIT user, I'm finding that DIT has been engendering in me some basic disciplines that I feel I've been lacking. Disciplines such as making a habit of planning - but not too far, breaking down large tasks into reasonably achievable pieces, percieving tasks as commitments, making good on my commintments in a timely manner, and being honest with myself about what I can and cannot reasonably commit to. Of all Mark's systems, DIT is the most difficult for me to use, but I find that this is because the system requires these disciplines to operate well. Disciplines that I had not developed as well as perhaps I should have. For this reason, I continue to stick with DIT, hoping to engender in myself these disciplines. So far, so good. I've gotten one audit under my belt, and I find myself curiously motivated to avoid having to do a second. (not so curious really, it had a similar flavor to David Allen's weekly review - very uncomfortable). The most important difference between GTD's weekly review, and DIT's audit is that you can avoid DIT's audit if you can make effective commitments and stay on top of them. In GTD, it doesn't matter if you're disciplined about making and keeping commitments or not. You have to pull your tee - I mean review your system once a week regardless.

Now to Mark's friend, I recall a chinese proverb, "If you haven't decided where you are going, you'll wind up where you are heading." If you don't mind winding up just anywhere (@Alan - was it okay with you that you wound up at the gym instead of your home?) then I'm sure that philosophy will be your bread and butter. But really, I don't care to try it. I've got places I want to go in life.

Finally, I'd just like to say that choosing a system is like choosing a major: http://xkcd.com/1052/
