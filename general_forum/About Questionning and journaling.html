I'm doing something similar - one notebook for questioning and thinking, and another one for my FVP list. I was combining them in a sort of free-form hybrid but that just made things messy. The pages with thoughts and questioning turns into a big blob that is hard to process when I'm in the FVP flow. It's much easier to process the questioning/thinking notebook (and capture actionable tasks into FVP) by doing the questioning/thinking separately. This also helps keep the questioning/thinking more focused, without the distraction of the rest of my FVP list.

Seraphim and Jupiter:

<< I'm doing something similar - one notebook for questioning and thinking, and another one for my FVP list >>

My notebook has become my inseparable companion, but like you I don't use it for time management lists - though I do occasionally use it for shorter lists like shopping lists.

The main rule is that I have no rules about what goes in it or how it's written. However I haven't quite kept to that because I found three things helped to get the most out of it:

1. I date the first entry of each day.

2. I number the pages as I go along.

3. I have an index at the back of the book for things I want to find again.

Apart from being a vehicle for thinking in writing, the book is a fascinating read for me because it shows me how my thinking, my interests and my priorities change over a period. Reading back in it is often a way of linking ideas together or resurrecting something I put aside. So far I haven't missed a day since I started it on 5 October this year - but there's no rule that I have to write something every day.

It would probably mean absolutely nothing to anyone else who looked through it.

Yes, I learned long ago that everything should be dated with day, month and YEAR - however ephemeral it seems to be. It's surprising how often the year turns out to be the clue. I also spell the month rather than number it in order to avoid confusion with the American practice.
