Here are verbatim excerpts from The Pathway to Awesomeness where Mark Forster discusses resistance (with page numbers and surrounding context).

🧭 Theme: Resistance
1. Introduction – “Path of Least Resistance” (p. 7)

“Like water, humans tend to follow the path of least resistance, and if we want to get somewhere then we need to make sure that we have constructed the river banks that will take us there.”

🟢 Commentary:
This early metaphor is foundational to Zero Resistance and Autofocus. Forster views productivity as shaping the environment (the “banks”) to direct natural human tendencies, not as an act of willpower.

2. “Time to Take Control” (p. 10)

“This is usually the point at which our minds start rebelling. We have probably got strong resistance to the idea of getting out of debt (or whatever issue we are trying to resolve). Our minds are not going to let go of something so easily—after all we spend a lot of time and effort getting where we are!”

🟢 Commentary:
Resistance is described as psychological inertia — not laziness, but a defense of current identity. Forster later operationalizes this in Zero Resistance by encouraging work only where “there is no resistance.”

3. “Guilty Goals” (p. 52)

“Not admitting your fears or other emotions about the goal may lead to your trying to take a path which is not right for you. This means your goal will ultimately flounder because your unacknowledged resistance will lead you to sabotage your own efforts.”

🟢 Commentary:
Here, “resistance” becomes diagnostic — it signals misalignment between intention and authentic desire. This anticipates the DREAMS and Zero Resistance idea that resistance marks what not to force.

4. “Fighting Backlogs” (p. 86)

“Even work you don’t particularly like becomes much less resistance-inducing if you are on top of it… You need to fight backlogs like the plague. Never allow yourself to run up against deadlines.”

🟢 Commentary:
A bridge between Do It Tomorrow (which introduced “no backlogs”) and Zero Resistance. Resistance here arises from backlog pressure — the environment itself creates resistance.

5. “The Secret to Doing the Right Thing” (p. 88)

“Human beings tend to follow the path of least resistance. So if it’s easier to do the right thing, then the right thing is what you will do… The secret… is to arrange things so that it is easier to do the right thing rather than the wrong thing.”

🟢 Commentary:
This is the central insight later formalized in Zero Resistance (2022) — that productivity succeeds when friction is minimized in the direction of meaningful work.
