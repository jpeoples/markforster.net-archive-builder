I've just written in SuperFocus (C1):

"Spend $75/month to drink one Monster every day, even though they don't really taste that good."

I'm wondering if that will work on my intuitive side to fight this silly addiction of mine to "Monster" sports drinks (luckily, the sugar-free kind).

Here's what I'm thinking: as I scan the list, (a) this item will serve as a periodic reminder, and (b) it will hopefully not stand out. Since I will see it while scanning, I'll be in a very receptive, intuitive mode, perhaps enhancing the message's effect.

Add to that the element of surprise, as I intend to add it to select pages at random, maybe 1/3 of my pages. It needs to be a novelty whenever I see it, or it will lose its impact. For extra novelty, perhaps I will word it differently each time. Also, I have used reverse psychology, framing it as a strikingly bad proposition. I am counting on my skeptical rebellious side to say, "Bah, I am *not* doing that!"

I haven't given much thought to this item's lifespan on the list, but I suppose dismissal would be a victory, or perhaps it will get "done" and crossed out.

We shall see. I will report back later. If you would like to try something similar, please share your experience here.

Thanks!

This has worked extremely well.
I went nine days energy-drink-free (confidence level: 0.005%), and it wasn't even difficult.

Now, since my goal was never to give them up completely—just to break the addiction and avoid spending ludicrous amounts of money on them—I have chosen to enjoy them on weekends only.

Last Sat., I bought two (special price!) and drank one Sat., one Sun. I've had no trouble avoiding them since and am looking forward to another brace of them this weekend.

In C1, waiting to be dismissed, still stands that original item: "Spend $75/month to drink one Monster every day, even though they don't really taste that good."

Funny thing is, now that I've stopped overdoing it, the ones I drank last weekend actually tasted good—it's a double win!
