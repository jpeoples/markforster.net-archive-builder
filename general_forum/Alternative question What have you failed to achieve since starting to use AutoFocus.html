Hi Laurence

What I have found with AF is that by seemingly creating backlog situations it is merely highlighting an issue. You realise you do not like checking email - your method previously was to schedule it - which worked well for you. However you now have the opportunity to see if there is a better way. I got to exactly the same poistion with my email and realised that by "just doing it" I was not addressing the issue, which was that I had more email coming in than I could realistically deal with in any given day. I therefore looked at that mail and took decisions to (a) cancel some subscriptions (b) set up rules to (i) instantly file certain newsletters so that I can review them in my own time and (ii) categorise emails so that I can scan and action specific groups of mail in isolation and (c) changed the way I check and action email. That change has significantly impacted my email in that I don't end up with the massive backlogs I had previously (with erratic time availability previously I "declared a DIT backlog" with alarming regularity!) yet feel in control of my mail.

Laurence it may be that the best method for you is to "just do it" by scheduling but be aware that AF gives you the space to look at the question "is there a better way?"

What have I failed to achieve since starting AF? I failed to achieve completion of a clutter clearing project I had started last year and I failed to complete a backlog project I also started last year. Mark - What I like most about AF, is that whatever I am doing, I am in the flow, i.e. without resistance, which must make for more efficiency on that particular task in the long run, per unit of attention.

What is happening is that my attention is getting spread about more, while at the same time the quality of that attention is improving. This means that ultimately things should get done better, and more things get done - but it will take longer to get them done in calendar terms. For this reason I have considered adding some kind of filter or buffer to regulate how much or what gets added to the system, though I would prefer not to add this left brain activity if possible.

As you see, although I have been using AF for three months now, I am still pondering its benefits and possible ways to improve it. So in a nutshell, I am doing it because I really like it, and that, rightly or wrongly, may be the main advantage for me so far!

Laurence

I really like the way you describe the increase in efficiency as relating to the feeling of being "in the flow".

The longer you use AF the more you can look back and see "how" it has changed things. I have posted before about how AF identifies reasons for resistance, and that we then have to choose whether or not to act on that knowledge. When I look back at the areas where I have struggled with AF I believe that they relate almost exclusively to areas where, for whatever reason, I have chosen not to act on that knowledge.

About two weeks ago I made the decision to act on something AF had shown me pretty early on which resulted in my dumping several hundred items into AF to address a particular issue. I started that process on 21 March at which time I had 521 open tasks. That crept up to 708 over the period but today, when I completed the bulk of that project, my number of open tasks is currently standing at 482 - less than when I started that exercise (and actually less than when I started keeping detailed statistics on 23 February. Clearing that backlog was not without a degree of boredom (and lots of cups of coffee!) so I cannot say I was always in the flow, but the resistance level was low, and the fear factor significantly reduced.

Only one more significant backlog to address now ...... :-)
