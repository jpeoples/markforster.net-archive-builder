I am finding that SF works best for me when I put everything in it (during my normal working hours) including time-dependent tasks and tasks I would do anyway. I mention this because, in some of the commentary on SF I read that things I'd do anyway and calendared tasks and nondiscretionary tasks don't get written into SF. SF does not contain things we'd do anyway or things on the calendar (diary).

My goal is to write EVERYTHING down before I do it. I've been striving for this for about a week. I find this practice more significant than the particular rules for C2. Mark talks about writing things down in the DIT book. He mentioned something about writing creating a buffer between the impulse and action.

David Hume said that "reason is and ought only to be the slave of the passions." And it is true that the emotions are what motivate people to do tasks. Reason alone is inert. Hume notwithstanding, I like that the writing buffer allows my passions to cool ever so slightly so that reason has a little more time to calculate a better course of action.

ubi and Jupiter,

Well, I tried to word my post carefully by using the words "try" and "strive" to write everything.

When the phone rings, I answer it. I do not first write "phone."

When Mary walks into my office I talk to her. I do not first write "Mary."

I only use SF during normal business hours. I work away from home. I only use SF at the office. So, dressing does not get included.

But I eat a few times a day at work. I will write "eat" before I do it. (Or, I try to. Sometimes I forget.)

Before I leave my office to go to the bathroom I write "WC".

I am finding that this is a wonderful discipline that I sorely need.

When I started with SF I rarely used C2. But I was taking microbreaks without documenting them on the list. Now, as I try to be more self-aware, if I break off from one activity, I log that change in my list.

What I have learned after one week is that this practice is forcing me to be more honest with myself. It forces me to step back and ask, "What am I doing?" That is a very simple question. Sometimes I realize the answer is that I am avoiding some other aversive task. Somtimes, I realize that and continue to avoid it. But I believe that my increased self-awareness is a good thing.

As I said, I've only been doing this for about a week. It's still a work in progress. I will let you know how it develops.

When I was coaching, I used a simple method with very impulsive people. I told them that they must write everything down before doing it. Interposing a higher level activity (writing) between impulse and action causes the brain to switch from reactive mode to rational mode. This is a significant difference because it actually involves using different parts of the physical brain.
