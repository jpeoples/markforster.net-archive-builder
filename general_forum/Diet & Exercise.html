Two ways to go:
1) Be convinced that the SINGLE MOST important new habit in your life is exercise, and nothing in this world is going to keep you from your daily commitment. As long as you keep your focus on this one thing, you will find a way to make it happen.

2) Remain unconvinced, but make your new exercise habit as easy as possible to keep: You only need to get your exercise clothes on every day. You don't need to actually work. Seriously.

Because this commitment is so easy and painless, you actually will develop the habit. And you will find you actually do get some exercise done. Your worst objections don't stop you, because you can convince yourself it won't be that bad and you are free to quit if it does.

I read of a guy who trained to run a marathon following the maxim, "Every day I must run half as much as the previous day". So if Monday he ran 2 miles, Tuesday he only had to run 1 mile. If he did 1 on Tuesday he only had to do 1/2 on Wednesday. What happened is that some days he did only that half. Other days, he was energetic and did more than was required, and actually increased his mileage. The habit grew out of enjoyment rather than pressure.

Losing weight, argues Gary Taubes in a manner that I found convincing, is not about simply decreasing calories in and/or increasing calories out.

Of course, the first law of thermodynamics holds. But that simplistic equation leads people to assume that all calories are metabolically equivalent.

Taubes presents many journal articles supporting the conclusion that calorie QUALITY is as important, if not more important, than calorie QUANTITY. Some kinds of dietary calories can provide us with energy that is readily accessible for our cells to metabolize, so that we feel energetic and eager to increase the "calories out" side of the equation.

Other dietary calories can be consumed, and yet we feel even more hungry and more lethargic and fatigued, thereby reducing our calorie expenditure. Thus, these calories lead us to both increase intake and decrease expenditure, leading to weight gain.

Yes, the first law of thermodynamics cannot be avoided. But it can best be deployed by those whose goal is weight loss by recognizing that it is not true that "a calorie is a calorie."

Calories have qualities or kinds. Some kinds of calories increase satiation and energy expenditure. Some kinds of calories decrease satiation and energy expenditure. It would behoove the prospective dieter to discover for themself which kinds are the former and which kinds are the latter.

Good Calories Bad Calories

http://www.amazon.com/Good-Calories-Bad-Gary-Taubes/dp/1400040787

http://nymag.com/news/sports/38001/

I hate to mention this, but I can't resist, that the entire question of weight loss is completely academic to me. I have, all my life, battled against being underweight. Nonetheless, the ideas Taubes presents, have helped me normalize my weight.

Nutritional debates quickly tend to become heated and fanatical. I find Taubes to be quite level-headed in his approach. He concludes his book not by claiming that he knows all the answers and has discovered the best diet. There are not doubt many effective diets with many different macronutrient ratios. We all would be served by better, rigorous research that teases out the causal roles of the factors Taubes considers. In the meantime, we can each, individually, take a more experimental approach in our own diets.

I have found that about 70% of what Taubes writes is helpful in my personal situation, and about 30% just doesn't work. Yet I know that the 30% that doesn't work for me, works fantastically for other people.
