I've been using DWM for nearly three weeks now and I suspect, after a year of attempting to stick to AF, that this may actually be the one for me.

My problem has always been that I don't spend much time on-list, so I don't cycle through it very often, and I tended to cheat the dismissal rules on AF. I needed a system that I could look at quickly, find a task that needed doing, and do it - and a dismissal system I might actually stick to.

With DWM, I was initially worried that there would be too many pages to flip through to find what I needed to do quickly. I have RSI, so actually flipping through 15 pages to see the month's worth of tasks is sometimes a bit of a pain - with certain types of notebooks at least. But I've now settled on a little soft-back sewn notebook that's much easier to flick through than the ADOC/Circa type book I was using before. I'm writing two days on each page, giving me up to nine tasks on each day. For me, that's usually enough. It allows me to see the whole active list by flipping over eight pages, which is OK.

I go through the list at the beginning of the day, and put an asterisk next to each task that really needs to be done that day (or on either Saturday or Sunday if it's the weekend) and so I can easily see what needs doing when I look at the book during the day. If I feel like doing anything else, I can scan the list quickly to find something that appeals to me.

Tasks in the future that I'm resistant to slowly march closer to the present, during which time I often find I gradually come round to the idea of actually doing them - ie they don't scare me so much (my procrastination is perfectionism-based, and I often don't start tasks because I'm afraid I won't do them well enough). With AF, they hung around on the list, making me feel guilty that I wasn't doing them. With DWM, they're safely in the future for a while.

The fact that there is today's date on the page that is about to be dismissed seems to make it harder to ignore it and cheat. Today was the first day I had tasks left to do, and I was tempted to cheat as I had two tasks left and not a lot of time to work on them, but I did in fact do a little on each one and saved them.

I'll see what happens in the coming weeks, or when things get stressful. But I think this might be the system for me, the one that suits both my working pattern and my psychology - so thank you Mark.

I, too, feel that this is the system for me.

When I first started it, I was in the midst of preparing for comprehensive exams, so I added a major modification: Instead of cycling through the 28 pages repeatedly, I went through it once in the morning, pulling out the tasks I thought I would actually do that day. I can see why I felt I had to do that ~ it was a crazy couple of weeks, and I knew I would not be able to cycle through the list more than once, if even once, and I had to get the essential tasks done.

But now I've had a week of normal life again, and I decided that Mark's rules will work best during normal times.

I also made my own pages, and bound them with a plastic comb binder, because I can add and remove pages to keep the book slim.

My newly designed pages have a narrow column on the left for calendar items, and the rest of the page is for the list. I am cycling through the list several times a day, for both work and home, and it's working out great. I like having the calendar on the pages, too, because it keeps reminding me of upcoming events. It also seems to make a stronger connection in my mind that there are things coming up for expiration, and when they do expire, I think of them as "in the past."

Psychologically, I feel like I am doing things "ahead of time," maybe because they are on dates for future pages. With previous systems, including AF, I just felt like it was a huge list of things that have been on the list seemingly forever, and I felt "behind."
