Kenny,

>>> Quick question...in AF4, if you have items on your list (either in backlog or active), how do you close/convert the lists? <<<

One small point. You never "close" the "open list". You convert it to a "closed list" at the appropriate tiime.

>>> For example, if I am at work (in the office) and have "mow lawn" on the list, I could not dismiss backlogged items since I could not do the task even if I wanted to. <<

If you follow the rules exactly it might look like this:

- You are at work and make the last pass over the closed list
- There is nothing to do on that list
- You highlight the remaining open items
- You make a pass over the open list
- You draw a line at the end of the open list (marking the end of the new closed list)
- You come back to the closed list and decide for each item what to do with it
- "Mow the Lawn" would be copied to the end of the NEW closed list (after the line you just drew)
- When you get home and make your next full pass over the closed list, you'll hit "Mow the Lawn" so you get out the mower and do it.

Easy Peasy. ;-)

Liz said it perfectly ... my way of putting it is "one life, one list". To the extent that I want to focus my thinking on one area, I use "sub lists" within my SINGLE book that contains it all. Firstly, I'm not organized enough to be sure I take the right list with me ... likely as not, I'd grab the wrong one and see all of the house renovation tasks while I was starting a software design meeting. (Don't laugh, I once drove from DC to Philly for a customer meeting and left my suit hanging in its suit bag on the hall tree. Trust me, if there is a way to screw these things up, I've already found it ;-)

The second factor is that I've found that I never know when I might want any particular bit of information. I might be at work and be talking about plumbing while getting coffee and someone tells me that he's done that and what is the size of the pipe, or whatever ... if it is all in one book, I have it, otherwise I've lost the opportunity. Of course, I also make calls at all kinds of odd times so I need everything there. It has NEVER been a problem to have the ratings of a water heater mixed in with software design specs with the details of the next vacation I just booked. It is trivial to keep that sorted out ... not so trivial to manage a bunch of books.

But that is just a matter of personal preference. We all work differently.

As to the second point, the size of the list, I don't see that as a problem in AF. AF requires you to look at ONLY ONE TASK at a time, make a decision, then move to the next. It is very robotic that way. If you look at all of the pages of your book it might seem large, but if you are processing the list ... it is one task at a time ... one foot in front of the other.

As to sub lists, I do that to keep things organized, not so much to keep the list short. IOW, when I'm thinking "Build Deck" I want to see the tasks associated with that in some kind of order. I don't want to see: "Apply Finish Coat of Sealer" before "Pour Foundation". LOL ;-) THAT is what would drive me over the edge ;-) So I have sub lists for "Talk to Wife", "XMAS gifts this year", "Build Deck", "Overhaul Computers", "Organize Files", etc. Anything that has a few sequenced tasks that are not completely trivial. On that note, I will also have a broad "covering task" where there are a number of steps that ARE trivial but I don't write them down. "Work in Yard" might be one of those. When I get out there I can SEE what I have to do ... pull weeds, spray, mow, etc. I don't find I need to plan that kind of thing.
