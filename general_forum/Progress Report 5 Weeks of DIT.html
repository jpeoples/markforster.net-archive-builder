It’s been five weeks since I created my first closed list on September 15 and it’s time for me to assess my progress with DIT.

First, I will explain what aspects of DIT I am not doing.

I have not been doing Current Initiatives. I am not opposed to them. I just haven’t found the need or desire to implement them yet.

I have not done any kind of timed bursts or any other of the timing methods that Mark discusses. I have done similar things in the past. Again, they can be a very effective tool and it’s good to know that they are available if and when I need them.

I have used the self-talk recommendations a few times in the early weeks, but haven't had to use them since. I am referring to the kind of talk that has the form: "I won't write the employee evaluation now, I will just take out this folder."

Second, I will give some explanation of the way I have chosen to do DIT.

My basic tool for doing DIT is an Excel spreadsheet. I have created a template with two sheets or tabs. The first sheet is my main sheet. The second sheet is my backlog sheet, to be used if necessary.

The first sheet has four sections: Will-Do, Today, Immediate, and Tomorrow. In my Excel template I have listed all my recurring daily tasks in italics. I type in each day's Will-Dos and the print it out. Anything that I add to Immediate or Tomorrow gets handwritten in. The next morning I take the previous day's list to guide me what to type in.

I use the Outlook Calendar as a kind of Task Diary. It has my scheduled appointments and it also has tasks that I want to do on days further in the future than Tomorrow. (I also have a “tickler” file which does much the same thing.)

What I have found is that, as much as possible, I like to see all my work in one place. So if there are any tasks on my daily Outlook Calendar when I do my planning, I will add that task onto my Will-Do Excel list. This Excel printout becomes my "dashboard" for the entire day. I know that Mark talks about using the task diary and folding the corners of a page in when that day's tasks are complete. I fully understand the convenience of doing that, as opposed to rewriting yesterday's undone tasks on today's Will-Do list. In the past, I used to find it incredibly draining to carry forward and rewrite the same task day after day.

But now, with DIT, I insist that I rewrite any undone task from yesterday's list. I require that I do this as another way to discourage me from leaving any undone items on my Will-Do list. Better to just do them than to rewrite them another day.

As I’ve mentioned elsewhere on this forum, for the first three weeks or so I was abusing my Tomorrow list. I actually didn’t even title it “Tomorrow,” I titled it “Future.” As I reread the book I realized what a mistake this was. My future list became a dumping ground for tasks and projects that I was resisting facing head-on. I kept recopying them day after day, carrying them forward. I was creating a debilitating and demoralizing overhanging backlog and didn’t understand what I was doing. When I finally did come to my senses, I declared my very first backlog and worked my way out of it.

From Day 1 of my DIT implementation, I have been creating my Will-Do list in the morning. I know that this means that I am reducing the buffer between the time work arrives and the time I plan it, but this has worked for me. At some vague future time, I might try planning the evening before, but for now, I am sticking with planning in the morning. I believe that creating my plan is one of the most important things I can be doing. I am definitely a “morning person.” I like planning when I am fresh, alert, and energetic. I don’t think I am at my best at the end of the day and I don’t want to be rushing through my next day’s plan then.

In the morning, when I am planning out my list, I will go through all my e-mails (I don’t usually get a huge number of them). Any e-mail that requires action, I will write on my Will-Do list. I will open all the mail that I received the day before. Any snail mail that requires actions (even if that means further, careful reading) I will write on my Will-Do list. I avoid putting on my Will-Do list “review yesterday’s e-mail” or “review yesterday’s snail mail.”

As I go through the prior day's e-mail and papers I will, occasionally, do some things that can be done quite quickly, rather than write them on the list. I think that this violates a strict interpretation of the Closed List principle, since I am both working, and creating a list of my work, at the same time. But I feel that I have very strict limits (a kind of "two-minute" rule) and this has not presented a problem for me.

So what have I learned from these past five weeks?

First, I learned that I had been working woefully inefficiently prior to using DIT.

Second, I realized that I had a lot more work than I thought. When I was doing GTD I had huge lists of items with either no deadlines or meaningless deadlines. Most of what I do has no definite deadlines. This meant that I could easily fool myself into thinking that I had plenty of time to do other things. Now, with DIT, I understand that there are many important things that I need to be doing, and now, with DIT, I am spending my time doing them.

Third, by using DIT I have changed the way I work and I have changed the kind of person I am. I used to engage frequently in what Mark calls “displacement” activities as a means of avoiding important but resistance-inducing activities. Now, I am fully committed to completing my list every day. I still don’t want to make difficult phone calls, have difficult conversations, or write difficult analyses. But, each day I see a long list of items that I am committed to completing by the end of the day and there is no one else to do them but me. So I jump in and do them. I have become more confident and have experienced a real boost in energy. I attack my projects in a manner that has surprised me.

And I have experienced some serious successes in this short period of time. I still face many daunting challenges, but some problems that have haunted me for a very long time have been resolved. It’s not entirely due to DIT, but I give DIT a large part of the credit.

I am not the first to point out that GTD is much more about writing thoughts down than about getting things done. Now don't get me wrong. It's extraordinarily important to have a good system for writing thoughts down, and GTD provides such a system. But it does not, in itself, provide a structure that is conducive to converting those written thoughts into completed actions. And that is precisely where DIT comes in. DIT gets me from written to done.

That finishes my progress report.

Now I would like to add a few comments about my choice to plan my Will-Do list the same morning instead of the prior evening.

As I understand him, Mark explains the need for a buffer by postulating that this time difference between when work shows up and when work gets done allows the rational mind to kick in and prevent the reactive mind from controlling decision-making. Mark suggests that the reactive mind is not very good at decision-making because it tends to think only about the very near-term time horizon, and decisions based on only near-term considerations tend not to advance our overall well-being as much as decisions based on long-term considerations.

So far, so good. But this suggests that it might be even better to plan in detail farther out than one day. Why not "Do It a Week from Tomorrow?" In DIWT, all of the work that comes in today is scheduled for eight days from today. What's wrong with DIWT?

I would suggest that the problem with DIWT is that between today and a week from tomorrow so much new information will arrive that it's almost a certainty that any Will-Do list that I create for a week from tomorrow will become obsolete long before a week from tomorrow arrives. It's hard to make decisions today about what to do a week from tomorrow, from an information perspective. From an information perspective, we want to make decisions as close as possible to the time that we are going to implement the decision. It's a lot easier to decide whether to have a picnic today, when our knowledge of today's weather is quite good, than it is to have made that decision eight days ago.

So, if I understand things correctly, we have two opposing considerations. On the one hand, we want to make the buffer between the time of the decision and the time of the action as large as possible to increase the likelihood that we will disable the reactive mind and advance our long-term interests. On the other hand we want to make the buffer as small as possible to maximize the information relevant to the decision.

Once again, make the buffer too small and we get tossed to and from by the shifting currents of daily randomness. Make the buffer too large and we are acting based on very stale information.

The trick is to satisfy these opposing tendencies. And that's the beauty of DIT.

I would suggest that, in fact, it does not take a very large buffer to depotentiate the reactive mind. The emotional feelings that dictate reactive decisions tend to dissipate rapidly--usually in a matter of minutes. So it has been my experience that planning in the morning has not led me to feel as if I am being any less rational than if I planned the prior evening. In fact, if anything, I feel that I am being more rational, since I am able to take into account more current information, based on e-mails and voicemails that came in overnight.

One point I forgot to reply to earlier from Moises:

The primary purpose of the buffer is not to prevent the reactive mind from dominating (though it does do that), but to enable us to impose order on the random events of our working day by batching up our work so we can do it more speedily and efficiently.

If the buffer is longer than a day, the amount of work in a batch is likely to be daunting and the delay in replying to/actioning items is unacceptably long. If it's shorter than a day one is likely to jeopardise the connection between the amount of incoming work equalling the amount of outgoing work.

Let’s examine simplified versions of the person who plans the night before and the person who plans the same morning.

1. They both empty their in-box only once every 24 hours.
2. They both create a Will-Do list immediately upon emptying the in-box.
3. They both have neutralized the reactive mind.
4. They both reap the benefits of batching.
5. The night planner has a lag (15 hours?) between creating the list and commencing work on it.
6. The day planner has no lag between creating the list and commencing work on it.

Is the choice of day or night planning purely a matter of personal taste, or is there some DIT principle that should nudge us one way or the other?
