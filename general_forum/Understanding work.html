Last week I discovered myself slightly unbalanced by working a never-ending list. To rectify that, I'm reviewing my home and work lists to grok what all the work is about.

So far I have marked all tasks:

+ important/priority
' small
\ routine/low urgency

Seeing those marks really illuminates what's going on for me. Each page has a mixture with some of each. I can study this mix and decide if it reflects what I ought to be doing. Next I'm clarifying the things I want to do from the things I feel I should do. In the process, many tasks may get booted.

I also want to look through things to see what the purpose of each task. I aim to identify goals I'm working towards. Some stuff may be combined into projects. I aim to clarify projects, and rephrase them as something I clearly want to do.

I hope this process will make my work more focused, purposeful, and less random-feeling. I expect I'll only need to do it once. After this, I can markup and review as I work.

Thanks for the new word.

To grok (pronounced /ˈɡrɒk/) is to share the same reality or line of thinking with another physical or conceptual entity. Author Robert A. Heinlein coined the term in his best-selling 1961 book Stranger in a Strange Land. In Heinlein's view, grokking is the intermingling of intelligence that necessarily affects both the observer and the observed. From the novel:

Grok means to understand so thoroughly that the observer becomes a part of the observed—to merge, blend, intermarry, lose identity in group experience. It means almost everything that we mean by religion, philosophy, and science—and it means as little to us (because of our Earthly assumptions) as color means to a blind man.

To your point, shouldn't you start with the goals first and then move to the tasks? I too have tried to move away from the never ending list and have been experimenting with a daily list of six important items set the night before pulled from my project lists. So, during the day I only work the six items until they are done, or a truly more important items which arises during the day trumps something on the list.

This six item method is based upon the supposed story of Charles Schwab of Bethlehem steel and how he discovered this method. It seems to work and cuts down on list reviews until such time as you are done with the six items. It also seems to get me to complete more tasks whereas with AF, I could just work a bit, cross a task off and then re-write it at the end.

May I suggest to treat each task as a 3 step thing.
You have: Prep, Doing and Clean-up.
If every time you have done something, you clean-up after, it becomes a habit and you don't even have to follow-up on that as a specific task later...

Obviously, clean-up is more then just the physical thing. It also means identifying what you have accomplished and what's next about that. You are still on the inertia of that things so it's more easy to do it now then when you pick it up again later...

I've spend ages trying to put things like 'do my accounts paperwork' on a 'to-do' list (either AF or some other) only to end up igmoring it. However if I have a habit that I spend a hour on a Monday and a Friday doing this task, then I'm much more likely to get some progress.
