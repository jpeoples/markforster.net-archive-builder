Steve

As Mark says above "A task which stands out should feel as if it's ready to be done, as if any resistance to doing it has evaporated." The "standing out" with AF is very different to what you are describing - but in itself is hard to explain because it is basically a "feeling".

To try to explain that "feeling" - for example, I may be sitting at my desk working and suddenly think "I feel like a cup of coffee". The ability to make that cup of coffee or the availability of the tools to make it have not altered - the only thing that has changed is that I suddenly "feel like a cup off coffee" or in AF terminology that desire is "standing out". If I had a list with "coffee" on it, sometimes I would "feel like" having a cup of coffee and sometimes I wouldn't. Resistance would not even come into the equation - obviously I could choose whether or not to act on that "feeling" - AF would encourage you to do so.

That "feeling" has little to do with a rational response - or to outside curcumstances. The effect of outside circumstances may influence our action but NOT the feeling. If I have someone coming round for coffee I will make coffee, drink it, and no doubt enjoy it. Whether or not I feel like it is, at that point, largely irrelevant. The action of that task in that scenario is, in effect, a scheduled task. I may or may not feel like it - but I will do it because it is a scheduled task.

Suppose I am working at my desk and someone telephones me and asks me whether I want to go to the cinema. Regardless of whether I am busy there will be an immediate involuntary subconscious response - I will either "feel like" going or I will not. Whether I can realistically go is irrelevant - the reasons for wanting to go or not, for this scenario are irrelevant. What is important is that "feeling". THAT is what you are looking for with AF.

It sounds bizarre (and frankly feels quite bizarre) but under AF you do find yourself "feeling" like doing things you would not expect to feel like doing. That is the amazing beauty of the system - that it does somehow create the environment by which you "feel" like doing the things you have previously procrastinated on. If you continue to feel immense resistence to things I would venture to suggest that that has nothing to do with procrastination but rather is an indication that either you do not subconsciously wish to do the item concerned or that you are trying to enforce a "rational" timeframe upon the item.

Going back to the cinema analogy - if I were to feel that I didn't want to go then there would be a reason - maybe I want to do something else, maybe I don't want to see the film that's on, maybe I hate cinema's. Whatever the reason the feeling itself is what is valid - AF, by it's dismissal policy enables us to look at the reason for "not feeling like doing" something. With regard to the coffee analogy - it may be that I have put it on my list because it is "the thing to do" but I hate coffee and therefore will never in any shape or form "feel" like doing it. However, I like tea, so I can rephrase the task as "make tea" or even just "get a drink". Now when I see that task the resistance has vanished ......

Obviously those are very simplistic examples but i hope that helps in some way - Dean that may also answer some of your question?

@Mark

I will give you an example to try and explain things further.

I love fixing computer problems; in fact there isn’t a problem I can’t either resolve or workaround. When faced with this type of task all of the skills that hinder me in normal day to day things, come to the forefront. Things like whole system thinking, logic, attention to detail and analytical thinking etc. I can get so much into the zone when dealing with an issue that I can suffer easily from hyper-focus, and then I forget to eat, sleep etc. It is like all consuming at the time, because I love that part. Finding out how things tick and what stops them from ticking :)

On the flip side to this, when the work is done then to me the mundane/tedious aspects come into play. I have to raise an invoice for the work that I have done – I don’t like this part. I like doing the work; I don’t like doing the admin. But obviously this is an important aspect, but even though I know and realise the importance. I never feel like doing it, I never want to do it and given a choice I would much sooner do something else.

Here is where DIT wins through for me. I do the work today, then as a matter of process I put on tomorrow’s list ‘Raise invoice for xyz’. In today, do tomorrow. The process in itself is now almost automated. Because I am not going to do anything with it until tomorrow, it is out of my mind and I don’t dwell on it. It is now a planned action for tomorrow. Tomorrow my aim/goal/target is to clear my list – that is where I aim my focus. It really isn’t something I need to think anything more about, it is on the list. I aim to at least attempt its completion.

Autofocus on the other hand – ‘Raise invoice for xyz’ gets added to the end of the list. Now I don’t have a daily aim/goal/target – it is left to my own intuition. Here I know ‘Raise invoice for xyz’ is important it will leap out at me. But I don’t like doing that part and the process ‘in today/do tomorrow’ is no longer at play. So it will not get done because I don’t enjoy that part and I can not summon enough enthusiasm, left to my own devices, to address this area. In a way DIT puts the process on Autopilot for me – Autofocus allows me time to think. Time to think builds resistance because I think of all the reasons not to do something.

Hi Steve

That's a really clear illustration and if you have identified a way of dealing with those invoices that's great. From my experience with AF so far I would have no problem with putting that task on a schedule and doing it "tomorrow". If your "project management system" is such that you raise an invoice the day after completing the work then it would be part of your procedures to raise that invoice on the following day, i.e. at a scheduled time. If you put it on your schedule, and always action your scheduled items, that will deal with that type of scenario.

What I would suggest though is that you look at your overall administration and whether this is something you can outsource. You may not feel able to do that at present but I would imagine that your hourly rate is higher than you would expect to pay for administration services. Sometimes we fight our own systems when the solution is to delegate the task full stop. In the interim, or as an alternative, it should be easy enough to set up your system to virtually produce your invoices automatically. If you use a suitable accounts package invoice creation for the type of work you do could be almost instantaneous once set up.

One trick to review your current procedures is to treat yourself as your own client - imagine someone coming to you and saying "I need a solution to .......". Planning for that other person is always easier because you are not emotionally tied to the problem.
