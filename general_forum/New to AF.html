Welcome to the forum Mitch! AF is a flexible system that can be implemented in many ways. Regardless of your apporach, it buys you:
* everything is captured
* you are constantly working on many things
* you are kept aware of things to work on
* you can't neglect things unintentionally.

Your approach above is perfectly valid. It does suggest that GTD well ingrained in your brain. That may be okay, but AutoFocus users tend not to emphasize contexts the way David Allen does. Putting a context on all your tasks as you suggest and then doing the lot together may work. [Anybody else tried this? How'd it go?]

Here's another way, not necessarily better:

My AF list consists of projects, tasks, things to think about, etc. If there are a bunch of thing I want to do as a unit, there will only be one entry in AF for that bunch. In AF I write "Meet with Boss to discuss stuff", and "go shopping". Separately, I keep lists "To discuss with boss", "shopping list". Because it's convenient, I might initially add "Buy Bread" to AF , but when I get around to processing that entry, I'll realize that's something I should move to the shopping list and do so.

I'm using AF4. I have four or five major "contexts" which includes three different physical locations. I've found it works best for me to have one notebook for everything.

When I'm working (either working from home, or at the office), I just skip over the personal tasks -- unless it's actually a small, pressing task that I can do while at work in good conscience, such as make a phone call to the mechanic or something. If a larger personal task presents itself as REALLY needing to be done NOW, then maybe that means it's time for lunch break. :-)

When it's quiet at home in the evening when the kids are in bed, I have some tasks like personal reading, discussing some things with my wife, and other things. I just skip over the rest.

On the weekends, I skip over the work stuff and focus on the personal / family stuff.

It generally works really well. It usually takes me a few days to get to the point where I go through my closed list and nothing wants to be done -- so the stuff that gets highlighted really does DESERVE to be highlighted.

If I find that stuff is getting highlighted that SHOULDN'T be getting highlighted, then that means my time is out of balance. If the personal tasks are getting neglected, then maybe it means I am working too much overtime and need to pull back for a week or two. Etc.

I also "tag" a few different kinds of items -- usually stuff to discuss with my wife. I just put her name over on the right margin -- it's easier to find when scanning the pages.

I put shopping lists and things like that in the back of my AF book. Let's say I think of something I need to get at the hardware store. I'll add it to my hardware store list in the back of the book, and then I'll add "hardware store" to my AF list. When I come across the task while processing AF, and it "feels ready", then I'll look on my calendar and make time for going to the hardware store.

Just some ideas -- the main thing is just stick with it and you'll eventually find a groove that works for you. If some task or idea or goal arises and you just don't know what to do with it, just add it to the AF list and let it percolate for awhile till you figure out what to do with it. That way nothing is ever lost.

I really like that aspect of AF over GTD -- you aren't "allowed" to add something to GTD till you have figured out the "next action". AF isn't so strict -- you can give it thoughts, ideas, partially thought out tasks, etc., and it will handle them all just fine.

Seraphim wrote:
"I really like that aspect of AF over GTD -- you aren't "allowed" to add something to GTD till you have figured out the "next action". AF isn't so strict -- you can give it thoughts, ideas, partially thought out tasks, etc., and it will handle them all just fine."

This indeed is the major advantage of AF contrary to GTD: self-regulating your commitments (AF) instead of disciplining yourself to make front-end decisions for *everything* (GTD), even for stuff that needs to be done although this stuff is not feasible. AF's dismissal rules show you what goes wrong very fast, while with GTD it's possible to stay on the wrong track for a long time. In GTD there is no place for failure, AF allows you to learn from failure.
