Yesterday I was looking at my filing system, which is a constant source of trouble for me. It needs so much maintaining, more than I am giving it, and therefore it's always a mess, which makes me even more unwilling to put efforts in it... I really would love to learn of a system that handles all the paper/tax/bookkeeping/letters/newspaperclippings/etc. stuff the way AF handles task management!

Two glimpses of hope: I have developed two principles that help me to keep the mess lower than I deserve it. They are, in a way, self organizing things, and I'd like to present them here in the hope that maybe someone has something to add.

1. AUTOCLEAN: Handles all private bills, receipts, whatever - all these amounts of printed papers you collect around the stuff you buy for your household and most likely never need again, except one time in five years, but then urgently! (For example, something gets broken on which you still have guarantee: Now go and find the bill to prove it.)

I decided to put all these papers into one hanging folder, labeled RECEIPTS, BILLS, SALES SLIPS. "Put" means: I simply throw everything in and don't care for it anymore. My experience is that I need something out of this folder on a rate of once a year at the most: So, its better not to put the effort into a neat filing but in searching the place where it has to be - somewhere - once I should need it. (Once you need it, the motivation is sufficiently higher anyway ;-D)

OK - but this folder would grow, grow, grow! So I installed a second one, labeled OLD RECEIPTS ETC. The folder is full after two years. So, on new years morning of every odd year, I throw everything that is in to OLD folder in the paper basket (without looking at it) and EXCHANGE it with the other (I exchange the labels as well, of course). Now, I have an empty folder to fill again for two other years.

This little routine allows to find everything of the last 2 years minimum, which had been sufficient for more than 10 years now, and keeps this part of my filing system clean.

2. AUTOORDER: I use magazine files for my projects, for preparation of holidays, for everything else that includes paperwork. (I use a system called MAPPEI, http://www.mappei.net ) The file folders stand in boxes and are labeled on the top, so I can find everything quickly.

The problem is that the number of file folders grow over time. You start with one box, which is full one day, so you need a second etc. To keep it manageable, you need to put things out. You have to identify file folders you don't need anymore to throw them away, or you need at least not so often that you have to have them at your desk but elsewhere.

I used to have file folders of the same type together, ordered alphabetically: All writing projects in these boxes, travel preparations in another box, computer stuff in another etc. But I gave up this system when I came across a great idea: Someone suggested to have one box (the one on my desk) as the Main Box, and whenever I take one file folder out somewhere, I put it back into the Main Box, and IN FRONT OF ALL OTHER FOLDERS THAT ARE IN IT.

This way, the folders order themselves! I always have the most recent folders in front of me, the ones I will most likely need again. Once I don't need a file anymore, it moves slowly into the back, and once the Main Box gets full, I only have to move the last ones out; they go into my other boxes on the shelf. In front, of course. (And once these are full, I can throw away the last ones!)

===

These are my 2 little tricks - but I would like to know more of them. More tricks how to get things to do themselves!

Being a writer, I am collecting lots of clips from newspapers, magazines etc. My approach is to just collect anything that "rings a bell in me" (or "stands out" :-D) and throw it in a large folder. After a while, this folder is bursting with paper. Then I have a session where I sit on the floor, having the content of the folder before me, and try to group what I have. Usually I find most of the items fitting easily together, because they are about subjects I am interested in, so I end up with a dozen piles that can be stowed away under labels like "ecosystem", "archaeology" or "spacecrafts". About 20% of the collected material however resists ordering - so I let it in the large folder for the next time. Might be until next time these lonely items will have found sisters and brothers...

The beauty of that AUTOARCHIVE approach is that you don't have to have a predefined structure. YOU'LL FIND OUT what you're interested in on the way. In my first session I was surprised to find that I have a strong interest in bizarre numbers and statistics; I wasn't aware of that!

I have, however, neglected to take care of my archive for a long time. I've created the task *ARCHIVE SESSION* now on my AF list...

Some things I do along these lines:

1) Manage bills electronically. Use your bank as your filing system. You can now get eBills from lots of places. You can instruct your bank to pay by the due date and just review to see if you have a problem each month before that date. You can go paperless with many companies ... they will email you the bill or you can get it from their site.

2) When you buy a gizmo, go to the company website and see if they have the manual in PDF format. If so, download and file it. Throw away some of the manuals and such. Do you REALLY need an operating manual on your toaster?

3) Rip out the English section of manuals and throw away all the other languages. (Unless you like reading multilingual transliterations for fun ;-)

4) Keep a file for things that you will possibly return. Think about it though. Are you really likely to take a company up on its "lifetime warranty" for an item that cots $5.00? I would not. The postage alone would not make it worth it to get a replacement and often you'd want something better after a few years anyway. That being the case, the file can stay small.

5) Keep a separate file (usually an envelope) for short term receipts. IOW, if something has a 60 day return, put it in that envelop so you can purge it after that time.

6) Scan receipts and statements. I got a Fujitsu ScanSnap and it works like a charm to scan everything. I "file" the pape, after scan,r in a box for destruction later. I don't immediately shred because ... well, because computers are unreliable. (But see above, most statements are available for download now so you don't really even have to scan any more.) But if you do that, back up your files to CD or DVD periodically.

7) Purge your files continually. I keep a tall colored marker that I move through the files. When I have a free moment I pull a file and purge it, then move the marker along. That way file maintenance does not become a big project to be dreaded.

6) Separate current files from archive files. IOW, you are going to access the file for your electric company monthly so keep it in your bottom desk drawer along with mortgage, phone, etc. Other files are accessed only yearly or less frequently so can be more out of the way. Others are simply for protection ... old taxes are like this ... save for six years in case of an audit. They can be in the garage or basement in boxes with the "destroy date" marked on the box.

Just some ideas.

And two more tips for you ...

1) If you have a bulb separate again, use a potato to get it out, not pliers. Cut the potato, jam it into the socket and twist. The jagged parts of the broken base wiill be caught by the potato and zip, it usually comes right out ... with less chance of damaging something.

2) (And this is one I have to keep reminding myself of) the internet is a source of infinite "tips". Open Google and ask a question and, likely as not, someone has written an article on "making your own cleaning solutions" or "what season is best to prune a Japanese Maple" or "how do you deadhead roses" or ... it is actually pretty amazing.
