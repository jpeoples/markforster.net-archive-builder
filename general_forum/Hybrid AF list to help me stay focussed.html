For unknown reasons, I've bee struggling a bit with the 'pure' AF list, even though logically it seems like an ideal approach to staying on top of things.
I've been trying something over the past few days that seems to show some promise and wonder if any of you hardened AF-ficionados have any views.

I still have my long AF list and still (usually) process it in the prescribed manner. However, I've added a recurring item - 'Process day list' - which consists of the following steps.

1. scan through the AF list and make a note on a separate piece of paper (usually the back of an old envelope) of the few things that are important or urgent enough to warrant it.

2. Add any other time sensitive stuff if necessary, like visits, calls, errands etc.

3. work through the shorter day list for as long as I can stick it or until I've got the stuff that needs to be done today finished.

4. Go back to the AF list and process as normal, potentially going back to the day list (because its a recurring item on the AF list).

For me, this has a couple of benefits.

* It reduces distraction or the danger of cherry picking the simple stuff.
* It also allows me to use the 'burst mode' approach that Mark described in Get Everything Done.
* Its a great way of using all those junk mail envelopes that would otherwise go straight into recycle.
* As I tend to travel a fair bit with the job, it allows me to build more structure into my working life.

Comments anyone?

Yup, that is exactly how I've done AF since the days of AF1. I think of it this way:

- AF is my collection point
- My "today" list is my "key" TM tool
- I process the AF list only when I am finished the "today" list or can't proceed for the moment

I also use a system of "focus areas" to help guide what I cull from the list. IOW, this week I am focused on cleaning up shopping for the holidays so anything that supports that gets priority.

It is a very effective way of keeping focused on what needs to get done.

I find that AF4 works fine "as written" for my needs 95% of the time. But if I'm away from the list for a few days (illness or travel or whatever), I do this:

(1) Go through the whole AF4 list from beginning to end and move any "pressing" items to the end of the Open List. This takes about 10-15 minutes, sometimes less, since my total page count tends to stay around 10-12.

(2) After I'm done with (1), I start working through the Open List. From this point I just follow the normal AF4 rules.

(3) If those "pressing" items are still really "pressing", I find myself skipping through the items on the Open List till I come to the first of those "pressing" items. And then I just work through those as normal.

Works great.

I prefer this over a separate "daily" list because it's too easy for that single daily list to become a list of daily lists and then you're back to piles of scraps of lists and stickies and stuff. At least that's what would usually happen to me. :-)

I do something very similar.

Walter:

1) When I copy a task, I put a dot next to the item. When I return to the master list, I cross it out. If the task was not completed, I re-enter it.

2) Whether I apply AF rules to the sublist, depends on how large list might grow while I am away from the master. Usually, I only have ten or fewer items on the sub-list at a time. But if I get caught up away from master list for most of the day and a lot of new tasks arise, I start applying the typical AF rules for that list.

Also, I re-enter everything from my sub-list to the master once I return to it, even if it is a task that appeared and was completed without ever entering the master list. Keeping as many tasks worked on as possible on my master list gives me a simple record of when things were done and I believe allows me to be more confident in doing the task which stands out.

Hope this helps. Good luck.

I extract the next half-day's tasks into a separate list, for the reasons of focus/distraction minimisation that others have mentioned. But though it has these advantages, it has one disadvantage.

The mood that you have now may not be the mood that you have in a couple of hours' time. One of the ways AF keeps productivity high is that it keeps you doing at least something, rather than messing about. In objective terms, the something that you are doing may not be the most lucrative, or important; but it's better than spinning wheels, if that is what would otherwise happen at that moment. In due course, your mood will change, and your subconscious process will naturally pick out more valuable things to be doing.

I think that this characteristic of AF is lost with a "today's task list". I've found a half day list a reasonable compromise.
